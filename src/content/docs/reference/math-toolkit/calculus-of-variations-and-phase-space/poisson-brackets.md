## Summary

A **Poisson bracket** is the inverse form of a symplectic structure. In canonical coordinates $(q^i,p_i)$, with the symplectic convention

$$
\Omega=\delta q^i\wedge\delta p_i,
$$

the Poisson bracket of two functions $F(q,p)$ and $G(q,p)$ is

$$
\{F,G\}
=
\frac{\partial F}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial p_i}
-
\frac{\partial F}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial q^i}.
$$

The canonical brackets are

$$
\{q^i,q^j\}=0,
\qquad
\{p_i,p_j\}=0,
\qquad
\{q^i,p_j\}=\delta^i{}_j.
$$

Hamiltonian evolution is bracket evolution:

$$
\dot F=\{F,H\}+\frac{\partial F}{\partial t}.
$$

For fields on a spatial slice,

$$
\{F,G\}
=
\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\left[
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\phi^a(\mathbf x)}
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\pi_a(\mathbf x)}
-
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\pi_a(\mathbf x)}
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\phi^a(\mathbf x)}
\right].
$$

In particular,

$$
\{\phi^a(t,\mathbf x),\pi_b(t,\mathbf y)\}
=
\delta^a{}_b\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

The bracket has three jobs in QFT. It gives classical time evolution, identifies which charges generate which transformations, and supplies the classical expression that becomes the quantum commutator or anticommutator after quantization.

## Prerequisites

You should know [Symplectic Forms](/math-toolkit/calculus-of-variations-and-phase-space/symplectic-forms/), [Legendre Transforms](/math-toolkit/calculus-of-variations-and-phase-space/legendre-transforms/), and [Boundary Terms and Functional Differentiability](/math-toolkit/calculus-of-variations-and-phase-space/boundary-terms-and-functional-differentiability/). The field-theory formulas also use the distributional normalization from [Delta Functions](/math-toolkit/analysis-distributions-fourier/delta-functions/).

This page uses the same sign convention as the symplectic-form page:

$$
\Omega=\delta q^i\wedge\delta p_i,
\qquad
\iota_{X_H}\Omega=\delta H.
$$

With this choice, $\dot F=\{F,H\}$.

## Core idea

A symplectic form converts a function into a vector field:

$$
\iota_{X_F}\Omega=\delta F.
$$

Given two functions $F$ and $G$, we can convert both into Hamiltonian vector fields and then pair those vector fields using $\Omega$:

$$
\{F,G\}=\Omega(X_F,X_G).
$$

This is the coordinate-free definition of the Poisson bracket in the convention used here.

In canonical coordinates, this reduces to the familiar formula

$$
\{F,G\}
=
\frac{\partial F}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial p_i}
-
\frac{\partial F}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial q^i}.
$$

The bracket measures how one observable changes under the Hamiltonian flow generated by the other. Because of our sign convention,

$$
X_G(F)=\{F,G\}.
$$

Therefore $G$ generates the transformation of $F$ through

$$
\delta_G F=\epsilon\{F,G\}.
$$

This is the sentence that keeps canonical mechanics, Noether charges, and canonical quantization tied together.

## Setup and conventions

Let $\mathcal P$ be phase space with canonical coordinates $(q^i,p_i)$. For functions $F,G\in C^\infty(\mathcal P)$, define

$$
\{F,G\}
=
\frac{\partial F}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial p_i}
-
\frac{\partial F}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial q^i}.
$$

The bracket is bilinear, antisymmetric, and satisfies the Leibniz rule:

$$
\{F,G\}=-\{G,F\},
$$

and

$$
\{F,GH\}=\{F,G\}H+G\{F,H\}.
$$

It also satisfies the Jacobi identity:

$$
\{F,\{G,H\}\}
+
\{G,\{H,F\}\}
+
\{H,\{F,G\}\}
=0.
$$

These three properties say that observables form a Lie algebra under the Poisson bracket, and that the bracket acts as a derivation on products.

The basic canonical brackets follow immediately:

$$
\{q^i,q^j\}=0,
$$

$$
\{p_i,p_j\}=0,
$$

and

$$
\{q^i,p_j\}=\delta^i{}_j.
$$

When the phase space is infinite-dimensional, derivatives become functional derivatives and Kronecker deltas become Dirac deltas.

## Derivation from the symplectic form

Start from

$$
\Omega=\delta q^i\wedge\delta p_i.
$$

The Hamiltonian vector field generated by $F$ is

$$
X_F
=
\frac{\partial F}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial}{\partial q^i}
-
\frac{\partial F}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial}{\partial p_i}.
$$

Similarly,

$$
X_G
=
\frac{\partial G}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial}{\partial q^i}
-
\frac{\partial G}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial}{\partial p_i}.
$$

Pair them using $\Omega$:

$$
\Omega(X_F,X_G)
=
\frac{\partial F}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial p_i}
-
\frac{\partial F}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial q^i}.
$$

Thus

$$
\{F,G\}=\Omega(X_F,X_G).
$$

Equivalently, in matrix notation let

$$
z^A=(q^1,\ldots,q^n,p_1,\ldots,p_n).
$$

Then

$$
\{F,G\}
=
\frac{\partial F}{\partial z^A}\Pi^{AB}\frac{\partial G}{\partial z^B},
$$

where the Poisson tensor has components

$$
\Pi^{AB}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & I\\
-I & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

This is the object that maps differentials to Hamiltonian vector fields in the bracket convention of this page. If one represents the two-form $\Omega=\delta q^i\wedge\delta p_i$ by the covariant matrix $\Omega_{AB}$ in the same ordered coordinates $z^A=(q,p)$, then $\Pi^{AB}$ is the negative of the ordinary matrix inverse of $\Omega_{AB}$. That minus sign is just the sign convention encoded in $\iota_{X_F}\Omega=\delta F$ and $\{F,G\}=\Omega(X_F,X_G)$. Conceptually, $\Omega$ maps vectors to covectors, while $\Pi$ maps covectors to vectors.

## Hamiltonian evolution

Let $H$ be the Hamiltonian. Then for any time-independent observable $F(q,p)$,

$$
\dot F=X_H(F)=\{F,H\}.
$$

If $F$ has explicit time dependence, then

$$
\frac{dF}{dt}=\{F,H\}+\frac{\partial F}{\partial t}.
$$

For the coordinates themselves,

$$
\dot q^i=\{q^i,H\}=\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i},
$$

and

$$
\dot p_i=\{p_i,H\}=-\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}.
$$

So the Poisson bracket is not an extra structure after Hamilton's equations. It is Hamilton's equations written as an algebra of observables.

## Conserved quantities and symmetries

A time-independent function $Q$ is conserved if

$$
\dot Q=\{Q,H\}=0.
$$

It generates an infinitesimal canonical transformation of any observable $F$ by

$$
\delta_QF=\epsilon\{F,Q\}.
$$

If $Q$ is conserved, this transformation is a symmetry of the Hamiltonian evolution.

For example, suppose $P_i$ is the generator of spatial translations. Then for a scalar field,

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf x),P_i\}=\partial_i\phi(\mathbf x).
$$

Similarly, if a charge $Q$ generates an internal symmetry $\delta\phi^a=R^a(\phi)$, then one expects

$$
\{\phi^a(\mathbf x),Q\}=R^a(\phi(\mathbf x)).
$$

This is the Hamiltonian version of Noether's theorem: charges generate transformations through brackets.

## Field-theory Poisson brackets

For canonical field variables $\phi^a(\mathbf x)$ and $\pi_a(\mathbf x)$, the canonical symplectic form is

$$
\Omega_\Sigma
=
\int_\Sigma d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\delta\phi^a(\mathbf x)\wedge\delta\pi_a(\mathbf x).
$$

The corresponding Poisson bracket between functionals $F[\phi,\pi]$ and $G[\phi,\pi]$ is

$$
\{F,G\}
=
\int_\Sigma d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\left[
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\phi^a(\mathbf x)}
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\pi_a(\mathbf x)}
-
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\pi_a(\mathbf x)}
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\phi^a(\mathbf x)}
\right].
$$

The elementary field brackets are

$$
\{\phi^a(t,\mathbf x),\phi^b(t,\mathbf y)\}=0,
$$

$$
\{\pi_a(t,\mathbf x),\pi_b(t,\mathbf y)\}=0,
$$

and

$$
\{\phi^a(t,\mathbf x),\pi_b(t,\mathbf y)\}
=
\delta^a{}_b\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

The time argument is usually suppressed because the bracket is equal-time. The delta function is spatial, not spacetime:

$$
\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

This distinction matters. Equal-time canonical brackets are not Lorentz-covariant-looking objects, even when the theory is Lorentz invariant.

## Smeared field brackets

Pointwise field brackets are distributional. The cleaner way to read them is through smearing. Let

$$
\Phi[f]=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,f_a(\mathbf x)\phi^a(\mathbf x),
$$

and

$$
\Pi[g]=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,g^a(\mathbf x)\pi_a(\mathbf x).
$$

Their functional derivatives are

$$
\frac{\delta\Phi[f]}{\delta\phi^a(\mathbf x)}=f_a(\mathbf x),
\qquad
\frac{\delta\Phi[f]}{\delta\pi_a(\mathbf x)}=0,
$$

and

$$
\frac{\delta\Pi[g]}{\delta\pi_a(\mathbf x)}=g^a(\mathbf x),
\qquad
\frac{\delta\Pi[g]}{\delta\phi^a(\mathbf x)}=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\{\Phi[f],\Pi[g]\}
=
\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,f_a(\mathbf x)g^a(\mathbf x).
$$

This is the precise meaning of

$$
\{\phi^a(\mathbf x),\pi_b(\mathbf y)\}
=
\delta^a{}_b\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

The unsmeared formula is a compact distributional shorthand for the smeared pairing.

## Example: scalar field evolution

For the real scalar Hamiltonian

$$
H=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\left[
\frac12\pi^2
+
\frac12\nabla\phi\cdot\nabla\phi
+V(\phi)
\right],
$$

we have

$$
\frac{\delta H}{\delta\pi(\mathbf x)}=\pi(\mathbf x),
$$

and, assuming boundary terms vanish,

$$
\frac{\delta H}{\delta\phi(\mathbf x)}
=
-\nabla^2\phi(\mathbf x)+V'(\phi(\mathbf x)).
$$

Then

$$
\dot\phi(\mathbf x)
=
\{\phi(\mathbf x),H\}
=
\frac{\delta H}{\delta\pi(\mathbf x)}
=
\pi(\mathbf x),
$$

and

$$
\dot\pi(\mathbf x)
=
\{\pi(\mathbf x),H\}
=
-\frac{\delta H}{\delta\phi(\mathbf x)}
=
\nabla^2\phi(\mathbf x)-V'(\phi(\mathbf x)).
$$

Combining them gives

$$
\ddot\phi-\nabla^2\phi+V'(\phi)=0.
$$

The same result appeared on the symplectic-form page, but now it is packaged as bracket evolution.

## Example: translations of a scalar field

For a scalar field with canonical momentum $\pi$, the generator of spatial translations is

$$
P_i=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,\pi(\mathbf x)\partial_i\phi(\mathbf x),
$$

up to boundary conventions. Then

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf y),P_i\}
=
\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\{\phi(\mathbf y),\pi(\mathbf x)\}\partial_i\phi(\mathbf x).
$$

Using

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf y),\pi(\mathbf x)\}
=
\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf y-\mathbf x),
$$

we get

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf y),P_i\}=\partial_i\phi(\mathbf y).
$$

Thus $P_i$ generates translations of the field. Depending on whether one uses active or passive conventions for translations, an overall sign may be assigned differently to the transformation parameter. The bracket calculation fixes the convention.

## Functional differentiability comes first

The bracket formula assumes that $F$ and $G$ have good functional derivatives. This is not automatic. If

$$
\delta F
=
\int_\Sigma d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\left[
A_a\delta\phi^a+B^a\delta\pi_a
\right]
+
\int_{\partial\Sigma}\beta,
$$

then $F$ is not represented by the bulk derivatives $(A_a,B^a)$ unless the boundary term vanishes under the chosen boundary conditions or is canceled by an added boundary functional.

This is why Hamiltonian generators in gauge theory and gravity often need surface terms. A generator that is not functionally differentiable does not have a well-defined Hamiltonian vector field, and its Poisson bracket with other observables is not well-defined.

The practical workflow is:

1. Vary the candidate functional.
2. Integrate by parts.
3. Check the boundary term.
4. Only then read off functional derivatives and compute brackets.

Skipping step 3 is a reliable way to manufacture spurious charges or spurious gauge transformations.

## Constraints and weak equality

In constrained Hamiltonian systems, not every point of the canonical phase space is physically allowed. A constraint is a function or functional $C_a$ required to vanish:

$$
C_a\approx0.
$$

The symbol $\approx$ means **weak equality**: equality on the constraint surface. It should not be used before taking Poisson brackets. If $C\approx0$, it does not follow that $\{C,F\}=0$.

A constraint is **first-class** if its bracket with all constraints vanishes on the constraint surface:

$$
\{C_a,C_b\}\approx f_{ab}{}^c C_c.
$$

First-class constraints typically generate gauge transformations. A constraint is **second-class** if the matrix of brackets among constraints is invertible on the constraint surface. Second-class constraints do not generate gauge redundancy; instead, they remove variables by modifying the bracket.

The modified bracket for second-class constraints is the **Dirac bracket**. It is the bracket that correctly restricts the dynamics to the constraint surface. The details belong on the constraints page, but the moral belongs here:

$$
\text{the canonical Poisson bracket is not always the final physical bracket.}
$$

Gauge fixing and reduction can change the bracket one should use for the remaining variables.

## Gauge generators and boundary charges

In gauge theory, constraints often generate gauge transformations. For example, the Abelian Gauss constraint has schematic generator

$$
G[\alpha]
=
\int_\Sigma d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,\alpha(\mathbf x)\partial_iE^i(\mathbf x),
$$

possibly plus boundary terms. With the canonical bracket

$$
\{A_i(\mathbf x),E^j(\mathbf y)\}=\delta_i{}^j\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

one finds, after integrating by parts and fixing signs consistently,

$$
\{A_i,G[\alpha]\}\sim\partial_i\alpha.
$$

If $\alpha$ vanishes at the boundary, this is a pure gauge direction. If $\alpha$ does not vanish at the boundary, the generator usually needs an added surface term to be differentiable, and the transformation may carry a charge.

This is the Hamiltonian bracket version of the presymplectic statement from the symplectic-form page. Gauge transformations are null directions only after the constraint surface and boundary behavior have been specified.

## Equal-time brackets and covariance

Canonical Poisson brackets are usually written on a spatial slice. That makes them look noncovariant:

$$
\{\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)\}
=
\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

This is not a contradiction with Lorentz invariance. The canonical formalism chooses a time coordinate to describe the same invariant dynamics. Different choices of Cauchy slice give equivalent evolution when the theory is well-defined and the symplectic flux through boundaries is controlled.

There is also a covariant bracket, the **Peierls bracket**, defined through the response of observables to perturbations of the action. In ordinary globally hyperbolic settings, the Peierls bracket agrees with the canonical Poisson bracket after choosing a Cauchy slice. It is often conceptually cleaner in covariant field theory, while canonical brackets are usually quicker for calculations.

## From Poisson brackets to quantum brackets

Canonical quantization replaces classical brackets by quantum brackets. For bosonic variables, the heuristic rule is

$$
\{F,G\}
\longrightarrow
\frac{1}{i}[\widehat F,\widehat G],
$$

or equivalently

$$
[\widehat F,\widehat G]
=
i\widehat{\{F,G\}}
$$

when operator-ordering issues are absent. With $\hbar$ restored, this is

$$
[\widehat F,\widehat G]
=
i\hbar\widehat{\{F,G\}}.
$$

For canonical fields in units $\hbar=1$, this gives

$$
[\widehat\phi^a(t,\mathbf x),\widehat\pi_b(t,\mathbf y)]
=
i\delta^a{}_b\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

For fermionic fields, the classical phase-space structure is graded and the quantum equal-time relations are anticommutators rather than ordinary commutators. Grassmann and Berezin technology appear in the functional-integral chapter; the canonical graded bracket belongs to the spinor and constrained-system discussions.

The replacement rule is powerful but not magic. Operator ordering, domains, regularization, constraints, anomalies, and renormalization can all obstruct naive quantization.

## Poisson manifolds beyond symplectic manifolds

A symplectic form is nondegenerate, so it has an inverse. More generally, a manifold may have a Poisson bracket even if no globally invertible symplectic form exists. In that case the fundamental object is a Poisson tensor $\Pi$, not necessarily the inverse of a nondegenerate $\Omega$:

$$
\{F,G\}=\Pi(dF,dG).
$$

The Jacobi identity imposes a nonlinear condition on $\Pi$. Symplectic manifolds are the special case where $\Pi$ is invertible everywhere.

Why mention this here? Because constrained systems, reduced phase spaces, moduli spaces, sigma models, Chern–Simons theory, and deformation quantization often naturally produce Poisson structures that are not globally just $\delta q\wedge\delta p$ in disguise.

For most canonical QFT calculations, however, the standard field-theory bracket is the workhorse.

## Common pitfalls

**Using the wrong sign convention.** This page uses $\Omega=\delta q^i\wedge\delta p_i$ and $\dot F=\{F,H\}$. If a reference uses the opposite symplectic sign, its bracket convention may differ by a sign.

**Forgetting that field brackets are equal-time brackets.** The delta function in $\{\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)\}$ is spatial, not spacetime.

**Taking brackets before checking differentiability.** If a functional has leftover boundary terms in its variation, its bracket is not well-defined without more boundary data or counterterms.

**Setting constraints strongly too early.** If $C\approx0$, do not replace $C$ by zero before computing brackets. Weak equality is imposed after bracket operations.

**Confusing gauge transformations with physical symmetries.** A constraint-generated transformation that vanishes at the boundary may be pure gauge. A similar transformation with nonzero boundary parameter may carry a charge.

**Quantizing by slogan.** The rule $\{\cdot,\cdot\}\to(1/i)[\cdot,\cdot]$ is only the beginning. Composite operators, ordering, anomalies, constraints, and regularization matter.

**Ignoring smearing.** Unsmeared field brackets are distributions. Smearing makes the formulas mathematically meaningful and prevents delta-function mistakes.

## Research status

Poisson brackets in finite-dimensional Hamiltonian mechanics are standard. Their field-theory extension is also standard in classical field theory and canonical quantization, provided the field space, boundary conditions, and regularization are controlled.

Active issues arise where brackets meet gauge symmetry, boundaries, and infinite-dimensional analysis. Examples include gravitational surface charges, asymptotic symmetry algebras, edge modes, subregion phase spaces, covariant phase-space brackets, and quantization of constrained or singular Poisson structures.

In quantum field theory, canonical brackets are often formal until regulated. They become precise in lattice, finite-volume, free-field, perturbative, or algebraic settings with appropriate domains and operator definitions. Composite-operator brackets can acquire contact terms and anomalies, so classical bracket algebra should not be assumed to survive quantization unchanged.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Basic canonical brackets

Using

$$
\{F,G\}=\frac{\partial F}{\partial q}\frac{\partial G}{\partial p}-\frac{\partial F}{\partial p}\frac{\partial G}{\partial q},
$$

compute $\{q,p\}$, $\{q,q\}$, and $\{p,p\}$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $\{q,p\}$,

$$
\frac{\partial q}{\partial q}=1,
\qquad
\frac{\partial p}{\partial p}=1,
\qquad
\frac{\partial q}{\partial p}=0,
\qquad
\frac{\partial p}{\partial q}=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\{q,p\}=1.
$$

For $\{q,q\}$,

$$
\{q,q\}=1\cdot0-0\cdot1=0.
$$

For $\{p,p\}$,

$$
\{p,p\}=0\cdot1-1\cdot0=0.
$$

Thus

$$
\{q,p\}=1,
\qquad
\{q,q\}=0,
\qquad
\{p,p\}=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Harmonic oscillator evolution

Let

$$
H=\frac{p^2}{2m}+\frac12m\omega^2q^2.
$$

Use Poisson brackets to compute $\dot q$ and $\dot p$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Hamiltonian evolution is

$$
\dot q=\{q,H\}=\frac{\partial H}{\partial p}=\frac{p}{m}.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\dot p=\{p,H\}=-\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}=-m\omega^2q.
$$

Combining these gives

$$
\ddot q+\omega^2q=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Smeared scalar-field bracket

Let

$$
\Phi[f]=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,f(\mathbf x)\phi(\mathbf x),
$$

and

$$
\Pi[g]=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,g(\mathbf x)\pi(\mathbf x).
$$

Compute $\{\Phi[f],\Pi[g]\}$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The functional derivatives are

$$
\frac{\delta\Phi[f]}{\delta\phi(\mathbf x)}=f(\mathbf x),
\qquad
\frac{\delta\Phi[f]}{\delta\pi(\mathbf x)}=0,
$$

and

$$
\frac{\delta\Pi[g]}{\delta\pi(\mathbf x)}=g(\mathbf x),
\qquad
\frac{\delta\Pi[g]}{\delta\phi(\mathbf x)}=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\{\Phi[f],\Pi[g]\}
=
\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,f(\mathbf x)g(\mathbf x).
$$

This is the smeared form of $\{\phi(\mathbf x),\pi(\mathbf y)\}=\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)$.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Momentum generates translations

Let

$$
P_i=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,\pi(\mathbf x)\partial_i\phi(\mathbf x).
$$

Assuming boundary terms vanish, show that

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf y),P_i\}=\partial_i\phi(\mathbf y).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Using the canonical bracket,

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf y),P_i\}
=
\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\{\phi(\mathbf y),\pi(\mathbf x)\}\partial_i\phi(\mathbf x).
$$

Since

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf y),\pi(\mathbf x)\}
=
\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf y-\mathbf x),
$$

we get

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf y),P_i\}
=
\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf y-\mathbf x)\partial_i\phi(\mathbf x)
=
\partial_i\phi(\mathbf y).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5: Do not set constraints to zero too early

Let a system have canonical variables $(q,p)$ and a constraint

$$
C=p.
$$

Even though $C\approx0$, compute $\{q,C\}$. What does this show?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Using the canonical bracket,

$$
\{q,C\}=\{q,p\}=1.
$$

Thus $C\approx0$ does not imply $\{q,C\}=0$. Weak equality is imposed after taking brackets, not before. The constraint $C$ generates a shift of $q$.

</details>

### Exercise 6: Field equation from the bracket

For the free scalar Hamiltonian

$$
H=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,
\left[
\frac12\pi^2+\frac12\nabla\phi\cdot\nabla\phi+\frac12m^2\phi^2
\right],
$$

show that Poisson bracket evolution gives the Klein–Gordon equation.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The functional derivatives are

$$
\frac{\delta H}{\delta\pi}=\pi,
$$

and

$$
\frac{\delta H}{\delta\phi}=-\nabla^2\phi+m^2\phi,
$$

assuming boundary terms vanish. Therefore

$$
\dot\phi=\{\phi,H\}=\pi,
$$

and

$$
\dot\pi=\{\pi,H\}=-\frac{\delta H}{\delta\phi}
=\nabla^2\phi-m^2\phi.
$$

Using $\pi=\dot\phi$ gives

$$
\ddot\phi=\nabla^2\phi-m^2\phi,
$$

or

$$
\ddot\phi-\nabla^2\phi+m^2\phi=0.
$$

With the mostly-minus convention, this is

$$
(\Box+m^2)\phi=0.
$$

</details>
