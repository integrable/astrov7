## Summary

The exterior algebra $\Lambda V$ is the algebra generated by a vector space $V$ with an antisymmetric product $\wedge$ satisfying

$$
v\wedge v=0,
\qquad
v\wedge w=-w\wedge v.
$$

It decomposes into exterior powers,

$$
\Lambda V=\bigoplus_{k=0}^{\dim V}\Lambda^k V,
$$

where $\Lambda^k V$ is the space of fully antisymmetric $k$-tensors. If $e_1,\dots,e_n$ is a basis of $V$, then a basis of $\Lambda^kV$ is

$$
e_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge e_{i_k},
\qquad
1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le n.
$$

Therefore

$$
\dim\Lambda^kV={n\choose k}.
$$

For QFT, exterior algebra is the quiet machine behind forms and fermions. It explains why field strengths are two-forms, why $F\wedge F$ is a natural topological density, why determinants live on top exterior powers, why Pfaffians come from antisymmetric bilinear forms, and why Grassmann variables look like basis one-forms with multiplication promoted to a formal algebra.

## Exterior powers

Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$. The $k$th exterior power $\Lambda^kV$ is the vector space spanned by symbols

$$
v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_k,
$$

that are multilinear and alternating in the vectors $v_1,
\dots,v_k$.

Multilinear means linear in each slot. Alternating means the expression vanishes whenever two slots are equal:

$$
v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_i\wedge\cdots\wedge v_i\wedge\cdots\wedge v_k=0.
$$

Over characteristic zero, this is equivalent to saying that swapping two adjacent entries changes the sign:

$$
v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_i\wedge v_{i+1}\wedge\cdots\wedge v_k
=-v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_{i+1}\wedge v_i\wedge\cdots\wedge v_k.
$$

More generally, for any permutation $\sigma\in S_k$,

$$
v_{\sigma(1)}\wedge\cdots\wedge v_{\sigma(k)}
=\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)
 v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_k.
$$

The low-degree cases are worth memorizing:

$$
\Lambda^0V\cong\mathbb F,
\qquad
\Lambda^1V\cong V,
\qquad
\Lambda^2V=\text{antisymmetric two-tensors},
$$

and

$$
\Lambda^kV=0\qquad\text{for }k>n.
$$

The top exterior power $\Lambda^nV$ is one-dimensional. It is the algebraic home of oriented volume.

## Construction from tensor algebra

The tensor algebra of $V$ is

$$
T(V)=\bigoplus_{k\ge0}V^{\otimes k}.
$$

The exterior algebra is the quotient

$$
\Lambda V=T(V)/I,
$$

where $I$ is the two-sided ideal generated by all tensors $v\otimes v$. In the quotient,

$$
v\wedge v=0.
$$

Since

$$
(v+w)\wedge(v+w)=0,
$$

we get

$$
v\wedge w+w\wedge v=0,
$$

so

$$
v\wedge w=-w\wedge v.
$$

This quotient definition is compact and powerful: exterior algebra is the freest associative algebra in which repeated vectors square to zero.

For finite-dimensional $V$ over $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$, one may equivalently define $\Lambda^kV$ as the image of the antisymmetrization projector

$$
P_{\rm alt}=\frac1{k!}\sum_{\sigma\in S_k}
\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\rho(\sigma)
$$

on $V^{\otimes k}$. The quotient viewpoint emphasizes the product. The projector viewpoint emphasizes components.

## Basis and dimension

Let $e_1,
\dots,e_n$ be a basis of $V$. Because the wedge product is alternating, any wedge with repeated basis vectors vanishes, and any wedge can be reordered at the cost of a sign. Therefore

$$
e_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge e_{i_k},
\qquad
1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le n,
$$

forms a basis of $\Lambda^kV$.

There are ${n\choose k}$ increasing $k$-tuples, so

$$
\dim\Lambda^kV={n\choose k}.
$$

For example, in $n=4$ dimensions,

| Degree | Dimension | Typical geometric object |
|---:|---:|---|
| $0$ | $1$ | scalar |
| $1$ | $4$ | vector or covector, depending on whether using $V$ or $V^*$ |
| $2$ | $6$ | two-form, field strength |
| $3$ | $4$ | three-form |
| $4$ | $1$ | volume form |

For differential forms on a four-dimensional spacetime, these dimensions apply fiberwise to $\Lambda^kT_x^*M$.

## Wedge product and graded signs

The wedge product maps

$$
\Lambda^pV\times\Lambda^qV\to\Lambda^{p+q}V.
$$

For decomposable elements,

$$
(v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_p)
\wedge
(w_1\wedge\cdots\wedge w_q)
=
v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_p\wedge w_1\wedge\cdots\wedge w_q.
$$

It is associative:

$$
(\alpha\wedge\beta)\wedge\gamma
=\alpha\wedge(\beta\wedge\gamma).
$$

It is not commutative. It is graded-commutative:

$$
\alpha\wedge\beta=(-1)^{pq}\beta\wedge\alpha,
\qquad
\alpha\in\Lambda^pV,
\quad
\beta\in\Lambda^qV.
$$

The exponent $pq$ counts how many swaps are needed to move $p$ factors past $q$ factors. This sign is one of the basic sign rules of modern geometry and QFT.

Consequences:

$$
\alpha\wedge\alpha=0
\qquad\text{if }\alpha\text{ has odd degree},
$$

because

$$
\alpha\wedge\alpha=(-1)^{p^2}\alpha\wedge\alpha=-\alpha\wedge\alpha
$$

when $p$ is odd and the field has characteristic zero.

For even-degree elements, $\alpha\wedge\alpha$ need not vanish. In four-dimensional gauge theory, $F$ is a two-form, so $F\wedge F$ is generally nonzero.

## Exterior algebra versus alternating tensors

There is a canonical pairing between $\Lambda^kV^*$ and $k$ vectors:

$$
\omega(v_1,
\dots,v_k).
$$

Thus elements of $\Lambda^kV^*$ are alternating $k$-linear forms on $V$. In components,

$$
\omega=\frac1{k!}\omega_{i_1\dots i_k}
\varepsilon^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge\varepsilon^{i_k},
$$

where

$$
\omega_{i_1\dots i_k}=\omega(e_{i_1},
\dots,e_{i_k})
$$

is fully antisymmetric.

The factor $1/k!$ appears because the sum over all indices counts each increasing basis wedge $k!$ times. This is not a convention one can casually drop without moving factors elsewhere.

For example, a two-form is written

$$
\omega=\frac12\omega_{ij}\varepsilon^i\wedge\varepsilon^j,
\qquad
\omega_{ij}=-\omega_{ji}.
$$

Evaluating on two vectors gives

$$
\omega(v,w)=\omega_{ij}v^i w^j.
$$

The factor $1/2$ in the expansion disappears in the evaluation because the antisymmetric double sum has two equal contributions.

## Component formula for the wedge product

Let $\alpha\in\Lambda^pV^*$ and $\beta\in\Lambda^qV^*$. With unit-weight antisymmetrization brackets,

$$
(\alpha\wedge\beta)_{i_1\dots i_{p+q}}
=\frac{(p+q)!}{p!q!}
\alpha_{[i_1\dots i_p}
\beta_{i_{p+1}\dots i_{p+q}]}.
$$

For one-forms,

$$
(\alpha\wedge\beta)_{ij}
=\alpha_i\beta_j-\alpha_j\beta_i.
$$

For a one-form $A$ and a two-form $F$,

$$
(A\wedge F)_{\mu\nu\rho}
=3A_{[\mu}F_{\nu\rho]}.
$$

For two two-forms $F$ and $G$,

$$
(F\wedge G)_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
=6F_{[\mu\nu}G_{\rho\sigma]}.
$$

These coefficients are a common source of mistakes. They come from the number of ways to choose which $p$ of the $p+q$ indices feed into $\alpha$.

## Determinants from top exterior powers

Let $A:V\to V$ be a linear map on an $n$-dimensional vector space. It induces a map

$$
\Lambda^nA:\Lambda^nV\to\Lambda^nV
$$

by

$$
(\Lambda^nA)(v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_n)
=Av_1\wedge\cdots\wedge Av_n.
$$

Since $\Lambda^nV$ is one-dimensional, $\Lambda^nA$ acts by multiplication by a scalar. That scalar is the determinant:

$$
Av_1\wedge\cdots\wedge Av_n
=(\det A)
 v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_n.
$$

This is not merely a pretty definition. It explains why determinants measure oriented volume scaling and why determinants multiply:

$$
\det(AB)=\det A\det B.
$$

Indeed, the induced top-exterior map satisfies

$$
\Lambda^n(AB)=(\Lambda^nA)(\Lambda^nB).
$$

This viewpoint later becomes important for Jacobians, changes of variables in path integrals, and the relation between Grassmann Gaussian integrals and determinants.

## Orientations and volume forms

An orientation of a real $n$-dimensional vector space is a choice of which ordered bases are positive. Equivalently, it is a choice of one of the two connected components of the set of nonzero elements of $\Lambda^nV$ up to positive scaling.

A volume form is a nonzero element

$$
\Omega\in\Lambda^nV^*.
$$

It assigns an oriented volume to $n$ vectors:

$$
\Omega(v_1,
\dots,v_n).
$$

If $\varepsilon^1,
\dots,\varepsilon^n$ is the dual basis, then

$$
\varepsilon^1\wedge\cdots\wedge\varepsilon^n
$$

is the coordinate volume form. It obeys

$$
(\varepsilon^1\wedge\cdots\wedge\varepsilon^n)(e_1,
\dots,e_n)=1.
$$

On a manifold, a volume form is a smooth choice of such top-degree covectors at each point. A metric gives a preferred density or volume form once an orientation is chosen, but orientation and metric are distinct structures.

## Levi-Civita symbols and tensors

The Levi-Civita symbol $\epsilon_{i_1\dots i_n}$ is defined in an oriented basis by

$$
\epsilon_{12\dots n}=1,
$$

and complete antisymmetry. It is a symbol until its transformation law is specified. In an oriented orthonormal basis one often uses it as the components of the volume form.

A standard determinant identity is

$$
\epsilon_{i_1\dots i_n}A^{i_1}{}_{j_1}\cdots A^{i_n}{}_{j_n}
=(\det A)\epsilon_{j_1\dots j_n}.
$$

This is just the top-exterior-power definition of determinant in components.

With a metric $g$, one can raise indices:

$$
\epsilon^{i_1\dots i_n}=g^{i_1j_1}\cdots g^{i_nj_n}\epsilon_{j_1\dots j_n}.
$$

In Lorentzian signature, raising all indices may introduce a sign. With the mostly-minus metric in four dimensions,

$$
\epsilon^{0123}=-\epsilon_{0123}
$$

if $\epsilon_{0123}=+1$ and $\eta=\operatorname{diag}(+---)$. Always check whether a source treats $\epsilon$ as a tensor, tensor density, or symbol.

## Interior product

Given $v\in V$, the interior product

$$
\iota_v:\Lambda^kV^*\to\Lambda^{k-1}V^*
$$

inserts $v$ into the first slot:

$$
(\iota_v\omega)(v_1,
\dots,v_{k-1})
=\omega(v,v_1,
\dots,v_{k-1}).
$$

It is a graded derivation of degree $-1$:

$$
\iota_v(\alpha\wedge\beta)
=(\iota_v\alpha)\wedge\beta+(-1)^p\alpha\wedge(\iota_v\beta),
\qquad
\alpha\in\Lambda^pV^*.
$$

The sign appears because $\iota_v$ has to pass through $p$ slots of $\alpha$ before acting on $\beta$.

On manifolds, the interior product combines with the exterior derivative $d$ to give Cartan’s formula for the Lie derivative:

$$
\mathcal L_X=d\iota_X+\iota_Xd.
$$

That formula is developed in [Lie Derivatives](/math-toolkit/geometry-of-fields/lie-derivatives/).

## Pullbacks and functoriality

A linear map $A:V\to W$ induces a pullback on covectors

$$
A^*:W^*\to V^*,
\qquad
(A^*\alpha)(v)=\alpha(Av).
$$

It extends to exterior powers by

$$
A^*(\alpha_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\alpha_k)
=A^*\alpha_1\wedge\cdots\wedge A^*\alpha_k.
$$

Thus

$$
A^*(\alpha\wedge\beta)=A^*\alpha\wedge A^*\beta.
$$

On manifolds, this becomes the pullback of differential forms by a smooth map. It is one of the reasons differential forms are so natural: they can be integrated over parameterized submanifolds without choosing coordinates on the target.

## Exterior algebra and differential forms

A differential $k$-form on a manifold $M$ is a smooth section of

$$
\Lambda^kT^*M.
$$

At each point $x\in M$,

$$
\omega_x\in\Lambda^kT_x^*M.
$$

Thus exterior algebra is the pointwise algebra of differential forms. The exterior derivative

$$
d:\Omega^k(M)\to\Omega^{k+1}(M)
$$

adds a differential operation satisfying

$$
d^2=0,
$$

and the graded Leibniz rule

$$
d(\alpha\wedge\beta)=d\alpha\wedge\beta+(-1)^p\alpha\wedge d\beta,
\qquad
\alpha\in\Omega^p(M).
$$

Gauge theory is dramatically cleaner in this notation. For an abelian gauge field $A\in\Omega^1(M)$,

$$
F=dA.
$$

In components,

$$
F=\frac12F_{\mu\nu}dx^\mu\wedge dx^\nu,
\qquad
F_{\mu\nu}=\partial_\mu A_\nu-\partial_\nu A_\mu.
$$

The Bianchi identity is simply

$$
dF=0.
$$

For a nonabelian gauge field $A$ valued in a Lie algebra,

$$
F=dA+A\wedge A,
$$

where the wedge product includes both exterior multiplication of forms and matrix or Lie-algebra multiplication. In components, with $A=A^aT_a$ and $[T_a,T_b]=f_{ab}{}^cT_c$,

$$
F^c_{\mu\nu}=\partial_\mu A^c_\nu-\partial_\nu A^c_\mu+f_{ab}{}^cA^a_\mu A^b_\nu,
$$

up to the sign convention for the Lie bracket and gauge coupling.

## Exterior algebra and Grassmann algebra

A finite-dimensional Grassmann algebra is exterior algebra in disguise. If $E$ is a vector space with basis $\theta^1,
\dots,\theta^N$, then

$$
\theta^i\theta^j=-\theta^j\theta^i,
\qquad
(\theta^i)^2=0.
$$

This is exactly the exterior relation. The algebra generated by the $\theta^i$ is

$$
\Lambda E.
$$

In fermionic path integrals, the $\theta^i$ are replaced by Grassmann variables associated with fermion modes. Berezin integration extracts the top-degree coefficient:

$$
\int d\theta^N\cdots d\theta^1\,\theta^1\cdots\theta^N=1,
$$

with ordering conventions fixed once and for all.

The determinant identity for Grassmann Gaussian integrals,

$$
\int \prod_i d\bar\psi_i d\psi_i\,
\exp(-\bar\psi_i A^i{}_j\psi^j)
=\det A,
$$

is an exterior-algebra statement about top-degree coefficients. Fermionic integrals produce determinants rather than inverse square roots precisely because Grassmann variables are exterior generators.

For the path-integral conventions and signs, see [Berezin Integration](/math-toolkit/functional-integrals-determinants/berezin-integration/) and [Functional Determinants](/math-toolkit/functional-integrals-determinants/functional-determinants/).

## Pfaffians from two-forms

Let $A_{ij}=-A_{ji}$ be an antisymmetric bilinear form on a $2n$-dimensional vector space. View it as a two-form

$$
A=\frac12A_{ij}\varepsilon^i\wedge\varepsilon^j.
$$

Then the top exterior power is

$$
\frac1{n!}A^{\wedge n}=\operatorname{Pf}(A)\,
\varepsilon^1\wedge\cdots\wedge\varepsilon^{2n},
$$

where $\operatorname{Pf}(A)$ is the Pfaffian. It satisfies

$$
\operatorname{Pf}(A)^2=\det A.
$$

This is the finite-dimensional algebra behind Majorana fermion path integrals. Complex Dirac fermions give determinants; real Majorana fermions give Pfaffians. The sign of a Pfaffian can carry important global information.

For more, see [Pfaffians](/math-toolkit/functional-integrals-determinants/pfaffians/).

## Exterior powers of representations

If a group $G$ acts linearly on $V$, then it acts on every exterior power:

$$
g\cdot(v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_k)
=(gv_1)\wedge\cdots\wedge(gv_k).
$$

Thus $\Lambda^kV$ is a representation of $G$. This is a workhorse in QFT representation theory.

Examples:

- For $SU(N)$, $\Lambda^k\mathbb C^N$ is the $k$-index antisymmetric representation.
- The top exterior power $\Lambda^N\mathbb C^N$ is trivial for $SU(N)$ because $\det g=1$.
- In four-dimensional Lorentzian geometry, two-forms split into self-dual and anti-self-dual parts after introducing a Hodge star and complexification.
- In gauge theory, $p$-form gauge fields transform as exterior powers of cotangent spaces and may also carry internal representation labels.

Exterior algebra therefore sits between linear algebra and representation theory: it packages antisymmetric tensor representations in a coordinate-free way.

## Common pitfalls

The wedge product is not the same as the tensor product. It is the antisymmetric quotient or antisymmetric projection of tensor multiplication.

Do not forget that components of a $k$-form are usually written with a factor $1/k!$:

$$
\omega=\frac1{k!}\omega_{i_1\dots i_k}dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}.
$$

Dropping this factor changes component formulas for $d$, $\wedge$, $F$, Chern classes, and topological terms.

Do not assume $\alpha\wedge\alpha=0$ for every form. It vanishes automatically only when $\alpha$ has odd degree. A two-form can wedge with itself nontrivially.

Do not identify the Levi-Civita symbol with a tensor without checking metric and density conventions. Raising indices in Lorentzian signature can change signs.

Do not confuse $\Lambda^kV$ with $\Lambda^kV^*$. Differential forms live in exterior powers of the cotangent space, not the tangent space. A metric can identify them, but that is extra structure.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Basis count

Let $V$ have basis $e_1,e_2,e_3,e_4$. List a basis of $\Lambda^2V$ and compute its dimension.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A basis is given by increasing pairs:

$$
e_1\wedge e_2,
\quad
e_1\wedge e_3,
\quad
e_1\wedge e_4,
\quad
e_2\wedge e_3,
\quad
e_2\wedge e_4,
\quad
e_3\wedge e_4.
$$

Thus

$$
\dim\Lambda^2V={4\choose2}=6.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Graded commutativity

Let $\alpha\in\Lambda^pV$ and $\beta\in\Lambda^qV$ be decomposable. Show that

$$
\alpha\wedge\beta=(-1)^{pq}\beta\wedge\alpha.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Write

$$
\alpha=v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_p,
\qquad
\beta=w_1\wedge\cdots\wedge w_q.
$$

To move $\beta$ past $\alpha$, each of the $q$ vectors $w_a$ must cross each of the $p$ vectors $v_i$. This requires $pq$ swaps. Each swap contributes a sign $-1$. Therefore the total sign is $(-1)^{pq}$:

$$
w_1\wedge\cdots\wedge w_q\wedge v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_p
=(-1)^{pq}v_1\wedge\cdots\wedge v_p\wedge w_1\wedge\cdots\wedge w_q.
$$

By multilinearity, the same formula holds for sums of decomposable elements.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Determinant from a wedge

Let $A:\mathbb R^2\to\mathbb R^2$ have matrix

$$
A=\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}
$$

in the basis $e_1,e_2$. Show that

$$
Ae_1\wedge Ae_2=(ad-bc)e_1\wedge e_2.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

We have

$$
Ae_1=ae_1+ce_2,
\qquad
Ae_2=be_1+de_2.
$$

Then

$$
Ae_1\wedge Ae_2
=(ae_1+ce_2)\wedge(be_1+de_2).
$$

Expanding and using $e_1\wedge e_1=e_2\wedge e_2=0$ gives

$$
Ae_1\wedge Ae_2
=ad\,e_1\wedge e_2+cb\,e_2\wedge e_1.
$$

Since $e_2\wedge e_1=-e_1\wedge e_2$,

$$
Ae_1\wedge Ae_2=(ad-bc)e_1\wedge e_2.
$$

Thus the scalar multiplying the top exterior basis is $\det A$.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Components of $A\wedge F$

Let $A$ be a one-form and $F$ a two-form. With unit-weight antisymmetrization brackets, show that

$$
(A\wedge F)_{\mu\nu\rho}=3A_{[\mu}F_{\nu\rho]}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The general component formula is

$$
(\alpha\wedge\beta)_{i_1\dots i_{p+q}}
=\frac{(p+q)!}{p!q!}
\alpha_{[i_1\dots i_p}\beta_{i_{p+1}\dots i_{p+q}]}.
$$

Here $p=1$ and $q=2$, so

$$
\frac{(p+q)!}{p!q!}=\frac{3!}{1!2!}=3.
$$

Therefore

$$
(A\wedge F)_{\mu\nu\rho}=3A_{[\mu}F_{\nu\rho]}.
$$

Expanded explicitly,

$$
3A_{[\mu}F_{\nu\rho]}
=A_\mu F_{\nu\rho}+A_\nu F_{\rho\mu}+A_\rho F_{\mu\nu},
$$

where the antisymmetry of $F$ has already combined terms.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Why a two-form can square

Let $F=dx^1\wedge dx^2+dx^3\wedge dx^4$ on $\mathbb R^4$. Compute $F\wedge F$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Let

$$
a=dx^1\wedge dx^2,
\qquad
b=dx^3\wedge dx^4.
$$

Then $F=a+b$. Since $a$ and $b$ are two-forms,

$$
a\wedge b=(-1)^{2\cdot2}b\wedge a=b\wedge a.
$$

Also $a\wedge a=0$ and $b\wedge b=0$ because repeated one-forms occur. Therefore

$$
F\wedge F=(a+b)\wedge(a+b)=a\wedge b+b\wedge a=2a\wedge b.
$$

So

$$
F\wedge F=2dx^1\wedge dx^2\wedge dx^3\wedge dx^4.
$$

This is why $F\wedge F$ can be a nonzero four-form in four-dimensional gauge theory.

</details>
