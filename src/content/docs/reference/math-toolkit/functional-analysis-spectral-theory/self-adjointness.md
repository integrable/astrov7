## Summary

In finite-dimensional quantum mechanics, “Hermitian” is usually enough. In infinite dimensions, that shortcut breaks. The correct condition for an observable or Hamiltonian is not merely that

$$
\langle A\psi,\phi\rangle=\langle\psi,A\phi\rangle
$$

on some convenient domain. The correct Hilbert-space condition is self-adjointness:

$$
A=A^*,
\qquad
D(A)=D(A^*).
$$

The equality of domains is the whole bite. A symmetric operator satisfies $A\subseteq A^*$; a self-adjoint operator satisfies $A=A^*$. The first says integration by parts produces no boundary term on the chosen domain. The second says the domain is maximal and supports the spectral theorem.

Self-adjointness is why formulas like

$$
U(t)=e^{-itH}
$$

are legitimate unitary time evolutions, why spectra of observables are real, and why functions of operators such as $e^{-tA}$, $(A-z)^{-1}$, and $\log\det A$ have a precise meaning. It is not pedantry. It is the analytic fuse box behind quantum theory.

## Core idea

The finite-dimensional theorem says a Hermitian matrix has real eigenvalues, an orthonormal eigenbasis, and a unitary exponential. In infinite dimensions, the corresponding theorem is not about symmetric formulas. It is about self-adjoint operators.

A densely defined operator $A:D(A)\subseteq\mathcal H\to\mathcal H$ is **symmetric** if

$$
\langle A\psi,\phi\rangle=\langle\psi,A\phi\rangle
$$

for all $\psi,\phi\in D(A)$. Equivalently,

$$
A\subseteq A^*.
$$

It is **self-adjoint** if

$$
A=A^*,
$$

which means both

$$
D(A)=D(A^*)
$$

and

$$
A\psi=A^*\psi\quad \text{on that common domain}.
$$

The distinction is invisible in finite dimensions because every operator is defined on the whole space. In infinite dimensions, the adjoint domain can be larger than the original domain. That extra room is where boundary conditions, extensions, and missing probability current live.

A compact dictionary:

| Word | Domain statement | Physical meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Symmetric | $A\subseteq A^*$ | Matrix elements look Hermitian on $D(A)$. |
| Self-adjoint | $A=A^*$ | Observable with spectral theorem and unitary generator. |
| Essentially self-adjoint | $\overline A$ is self-adjoint | The initial domain determines a unique self-adjoint operator. |
| Self-adjoint extension | $A\subseteq H=H^*$ | Extra boundary or extension data complete the operator. |
| Not extendable | no self-adjoint extension | The formal generator cannot represent an observable on that Hilbert space. |

A useful slogan is

$$
\text{symmetric} = \text{no boundary term on a chosen domain},
\qquad
\text{self-adjoint} = \text{maximal no-boundary-term domain}.
$$

## Adjoint and domain equality

Let $A$ be densely defined. A vector $\phi\in\mathcal H$ lies in $D(A^*)$ if there exists $\eta\in\mathcal H$ such that

$$
\langle A\psi,\phi\rangle=\langle\psi,\eta\rangle
$$

for all $\psi\in D(A)$. Then $A^*\phi=\eta$.

For differential operators, the adjoint domain is often found by integration by parts. The original domain may impose compact support or boundary conditions; the adjoint domain is usually larger because the test function in the second slot is allowed to have boundary values.

Here is the standard warning in its smallest form. Let

$$
P_0=-i\frac{d}{dx},
\qquad
D(P_0)=C_c^\infty(0,L),
$$

as an operator on $L^2([0,L])$. Then $P_0$ is symmetric. Its adjoint is

$$
P_0^*=-i\frac{d}{dx},
\qquad
D(P_0^*)=H^1([0,L]).
$$

Thus

$$
D(P_0)\ne D(P_0^*),
$$

so $P_0$ is not self-adjoint. It is not an observable yet. It is an unfinished differential expression on a small domain.

Self-adjoint extensions are obtained by imposing boundary conditions on $H^1([0,L])$ that make the boundary form vanish maximally. For momentum on the interval, the family is

$$
D(P_\theta)=\{\psi\in H^1([0,L])\mid \psi(L)=e^{i\theta}\psi(0)\},
\qquad
\theta\in\mathbb R/2\pi\mathbb Z.
$$

Each $P_\theta$ is self-adjoint. Each represents a different quantum system.

## Why self-adjointness matters

Self-adjointness gives four major structures.

First, the spectrum is real. More precisely, if $A=A^*$, then

$$
\sigma(A)\subseteq\mathbb R.
$$

Second, the resolvent exists off the real axis and satisfies the estimate

$$
\|(A-z)^{-1}\|\le \frac{1}{|\operatorname{Im}z|},
\qquad \operatorname{Im}z\ne 0.
$$

Third, the spectral theorem applies. There is a projection-valued measure $E_A(\lambda)$ such that

$$
A=\int_{\mathbb R}\lambda\,dE_A(\lambda).
$$

Then functions of $A$ are defined by

$$
f(A)=\int_{\mathbb R} f(\lambda)\,dE_A(\lambda),
$$

with the appropriate domain if $f$ is unbounded.

Fourth, Stone's theorem says that self-adjoint operators are exactly the generators of strongly continuous unitary one-parameter groups. If $H=H^*$, then

$$
U(t)=e^{-itH}
$$

is unitary and strongly continuous. Conversely, if $U(t)$ is a strongly continuous unitary one-parameter group, then there is a unique self-adjoint $H$ such that

$$
U(t)=e^{-itH}.
$$

This is the actual theorem behind unitary time evolution in quantum mechanics. A merely symmetric Hamiltonian may fail to generate unitary evolution; probability can leak into a boundary or singularity.

## Symmetric is not enough

A symmetric operator has real expectation values on its domain:

$$
\langle\psi,A\psi\rangle\in\mathbb R,
\qquad \psi\in D(A).
$$

That can fool the eye. Real expectation values on a small domain do not guarantee a real spectral theorem, unitary evolution, or a well-defined observable.

The momentum operator on the half-line is the clean example. Let

$$
P_0=-i\frac{d}{dx},
\qquad
D(P_0)=C_c^\infty(0,\infty)
$$

on $L^2(0,\infty)$. It is symmetric. Its adjoint acts by the same derivative on $H^1(0,\infty)$. The deficiency equations are

$$
P_0^*\psi=i\psi,
\qquad
P_0^*\psi=-i\psi.
$$

Solving gives

$$
-i\psi'=i\psi \Rightarrow \psi(x)=Ce^{-x},
$$

which is square-integrable on $(0,\infty)$, and

$$
-i\psi'=-i\psi \Rightarrow \psi(x)=Ce^x,
$$

which is not square-integrable. The deficiency indices are therefore

$$
(n_+,n_-)=(1,0).
$$

Since they are unequal, there is no self-adjoint extension. This matches the physical fact that the half-line has no unitary translation group generated by momentum. Translations want to move wavefunctions through the boundary.

## Deficiency indices

Let $A$ be a densely defined closed symmetric operator. The deficiency spaces are

$$
\mathcal N_+=\ker(A^*-i),
\qquad
\mathcal N_-=\ker(A^*+i).
$$

Their dimensions

$$
n_+=\dim\mathcal N_+,
\qquad
n_-=\dim\mathcal N_-
$$

are the deficiency indices. The particular numbers $\pm i$ are conventional; any nonreal spectral parameters in the upper and lower half-planes give equivalent dimensions.

Von Neumann's extension theorem says:

| Deficiency indices | Consequence |
|---|---|
| $(0,0)$ | $A$ is self-adjoint if closed, or essentially self-adjoint before closure. |
| $(n,n)$ with $n>0$ | Self-adjoint extensions exist and are parameterized by unitary maps $\mathcal N_+\to\mathcal N_-$. |
| $(n_+,n_-)$ with $n_+\ne n_-$ | No self-adjoint extension exists. |

The deficiency spaces measure missing boundary data. For ordinary differential operators, their dimensions often count the number of boundary conditions needed. For singular operators, they measure possible behavior near the singularity.

## Essential self-adjointness

A symmetric operator $A$ is **essentially self-adjoint** if its closure $\overline A$ is self-adjoint. Then there is no extension ambiguity: the initial dense domain already determines the physical operator uniquely.

This property is precious. It lets one define an operator first on a convenient core and later use the unique self-adjoint closure.

For example,

$$
P=-i\frac{d}{dx}
$$

on $C_c^\infty(\mathbb R)$ is essentially self-adjoint. Its closure is the self-adjoint momentum operator with domain $H^1(\mathbb R)$.

Similarly, the Schrödinger operator

$$
H=-\Delta+V
$$

on $C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^d)$ is essentially self-adjoint under many standard conditions on $V$, for example broad classes of real locally square-integrable potentials bounded below in an appropriate sense. The exact hypotheses belong to spectral theory, but the physical message is simple: sufficiently tame potentials on complete spaces often determine a unique quantum Hamiltonian.

By contrast, singular potentials, punctured spaces, finite intervals, and manifolds with boundary often require extension data. Those data are not mathematical decorations; they are boundary physics.

## Boundary forms

Suppose a formal differential operator $L$ has a maximal adjoint domain $D(L^*)$. The boundary form is

$$
B(\psi,\phi)=\langle L^*\psi,\phi\rangle-\langle\psi,L^*\phi\rangle.
$$

A symmetric domain is a subspace on which $B$ vanishes. A self-adjoint domain is, roughly, a maximal subspace on which $B$ vanishes.

For momentum on an interval,

$$
B(\psi,\phi)=i\left[\overline\psi\phi\right]_0^L.
$$

The condition

$$
\psi(L)=e^{i\theta}\psi(0)
$$

makes $B$ vanish maximally.

For the Laplacian on a region $\Omega$,

$$
L=-\Delta,
$$

Green's identity gives the boundary form

$$
B(\psi,\phi)=\int_{\partial\Omega} dS\,
\left(\partial_n\overline\psi\,\phi-\overline\psi\,\partial_n\phi\right),
$$

up to the sign convention for the outward normal. Dirichlet, Neumann, Robin, and more general boundary conditions are ways to choose maximal subspaces on which this form vanishes.

This is the cleanest conceptual connection between self-adjointness and boundary conditions.

## Examples

### Real multiplication operators

Let $m:X\to\mathbb R$ be measurable and define $M_m$ on $L^2(X,d\mu)$ by

$$
(M_m f)(x)=m(x)f(x),
$$

with domain

$$
D(M_m)=\{f\in L^2(X,d\mu)\mid mf\in L^2(X,d\mu)\}.
$$

Then $M_m$ is self-adjoint. This example is the spectral theorem in miniature: every self-adjoint operator is unitarily equivalent to multiplication by a real variable, in a suitably generalized direct-integral sense.

### Momentum on the real line

On $L^2(\mathbb R)$,

$$
P=-i\frac{d}{dx},
\qquad
D(P)=H^1(\mathbb R),
$$

is self-adjoint. Under Fourier transform, it becomes multiplication by $p$:

$$
\widetilde{P\psi}(p)=p\widetilde\psi(p).
$$

The self-adjointness is then the self-adjointness of multiplication by the real variable $p$.

### Momentum on a circle

On a circle of circumference $L$, the same derivative has inequivalent self-adjoint realizations

$$
D(P_\theta)=\{\psi\in H^1([0,L])\mid \psi(L)=e^{i\theta}\psi(0)\}.
$$

The normalized eigenfunctions are

$$
\psi_n(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt L}\exp\left(i\frac{2\pi n+\theta}{L}x\right),
\qquad n\in\mathbb Z,
$$

with eigenvalues

$$
p_n=\frac{2\pi n+\theta}{L}.
$$

The parameter $\theta$ can be interpreted as a flat background connection or Aharonov–Bohm holonomy. One sees here how topology and self-adjoint extension data can become physics.

### Laplacian on a half-line

On $L^2(0,\infty)$, the operator

$$
H=-\frac{d^2}{dx^2}
$$

has self-adjoint boundary conditions at $x=0$. A standard family is

$$
\psi'(0)=\alpha\psi(0),
\qquad \alpha\in\mathbb R,
$$

together with the limiting Dirichlet condition $\psi(0)=0$.

For $\alpha<0$, this operator has a bound state with negative energy. Thus changing the self-adjoint extension changes the spectrum. A boundary condition is a physical interaction localized at the endpoint.

### Harmonic oscillator

The harmonic oscillator Hamiltonian

$$
H=\frac12(P^2+X^2)
$$

is essentially self-adjoint on the Schwartz space $\mathcal S(\mathbb R)$. Its closure is self-adjoint and has discrete spectrum

$$
E_n=n+\frac12,
\qquad n=0,1,2,\ldots.
$$

The ladder-operator calculation is often done algebraically, but the spectral theorem is what makes the final Hamiltonian a genuine observable.

## Self-adjointness and QFT

QFT uses self-adjointness in several overlapping ways.

A Hamiltonian $H$ should be self-adjoint to generate unitary time evolution. A momentum operator $P_i$ should be self-adjoint to generate unitary translations. Angular momentum and boost generators require similar care, though boosts can be especially subtle because domains and localization issues are sharper.

A real scalar field smeared with a real test function,

$$
\phi(f)=\int d^dx\,f(x)\phi(x),
$$

is expected to be represented by a self-adjoint operator, or at least an essentially self-adjoint operator on a physically natural dense domain, in rigorous free-field settings. But the unsmeared symbol $\phi(x)$ is not an operator at a point. It is an operator-valued distribution. The self-adjointness question belongs to smeared fields and their domains.

Charges and currents are also delicate. A formal Noether charge

$$
Q=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,j^0(t,\mathbf x)
$$

may fail to exist as an operator without smearing, boundary conditions, infrared control, or renormalization. In gauge theory, Gauss-law constraints can turn naive charges into boundary observables. In anomalous theories, the failure of a current to define a conserved self-adjoint generator is not a bookkeeping issue; it is the physics.

Euclidean QFT hides self-adjointness behind reflection positivity and reconstruction. Lorentzian QFT exposes it through unitary time evolution. The two languages meet when Euclidean correlation functions reconstruct a Hilbert space with self-adjoint generators.

## Resolvents and Green functions

For a self-adjoint operator $A$, the resolvent

$$
R(z)=(A-z)^{-1}
$$

exists as a bounded operator for $z\notin\mathbb R$. In physics, Green functions are often boundary values of resolvents:

$$
(A-\lambda\mp i0)^{-1}.
$$

The $i0$ prescription is not only a contour trick. It remembers which boundary value of the resolvent one is taking and therefore which causal or spectral object is being selected.

For a positive self-adjoint operator, one can also define the heat operator

$$
e^{-tA}=\int_0^\infty e^{-t\lambda}\,dE_A(\lambda),
\qquad t>0.
$$

This is the analytic basis of heat-kernel regularization, zeta functions, one-loop determinants, and Schwinger proper-time formulas.

## Common pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Saying “Hermitian” when the domain is unknown.** For unbounded operators, the word “Hermitian” is ambiguous. Say symmetric, self-adjoint, or essentially self-adjoint.

**Pitfall 2: Checking only the formula.** The formal equality $A=A^*$ is meaningless without comparing domains.

**Pitfall 3: Assuming symmetric implies self-adjoint.** It does in finite dimensions. It does not in infinite dimensions.

**Pitfall 4: Forgetting self-adjoint extensions.** A symmetric differential operator may have many self-adjoint extensions. Each can define different spectra and different physics.

**Pitfall 5: Ignoring the half-line momentum example.** The operator $-i\,d/dx$ on $(0,\infty)$ is symmetric on compactly supported smooth functions but has no self-adjoint extension. Not every formal generator can be repaired.

**Pitfall 6: Treating $e^{-itA}$ as unitary before proving $A$ is self-adjoint.** Stone's theorem needs self-adjointness, not just symmetry.

**Pitfall 7: Applying determinant formulas before specifying the operator.** The determinant of $A$ depends on the self-adjoint realization, zero-mode treatment, and spectral regularization.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Symmetry of the interval momentum with phase boundary condition

Let

$$
P_\theta=-i\frac{d}{dx},
\qquad
D(P_\theta)=\{\psi\in H^1([0,L])\mid \psi(L)=e^{i\theta}\psi(0)\}.
$$

Show that $P_\theta$ is symmetric.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $\psi,\phi\in D(P_\theta)$,

$$
\langle P_\theta\psi,\phi\rangle-\langle\psi,P_\theta\phi\rangle
=i[\overline\psi\phi]_0^L.
$$

The boundary condition gives

$$
\overline{\psi(L)}\phi(L)
=e^{-i\theta}\overline{\psi(0)}e^{i\theta}\phi(0)
=\overline{\psi(0)}\phi(0).
$$

Thus the boundary term vanishes, and

$$
\langle P_\theta\psi,\phi\rangle=\langle\psi,P_\theta\phi\rangle.
$$

So $P_\theta$ is symmetric. Showing that it is self-adjoint requires the additional maximality statement that the adjoint domain obeys the same boundary condition.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Deficiency indices of momentum on the half-line

Let

$$
P_0=-i\frac{d}{dx},
\qquad
D(P_0)=C_c^\infty(0,\infty)
$$

on $L^2(0,\infty)$. Assuming $D(P_0^*)=H^1(0,\infty)$, compute the deficiency indices.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The first deficiency equation is

$$
P_0^*\psi=i\psi.
$$

Since $P_0^*=-i\,d/dx$, this gives

$$
-i\psi'=i\psi,
\qquad
\psi'=-\psi,
$$

so

$$
\psi(x)=Ce^{-x}.
$$

This is square-integrable on $(0,\infty)$, so $n_+=1$.

The second equation is

$$
P_0^*\psi=-i\psi,
$$

so

$$
-i\psi'=-i\psi,
\qquad
\psi'=\psi,
$$

and

$$
\psi(x)=Ce^x.
$$

This is not square-integrable unless $C=0$, so $n_-=0$. Therefore

$$
(n_+,n_-)=(1,0).
$$

Because the deficiency indices are unequal, $P_0$ has no self-adjoint extension.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Spectrum of momentum with twisted periodicity

For

$$
D(P_\theta)=\{\psi\in H^1([0,L])\mid \psi(L)=e^{i\theta}\psi(0)\},
$$

solve

$$
P_\theta\psi=p\psi.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The eigenvalue equation is

$$
-i\psi'=p\psi,
$$

so

$$
\psi(x)=Ce^{ipx}.
$$

The boundary condition gives

$$
e^{ipL}=e^{i\theta}.
$$

Therefore

$$
pL=\theta+2\pi n,
\qquad n\in\mathbb Z,
$$

and

$$
p_n=\frac{\theta+2\pi n}{L}.
$$

A normalized eigenfunction is

$$
\psi_n(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt L}e^{ip_nx}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Multiplication by a real function

Let $m:X\to\mathbb R$ be measurable and define

$$
D(M_m)=\{f\in L^2(X,d\mu)\mid mf\in L^2(X,d\mu)\},
\qquad
M_m f=mf.
$$

Show formally why $M_m$ is symmetric.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $f,g\in D(M_m)$,

$$
\langle M_m f,g\rangle
=\int_X d\mu\,\overline{m f}g.
$$

Since $m$ is real almost everywhere,

$$
\overline{m f}=m\overline f.
$$

Therefore

$$
\langle M_m f,g\rangle
=\int_X d\mu\,\overline f\,m g
=\langle f,M_m g\rangle.
$$

This proves symmetry. The full self-adjointness statement also requires showing that the adjoint domain is exactly $D(M_m)$.

</details>
