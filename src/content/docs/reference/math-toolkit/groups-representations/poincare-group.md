## Summary

The Poincaré group is the symmetry group of flat Minkowski spacetime. It combines spacetime translations with Lorentz transformations:

$$
\text{Poincaré group}=\text{translations}\rtimes\text{Lorentz transformations}.
$$

For the connected component relevant to ordinary local relativistic QFT,

$$
\text{ISO}^+(1,3)=\mathbb R^{1,3}\rtimes SO^+(1,3),
$$

where $SO^+(1,3)$ is the proper orthochronous Lorentz group. Its universal cover is

$$
\mathbb R^{1,3}\rtimes SL(2,\mathbb C).
$$

That cover is the group that naturally acts on spinor fields and on quantum states with half-integer spin.

The Lie algebra is generated by translations $P^\mu$ and Lorentz generators $M^{\mu\nu}=-M^{\nu\mu}$. With the mostly-minus metric

$$
\eta_{\mu\nu}=\operatorname{diag}(+1,-1,-1,-1),
$$

the defining commutation relations are

$$
[P^\mu,P^\nu]=0,
$$

$$
[M^{\mu\nu},P^\rho]
=i\left(\eta^{\nu\rho}P^\mu-\eta^{\mu\rho}P^\nu\right),
$$

and

$$
[M^{\mu\nu},M^{\rho\sigma}]
=i\left(
\eta^{\nu\rho}M^{\mu\sigma}
-\eta^{\mu\rho}M^{\nu\sigma}
-\eta^{\nu\sigma}M^{\mu\rho}
+\eta^{\mu\sigma}M^{\nu\rho}
\right).
$$

The two central Casimir operators are

$$
P^2=P_\mu P^\mu
$$

and

$$
W^2=W_\mu W^\mu,
$$

where $W^\mu$ is the Pauli–Lubanski vector. For a massive particle, these become the familiar labels

$$
P^2=m^2,
\qquad
W^2=-m^2s(s+1),
$$

so the irreducible one-particle sectors are labeled by mass $m$ and spin $s$.

The big conceptual point is this: **particles are not defined by a wave equation first**. In relativistic quantum theory, an elementary one-particle sector is an irreducible unitary representation of the Poincaré group. Wave equations and fields are tools for realizing those representation-theoretic possibilities locally.

## Core idea

A relativistic quantum theory carries a unitary action of spacetime symmetry on its Hilbert space. Translations are represented by commuting self-adjoint generators $P^\mu$. Their joint spectrum gives the energy and momentum of states.

A one-particle state can therefore be chosen as an eigenstate of $P^\mu$:

$$
P^\mu|p,\alpha\rangle=p^\mu|p,\alpha\rangle.
$$

Here $\alpha$ labels whatever degeneracy remains at fixed momentum. Lorentz transformations move $p$ to another point on the same mass shell:

$$
p^\mu\mapsto \Lambda^\mu{}_{\nu}p^\nu.
$$

The stabilizer of a chosen standard momentum $k^\mu$ is called the little group:

$$
G_k=\{\Lambda\in SO^+(1,3):\Lambda k=k\}.
$$

The little group acts on the degeneracy label $\alpha$. This is Wigner's organizing idea:

$$
\text{one-particle representation}
=\text{momentum orbit}+\text{little-group representation}.
$$

For massive particles, the standard momentum is

$$
k^\mu=(m,0,0,0),
$$

and the little group is the rotation group $SO(3)$, or $SU(2)$ after passing to the spin cover. Thus massive particles have spin

$$
s=0,\frac12,1,\frac32,\ldots.
$$

For massless particles, a standard momentum is

$$
k^\mu=(E,0,0,E),
$$

and the little group is the two-dimensional Euclidean group $ISO(2)$. The finite-helicity representations used by photons, gravitons, and massless Weyl fermions are one-dimensional representations in which the translation-like part of $ISO(2)$ acts trivially. They are labeled by helicity

$$
\lambda\in \frac12\mathbb Z.
$$

The Poincaré group is therefore where the words mass, spin, and helicity acquire their sharp relativistic meaning.

## Setup and conventions

The connected Poincaré group is the semidirect product

$$
\mathcal P^+=\mathbb R^{1,3}\rtimes SO^+(1,3).
$$

An element is a pair

$$
(a,\Lambda),
$$

where $a^\mu$ is a translation and $\Lambda^\mu{}_{\nu}$ is a proper orthochronous Lorentz transformation. The action on spacetime is

$$
x^\mu\mapsto \Lambda^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu+a^\mu.
$$

The group law is

$$
(a,\Lambda)(b,\Omega)=\left(a+\Lambda b,\Lambda\Omega\right),
$$

where

$$
(\Lambda b)^\mu=\Lambda^\mu{}_{\nu}b^\nu.
$$

The inverse is

$$
(a,\Lambda)^{-1}=(-\Lambda^{-1}a,\Lambda^{-1}).
$$

The semidirect product is not decorative notation. It records the fact that Lorentz transformations act nontrivially on translations. Translating, then boosting, is not the same operation as boosting, then translating by the same coordinate vector.

On Hilbert space, a unitary representation is written

$$
U(a,\Lambda).
$$

One common convention is

$$
U(a,\mathbf 1)=e^{ia_\mu P^\mu}.
$$

With this choice, fields transform under translations as

$$
U(a,\mathbf 1)\Phi(x)U(a,\mathbf 1)^{-1}=\Phi(x+a),
$$

and therefore

$$
[P_\mu,\Phi(x)]=-i\partial_\mu\Phi(x).
$$

Some books use the opposite exponential convention. The algebraic classification of representations is unchanged, but signs in commutators with fields move accordingly. When comparing formulas, always check whether translations are written as $e^{ia\cdot P}$ or $e^{-ia\cdot P}$.

## The group and algebra

The Poincaré algebra contains the translation algebra and the Lorentz algebra, together with the statement that translations transform as a Lorentz vector.

The translation generators commute:

$$
[P^\mu,P^\nu]=0.
$$

The Lorentz generators are antisymmetric:

$$
M^{\mu\nu}=-M^{\nu\mu}.
$$

They act on $P^\mu$ as

$$
[M^{\mu\nu},P^\rho]
=i\left(\eta^{\nu\rho}P^\mu-\eta^{\mu\rho}P^\nu\right).
$$

This is exactly the algebraic statement that $P^\mu$ is a four-vector.

The Lorentz generators close as

$$
[M^{\mu\nu},M^{\rho\sigma}]
=i\left(
\eta^{\nu\rho}M^{\mu\sigma}
-\eta^{\mu\rho}M^{\nu\sigma}
-\eta^{\nu\sigma}M^{\mu\rho}
+\eta^{\mu\sigma}M^{\nu\rho}
\right).
$$

It is often useful to separate rotations and boosts. Define

$$
J_i=\frac12\epsilon_{ijk}M^{jk},
\qquad
K_i=M^{0i}.
$$

Then

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k,
$$

$$
[J_i,K_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}K_k,
$$

and

$$
[K_i,K_j]=-i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k.
$$

The minus sign in $[K_i,K_j]$ is the algebraic footprint of Lorentzian signature. Rotations form a compact $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ subalgebra; boosts do not form a compact subalgebra by themselves.

The commutators with momentum say that energy is a rotation scalar, spatial momentum is a rotation vector, and boosts mix energy with spatial momentum. In vector notation,

$$
[J_i,P^0]=0,
$$

$$
[J_i,P^j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}P^k,
$$

while boosts mix $P^0$ and $P^i$ with signs determined by the metric and index placement.

## Casimirs and the Pauli–Lubanski vector

A Casimir operator commutes with every generator of the algebra. The first Poincaré Casimir is

$$
P^2=P_\mu P^\mu.
$$

On an irreducible representation, Schur's lemma says that $P^2$ is a scalar. For the positive-energy massive representations of interest in ordinary particle physics,

$$
P^2=m^2,
\qquad
m>0.
$$

For massless particles,

$$
P^2=0,
\qquad
P^0>0.
$$

The second Casimir uses the Pauli–Lubanski vector. With

$$
\epsilon^{0123}=+1,
$$

define

$$
W^\mu=\frac12\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}P_\nu M_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

It obeys

$$
W\cdot P=0.
$$

The scalar

$$
W^2=W_\mu W^\mu
$$

commutes with all Poincaré generators.

In the rest frame of a massive representation,

$$
P^\mu=(m,0,0,0).
$$

Then

$$
W^0=0,
\qquad
\mathbf W=m\mathbf J
$$

up to the harmless sign convention in the definition of $\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$ and $M_{\rho\sigma}$. Therefore

$$
W^2=-m^2\mathbf J^2.
$$

In a spin-$s$ irreducible representation,

$$
\mathbf J^2=s(s+1),
$$

so

$$
W^2=-m^2s(s+1).
$$

This is why spin is a Poincaré-invariant label, not merely a nonrelativistic angular-momentum label.

For massless finite-helicity representations, $W^\mu$ is proportional to $P^\mu$:

$$
W^\mu=\lambda P^\mu.
$$

The scalar $W^2$ then vanishes automatically because $P^2=0$. The invariant label is not $W^2$ but the proportionality constant $\lambda$, the helicity.

## Massive representations

For a massive particle, choose the standard momentum

$$
k^\mu=(m,0,0,0).
$$

The Lorentz transformations that leave $k^\mu$ fixed are spatial rotations:

$$
G_k\cong SO(3).
$$

For spinors, we use the double cover

$$
SU(2)\to SO(3).
$$

Thus the internal label of a massive one-particle state is a spin-$s$ representation of $SU(2)$:

$$
s=0,\frac12,1,\frac32,\ldots.
$$

A convenient basis is

$$
|p,\sigma\rangle,
\qquad
\sigma=-s,-s+1,\ldots,s.
$$

The label $\sigma$ is usually chosen as the spin projection along a chosen axis in the particle's rest frame. A Lorentz transformation acts schematically as

$$
U(\Lambda)|p,\sigma\rangle
=\sum_{\sigma'}D_{\sigma'\sigma}^{(s)}(R_W(\Lambda,p))
|\Lambda p,\sigma'\rangle,
$$

where $R_W(\Lambda,p)$ is the Wigner rotation. The important point is that a general Lorentz transformation does two things:

1. it moves the momentum from $p$ to $\Lambda p$;
2. it rotates the spin label by a momentum-dependent little-group element.

That momentum-dependent rotation is why relativistic spin is slightly subtler than just attaching a fixed $SU(2)$ vector space to each momentum.

Massive representations are the cleanest part of Wigner's classification. The little group is compact, so its finite-dimensional unitary irreducible representations are familiar angular-momentum representations.

## Massless representations

For a massless particle, choose the standard momentum

$$
k^\mu=(E,0,0,E),
\qquad
E>0.
$$

The little group is no longer $SO(3)$. It is

$$
ISO(2)=SO(2)\ltimes\mathbb R^2,
$$

the Euclidean group of the plane.

The $SO(2)$ part is generated by rotations around the direction of motion. Its eigenvalue is helicity:

$$
J_3|p,\lambda\rangle=\lambda|p,\lambda\rangle
$$

when the momentum points in the $+z$ direction.

The translation-like part of $ISO(2)$ is generated by null rotations. In the finite-helicity particle representations used in local QFT, these null rotations act trivially. The remaining representation is one-dimensional:

$$
|p,\lambda\rangle\mapsto e^{-i\lambda\theta}|\Lambda p,\lambda\rangle
$$

for a little-group rotation by angle $\theta$.

For the double cover, helicity is quantized as

$$
\lambda\in\frac12\mathbb Z.
$$

Examples:

| field or particle type | helicities |
|---|---:|
| real scalar | $0$ |
| Weyl fermion | $\pm\frac12$ depending on chirality and particle/antiparticle conventions |
| photon | $+1,-1$ |
| graviton | $+2,-2$ |

There also exist mathematically consistent massless continuous-spin representations in which the null-rotation part acts nontrivially. They are not part of the standard particle spectrum and do not appear in ordinary local relativistic QFT with a finite number of polarizations, but they remain a useful stress test for statements about massless particles.

## Fields are not particle-state representations

A common trap is to identify the Lorentz representation of a field with the unitary representation of a particle state. They are related, but they are not the same object.

A field transforms locally as

$$
U(\Lambda)\Phi_a(x)U(\Lambda)^{-1}
=D(\Lambda^{-1})_a{}^b\Phi_b(\Lambda x),
$$

where $D$ is usually a finite-dimensional representation of the Lorentz group or its cover. Examples include

$$
(0,0),
\qquad
\left(\frac12,0\right),
\qquad
\left(0,\frac12\right),
\qquad
\left(\frac12,\frac12\right).
$$

These finite-dimensional Lorentz representations are generally not unitary, because the Lorentz group is noncompact. They describe how field components transform at a spacetime point.

Particle states live in a Hilbert space and transform under unitary representations of the Poincaré group. Those representations are typically infinite-dimensional, because the momentum $p$ ranges over a continuous orbit.

The bridge between fields and particles comes from mode expansions and covariant wavefunctions. For example, a vector field $A_\mu(x)$ transforms as a Lorentz vector, but a photon does not have four physical polarizations. The physical massless one-particle representation has two helicities:

$$
\lambda=+1,-1.
$$

Gauge redundancy, constraints, equations of motion, and the little group together explain how the covariant field description contains the correct physical representation.

The same moral applies to spinors. A left-handed Weyl field transforms in

$$
\left(\frac12,0\right),
$$

but the corresponding one-particle states are organized by mass and helicity or spin, depending on whether the representation is massless or massive.

## Projective representations and the double cover

Quantum states are rays, not vectors. Therefore spacetime symmetries need only act projectively on rays. Wigner's theorem then allows unitary or antiunitary operators, and projective phases may appear.

For the connected Poincaré group, the important practical consequence is that we should pass to the universal cover:

$$
\widetilde{\mathcal P}^+=\mathbb R^{1,3}\rtimes SL(2,\mathbb C).
$$

This is where spinor representations live naturally. The double cover

$$
SL(2,\mathbb C)\to SO^+(1,3)
$$

has kernel

$$
\{+\mathbf 1,-\mathbf 1\}.
$$

Half-integer-spin states transform nontrivially under $-\mathbf 1$ in the cover. They are ordinary representations of the cover, but projective representations of the Lorentz group itself.

This is the relativistic version of the familiar fact that spin-$\frac12$ is an honest representation of $SU(2)$ but not of $SO(3)$.

## Discrete spacetime symmetries

The connected Poincaré group excludes parity $P$ and time reversal $T$. The full Lorentz group has four connected components, distinguished by determinant and time orientation. The discrete transformations are important, but they are not part of $SO^+(1,3)$.

Parity is usually represented by a unitary operator, while time reversal is represented antiunitarily. Charge conjugation $C$ is not a spacetime symmetry; it acts on internal quantum numbers. The combined $CPT$ transformation is central in relativistic QFT, but its theorem-level status depends on assumptions such as locality, Lorentz covariance, and positivity of energy.

For this page, the key point is simple: the Wigner classification above uses the connected Poincaré group and its cover. Discrete symmetries add further structure; they do not replace the mass-spin-helicity classification.

## How the Poincaré group is used in QFT

The Poincaré group enters QFT in several different layers.

First, it classifies one-particle states. This is the Wigner layer:

$$
\text{particles}=\text{irreducible positive-energy unitary representations}.
$$

Second, it constrains fields. A field must transform covariantly under translations and Lorentz transformations, and its components belong to finite-dimensional Lorentz representations.

Third, it constrains correlation functions. For scalar operators,

$$
\langle 0|\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)|0\rangle
$$

can depend only on Poincaré-invariant combinations, subject to spin indices, operator ordering, spectral conditions, and distributions.

Fourth, it constrains the $S$-matrix. Momentum conservation follows from translation invariance:

$$
\sum_{\text{in}}p^\mu=\sum_{\text{out}}p^\mu.
$$

Lorentz invariance constrains amplitudes to be built from invariant contractions of momenta, polarization data, spinor variables, and internal tensors.

Fifth, it motivates the invariant one-particle phase-space measure. On the positive-energy mass shell,

$$
\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}}
$$

is Lorentz invariant, with

$$
E_{\mathbf p}=\sqrt{\mathbf p^2+m^2}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\int\frac{d^4p}{(2\pi)^3}\,\theta(p^0)\delta(p^2-m^2)
=\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}}.
$$

This measure is why relativistic state normalizations and scattering formulas contain factors of $2E_{\mathbf p}$.

## Common pitfalls

**Confusing the Lorentz group with the Poincaré group.** The Lorentz group fixes the origin. The Poincaré group includes translations. Momentum and mass-shell orbits require translations and their generators $P^\mu$.

**Saying that spin is just a representation of the Lorentz group.** Massive particle spin is a representation of the little group $SO(3)$ or $SU(2)$. Lorentz transformations also move the momentum and induce Wigner rotations.

**Confusing field representations with particle representations.** A vector field has four components, but a photon has two helicities. A Dirac field has four components, but particle and antiparticle states are organized by Poincaré representations.

**Forgetting the double cover.** Half-integer spin is not an ordinary representation of $SO(3)$ or $SO^+(1,3)$. It is ordinary on the covers $SU(2)$ and $SL(2,\mathbb C)$.

**Treating helicity and spin as identical.** For massive particles, spin is defined in the rest frame. For massless particles, there is no rest frame; helicity is the little-group rotation charge along the direction of motion.

**Ignoring the null-rotation part of the massless little group without saying why.** Finite-helicity particles use representations where that part acts trivially. That is an extra statement, not the definition of the little group itself.

**Using finite-dimensional Lorentz representations as Hilbert-space representations.** Noncompact groups do not have nontrivial finite-dimensional unitary representations. Finite-dimensional Lorentz representations are field-component representations, not one-particle Hilbert-space representations.

## Research status

The Wigner classification of positive-energy unitary irreducible representations of the Poincaré group is established mathematical physics. For ordinary relativistic QFT, massive spin and massless helicity are standard and experimentally indispensable.

Active directions do not usually question this basic classification. They ask how it changes, extends, or reappears in special regimes: infrared sectors in gauge theories and gravity, asymptotic symmetries, celestial bases for scattering amplitudes, continuous-spin representations, higher-spin theories, curved backgrounds where global Poincaré symmetry is absent, and quantum-gravity settings where exact global spacetime symmetries may fail.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Verify the group law and inverse for

$$
\mathcal P^+=\mathbb R^{1,3}\rtimes SO^+(1,3).
$$

That is, show that

$$
(a,\Lambda)(b,\Omega)=\left(a+\Lambda b,\Lambda\Omega\right)
$$

and

$$
(a,\Lambda)^{-1}=(-\Lambda^{-1}a,\Lambda^{-1}).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The action of $(a,\Lambda)$ on spacetime is

$$
x\mapsto \Lambda x+a.
$$

Apply $(b,\Omega)$ first:

$$
x\mapsto \Omega x+b.
$$

Then apply $(a,\Lambda)$:

$$
\Omega x+b\mapsto \Lambda(\Omega x+b)+a
=\Lambda\Omega x+(a+\Lambda b).
$$

Therefore the product is

$$
(a,\Lambda)(b,\Omega)=\left(a+\Lambda b,\Lambda\Omega\right).
$$

For the inverse, require

$$
(a,\Lambda)(c,\Gamma)=(0,\mathbf 1).
$$

The Lorentz part gives

$$
\Lambda\Gamma=\mathbf 1,
\qquad
\Gamma=\Lambda^{-1}.
$$

The translation part gives

$$
a+\Lambda c=0,
\qquad
c=-\Lambda^{-1}a.
$$

Thus

$$
(a,\Lambda)^{-1}=(-\Lambda^{-1}a,\Lambda^{-1}).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2

Show that $P^2=P_\mu P^\mu$ commutes with $M^{\rho\sigma}$ using

$$
[M^{\rho\sigma},P^\mu]
=i\left(\eta^{\sigma\mu}P^\rho-\eta^{\rho\mu}P^\sigma\right).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use

$$
[M^{\rho\sigma},P^2]
=[M^{\rho\sigma},P_\mu P^\mu].
$$

Since the momenta commute with each other,

$$
[M^{\rho\sigma},P_\mu P^\mu]
=[M^{\rho\sigma},P_\mu]P^\mu+P_\mu[M^{\rho\sigma},P^\mu].
$$

Lowering the index in the commutator gives

$$
[M^{\rho\sigma},P_\mu]
=i\left(\delta^\sigma{}_{\mu}P^\rho-\delta^\rho{}_{\mu}P^\sigma\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
[M^{\rho\sigma},P_\mu]P^\mu
=i(P^\rho P^\sigma-P^\sigma P^\rho)=0.
$$

Similarly,

$$
P_\mu[M^{\rho\sigma},P^\mu]
=i(P^\sigma P^\rho-P^\rho P^\sigma)=0.
$$

So

$$
[M^{\rho\sigma},P^2]=0.
$$

Also $[P^\rho,P^2]=0$ is immediate from $[P^\rho,P^\mu]=0$. Hence $P^2$ is a Casimir.

</details>

### Exercise 3

In the rest frame of a massive particle, use

$$
W^0=0,
\qquad
\mathbf W=m\mathbf J
$$

to derive

$$
W^2=-m^2s(s+1).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In the rest frame,

$$
W^2=(W^0)^2-\mathbf W^2.
$$

Since $W^0=0$ and $\mathbf W=m\mathbf J$,

$$
W^2=-m^2\mathbf J^2.
$$

On a spin-$s$ irreducible representation of $SU(2)$,

$$
\mathbf J^2=s(s+1).
$$

Therefore

$$
W^2=-m^2s(s+1).
$$

This shows that the second Poincaré Casimir encodes spin for massive representations.

</details>

### Exercise 4

Explain why the little group of a massive standard momentum

$$
k^\mu=(m,0,0,0)
$$

is $SO(3)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A Lorentz transformation $\Lambda$ belongs to the little group if

$$
\Lambda k=k.
$$

Since

$$
k^\mu=(m,0,0,0),
$$

this condition says that the transformed time axis is the same time axis. A proper orthochronous Lorentz transformation preserving this timelike vector cannot include a boost, because a boost would give $k$ nonzero spatial components.

The remaining transformations act on the three spatial directions orthogonal to $k^\mu$ and preserve the Euclidean metric on that rest-space. They are ordinary spatial rotations:

$$
G_k\cong SO(3).
$$

For quantum states with half-integer spin, one uses the double cover $SU(2)$.

</details>

### Exercise 5

Show that

$$
\int d^4p\,\theta(p^0)\delta(p^2-m^2)f(p)
=\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{2E_{\mathbf p}}f(E_{\mathbf p},\mathbf p),
$$

where

$$
E_{\mathbf p}=\sqrt{\mathbf p^2+m^2}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Write

$$
p^2-m^2=(p^0)^2-\mathbf p^2-m^2=(p^0)^2-E_{\mathbf p}^2.
$$

Using

$$
\delta(g(x))=\sum_i\frac{\delta(x-x_i)}{|g'(x_i)|},
$$

with roots

$$
p^0=\pm E_{\mathbf p},
$$

gives

$$
\delta((p^0)^2-E_{\mathbf p}^2)
=\frac{1}{2E_{\mathbf p}}
\left[\delta(p^0-E_{\mathbf p})+\delta(p^0+E_{\mathbf p})\right].
$$

The factor $\theta(p^0)$ keeps only the positive-energy root:

$$
\theta(p^0)\delta((p^0)^2-E_{\mathbf p}^2)
=\frac{1}{2E_{\mathbf p}}\delta(p^0-E_{\mathbf p}).
$$

Therefore

$$
\int d^4p\,\theta(p^0)\delta(p^2-m^2)f(p)
=\int d^3\mathbf p\int dp^0\frac{\delta(p^0-E_{\mathbf p})}{2E_{\mathbf p}}f(p^0,\mathbf p),
$$

which gives

$$
\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{2E_{\mathbf p}}f(E_{\mathbf p},\mathbf p).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 6

A scalar field transforms as

$$
U(a,\mathbf 1)\phi(x)U(a,\mathbf 1)^{-1}=\phi(x+a).
$$

Assuming

$$
U(a,\mathbf 1)=e^{ia_\mu P^\mu},
$$

show that

$$
[P_\mu,\phi(x)]=-i\partial_\mu\phi(x).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Expand the unitary operator to first order in $a$:

$$
U(a,\mathbf 1)=1+ia_\mu P^\mu+O(a^2).
$$

Then

$$
U(a,\mathbf 1)\phi(x)U(a,\mathbf 1)^{-1}
=\phi(x)+ia_\mu[P^\mu,\phi(x)]+O(a^2).
$$

The right-hand side of the transformation law is

$$
\phi(x+a)=\phi(x)+a^\mu\partial_\mu\phi(x)+O(a^2).
$$

Equating terms linear in $a$ gives

$$
ia_\mu[P^\mu,\phi(x)]=a^\mu\partial_\mu\phi(x).
$$

After matching index placement consistently, this gives

$$
[P_\mu,\phi(x)]=-i\partial_\mu\phi(x).
$$

If one instead defines translations with $e^{-ia\cdot P}$, the sign in the commutator changes. The convention must be tracked together with the translation law.

</details>
