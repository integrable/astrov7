## Summary

A **group** is the abstract structure behind reversible transformations. It remembers how transformations compose, but it does not by itself say what they transform. A **group action** tells a group how to act on a space of fields, states, operators, vacua, couplings, or defects. A **representation** is a group action on a vector space by invertible linear maps.

For a group $G$, a representation on a vector space $V$ is a homomorphism

$$
\rho:G\to GL(V),
\qquad
\rho(g_1g_2)=\rho(g_1)\rho(g_2),
\qquad
\rho(e)=\mathbf 1_V.
$$

In QFT, this simple definition becomes the language of particle multiplets, Lorentz tensors, spinors, internal symmetries, gauge representations, selection rules, color factors, and allowed interactions.

An **algebra** is a vector space with a multiplication law. A **Lie algebra** is a vector space with an antisymmetric bracket satisfying the Jacobi identity. For matrix generators in the physicist convention used for compact groups in this volume,

$$
[T^a,T^b]=if^{ab}{}_cT^c.
$$

A group describes finite transformations. Its Lie algebra describes infinitesimal transformations near the identity. They are related, but they are not the same object. That distinction is the difference between remembering only local symmetry generators and remembering the global topology, center, and allowed representations of the group.

The slogan is:

$$
\text{symmetry in QFT}
=
\text{transformations}
+
\text{objects transformed}
+
\text{invariants allowed by the action}.
$$

## Core idea

A group answers one question:

$$
\text{How do transformations compose?}
$$

A representation answers a different question:

$$
\text{How do those transformations act on a particular vector space?}
$$

An invariant tensor answers a third question:

$$
\text{Which combinations survive the transformation?}
$$

QFT constantly needs all three. The group might be the Lorentz group, a rotation group, an internal flavor group, a color gauge group, a discrete symmetry group, or the Poincaré group. The vector space might be a space of fields, one-particle states, local operators, source terms, or multiplets of degenerate vacua. The invariant tensors decide which terms can be written in an action and which correlation functions or amplitudes are allowed.

For example, suppose complex scalar fields $\phi^i$ transform in a representation $R$ of an internal group $G$:

$$
\phi^i\mapsto R(g)^i{}_j\phi^j.
$$

A quadratic term

$$
\phi^\dagger_i\phi^i
$$

is invariant when $R(g)$ is unitary, because the transformation of $\phi$ and $\phi^\dagger$ cancel. A cubic term

$$
\lambda_{ijk}\phi^i\phi^j\phi^k
$$

is allowed only if $\lambda_{ijk}$ is an invariant tensor in $R^*\otimes R^*\otimes R^*$. A symmetry is therefore not only a decoration of the theory; it is a constraint on the possible theory.

This is why representation theory appears everywhere in QFT. It classifies objects and filters interactions.

## Setup and conventions

A **group** is a set $G$ with a product

$$
G\times G\to G,
\qquad
(g,h)\mapsto gh,
$$

satisfying four axioms:

1. **Closure:** $gh\in G$.
2. **Associativity:** $(gh)k=g(hk)$.
3. **Identity:** there is an element $e\in G$ with $eg=ge=g$.
4. **Inverse:** for every $g\in G$ there is $g^{-1}\in G$ with $gg^{-1}=g^{-1}g=e$.

Examples include:

$$
U(1)=\{z\in\mathbb C: |z|=1\},
$$

$$
SU(N)=\{U\in GL(N,\mathbb C): U^\dagger U=\mathbf 1,\,\det U=1\},
$$

and

$$
SO(N)=\{R\in GL(N,\mathbb R): R^TR=\mathbf 1,\,\det R=1\}.
$$

The product is ordinary multiplication of phases or matrices.

A group is **abelian** if

$$
gh=hg
$$

for all $g,h\in G$. Otherwise it is nonabelian. In QFT, this distinction is enormous. Abelian gauge theory has no photon self-interaction from the gauge kinetic term. Nonabelian Yang–Mills theory has gauge-boson self-interactions because the group multiplication is noncommutative at the infinitesimal level.

A **subgroup** $H\subseteq G$ is a subset that is itself a group under the same product. A subgroup is **normal**, written $H\triangleleft G$, if

$$
gHg^{-1}=H
$$

for all $g\in G$. Normal subgroups are precisely the subgroups by which one can form quotient groups $G/H$.

The **center** of $G$ is

$$
Z(G)=\{z\in G: zg=gz\text{ for all }g\in G\}.
$$

The center can look harmless in elementary group theory and then become very physical in QFT. It controls which representations descend to quotient groups, which line operators are genuine, and what higher-form symmetries may exist in gauge theory.

## Group actions

A group becomes physical only after it acts on something. A left action of $G$ on a set $X$ is a map

$$
G\times X\to X,
\qquad
(g,x)\mapsto gx,
$$

such that

$$
e x=x,
\qquad
 g_1(g_2x)=(g_1g_2)x.
$$

In QFT, $X$ might be:

- the space of classical field configurations;
- the Hilbert space of quantum states;
- a vector space of local operators;
- the set of vacua;
- the space of coupling constants;
- a set of line, surface, or boundary defects.

Given $x\in X$, the **orbit** of $x$ is

$$
Gx=\{gx:g\in G\}.
$$

The orbit is the set of all points reachable from $x$ by the symmetry action. In spontaneous symmetry breaking, the vacuum manifold is often described as an orbit of a symmetry group.

The **stabilizer** or **isotropy subgroup** of $x$ is

$$
G_x=\{g\in G: gx=x\}.
$$

If a vacuum $v$ is invariant under $H\subset G$, then $H$ is the unbroken subgroup and the broken directions are organized by the quotient-like space $G/H$ when the action is sufficiently nice.

For a finite group, orbit and stabilizer obey

$$
|Gx|=\frac{|G|}{|G_x|}.
$$

For a Lie group acting smoothly, the corresponding dimension statement is usually

$$
\dim(Gx)=\dim G-\dim G_x,
$$

when the orbit is a regular homogeneous space. This formula is one of the quiet engines behind Goldstone counting in simple relativistic cases, although subtleties enter in nonrelativistic systems and gauge theories.

## Homomorphisms, kernels, and quotients

A **group homomorphism** from $G$ to $H$ is a map

$$
\varphi:G\to H
$$

such that

$$
\varphi(g_1g_2)=\varphi(g_1)\varphi(g_2).
$$

The **kernel** is

$$
\ker\varphi=\{g\in G:\varphi(g)=e_H\}.
$$

The kernel is always a normal subgroup of $G$. The **image** is

$$
\operatorname{im}\varphi=\{\varphi(g):g\in G\}\subseteq H.
$$

A homomorphism is **injective** if its kernel is trivial. It is **surjective** if its image is all of $H$.

Representations are homomorphisms into groups of invertible linear maps. A representation may have a nontrivial kernel. When it does, some elements of the abstract group act trivially on that representation.

This is not a pedantic point. The fundamental two-dimensional representation of $SU(2)$ does not descend to a representation of $SO(3)=SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2$, because the central element $-\mathbf 1\in SU(2)$ acts as $-\mathbf 1$ rather than as the identity. Integer-spin representations descend to $SO(3)$; half-integer-spin representations do not.

So the Lie algebra alone does not know everything. The global group matters.

## Linear representations

A **linear representation** of $G$ on a vector space $V$ is a homomorphism

$$
\rho:G\to GL(V).
$$

If $V$ is finite-dimensional and a basis is chosen, then $\rho(g)$ is represented by an invertible matrix. But the representation is not the matrix. The matrix is a coordinate description of the linear map.

Two representations $\rho$ on $V$ and $\rho'$ on $V'$ are **equivalent** if there is an invertible linear map

$$
S:V\to V'
$$

such that

$$
\rho'(g)S=S\rho(g)
$$

for all $g\in G$. In matrix language,

$$
\rho'(g)=S\rho(g)S^{-1}.
$$

Changing basis changes matrices, not physics.

A subspace $W\subseteq V$ is **invariant** if

$$
\rho(g)W\subseteq W
$$

for every $g\in G$. A nonzero representation is **irreducible** if its only invariant subspaces are $0$ and $V$. Irreducible representations are the atoms from which many representation-theory decompositions are built.

A representation is **unitary** if $V$ has an inner product and

$$
\rho(g)^\dagger\rho(g)=\mathbf 1
$$

for every $g\in G$. For compact groups, finite-dimensional complex representations can be made unitary by averaging an inner product over the group. For noncompact groups, finite-dimensional representations are usually not unitary unless they are trivial; this is one reason Lorentz fields and particle Hilbert-space states are classified differently.

## Direct sums, tensor products, duals, and conjugates

If $V$ and $W$ carry representations $R$ and $S$, then their direct sum carries the representation

$$
(R\oplus S)(g)=
\begin{pmatrix}
R(g)&0\\
0&S(g)
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

This describes multiplets that transform independently.

Their tensor product carries

$$
(R\otimes S)(g)=R(g)\otimes S(g).
$$

Tensor products are how composite operators, two-particle states, and products of fields transform. Decomposing tensor products into irreducibles is the representation-theoretic version of “what channels can appear?”

The dual space $V^*$ carries the dual representation

$$
R^*(g)=(R(g)^{-1})^T.
$$

This is the representation naturally carried by covectors and lower indices. If $R$ is unitary, the Hermitian conjugate representation is often written using complex conjugate matrices:

$$
\overline R(g)=R(g)^*.
$$

In physics language, particles and antiparticles often transform in conjugate representations of internal symmetry groups. For $SU(N)$, the fundamental representation $N$ and antifundamental representation $\overline N$ are inequivalent for $N\ge 3$, but the fundamental of $SU(2)$ is pseudoreal. This difference later affects invariant tensors, Yukawa couplings, anomaly coefficients, and possible reality conditions on fermions.

## Invariant tensors

An **invariant vector** in a representation $V$ is a vector $v\in V$ such that

$$
\rho(g)v=v
$$

for every $g\in G$.

More generally, an invariant tensor is a tensor fixed by the induced action on a tensor product. If

$$
I\in V_1^*\otimes\cdots\otimes V_n^*,
$$

then $I$ can be used to build a scalar from fields

$$
I(\phi_1,\ldots,\phi_n).
$$

The expression is $G$-invariant exactly when $I$ is fixed by the dual tensor-product action.

In index notation, suppose

$$
\phi_k^{i_k}\mapsto R_k(g)^{i_k}{}_{j_k}\phi_k^{j_k}.
$$

A tensor $I_{i_1\cdots i_n}$ produces an invariant expression

$$
I_{i_1\cdots i_n}\phi_1^{i_1}\cdots\phi_n^{i_n}
$$

when

$$
I_{i_1\cdots i_n}
R_1(g)^{i_1}{}_{j_1}
\cdots
R_n(g)^{i_n}{}_{j_n}
=
I_{j_1\cdots j_n}.
$$

Infinitesimally, this becomes a sum of generator actions on every index. Schematically,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n
T^a_{R_k}\text{ acting on the }k\text{th index of }I
=0.
$$

This condition is one of the most useful tests in model building and amplitude construction. It tells you whether a proposed term is a singlet.

Important invariant tensors include:

$$
\delta^i{}_j,
\qquad
\epsilon_{i_1\cdots i_N}\text{ for }SU(N),
\qquad
f^{abc},
\qquad
d^{abc}\text{ for some groups and representations}.
$$

The Kronecker delta pairs a representation with its dual. The epsilon tensor produces determinant-like invariants. The structure constants $f^{abc}$ encode the Lie algebra in the adjoint representation. Symmetric invariant tensors such as $d^{abc}$ appear in anomaly and color-factor calculations.

## Selection rules and singlets

A basic principle is:

$$
\text{symmetry-invariant quantities must transform as the trivial representation}.
$$

The trivial representation is the one-dimensional representation in which every group element acts as $1$.

If a correlation function or amplitude is invariant under $G$, then the tensor product of all external representations must contain a singlet, otherwise the quantity vanishes. This is the abstract form of charge conservation and selection rules.

For $U(1)$, irreducible complex representations are labeled by charges $q$:

$$
\rho_q(e^{i\alpha})=e^{iq\alpha}.
$$

A product of operators with charges $q_1,\ldots,q_n$ is invariant only if

$$
q_1+q_2+\cdots+q_n=0.
$$

For nonabelian groups, “charge adds to zero” is replaced by “the tensor product contains the trivial representation.” This is a more subtle but more powerful statement.

## Algebras

An **algebra** over a field $\mathbb F$ is a vector space $A$ with a bilinear multiplication

$$
A\times A\to A,
\qquad
(a,b)\mapsto ab.
$$

Different kinds of algebra impose different rules on the multiplication.

An **associative algebra** obeys

$$
(ab)c=a(bc).
$$

Matrix algebras, operator algebras, and polynomial algebras are common examples.

A **commutative algebra** obeys

$$
ab=ba.
$$

Classical observables on phase space form a commutative algebra under pointwise multiplication, but they also carry a Poisson bracket.

A **Lie algebra** is a vector space $\mathfrak g$ with a bilinear bracket

$$
[\cdot,\cdot]:\mathfrak g\times\mathfrak g\to\mathfrak g
$$

that is antisymmetric,

$$
[X,Y]=-[Y,X],
$$

and satisfies the Jacobi identity,

$$
[X,[Y,Z]]+[Y,[Z,X]]+[Z,[X,Y]]=0.
$$

Every associative algebra gives a Lie algebra under the commutator bracket

$$
[A,B]=AB-BA.
$$

This is why matrices and quantum operators naturally generate Lie algebras.

In QFT, the word “algebra” appears in several related but different ways:

| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Lie algebra | infinitesimal symmetry generators and their brackets |
| operator algebra | algebra generated by quantum observables under products and sums |
| current algebra | equal-time or OPE algebra of currents |
| Clifford algebra | algebra generated by gamma matrices or quadratic forms |
| Grassmann algebra | algebra generated by anticommuting variables |
| universal enveloping algebra | associative algebra built from a Lie algebra |

Do not assume that all of these are the same kind of algebra. They share the idea of multiplication, but their rules and physical meanings differ.

## Lie algebras from symmetry generators

For a continuous group, infinitesimal transformations are generated by elements of a Lie algebra. In a representation $R$ of a compact group, physicists usually choose Hermitian generators $T_R^a$ and write finite transformations as

$$
R(g)=\exp(i\alpha_aT_R^a).
$$

The generators satisfy

$$
[T_R^a,T_R^b]=if^{ab}{}_cT_R^c.
$$

The real numbers $f^{ab}{}_c$ are the **structure constants** in this basis.

In quantum mechanics and QFT, continuous symmetries are represented on Hilbert space by unitary operators. For a unitary symmetry generated by Hermitian charges $Q^a$,

$$
U(\alpha)=\exp(i\alpha_a Q^a)
$$

up to a sign convention for active versus passive transformations. The charges obey the same Lie algebra, possibly with central extensions:

$$
[Q^a,Q^b]=if^{ab}{}_cQ^c+\text{central terms}.
$$

Classically, charges generate transformations by Poisson brackets. Quantum mechanically, they generate transformations by commutators. The precise sign depends on whether one writes $UOU^{-1}$ or $U^{-1}OU$, but the structural statement is stable: charges are the operators that realize infinitesimal symmetry transformations.

## Groups versus Lie algebras

The distinction between a group and its Lie algebra is one of the most important distinctions in this chapter.

A Lie algebra knows the local commutation relations near the identity. It does not, by itself, know the global topology of the group, the full center, or which Lie-algebra representations integrate to honest group representations.

The standard example is

$$
\mathfrak{su}(2)\cong\mathfrak{so}(3).
$$

The Lie algebras are the same, but the groups $SU(2)$ and $SO(3)$ are not the same. The group $SU(2)$ is the double cover of $SO(3)$:

$$
SO(3)\cong SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2.
$$

Integer-spin representations are representations of $SO(3)$. Half-integer-spin representations are representations of $SU(2)$ but not of $SO(3)$.

In gauge theory, this difference is not just a naming issue. Gauge theories with the same Lie algebra but different global gauge group can have different allowed line operators, magnetic charges, instanton sectors, and generalized symmetries. The algebra controls local gauge bosons; the group controls global sectors too.

## Gauge symmetry versus global symmetry

A global symmetry acts on physical states and operators. A gauge symmetry is a redundancy in the description.

This distinction is conceptually simple but technically rich. A global $U(1)$ symmetry may imply charge conservation and selection rules. A $U(1)$ gauge redundancy removes unphysical photon polarizations and requires constraints or gauge fixing. The same group name can appear in both contexts, but the physical meaning is different.

A helpful rough dictionary is:

| Structure | Global symmetry | Gauge redundancy |
|---|---|---|
| Parameter | constant or spacetime-independent for ordinary internal symmetry | arbitrary function of spacetime |
| Acts on physical states? | yes, usually | small gauge transformations act trivially on physical states |
| Current/charge | Noether current and conserved charge | constraint, Gauss law, boundary charge subtleties |
| Selection rules | yes | encoded through gauge-invariant operators |
| Local operators | may transform nontrivially | must be gauge-invariant to be physical |

The word “small” matters. Large gauge transformations, boundaries, defects, and asymptotic symmetries can produce genuine physical charges. Those subtleties are real, but they do not erase the basic distinction: gauge symmetry is not an ordinary symmetry of physical configurations; it is a redundancy whose invariant content must be extracted carefully.

## Examples in QFT

### Complex scalar with a U(1) symmetry

Let

$$
\phi\mapsto e^{iq\alpha}\phi.
$$

Then $\phi$ carries charge $q$, while $\phi^*$ carries charge $-q$. The kinetic and mass terms

$$
\partial_\mu\phi^*\partial^\mu\phi,
\qquad
m^2\phi^*\phi
$$

are neutral. A monomial

$$
\phi^n(\phi^*)^m
$$

has charge

$$
q(n-m).
$$

It is invariant only if $n=m$, unless the symmetry is explicitly broken or only a discrete subgroup is imposed.

### Multiplet in a nonabelian representation

Let $\phi^i$ transform in a representation $R$ of $G$:

$$
\phi^i\mapsto R(g)^i{}_j\phi^j.
$$

A quadratic invariant usually pairs $R$ with $R^*$:

$$
\phi^\dagger_i\phi^i.
$$

A cubic interaction needs an invariant tensor in $R^*\otimes R^*\otimes R^*$. If no such tensor exists, the cubic term is forbidden by the symmetry.

This is how representation theory becomes model-building technology. Instead of checking every group element, one decomposes tensor products and asks whether a singlet appears.

### Adjoint fields

A field $X=X^aT^a$ in the adjoint representation transforms as

$$
X\mapsto gXg^{-1}.
$$

Infinitesimally,

$$
\delta X=i[\alpha_aT^a,X]
$$

in Hermitian-generator conventions. The structure constants are the adjoint-representation matrices:

$$
(T^a_{\mathrm{adj}})^b{}_c=-if^{ab}{}_c
$$

with one common convention. The exact placement of signs depends on whether the adjoint action is written on components or on matrices, so pages doing component calculations should state the convention before using it.

Adjoint fields appear as gauge bosons, ghost fields, moment maps, Higgs fields in adjoint representations, and matrix-model variables.

### Spacetime transformations

For spacetime symmetries, fields transform both because their arguments move and because their components rotate. A scalar field under a Lorentz transformation $\Lambda$ transforms as

$$
\phi(x)\mapsto \phi(\Lambda^{-1}x).
$$

A vector field transforms as

$$
A^\mu(x)\mapsto \Lambda^\mu{}_\nu A^\nu(\Lambda^{-1}x).
$$

A spinor field transforms using a spin representation $S(\Lambda)$:

$$
\psi(x)\mapsto S(\Lambda)\psi(\Lambda^{-1}x).
$$

So spacetime representations mix two ideas: action on the base point $x$ and action on the field's internal component index. This is why the Lorentz group requires special care.

## Common pitfalls

**Confusing a group with a representation of the group.** $SU(2)$ is a group. The two-dimensional defining representation, the three-dimensional adjoint representation, and every spin-$j$ representation are different ways that group can act.

**Confusing a representation with a basis of matrices.** A basis change sends $R(g)$ to $SR(g)S^{-1}$. The representation did not change; only the coordinate description did.

**Assuming the Lie algebra determines the global group.** It does not. $SU(2)$ and $SO(3)$ have the same Lie algebra but different global representation theory. Gauge theories with the same Lie algebra can differ globally.

**Treating gauge symmetry as an ordinary physical symmetry.** Gauge redundancy removes descriptions; global symmetry relates physical configurations or states. Boundary charges and large transformations are important refinements, not permission to blur the basic distinction.

**Forgetting the dual representation.** Lower indices transform differently from upper indices. An invariant-looking contraction may fail if the indices actually live in the wrong representations.

**Thinking nonabelian charge conservation means each charge label separately adds to zero.** That is a $U(1)$ habit. For nonabelian groups, the correct statement is that the tensor product of representations must contain a singlet.

**Ignoring reality type.** Real, complex, and pseudoreal representations support different invariant bilinear forms and different possible reality conditions. This matters for Majorana fermions, global anomalies, and model building.

**Comparing trace normalizations blindly.** The symbols $T(R)$, $C_2(R)$, and $f^{abc}$ depend on generator normalization. The [Mathematical Conventions](/math-toolkit/conventions/) page fixes the default convention for compact simple groups.

## Research status

The material in this page is established finite-dimensional group and representation theory. The main convention-dependent ingredients are the choice of Hermitian versus anti-Hermitian generators and the normalization of traces and structure constants.

The active frontier begins when ordinary group symmetry is not the whole story. Modern QFT uses higher-form symmetries, categorical symmetries, non-invertible defects, operator algebras, quantum groups, vertex operator algebras, and representation categories. Those structures generalize the basic lesson of this page: symmetry is understood by how it acts and what invariants it permits.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Kernel of a homomorphism

Let $\varphi:G\to H$ be a group homomorphism. Show that $\ker\varphi$ is a normal subgroup of $G$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

First, $e_G\in\ker\varphi$ because

$$
\varphi(e_G)=e_H.
$$

If $a,b\in\ker\varphi$, then

$$
\varphi(ab^{-1})=\varphi(a)\varphi(b)^{-1}=e_He_H^{-1}=e_H,
$$

so $ab^{-1}\in\ker\varphi$. Thus $\ker\varphi$ is a subgroup.

To prove normality, take $k\in\ker\varphi$ and $g\in G$. Then

$$
\varphi(gkg^{-1})
=\varphi(g)\varphi(k)\varphi(g)^{-1}
=\varphi(g)e_H\varphi(g)^{-1}
=e_H.
$$

Therefore $gkg^{-1}\in\ker\varphi$, so $\ker\varphi$ is normal.

</details>

### Exercise 2: U(1) selection rule

Suppose local operators $\mathcal O_i$ have $U(1)$ charges $q_i$, so that

$$
\mathcal O_i\mapsto e^{iq_i\alpha}\mathcal O_i.
$$

Assuming the vacuum is invariant under the symmetry, show that

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle
$$

can be nonzero only if $\sum_i q_i=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under the symmetry, the product transforms as

$$
\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n
\mapsto
\exp\left(i\alpha\sum_{i=1}^n q_i\right)
\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n.
$$

If the vacuum and the path-integral measure or Hilbert-space inner product are invariant, then the correlation function must equal its transformed value:

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle
=
\exp\left(i\alpha\sum_i q_i\right)
\langle \mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle
$$

for all $\alpha$. If the correlator is nonzero, the phase must be $1$ for all $\alpha$, so

$$
\sum_i q_i=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Invariance of the Hermitian norm

Let $R(g)$ be a unitary representation on $\mathbb C^N$. Show that

$$
\phi^\dagger\phi
$$

is invariant under $\phi\mapsto R(g)\phi$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under the transformation,

$$
\phi^\dagger\phi
\mapsto
(R(g)\phi)^\dagger R(g)\phi
=
\phi^\dagger R(g)^\dagger R(g)\phi.
$$

Because the representation is unitary,

$$
R(g)^\dagger R(g)=\mathbf 1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\phi^\dagger R(g)^\dagger R(g)\phi=\phi^\dagger\phi.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Orbit and stabilizer in a simple action

Let $G=SO(3)$ act on $X=S^2\subset\mathbb R^3$ by rotations. For the north pole $n=(0,0,1)$, identify the orbit $Gn$ and stabilizer $G_n$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Any point on $S^2$ can be reached by rotating the north pole, so

$$
Gn=S^2.
$$

The rotations that leave $n$ fixed are rotations around the $z$-axis. These form a subgroup isomorphic to $SO(2)$. Thus

$$
G_n\cong SO(2).
$$

The orbit is the homogeneous space

$$
S^2\cong SO(3)/SO(2).
$$

The dimension count is

$$
\dim S^2=2=3-1=\dim SO(3)-\dim SO(2).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5: Singlet criterion for a bilinear

Let $\phi^i$ transform in $R$ and $\chi_i$ transform in the dual representation $R^*$. Show that $\chi_i\phi^i$ is invariant.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The transformations are

$$
\phi^i\mapsto R(g)^i{}_j\phi^j,
$$

and

$$
\chi_i\mapsto \chi_j(R(g)^{-1})^j{}_i.
$$

Then

$$
\chi_i\phi^i
\mapsto
\chi_k(R(g)^{-1})^k{}_i R(g)^i{}_j\phi^j
=
\chi_k\delta^k{}_j\phi^j
=
\chi_j\phi^j.
$$

So the contraction between a representation and its dual is invariant.

</details>
