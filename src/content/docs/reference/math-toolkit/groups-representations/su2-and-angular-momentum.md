## Summary

$SU(2)$ is the smallest nonabelian compact Lie group and the universal training ground for representation theory in QFT. It controls ordinary spin, isospin, weak-isospin doublets, angular-momentum addition, and the two $SU(2)$ factors that appear after complexifying the Lorentz algebra.

As a group,

$$
SU(2)=\{U\in GL(2,\mathbb C):U^\dagger U=\mathbf 1,\ \det U=1\}.
$$

Its Lie algebra has three Hermitian generators $J_i$ satisfying

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k,
\qquad i,j,k=1,2,3.
$$

The irreducible representations are labeled by

$$
j=0,\frac12,1,\frac32,\ldots,
$$

and the spin-$j$ representation has dimension

$$
\dim V_j=2j+1.
$$

A standard basis is $|j,m\rangle$, where

$$
J^2|j,m\rangle=j(j+1)|j,m\rangle,
\qquad
J_3|j,m\rangle=m|j,m\rangle,
$$

with

$$
m=-j,-j+1,\ldots,j-1,j.
$$

The core formulas are simple, but the conceptual payoff is large. $SU(2)$ teaches the difference between a group and its Lie algebra, between integer and half-integer spin, between a representation and a basis, and between a field multiplet and a physical spin state. Tiny group, obnoxiously load-bearing.

## Core idea

The Lie algebra of $SU(2)$ is the algebra of angular momentum. Once the commutator

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k
$$

is fixed, almost everything else follows from representation theory.

The three generators do not commute, so they cannot be simultaneously diagonalized. Instead, one diagonalizes the commuting pair

$$
J^2=J_1^2+J_2^2+J_3^2,
\qquad
J_3.
$$

The operator $J^2$ is central in any irreducible representation:

$$
[J^2,J_i]=0.
$$

Therefore Schur's lemma says that $J^2$ acts as a scalar on each irreducible representation. That scalar is conventionally written

$$
j(j+1).
$$

The remaining states in the same irreducible representation are generated by the ladder operators

$$
J_+=J_1+iJ_2,
\qquad
J_-=J_1-iJ_2.
$$

They obey

$$
[J_3,J_\pm]=\pm J_\pm,
$$

so if $|j,m\rangle$ has $J_3$ eigenvalue $m$, then $J_\pm|j,m\rangle$ has eigenvalue $m\pm1$, unless it vanishes.

The finite-dimensionality of unitary representations forces the ladder to stop. That produces a highest weight $m=j$ and a lowest weight $m=-j$. The allowed values of $j$ are integers and half-integers.

## Setup and conventions

We use Hermitian angular-momentum generators $J_i$. Finite rotations are written as

$$
U(R)=\exp(-i\theta_iJ_i)
$$

in a unitary representation. Some sources write $\theta_iJ_i$ with anti-Hermitian generators; that convention removes the explicit $i$ from the exponential and from the Lie-algebra bracket. Here we keep the physics convention.

The Pauli matrices are

$$
\sigma_1=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\sigma_2=\begin{pmatrix}0&-i\\i&0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\sigma_3=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}.
$$

They satisfy

$$
\sigma_i\sigma_j=\delta_{ij}\mathbf 1+i\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k,
$$

and

$$
[\sigma_i,\sigma_j]=2i\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k.
$$

The fundamental spin-$\frac12$ generators are

$$
J_i=\frac12\sigma_i.
$$

Thus

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k.
$$

We normalize the fundamental $SU(2)$ generators as

$$
\operatorname{tr}_F(J_iJ_j)=\frac12\delta_{ij}.
$$

This matches the common $SU(N)$ normalization

$$
\operatorname{tr}_F(T^aT^b)=\frac12\delta^{ab}
$$

when $N=2$.

For a general spin-$j$ irreducible representation $V_j$, we write

$$
J^2=J_iJ_i,
$$

with the repeated index summed over $1,2,3$. In the angular-momentum basis,

$$
J^2|j,m\rangle=j(j+1)|j,m\rangle,
$$

$$
J_3|j,m\rangle=m|j,m\rangle.
$$

We choose phases so that

$$
J_+|j,m\rangle=\sqrt{(j-m)(j+m+1)}\,|j,m+1\rangle,
$$

and

$$
J_-|j,m\rangle=\sqrt{(j+m)(j-m+1)}\,|j,m-1\rangle.
$$

Different phase conventions for $|j,m\rangle$ change Clebsch–Gordan coefficients by signs or phases, but they do not change representation-theoretic statements.

## The group SU(2)

An element of $SU(2)$ can be written as

$$
U=\begin{pmatrix}
a&b\\
-b^*&a^*
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
|a|^2+|b|^2=1.
$$

Thus, as a manifold,

$$
SU(2)\cong S^3.
$$

This topological fact matters. The group $SU(2)$ is simply connected, while $SO(3)$ is not. The two groups have the same Lie algebra, but they are not the same global group.

There is a two-to-one homomorphism

$$
SU(2)\to SO(3),
$$

whose kernel is

$$
\{+\mathbf 1,-\mathbf 1\}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
SO(3)\cong SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2.
$$

This is the first place where a reader sees why “the Lie algebra is all that matters” is a trap. Infinitesimally, $SU(2)$ and $SO(3)$ look identical. Globally, $SU(2)$ has half-integer spin representations and $SO(3)$ does not.

The element $-\mathbf 1\in SU(2)$ acts in the spin-$j$ representation as

$$
(-\mathbf 1)\mapsto (-1)^{2j}\mathbf 1_{V_j}.
$$

Therefore integer-spin representations descend to honest representations of $SO(3)$, while half-integer spin representations are faithful to the double cover $SU(2)$.

This is not merely a mathematical nicety. Spinor fields in relativistic QFT transform under double covers of rotation and Lorentz groups. A $2\pi$ rotation acts as $-1$ on a spinor state or spinor field, even though it acts trivially on ordinary spatial vectors.

## The Lie algebra su(2)

The mathematical Lie algebra of $SU(2)$ consists of traceless anti-Hermitian $2\times2$ matrices. In physics conventions, we multiply by $i$ and use traceless Hermitian matrices. A convenient basis is

$$
J_i=\frac12\sigma_i.
$$

The commutator is

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k.
$$

The algebra is simple: it has no nonzero proper ideals. It is also compact: in the adjoint basis, the Killing form is negative definite in anti-Hermitian conventions, or positive after the physicist's $i$ convention is used in traces.

For many QFT formulas, the essential invariant tensors of $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ are

$$
\delta_{ij},
\qquad
\epsilon_{ijk}.
$$

The first contracts adjoint indices. The second is the structure constant tensor.

Because the adjoint representation of $SU(2)$ is three-dimensional, it is isomorphic as a real representation to the vector representation of $SO(3)$. In fact, the adjoint action gives the double-cover map:

$$
U J_i U^{-1}=R_i{}^j(U)J_j,
\qquad
R(U)\in SO(3).
$$

The two elements $U$ and $-U$ give the same rotation matrix $R(U)$.

## Ladder operators

Define

$$
J_\pm=J_1\pm iJ_2.
$$

The commutation relations imply

$$
[J_3,J_+]=J_+,
\qquad
[J_3,J_-]=-J_-,
$$

and

$$
[J_+,J_-]=2J_3.
$$

If

$$
J_3|j,m\rangle=m|j,m\rangle,
$$

then

$$
J_3(J_+|j,m\rangle)=(m+1)(J_+|j,m\rangle),
$$

and

$$
J_3(J_-|j,m\rangle)=(m-1)(J_-|j,m\rangle).
$$

Thus $J_+$ raises the $J_3$ eigenvalue by one and $J_-$ lowers it by one. The ladder terminates because a finite-dimensional unitary representation cannot contain infinitely many orthonormal states with different $m$ values.

Let $|j,j\rangle$ be the highest-weight state:

$$
J_+|j,j\rangle=0.
$$

The rest of the representation is generated by repeated application of $J_-$:

$$
|j,j-1\rangle,
\quad
|j,j-2\rangle,
\quad
\ldots,
\quad
|j,-j\rangle.
$$

The norm formulas fix the coefficients. Since $J_-=(J_+)^\dagger$, positivity gives

$$
\|J_+|j,m\rangle\|^2
=\langle j,m|J_-J_+|j,m\rangle.
$$

Using

$$
J_-J_+=J^2-J_3^2-J_3,
$$

we find

$$
\|J_+|j,m\rangle\|^2
=j(j+1)-m(m+1)
=(j-m)(j+m+1).
$$

Therefore

$$
J_+|j,m\rangle
=\sqrt{(j-m)(j+m+1)}\,|j,m+1\rangle.
$$

Similarly,

$$
J_-|j,m\rangle
=\sqrt{(j+m)(j-m+1)}\,|j,m-1\rangle.
$$

This normalization is the one most often used for angular momentum in quantum mechanics and QFT.

## Classification of irreducible representations

The finite-dimensional irreducible unitary representations of $SU(2)$ are labeled by a nonnegative half-integer $j$:

$$
j\in\frac12\mathbb Z_{\ge0}.
$$

For each $j$, the $m$ values are

$$
m=-j,-j+1,\ldots,j-1,j,
$$

so the number of states is

$$
2j+1.
$$

The first few representations are:

| $j$ | dimension | common name | basis weights |
|---:|---:|---|---|
| $0$ | $1$ | singlet | $0$ |
| $\frac12$ | $2$ | doublet, spinor | $-\frac12,\frac12$ |
| $1$ | $3$ | triplet, vector, adjoint | $-1,0,1$ |
| $\frac32$ | $4$ | quartet | $-\frac32,-\frac12,\frac12,\frac32$ |
| $2$ | $5$ | quintet | $-2,-1,0,1,2$ |

For $SU(2)$, every finite-dimensional irreducible representation is self-dual. That is,

$$
V_j^*\cong V_j.
$$

But there is a useful distinction:

- integer-spin representations are real;
- half-integer-spin representations are pseudoreal.

The fundamental doublet is pseudoreal. There is an invariant antisymmetric tensor

$$
\epsilon_{ab},
\qquad
\epsilon_{12}=1,
$$

that identifies the fundamental representation with its dual. This tensor is the reason that $SU(2)$ doublet index gymnastics often looks different from $SU(N)$ with $N\ge3$.

For $U\in SU(2)$,

$$
U^T\epsilon U=\epsilon.
$$

This identity is the $SU(2)$ version of invariant antisymmetric spinor contraction.

## Tensor products and angular-momentum addition

The tensor product of two $SU(2)$ irreducible representations decomposes without multiplicities:

$$
V_{j_1}\otimes V_{j_2}
\cong
\bigoplus_{j=|j_1-j_2|}^{j_1+j_2}V_j,
$$

where $j$ increases in integer steps.

Examples include

$$
\frac12\otimes\frac12=0\oplus1,
$$

$$
\frac12\otimes1=\frac12\oplus\frac32,
$$

$$
1\otimes1=0\oplus1\oplus2.
$$

In dimension notation,

$$
2\otimes2=1\oplus3,
$$

$$
2\otimes3=2\oplus4,
$$

$$
3\otimes3=1\oplus3\oplus5.
$$

The first formula explains the familiar split of two spin-$\frac12$ states into a spin singlet and a spin triplet. The singlet is antisymmetric:

$$
|0,0\rangle
=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}
\left(
|\uparrow\downarrow\rangle-|\downarrow\uparrow\rangle
\right),
$$

while the triplet is symmetric:

$$
|1,1\rangle=|\uparrow\uparrow\rangle,
$$

$$
|1,0\rangle
=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}
\left(
|\uparrow\downarrow\rangle+|\downarrow\uparrow\rangle
\right),
$$

$$
|1,-1\rangle=|\downarrow\downarrow\rangle.
$$

The coefficients appearing in the change of basis

$$
|j_1,m_1\rangle\otimes |j_2,m_2\rangle
=\sum_{j,m}
C^{j,m}_{j_1,m_1;j_2,m_2}|j,m\rangle
$$

are Clebsch–Gordan coefficients. The selection rule is

$$
m=m_1+m_2.
$$

Different phase conventions change the signs of some coefficients. The decomposition itself does not care about those signs.

## Characters

The character of a representation is the trace of a group element:

$$
\chi_R(g)=\operatorname{tr}_R\rho_R(g).
$$

For compact groups, characters are powerful because they are invariant under conjugation and encode irreducible decompositions.

Every $SU(2)$ element is conjugate to one of the form

$$
\exp(-i\theta J_3).
$$

In the spin-$j$ representation,

$$
\chi_j(\theta)=\sum_{m=-j}^{j}e^{-im\theta}.
$$

This finite geometric series gives

$$
\chi_j(\theta)
=
\frac{\sin\left((2j+1)\theta/2\right)}{\sin(\theta/2)}.
$$

For the fundamental doublet,

$$
\chi_{1/2}(\theta)=2\cos\frac\theta2.
$$

For the triplet,

$$
\chi_1(\theta)=1+2\cos\theta.
$$

Character multiplication encodes tensor products. For example,

$$
\chi_{1/2}(\theta)^2
=\left(2\cos\frac\theta2\right)^2
=1+2\cos\theta+1
=\chi_0(\theta)+\chi_1(\theta).
$$

Thus

$$
\frac12\otimes\frac12=0\oplus1.
$$

Characters are often the cleanest way to check angular-momentum decompositions without writing Clebsch–Gordan coefficients explicitly.

## Casimirs and trace normalizations

For a spin-$j$ representation,

$$
J_iJ_i=j(j+1)\mathbf 1_{2j+1}.
$$

Thus the quadratic Casimir is

$$
C_2(j)=j(j+1).
$$

The Dynkin index $T(j)$ is defined by

$$
\operatorname{tr}_{V_j}(J_iJ_k)=T(j)\delta_{ik}.
$$

Taking $i=k$ and summing over $i$ gives

$$
\operatorname{tr}_{V_j}(J_iJ_i)=3T(j).
$$

But

$$
\operatorname{tr}_{V_j}(J_iJ_i)=j(j+1)\dim V_j=j(j+1)(2j+1).
$$

Therefore

$$
T(j)=\frac13j(j+1)(2j+1).
$$

For the fundamental $j=\frac12$,

$$
T\left(\frac12\right)=\frac12.
$$

For the adjoint $j=1$,

$$
T(1)=2,
\qquad
C_2(1)=2.
$$

In gauge theory, these numbers enter beta functions, anomaly coefficients, and color factors. The group-theory table may look like bookkeeping, but it is the bookkeeping that decides a loop coefficient.

## Rotations, spin, and fields

In nonrelativistic quantum mechanics, angular momentum labels states under spatial rotations. In relativistic QFT, the story splits.

A local field transforms in a finite-dimensional representation of the Lorentz group or its double cover. Its spinor or vector indices tell us which representation the field carries.

A one-particle state transforms in a unitary irreducible representation of the Poincaré group. For massive particles in $3+1$ dimensions, the little group is $SO(3)$, or more precisely its double cover $SU(2)$ at the spin level. The physical spin label is again

$$
s=0,\frac12,1,\frac32,\ldots.
$$

This is why the $SU(2)$ angular-momentum algebra appears in both field-index technology and particle-state classification. It is the same compact algebra, used in two related but not identical representation problems.

For example:

- a scalar field has Lorentz representation $(0,0)$ and creates spin-$0$ particles;
- a Weyl spinor field has a spinor Lorentz representation and creates spin-$\frac12$ particles;
- a vector field has a Lorentz vector representation, but a massless gauge boson has physical helicities $\pm1$, not three spin states.

That last bullet is the kind of thing that makes students suspect the subject is messing with them. It is not. It is just that field representations, gauge redundancy, and physical Hilbert-space representations are different layers.

## Isospin and internal SU(2)

The same group $SU(2)$ also appears as an internal symmetry. Then it does not rotate space; it rotates field components.

For example, an isospin doublet may be written as

$$
\Psi=\begin{pmatrix}\psi_1\\ \psi_2\end{pmatrix},
$$

with transformation

$$
\Psi\mapsto U\Psi,
\qquad
U\in SU(2).
$$

Infinitesimally,

$$
\delta\Psi=-i\theta_i\frac{\sigma_i}{2}\Psi.
$$

The invariant antisymmetric tensor $\epsilon_{ab}$ allows an invariant contraction of two doublets:

$$
\epsilon_{ab}\psi^a\chi^b.
$$

Because $2\otimes2=1\oplus3$, two doublets can combine into a singlet or triplet. In model building, this decomposition decides which interactions are allowed by the symmetry.

Internal $SU(2)$ and spacetime spin $SU(2)$ use the same mathematics but act on different vector spaces. Confusing them is a rite of passage; staying confused is optional.

## SU(2) versus SO(3)

The Lie algebras are isomorphic:

$$
\mathfrak{su}(2)\cong\mathfrak{so}(3).
$$

But the groups differ globally:

$$
SO(3)\cong SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2.
$$

The difference appears in representation theory. $SU(2)$ has irreducible representations for every half-integer $j$. $SO(3)$ has only integer-spin representations.

A spin-$j$ representation of $SU(2)$ descends to a representation of $SO(3)$ exactly when the center element $-\mathbf 1$ acts trivially:

$$
(-1)^{2j}=1.
$$

This requires $2j$ to be even, so $j$ must be an integer.

In QFT, this distinction is the compact-model version of a broader rule: the global form of a symmetry group matters. Two theories may have the same Lie algebra but different allowed line operators, bundles, topological sectors, or representations.

## The adjoint representation

The adjoint representation of a Lie group acts on its Lie algebra by conjugation. For $SU(2)$,

$$
\operatorname{Ad}_U(X)=UXU^{-1}.
$$

Taking $X=X_iJ_i$, the transformed coefficients are related by a real orthogonal $3\times3$ matrix:

$$
UX_iJ_iU^{-1}=X_iR^j{}_i(U)J_j.
$$

Thus the adjoint representation of $SU(2)$ is three-dimensional and is equivalent to spin $j=1$.

At the Lie-algebra level, the adjoint generators are

$$
(J_i^{\mathrm{ad}})_{jk}=-i\epsilon_{ijk}
$$

or, with indices moved depending on convention,

$$
(J_i^{\mathrm{ad}})^j{}_k=-i\epsilon_{ijk}.
$$

They satisfy the same commutation relations:

$$
[J_i^{\mathrm{ad}},J_j^{\mathrm{ad}}]
=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k^{\mathrm{ad}}.
$$

Gauge bosons of an $SU(2)$ gauge theory carry an adjoint index, so the gauge-field components are naturally triplet-valued:

$$
A_\mu=A_\mu^iJ_i.
$$

Again, the representation-theory statement is simple: the Lie algebra has dimension three.

## Symmetric powers and a useful construction

Every spin-$j$ representation of $SU(2)$ can be constructed as a symmetric power of the fundamental doublet:

$$
V_j\cong \operatorname{Sym}^{2j}(\mathbb C^2).
$$

This formula is worth remembering. It says that the spin-$j$ representation is made from $2j$ symmetrized spin-$\frac12$ indices.

For example,

$$
V_{1/2}=\mathbb C^2,
$$

$$
V_1=\operatorname{Sym}^2(\mathbb C^2),
$$

$$
V_{3/2}=\operatorname{Sym}^3(\mathbb C^2).
$$

The dimension of $\operatorname{Sym}^n(\mathbb C^2)$ is $n+1$. Setting $n=2j$ gives

$$
\dim V_j=2j+1.
$$

This construction is the prototype for higher-rank representation theory: build irreducible representations by symmetrizing and antisymmetrizing indices, then remove traces or impose relations. For $SU(2)$, life is kind and one row of symmetrized boxes is enough.

## Clebsch–Gordan coefficients versus invariant tensors

Clebsch–Gordan coefficients do two related jobs.

First, they give the unitary change of basis from a product basis to an irreducible basis:

$$
|j_1,m_1\rangle|j_2,m_2\rangle
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
|j,m\rangle.
$$

Second, they are invariant tensors. They define $SU(2)$-equivariant maps

$$
V_{j_1}\otimes V_{j_2}\to V_j.
$$

In QFT language, invariant tensors are what let us write symmetry-invariant couplings. If fields transform in $V_{j_1}$, $V_{j_2}$, and $V_j^*$, a Clebsch–Gordan map gives a possible invariant interaction.

For example, two doublets can form a singlet:

$$
\epsilon_{ab}\psi^a\chi^b.
$$

They can also form a triplet:

$$
\psi^a(\sigma_i)_a{}^b\chi_b,
$$

with the appropriate index convention. The same tensor-product decomposition underlies both formulas.

## Projective representations and spin

Quantum states are rays, not vectors. This means that symmetries can act projectively on Hilbert space. A projective representation of $SO(3)$ may lift to an ordinary representation of its double cover $SU(2)$.

This is the natural home of half-integer spin. A spinor is not an ordinary representation of $SO(3)$; it is an ordinary representation of $SU(2)$, or equivalently a projective representation of $SO(3)$.

A $2\pi$ rotation corresponds to $-\mathbf 1\in SU(2)$, not to $+\mathbf 1$. In the spin-$j$ representation it acts as

$$
e^{-i2\pi J_3}=(-1)^{2j}\mathbf 1.
$$

Thus for $j=\frac12$,

$$
e^{-i2\pi J_3}=-\mathbf 1.
$$

For $j=1$,

$$
e^{-i2\pi J_3}=+\mathbf 1.
$$

A $4\pi$ rotation is always trivial in an ordinary $SU(2)$ representation.

This is not a paradox because the physical state is a ray. The sign under a $2\pi$ rotation becomes physically meaningful through interference and through the structure of multiparticle states, not because the isolated ray has changed into a different ray.

| Object | Meaning for SU(2) | QFT use |

| $J_i$ | Hermitian generators | spin, isospin, charges |
| $[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k$ | Lie algebra | infinitesimal symmetry |
| $J^2$ | quadratic Casimir | representation label |
| $j$ | spin/highest weight | multiplet type |
| $m$ | $J_3$ weight | component label |
| $J_\pm$ | ladder operators | component recursion |
| $V_j$ | spin-$j$ irrep | field or state multiplet |
| $2j+1$ | dimension | number of components |
| $V_{j_1}\otimes V_{j_2}$ | product representation | composite operators, couplings |
| Clebsch–Gordan coefficients | change of basis/invariant maps | allowed tensor structures |
| $\chi_j(\theta)$ | character | decomposition checks |
| $SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2$ | $SO(3)$ | integer-spin rotations |

## Common pitfalls

**Forgetting the global group.** The Lie algebras $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ and $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ are the same, but $SU(2)$ and $SO(3)$ have different global representation theory. Half-integer spin is visible only after choosing the double cover.

**Calling every two-component object a spinor.** A doublet under an internal $SU(2)$ is mathematically a two-dimensional representation, but it is not automatically a spacetime spinor. A weak-isospin doublet and a Lorentz Weyl spinor transform under different groups.

**Mixing Hermitian and anti-Hermitian generators.** Physicists usually write Hermitian $J_i$ and $[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k$. Mathematicians often write anti-Hermitian generators and no explicit $i$. Translation is harmless only if it is done everywhere.

**Treating Clebsch–Gordan coefficients as invariant facts independent of phase conventions.** The decomposition $j_1\otimes j_2=\oplus_j j$ is invariant. Individual Clebsch–Gordan coefficient signs depend on basis and phase conventions.

**Confusing field spin with particle spin in gauge theories.** A vector field has a Lorentz vector index. A massless gauge boson has physical helicities $\pm1$ after gauge redundancy and constraints are accounted for. The field representation is not the whole Hilbert-space story.

**Forgetting pseudoreality of the doublet.** The $SU(2)$ fundamental is equivalent to its dual via an antisymmetric invariant tensor. This is not true for the fundamental of $SU(N)$ with $N\ge3$.

[SL(2,C) and the Lorentz Group](/math-toolkit/groups-representations/sl2c-and-lorentz-group/) uses two complexified $SU(2)$-type algebras to organize finite-dimensional Lorentz representations. The labels $(j_L,j_R)$ are built out of the same spin-$j$ representation theory developed here.

The later Poincaré group page should explain why particle states are classified by unitary irreducible representations of the full spacetime symmetry group, not only by finite-dimensional Lorentz representations. The later Clifford algebras and spinors chapter should explain how gamma matrices package spinor representations and Dirac equations.

## Research status

The representation theory of compact $SU(2)$ is completely established. Its use in angular momentum, spin, isospin, and tensor-product decomposition is standard graduate physics.

Modern research extends this basic structure in several directions. Quantum groups deform $SU(2)$ representation theory and appear in integrable systems, knot invariants, Chern–Simons theory, and low-dimensional topological phases. Spin networks and recoupling theory use $SU(2)$ data in quantum geometry and topological field theory. Categorical versions of symmetry replace ordinary representation categories by richer tensor categories. Those topics should be treated only after the ordinary $SU(2)$ backbone is solid.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Using

$$
J_i=\frac12\sigma_i,
$$

prove that

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The Pauli matrices satisfy

$$
[\sigma_i,\sigma_j]=2i\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k.
$$

Therefore

$$
[J_i,J_j]
=\left[\frac12\sigma_i,\frac12\sigma_j\right]
=\frac14[\sigma_i,\sigma_j].
$$

Substituting the Pauli commutator gives

$$
[J_i,J_j]
=\frac14(2i\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k)
=\frac{i}{2}\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k.
$$

Since $J_k=\sigma_k/2$,

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2

Show that

$$
J_-J_+=J^2-J_3^2-J_3.
$$

Use this to derive the coefficient in

$$
J_+|j,m\rangle
=\sqrt{(j-m)(j+m+1)}\,|j,m+1\rangle.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Start from

$$
J_-=J_1-iJ_2,
\qquad
J_+=J_1+iJ_2.
$$

Then

$$
J_-J_+=(J_1-iJ_2)(J_1+iJ_2)
=J_1^2+J_2^2+iJ_1J_2-iJ_2J_1.
$$

The last two terms are

$$
i[J_1,J_2]=i(iJ_3)=-J_3.
$$

Thus

$$
J_-J_+=J_1^2+J_2^2-J_3.
$$

Since

$$
J^2=J_1^2+J_2^2+J_3^2,
$$

we get

$$
J_-J_+=J^2-J_3^2-J_3.
$$

Now compute the norm:

$$
\|J_+|j,m\rangle\|^2
=\langle j,m|J_-J_+|j,m\rangle.
$$

Using the identity above,

$$
\|J_+|j,m\rangle\|^2
=j(j+1)-m^2-m.
$$

Factor the expression:

$$
j(j+1)-m(m+1)=(j-m)(j+m+1).
$$

With the conventional positive coefficient,

$$
J_+|j,m\rangle
=\sqrt{(j-m)(j+m+1)}\,|j,m+1\rangle.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3

Decompose

$$
\frac12\otimes\frac12
$$

into irreducible $SU(2)$ representations, and write explicit singlet and triplet states.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The angular-momentum addition rule gives

$$
\frac12\otimes\frac12=0\oplus1.
$$

The product space has dimension $2\cdot2=4$, while the right-hand side has dimension

$$
1+3=4.
$$

Let

$$
|\uparrow\rangle=\left|\frac12,\frac12\right\rangle,
\qquad
|\downarrow\rangle=\left|\frac12,-\frac12\right\rangle.
$$

The triplet states are

$$
|1,1\rangle=|\uparrow\uparrow\rangle,
$$

$$
|1,0\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}
\left(|\uparrow\downarrow\rangle+|\downarrow\uparrow\rangle\right),
$$

$$
|1,-1\rangle=|\downarrow\downarrow\rangle.
$$

The singlet state is

$$
|0,0\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}
\left(|\uparrow\downarrow\rangle-|\downarrow\uparrow\rangle\right).
$$

The triplet is symmetric under exchange of the two factors; the singlet is antisymmetric.

</details>

### Exercise 4

Show that the spin-$j$ character is

$$
\chi_j(\theta)=\frac{\sin((2j+1)\theta/2)}{\sin(\theta/2)}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For a rotation generated by $J_3$,

$$
\chi_j(\theta)=\sum_{m=-j}^{j}e^{-im\theta}.
$$

This is a finite geometric series. Write $m=-j+n$, where $n=0,1,\ldots,2j$. Then

$$
\chi_j(\theta)
=e^{ij\theta}\sum_{n=0}^{2j}e^{-in\theta}.
$$

The geometric sum is

$$
\sum_{n=0}^{2j}e^{-in\theta}
=\frac{1-e^{-i(2j+1)\theta}}{1-e^{-i\theta}}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\chi_j(\theta)
=e^{ij\theta}\frac{1-e^{-i(2j+1)\theta}}{1-e^{-i\theta}}.
$$

Use

$$
1-e^{-ia}=e^{-ia/2}(e^{ia/2}-e^{-ia/2})
=2ie^{-ia/2}\sin(a/2).
$$

After the phases cancel, one obtains

$$
\chi_j(\theta)=\frac{\sin((2j+1)\theta/2)}{\sin(\theta/2)}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5

Compute the quadratic Casimir $C_2(j)$ and Dynkin index $T(j)$ for $j=\frac12$ and $j=1$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The quadratic Casimir is

$$
C_2(j)=j(j+1).
$$

For $j=\frac12$,

$$
C_2\left(\frac12\right)=\frac12\frac32=\frac34.
$$

For $j=1$,

$$
C_2(1)=1\cdot2=2.
$$

The Dynkin index is

$$
T(j)=\frac13j(j+1)(2j+1).
$$

For $j=\frac12$,

$$
T\left(\frac12\right)
=\frac13\frac12\frac32(2)
=\frac12.
$$

For $j=1$,

$$
T(1)=\frac13(1)(2)(3)=2.
$$

Thus the fundamental doublet has

$$
C_2=\frac34,
\qquad
T=\frac12,
$$

and the adjoint triplet has

$$
C_2=2,
\qquad
T=2.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 6

Explain why the spin-$\frac12$ representation of $SU(2)$ does not descend to an ordinary representation of $SO(3)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The quotient map is

$$
SU(2)\to SO(3)\cong SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2,
$$

where the kernel is

$$
\{+\mathbf 1,-\mathbf 1\}.
$$

A representation of $SU(2)$ descends to $SO(3)$ only if every element of the kernel acts trivially. In the spin-$j$ representation,

$$
-\mathbf 1\mapsto (-1)^{2j}\mathbf 1.
$$

For $j=\frac12$,

$$
(-1)^{2j}=(-1)^1=-1.
$$

Therefore $-\mathbf 1$ acts as $-\mathbf 1$ on the doublet, not as the identity. The spin-$\frac12$ representation is an honest representation of $SU(2)$, but only a projective representation of $SO(3)$.

</details>
