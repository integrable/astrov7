## Summary

Grassmann algebra is the algebra of anticommuting generators. If $\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n$ are Grassmann generators, then

$$
\theta_i\theta_j=-\theta_j\theta_i,
\qquad
\theta_i^2=0.
$$

The second relation is not an extra assumption when the ground field has characteristic different from $2$: it follows from $\theta_i\theta_i=-\theta_i\theta_i$. A Grassmann variable is therefore nilpotent, not small. Powers terminate because repeated generators vanish.

This algebra is the finite-dimensional prototype behind fermionic path integrals. Bosonic Gaussian integration integrates ordinary variables and produces inverse determinant factors. Fermionic integration first replaces fields by Grassmann variables, and then Berezin integration extracts top-degree coefficients. That is why fermion loops, ghosts, Dirac determinants, Majorana Pfaffians, and many sign rules are all controlled by exterior algebra.

The moral is simple but merciless:

$$
\text{fermions are not obtained by putting minus signs into bosonic formulas;} 
\qquad
\text{the algebra itself is different.}
$$

## Core idea

A Grassmann algebra on $n$ generators is the exterior algebra

$$
\Lambda( V )
=\Lambda^0(V)\oplus\Lambda^1(V)\oplus\cdots\oplus\Lambda^n(V),
$$

where $V$ is the vector space spanned by $\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n$. A basis is given by ordered monomials

$$
\theta_{i_1}\theta_{i_2}\cdots\theta_{i_k},
\qquad
1\le i_1<i_2<\cdots<i_k\le n.
$$

Every element can be written uniquely as

$$
F(\theta)
=F_0+
\sum_i F_i\theta_i+
\sum_{i<j}F_{ij}\theta_i\theta_j+
\cdots+
F_{12\cdots n}\theta_1\theta_2\cdots\theta_n.
$$

There are only $2^n$ basis monomials. The algebra is finite because each generator can appear at most once.

The two pieces of structure that QFT uses constantly are:

1. **Grading.** Elements have degree modulo $2$: even elements have even degree, odd elements have odd degree.
2. **Antisymmetry.** Moving odd objects past odd objects produces a minus sign.

For homogeneous elements $A$ and $B$, with Grassmann parity $|A|,|B|\in\{0,1\}$, the sign rule is

$$
AB=(-1)^{|A||B|}BA.
$$

This formula is the small machine that powers a startling amount of fermionic QFT bookkeeping.

## Setup and conventions

We work over $\mathbb C$ unless otherwise stated. The finite Grassmann algebra generated by $\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n$ is the associative unital algebra defined by

$$
\theta_i\theta_j+\theta_j\theta_i=0.
$$

The unit is $1$. A scalar $a\in\mathbb C$ is even and commutes with everything. The generators $\theta_i$ are odd.

The ordered monomial associated with a subset $I=\{i_1<\cdots<i_k\}$ is

$$
\theta_I=\theta_{i_1}\cdots\theta_{i_k}.
$$

The empty subset gives $\theta_{\varnothing}=1$. Any element has a unique expansion

$$
F=\sum_{I\subseteq\{1,\ldots,n\}} F_I\theta_I.
$$

The coefficient $F_{\varnothing}$ is often called the **body** of $F$, and the remaining nilpotent part is sometimes called the **soul**. This language is optional, but the body criterion is useful: an even element $F$ is invertible exactly when its body is nonzero. If

$$
F=a+N,
\qquad a\ne0,
\qquad N^{r+1}=0,
$$

then

$$
F^{-1}=a^{-1}\sum_{k=0}^{r}(-a^{-1}N)^k.
$$

Odd elements are never invertible. They have zero body and are nilpotent.

## Exterior-algebra viewpoint

Grassmann algebra is not merely analogous to exterior algebra; in finite dimension it is exterior algebra. If $V$ has basis $e_i$, identify

$$
\theta_i\leftrightarrow e_i,
\qquad
\theta_i\theta_j\leftrightarrow e_i\wedge e_j.
$$

The antisymmetry

$$
e_i\wedge e_j=-e_j\wedge e_i
$$

is exactly the Grassmann sign rule.

This viewpoint gives useful intuition. A product of $k$ different generators is an oriented $k$-volume element. Reversing the order changes orientation. Repeating a direction gives zero because an oriented volume with two identical directions vanishes.

For example, with three generators,

$$
\theta_2\theta_1\theta_3=-\theta_1\theta_2\theta_3,
$$

while

$$
\theta_1\theta_3\theta_1=0.
$$

The top monomial $\theta_1\cdots\theta_n$ is a one-dimensional orientation line. Berezin integration will simply extract its coefficient.

## Parity and sign bookkeeping

The Grassmann algebra has a $\mathbb Z_2$ decomposition

$$
\Lambda=\Lambda_{\bar 0}\oplus\Lambda_{\bar 1},
$$

where

$$
\Lambda_{\bar 0}=\Lambda^0\oplus\Lambda^2\oplus\cdots,
\qquad
\Lambda_{\bar 1}=\Lambda^1\oplus\Lambda^3\oplus\cdots.
$$

An element of $\Lambda_{\bar 0}$ is even; an element of $\Lambda_{\bar 1}$ is odd. Parity is the grading that matters for signs in QFT.

If $A$ and $B$ are homogeneous, then

$$
AB=(-1)^{|A||B|}BA.
$$

Thus:

| $A$ | $B$ | Sign when exchanged |
|---|---|---|
| even | even | $+$ |
| even | odd | $+$ |
| odd | even | $+$ |
| odd | odd | $-$ |

This is why a fermionic source must anticommute with a fermionic field. If $\eta$ and $\psi$ are both odd, then

$$
\eta\psi=-\psi\eta.
$$

Forgetting whether a source is odd is one of the fastest ways to lose a sign in a fermionic generating functional.

## Examples with few generators

With one generator $\theta$, every element is

$$
F=a+b\theta.
$$

Since $\theta^2=0$, every function of $\theta$ is linear. The exponential terminates:

$$
e^{b\theta}=1+b\theta.
$$

With two generators $\theta_1,\theta_2$, every element is

$$
F=a+b_1\theta_1+b_2\theta_2+c\theta_1\theta_2.
$$

The monomial $\theta_2\theta_1$ is not new; it is

$$
\theta_2\theta_1=-\theta_1\theta_2.
$$

With three generators, the basis is

$$
1,
\quad
\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3,
\quad
\theta_1\theta_2,\theta_1\theta_3,\theta_2\theta_3,
\quad
\theta_1\theta_2\theta_3.
$$

The dimensions are

$$
1+3+3+1=2^3.
$$

The binomial coefficients appear because a degree-$k$ monomial is determined by choosing $k$ distinct generators.

## Linear changes of Grassmann variables

Let

$$
\eta_i=M_i{}^j\theta_j,
$$

where $M$ is an ordinary invertible $n\times n$ matrix with commuting entries. Then the top monomial transforms as

$$
\eta_1\eta_2\cdots\eta_n
=(\det M)\theta_1\theta_2\cdots\theta_n.
$$

This is the same determinant that appears when transforming an ordinary oriented volume form. It follows directly from antisymmetry: the only terms that survive are those with all indices different, and their signs are the signs of permutations.

This formula is the seed of the inverse Jacobian in Berezin integration. For ordinary variables, the measure transforms with $|\det M|$. For pure Grassmann variables, the integration measure transforms with $(\det M)^{-1}$. The top monomial already absorbed the determinant, so the measure must compensate.

That inverse Jacobian is not a cute technicality. It is why fermionic Gaussian integrals produce determinants upstairs rather than downstairs.

## Left and right derivatives

Differentiating Grassmann variables requires a convention because derivatives may be odd. We use left derivatives by default.

The left derivative is defined by

$$
\frac{\partial^L\theta_j}{\partial\theta_i}=\delta_{ij},
$$

and the graded Leibniz rule

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_i}(AB)
=
\left(\frac{\partial^L A}{\partial\theta_i}\right)B
+(-1)^{|A|}A
\left(\frac{\partial^L B}{\partial\theta_i}\right),
$$

for homogeneous $A$. The derivative is odd, so it picks up a sign when moved past an odd factor.

For an ordered monomial,

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_i}
(\theta_{j_1}\theta_{j_2}\cdots\theta_{j_k})
=
\sum_{r=1}^k(-1)^{r-1}\delta_{i j_r}
\theta_{j_1}\cdots\widehat{\theta_{j_r}}\cdots\theta_{j_k}.
$$

The hat means that the factor is omitted.

For example,

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_1}(\theta_1\theta_2)=\theta_2,
\qquad
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_2}(\theta_1\theta_2)=-\theta_1.
$$

Right derivatives are also common. They are defined so that

$$
(\theta_{j_1}\cdots\theta_{j_k})
\frac{\overleftarrow{\partial}^R}{\partial\theta_i}
=
\sum_{r=1}^k(-1)^{k-r}\delta_{i j_r}
\theta_{j_1}\cdots\widehat{\theta_{j_r}}\cdots\theta_{j_k}.
$$

For example,

$$
(\theta_1\theta_2)\frac{\overleftarrow{\partial}^R}{\partial\theta_1}=-\theta_2,
\qquad
(\theta_1\theta_2)\frac{\overleftarrow{\partial}^R}{\partial\theta_2}=\theta_1.
$$

The two derivative conventions are both legitimate. What is not legitimate is silently switching between them.

## Variations and functional derivatives

For left derivatives, a small variation is written

$$
\delta F=\delta\theta_i
\frac{\partial^L F}{\partial\theta_i},
$$

with summation over $i$. The variation $\delta\theta_i$ is odd. If one instead wants the variation on the right, right derivatives are natural:

$$
\delta F=
F\frac{\overleftarrow{\partial}^R}{\partial\theta_i}\delta\theta_i.
$$

Field theory uses the same idea with labels $x$ or momenta replacing finite indices:

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta_i}
\leadsto
\frac{\delta}{\delta\psi(x)}.
$$

The sign rule remains local in the algebra, but the labels may also carry spinor, flavor, color, and spacetime indices. A typical fermionic source term is written schematically as

$$
\int d^dx\,[\bar\eta(x)\psi(x)+\bar\psi(x)\eta(x)],
$$

where $\eta$ and $\bar\eta$ are Grassmann-odd sources. They anticommute with the fields.

## Quadratic forms

Grassmann quadratic forms differ from ordinary quadratic forms.

For one set of real Grassmann variables $\chi_a$, the expression

$$
\sum_{a,b}\chi_a M_{ab}\chi_b
$$

only depends on the antisymmetric part of $M$. Indeed,

$$
\chi_a M_{ab}\chi_b
=
\frac12\chi_a(M_{ab}-M_{ba})\chi_b.
$$

The symmetric part drops out because $\chi_a\chi_b=-\chi_b\chi_a$.

This is why a real fermion Gaussian is controlled by an antisymmetric matrix and gives a Pfaffian rather than an ordinary determinant. With complex Dirac variables, one usually treats $\psi_i$ and $\bar\psi_i$ as independent Grassmann variables and writes

$$
\bar\psi_i A_{ij}\psi_j.
$$

Here $A$ need not be antisymmetric, because $\bar\psi$ and $\psi$ are independent sets of generators. The corresponding Berezin Gaussian gives $\det A$.

This distinction is the finite-dimensional version of the difference between Dirac and Majorana fermion path integrals.

## Exponentials and nilpotency

If $N$ is nilpotent, then its exponential is a finite polynomial:

$$
e^N=\sum_{k=0}^r\frac{N^k}{k!}
\qquad
\text{if }N^{r+1}=0.
$$

This matters because fermionic actions are often quadratic or polynomial in Grassmann variables. The exponential $e^{-S_F}$ is not a convergent infinite series in the Grassmann variables. It is a finite polynomial whose top-degree coefficient is selected by Berezin integration.

For one complex pair,

$$
e^{-\bar\psi a\psi}
=1-\bar\psi a\psi
=1+a\psi\bar\psi.
$$

With the standard measure convention on the next page, this gives

$$
\int d\bar\psi\,d\psi\,e^{-\bar\psi a\psi}=a.
$$

That one-line calculation is the smallest fermionic determinant.

## Grassmann variables are not fermion operators

Grassmann variables are algebraic generators. Fermion creation and annihilation operators are operators on a Hilbert space. They both anticommute, but they are not the same kind of object.

A canonical fermion operator obeys

$$
\{a_i,a_j^\dagger\}=\delta_{ij},
\qquad
\{a_i,a_j\}=0.
$$

A Grassmann generator obeys

$$
\theta_i\theta_j+\theta_j\theta_i=0,
\qquad
\theta_i^2=0,
$$

but it does not act on a Hilbert space unless one builds a representation. In coherent-state derivations of fermionic path integrals, Grassmann variables appear as labels of fermionic coherent states, not as the original operators themselves.

The path integral trades operator algebra for Grassmann coefficient algebra. That trade is powerful, but the distinction prevents many conceptual headaches.

## Complex conjugation

For complex Grassmann variables, one often introduces independent generators $\psi_i$ and $\bar\psi_i$. In Euclidean functional integrals, $\bar\psi$ is usually treated as independent from $\psi$, not literally as the complex conjugate of $\psi$ along an ordinary integration contour.

When a conjugation operation is used algebraically, a common convention is that it reverses order:

$$
(AB)^*=B^*A^*.
$$

Thus, if $\psi_i^*=\bar\psi_i$, then

$$
(\psi_i\psi_j)^*=\bar\psi_j\bar\psi_i.
$$

Different authors choose different detailed conventions, especially in Lorentzian signature, Euclidean continuation, Majorana conditions, and supersymmetry. A page using a nonstandard conjugation rule should say so explicitly.

## Why QFT needs this algebra

Grassmann algebra enters QFT in several related places.

**Fermionic path integrals.** Dirac and Weyl fields are represented by Grassmann-valued fields in the path integral. Free fermionic integration produces the inverse Dirac operator as a propagator and the determinant of the Dirac operator as a normalization factor.

**Ghost fields.** Faddeev–Popov ghosts in gauge theory are Grassmann-odd fields. In many gauges they are Lorentz scalars but still anticommute. Their loops carry fermionic signs because their variables are Grassmann-odd, not because they have spin one-half.

**BRST and cohomology.** BRST transformations mix gauge symmetry with Grassmann-odd ghost variables. The algebraic structure is graded, and many identities are just careful applications of parity and nilpotency.

**Differential forms and supersymmetry.** Exterior algebra, de Rham forms, and Grassmann variables share the same sign rules. Superspace extends spacetime by anticommuting coordinates. The same algebraic habits reappear there.

**Determinants and Pfaffians.** Fermionic Gaussian integration turns quadratic forms into determinants or Pfaffians. These factors carry important information: anomalies, zero modes, phases, global signs, and sign problems.

## Common pitfalls

**Treating Grassmann variables as small ordinary numbers.** A Grassmann generator is nilpotent, not infinitesimal. One should not imagine taking $\theta\to0$ in an ordinary analytic sense.

**Forgetting that sources are odd.** Fermionic sources anticommute with fermionic fields. Moving $\eta$ past $\psi$ costs a minus sign.

**Using left and right derivatives interchangeably.** Both conventions are fine, but they give different-looking signs. State a convention, then stick to it.

**Writing a symmetric real fermion quadratic form.** For variables $\chi_a\chi_b$, only the antisymmetric part of the matrix contributes. Real fermion Gaussians naturally produce Pfaffians.

**Confusing Grassmann variables with fermion operators.** Grassmann variables do not satisfy $\{\theta_i,\theta_j^*\}=\delta_{ij}$. They are algebraic integration variables, not Hilbert-space ladder operators.

**Assuming $\bar\psi$ is always the literal complex conjugate of $\psi$.** In Euclidean path integrals, $\psi$ and $\bar\psi$ are typically independent generators. Reality conditions require separate care.

**Dropping zero modes.** Grassmann zero modes do not produce divergent Gaussian integrals. They produce vanishing integrals unless insertions saturate the missing top-degree variables. That difference is central in instanton calculations and anomaly physics.

## Research status

Finite-dimensional Grassmann algebra is completely settled. It is exterior algebra with a $\mathbb Z_2$ grading, and all sign rules follow from antisymmetry.

The research-level subtlety appears when the same algebra is attached to infinitely many field modes. Then the formal object

$$
\psi(x)=\sum_a \psi_a u_a(x)
$$

requires a choice of regulator, basis, function space, and limiting procedure. Determinants and Pfaffians may have phases or signs that are not visible in local perturbation theory. Zero modes can force correlation functions to include specific fermion insertions. Chiral rotations can produce anomalous Jacobians once the continuum limit is regulated.

So the algebra is elementary, but the continuum bookkeeping is not always harmless.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Basis count

For $n$ Grassmann generators, prove that the algebra has dimension $2^n$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Every nonzero monomial can contain each generator at most once, because $\theta_i^2=0$. After using anticommutation, every monomial can be written in ordered form

$$
\theta_{i_1}\cdots\theta_{i_k},
\qquad
1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le n.
$$

Such monomials are labeled by subsets $I\subseteq\{1,\ldots,n\}$. There are $2^n$ subsets, hence $2^n$ basis monomials.

Equivalently,

$$
\dim\Lambda( V )=\sum_{k=0}^n\binom{n}{k}=2^n.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Left derivative signs

Using the left derivative convention, compute

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_2}
(\theta_1\theta_2\theta_3).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The derivative must pass one odd generator, $\theta_1$, before hitting $\theta_2$. Therefore it produces a minus sign:

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_2}
(\theta_1\theta_2\theta_3)
=-\theta_1\theta_3.
$$

The general formula is

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_i}
(\theta_{j_1}\cdots\theta_{j_k})
=
\sum_{r=1}^k(-1)^{r-1}\delta_{i j_r}
\theta_{j_1}\cdots\widehat{\theta_{j_r}}\cdots\theta_{j_k}.
$$

Here $r=2$, so $(-1)^{r-1}=-1$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Top monomial under a linear map

Let

$$
\eta_1=a\theta_1+b\theta_2,
\qquad
\eta_2=c\theta_1+d\theta_2.
$$

Show that

$$
\eta_1\eta_2=(ad-bc)\theta_1\theta_2.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Expand:

$$
\eta_1\eta_2
=(a\theta_1+b\theta_2)(c\theta_1+d\theta_2).
$$

The terms with repeated generators vanish:

$$
a c\theta_1^2=0,
\qquad
bd\theta_2^2=0.
$$

The remaining terms are

$$
ad\theta_1\theta_2+bc\theta_2\theta_1
=ad\theta_1\theta_2-bc\theta_1\theta_2.
$$

Thus

$$
\eta_1\eta_2=(ad-bc)\theta_1\theta_2.
$$

The coefficient is $\det M$ for

$$
M=\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\ c&d\end{pmatrix}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Symmetric quadratic forms vanish

Let $S_{ij}=S_{ji}$ be an ordinary symmetric matrix. Show that

$$
\theta_i S_{ij}\theta_j=0.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Rename dummy indices and use symmetry:

$$
\theta_i S_{ij}\theta_j
=\theta_j S_{ji}\theta_i.
$$

Since $S_{ji}=S_{ij}$ and $\theta_j\theta_i=-\theta_i\theta_j$,

$$
\theta_j S_{ji}\theta_i
=-\theta_i S_{ij}\theta_j.
$$

Therefore the expression equals its own negative, so it is zero.

Equivalently, only the antisymmetric part contributes:

$$
\theta_i M_{ij}\theta_j
=\frac12\theta_i(M_{ij}-M_{ji})\theta_j.
$$

</details>
