## Summary

Monte Carlo methods estimate integrals by random sampling. In QFT the integrals are usually enormous: a lattice scalar field with $N$ sites already has an $N$-dimensional configuration space, and a gauge field has group-valued variables on many links. Deterministic quadrature has no chance. Monte Carlo survives because its basic error scales like

$$
\text{standard error}\sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{N_{\rm samples}}},
$$

rather than exponentially badly with the number of integration variables.

The standard Euclidean target is an expectation value

$$
\langle \mathcal O\rangle
=
\frac{1}{Z}\int \mathcal D\phi\,\mathcal O[\phi]e^{-S_E[\phi]},
$$

where

$$
Z=\int \mathcal D\phi\,e^{-S_E[\phi]}.
$$

If $e^{-S_E}$ is real, positive, and normalizable after regularization, it defines a probability measure. Monte Carlo then generates field configurations

$$
\phi_1,\phi_2,\ldots,\phi_N
$$

with that probability distribution and estimates

$$
\langle \mathcal O\rangle
\approx
\overline{\mathcal O}
=
\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\mathcal O[\phi_i].
$$

The catch is that real QFT simulations are rarely independent coin flips. Samples are usually generated by a Markov chain, and the uncertainty is controlled not just by the variance of $\mathcal O$ but by the autocorrelation time of the chain.

## Prerequisites

You should know basic probability, expectation values, variance, Gaussian integrals, and the Euclidean path-integral weight $e^{-S_E}$. Familiarity with lattice discretization and Markov chains helps, but the page introduces the essential definitions.

We use $N$ for the number of samples and reserve $V$ for volume when possible. A configuration is denoted $x$ in abstract probability formulas and $\phi$ in field-theory formulas.

## Why Monte Carlo matters in QFT

Perturbation theory expands around a Gaussian. Monte Carlo does something different: it tries to sample the full interacting measure. That makes it one of the central nonperturbative numerical tools in Euclidean QFT and statistical mechanics.

Typical uses include:

| Application | Monte Carlo role |
|---|---|
| lattice scalar field theory | sample field configurations and estimate correlators |
| lattice gauge theory | sample link variables with gauge action and fermion determinant |
| statistical spin systems | estimate magnetization, susceptibility, Binder cumulants, and critical exponents |
| disordered systems | average over thermal fluctuations and disorder realizations |
| matrix models | sample eigenvalues or matrices at finite $N$ |
| Euclidean quantum mechanics | sample paths and tunneling observables |
| finite-temperature QFT | estimate thermal expectation values after Wick rotation |

The method is conceptually simple but operationally unforgiving. Bad equilibration, long autocorrelations, wrong error bars, or hidden sign problems can make a beautiful simulation lie with a straight face.

## Plain Monte Carlo integration

Let $X$ be a random variable with probability density $p(x)$. For an observable $O(x)$,

$$
\mu=\mathbb E[O(X)]
=\int dx\,p(x)O(x).
$$

Given independent samples $X_1,\ldots,X_N$ drawn from $p$, the Monte Carlo estimator is

$$
\overline O_N=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N O(X_i).
$$

It is unbiased:

$$
\mathbb E[\overline O_N]=\mu.
$$

If the variance

$$
\sigma_O^2=\mathbb E[(O-\mu)^2]
$$

is finite, then

$$
\operatorname{Var}(\overline O_N)=\frac{\sigma_O^2}{N}.
$$

Thus the standard error is

$$
\Delta \overline O_N=\frac{\sigma_O}{\sqrt N}.
$$

The central limit theorem says that, under ordinary hypotheses,

$$
\sqrt N(\overline O_N-\mu)
\Rightarrow
\mathcal N(0,\sigma_O^2).
$$

The disappointing news is that $1/\sqrt N$ convergence is slow. The excellent news is that the exponent is independent of the dimension of the integration domain. The hidden villain is the variance: in a high-dimensional or badly matched problem, $\sigma_O^2$ can be enormous.

## Importance sampling

Suppose the desired integral is

$$
I=\int dx\,f(x).
$$

Choose a probability density $p(x)>0$ wherever $f(x)$ contributes. Then

$$
I=\int dx\,p(x)\frac{f(x)}{p(x)}
=\mathbb E_p\!\left[\frac{f(X)}{p(X)}\right].
$$

The Monte Carlo estimator is

$$
I_N=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\frac{f(X_i)}{p(X_i)}.
$$

The variance depends on the choice of $p$. Good importance sampling chooses $p$ large where $|f|$ is large.

For Euclidean QFT, the natural choice is

$$
p(\phi)=\frac1Z e^{-S_E[\phi]}.
$$

Then

$$
\langle \mathcal O\rangle
=\mathbb E_p[\mathcal O].
$$

This is why positive Euclidean actions are numerically precious. They turn a path integral into a probability problem.

## Reweighting

Sometimes samples are generated from a convenient measure $p_0$ but expectations are needed in a nearby measure $p_1$. If

$$
p_1(x)=\frac1{Z_1}e^{-S_1(x)},
\qquad
p_0(x)=\frac1{Z_0}e^{-S_0(x)},
$$

then

$$
\langle O\rangle_1
=
\frac{\langle O w\rangle_0}{\langle w\rangle_0},
$$

where

$$
w(x)=e^{-[S_1(x)-S_0(x)]}.
$$

This is reweighting. It is useful for small parameter changes, but it can fail catastrophically when the important configurations of $p_1$ are rare under $p_0$. That failure is called an overlap problem.

Reweighting is not magic. It moves the difficulty into the variance of the weights.

## Markov-chain Monte Carlo

In high-dimensional field spaces, direct independent sampling from $p(\phi)$ is usually impossible. Markov-chain Monte Carlo constructs a sequence

$$
\phi_0\to\phi_1\to\phi_2\to\cdots
$$

whose stationary distribution is $p(\phi)$.

A Markov transition kernel $T(\phi\to\phi')$ preserves $p$ if

$$
\int d\phi\,p(\phi)T(\phi\to\phi')=p(\phi').
$$

A sufficient condition is detailed balance:

$$
p(\phi)T(\phi\to\phi')
=
p(\phi')T(\phi'\to\phi).
$$

Detailed balance says that, at equilibrium, probability flow from $\phi$ to $\phi'$ is balanced by the reverse flow.

It is sufficient, not logically necessary. Some modern algorithms intentionally violate detailed balance while still preserving the target distribution. But detailed balance is the standard starting point.

## Metropolis–Hastings

The Metropolis–Hastings algorithm uses a proposal density $q(\phi\to\phi')$ and an acceptance probability $A(\phi\to\phi')$.

A proposed move is accepted with probability

$$
A(\phi\to\phi')
=
\min\!\left(1,
\frac{p(\phi')q(\phi'\to\phi)}{p(\phi)q(\phi\to\phi')}
\right).
$$

For a Euclidean action $p(\phi)\propto e^{-S_E[\phi]}$, this becomes

$$
A(\phi\to\phi')
=
\min\!\left(1,
 e^{-[S_E(\phi')-S_E(\phi)]}
\frac{q(\phi'\to\phi)}{q(\phi\to\phi')}
\right).
$$

If the proposal is symmetric,

$$
q(\phi\to\phi')=q(\phi'\to\phi),
$$

then

$$
A(\phi\to\phi')
=
\min\!\left(1,e^{-\Delta S_E}\right).
$$

Large action-increasing moves are rarely accepted; action-decreasing moves are always accepted. This does not mean the chain minimizes the action. It samples the equilibrium distribution, where entropy competes with action.

## Local updates and field configurations

For a lattice scalar theory with action

$$
S_E[\phi]
=\sum_x\left[
\frac12\sum_\mu (\phi_{x+\hat\mu}-\phi_x)^2
+V(\phi_x)
\right],
$$

a simple local proposal changes one site:

$$
\phi_x\to\phi_x+\delta,
$$

where $\delta$ is drawn from a symmetric distribution. The change in action $\Delta S_E$ depends only on the site $x$ and its neighbors, so the update is cheap.

Local updates are simple and robust. They can also be painfully slow near critical points, because long-wavelength modes decorrelate only by many small local changes.

This is critical slowing down.

## Autocorrelation

Markov-chain samples are correlated. For an observable $O_i=O(\phi_i)$, define the autocovariance

$$
C(t)=\langle (O_i-\mu)(O_{i+t}-\mu)\rangle.
$$

The normalized autocorrelation is

$$
\rho(t)=\frac{C(t)}{C(0)}.
$$

The integrated autocorrelation time is

$$
\tau_{\rm int}
=\frac12+\sum_{t=1}^{\infty}\rho(t).
$$

For a long Markov chain, the variance of the sample mean is approximately

$$
\operatorname{Var}(\overline O)
\approx
\frac{2\tau_{\rm int}\sigma_O^2}{N}.
$$

Thus the effective number of independent samples is

$$
N_{\rm eff}\approx \frac{N}{2\tau_{\rm int}}.
$$

This is the formula that saves you from fake precision. A run with one million highly correlated configurations may contain far fewer independent measurements than its raw length suggests.

## Equilibration and burn-in

A Markov chain needs time to forget its initial condition. Early samples are often discarded as burn-in or thermalization.

There is no universal burn-in number. You should check observables as functions of Monte Carlo time, compare hot and cold starts, monitor slow modes, and verify stability under discarding more initial data.

A chain can look equilibrated in simple observables while remaining stuck in a topological sector or rare region. This is one reason QFT Monte Carlo is both powerful and treacherous.

## Error bars

For independent samples, one estimates

$$
\widehat\sigma_O^2
=\frac1{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^N(O_i-\overline O)^2,
$$

and

$$
\Delta\overline O
=\frac{\widehat\sigma_O}{\sqrt N}.
$$

For correlated samples, one must account for autocorrelation. Common practical methods include:

| Method | Idea |
|---|---|
| binning | average neighboring samples into bins longer than the autocorrelation time |
| jackknife | recompute estimator leaving out one bin at a time |
| bootstrap | resample bins to estimate uncertainty |
| autocorrelation fit | estimate $\tau_{\rm int}$ directly from the time series |

For nonlinear observables, such as masses extracted from logarithms of correlators, jackknife and bootstrap methods are often safer than naive propagation of errors.

## Correlation functions

Suppose a lattice simulation samples fields $\phi$. A two-point function is estimated by

$$
C(x-y)=\langle \phi_x\phi_y\rangle
\approx
\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N \phi_x^{(i)}\phi_y^{(i)}.
$$

If translation invariance is present, one improves statistics by averaging over source positions:

$$
C(r)=\frac1{N}\sum_{i=1}^N\frac1{V}\sum_x
\phi_x^{(i)}\phi_{x+r}^{(i)}.
$$

At large Euclidean time separation, a massive theory often has

$$
C(t)\sim A e^{-mt}+\cdots,
$$

so the effective mass can be estimated by

$$
m_{\rm eff}(t)=\log\frac{C(t)}{C(t+1)}
$$

in lattice units, for a simple one-exponential decay. In finite volume with periodic time, cosh variants are usually more appropriate.

Extracting masses, gaps, and matrix elements is not just measuring correlators; it is fitting noisy correlated data with excited-state contamination.

## Acceptance rate and step size

For a random-walk Metropolis proposal, the proposal width controls the acceptance rate.

If the proposal steps are too small, most moves are accepted but the chain diffuses slowly. If the proposal steps are too large, most moves are rejected and the chain again moves slowly. A good acceptance rate depends on the algorithm and dimension, but the real diagnostic is not acceptance by itself. It is autocorrelation time for the observables of interest.

Never optimize an algorithm only by making the acceptance rate look respectable. A respectable acceptance rate can still sample the wrong parts of configuration space slowly.

## Critical slowing down

Near a second-order phase transition, the correlation length $\xi$ grows. Local algorithms often have autocorrelation time scaling like

$$
\tau\sim \xi^z,
$$

where $z$ is a dynamic critical exponent depending on the algorithm.

This is critical slowing down. It means that near the continuum limit, where $\xi/a$ becomes large, simulations can become increasingly expensive.

Cluster algorithms, multigrid ideas, Fourier acceleration, overrelaxation, and hybrid Monte Carlo are partly designed to reduce this slowing down. They do not remove every difficulty, but they change the scaling enough to matter.

## Hybrid Monte Carlo preview

Hybrid Monte Carlo, or HMC, is widely used in lattice field theory. It augments field variables $\phi$ by fictitious momenta $\pi$ and samples the joint density

$$
p(\phi,\pi)\propto
\exp\!\left[-S_E(\phi)-\frac12\pi^T\pi\right].
$$

One proposes long-distance moves by approximately integrating Hamiltonian dynamics in fictitious Monte Carlo time, then accepts or rejects the result with a Metropolis step.

The advantage is that HMC can move through configuration space coherently rather than by tiny local random steps. The accept/reject step corrects finite integration-step errors, provided the integrator is reversible and volume-preserving.

HMC is not merely a trick for scalar fields. With pseudofermions, it becomes a central algorithmic tool for dynamical fermions in lattice gauge theory.

## The sign problem

Monte Carlo importance sampling needs a positive probability measure. If the weight is complex or sign-changing,

$$
w(x)=|w(x)|e^{i\theta(x)},
$$

one may sample from $|w|$ and reweight:

$$
\langle O\rangle
=
\frac{\langle O e^{i\theta}\rangle_{|w|}}
{\langle e^{i\theta}\rangle_{|w|}}.
$$

The denominator is the average phase. In many important systems it decays exponentially with volume:

$$
\langle e^{i\theta}\rangle_{|w|}
\sim e^{-V\Delta f}.
$$

Then the signal-to-noise ratio becomes exponentially bad. This is the sign problem.

Examples include finite-density QCD, real-time path integrals, frustrated quantum magnets, and many fermionic systems away from special symmetry points.

This is not a mere implementation annoyance. It is a structural obstruction to straightforward importance sampling.

## Ergodicity and topological freezing

A Markov chain is useful only if it can explore the relevant configuration space. Ergodicity means that the chain can reach all important regions, at least in principle.

In lattice gauge theory and sigma models, configuration space may decompose into sectors labeled approximately by topology at fine lattice spacing. Local updates may have difficulty moving between sectors. The chain then samples one topological sector for a very long time.

This is topological freezing. It can produce underestimated errors and biased observables even when local correlators look calm.

Diagnostics include monitoring topological charge histories, running from different initial sectors, using open boundary conditions in Euclidean time, or designing algorithms that improve tunneling.

## Finite volume and continuum limits

Monte Carlo error is only one part of the uncertainty budget. A lattice calculation usually has at least three limits:

$$
N_{\rm samples}\to\infty,
\qquad
V\to\infty,
\qquad
a\to0.
$$

Statistical errors decrease with more samples. Finite-volume errors decrease as the spatial volume grows relative to physical correlation lengths. Discretization errors decrease as the lattice spacing $a$ is sent to zero with renormalized physical quantities held fixed.

A result with tiny statistical error but no continuum extrapolation is not a continuum QFT result. A result with a continuum extrapolation but hidden autocorrelation can be just as dangerous. Numerical QFT is a three-legged stool: statistics, volume, and cutoff.

## Monte Carlo versus perturbation theory

Monte Carlo estimates expectation values directly from the full regulated measure. Perturbation theory expands around a solvable Gaussian. They answer overlapping but distinct questions.

Perturbation theory is excellent for analytic dependence on small couplings, UV counterterms, and diagrammatic structure. Monte Carlo is excellent for nonperturbative regimes with positive Euclidean weights. Neither dominates the other; the strongest QFT calculations often use both.

For example, perturbation theory may compute short-distance matching coefficients, while Monte Carlo supplies long-distance matrix elements.

## Common pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: using the independent-sample error formula for a Markov chain

For correlated samples, the error is enlarged by approximately $2\tau_{\rm int}$. Ignoring autocorrelation produces fake precision.

### Pitfall 2: confusing equilibration with a stable plot

A time series that looks stable may still be trapped in a sector or missing rare configurations. Stability is evidence, not proof.

### Pitfall 3: treating acceptance rate as the only algorithm metric

The goal is low error per unit computational cost. Acceptance rate is only one piece. Autocorrelation time matters more.

### Pitfall 4: reweighting across nonoverlapping ensembles

If the target ensemble rarely appears in the sampled ensemble, reweighting estimates become dominated by a few rare configurations. Error bars may become meaningless.

### Pitfall 5: ignoring the sign problem

A complex phase cannot be wished into a probability measure. Reweighting by the phase often has exponentially bad signal-to-noise.

### Pitfall 6: forgetting finite-size and cutoff effects

Monte Carlo samples a regulated finite system. The continuum infinite-volume QFT requires controlled extrapolation.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Variance of the sample mean

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_N$ be independent samples of a random variable $X$ with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Show that

$$
\operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N X_i\right)=\frac{\sigma^2}{N}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Let

$$
\overline X=\frac1N\sum_i X_i.
$$

Using independence,

$$
\operatorname{Var}(\overline X)
=\frac1{N^2}\sum_{i,j}\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j).
$$

For independent samples,

$$
\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j)=0
\quad\text{if }i\ne j,
$$

and

$$
\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_i)=\sigma^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\operatorname{Var}(\overline X)
=\frac1{N^2}N\sigma^2
=\frac{\sigma^2}{N}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Detailed balance for symmetric Metropolis

Let $p(x)\propto e^{-S(x)}$ and let $q(x\to y)=q(y\to x)$. Show that the Metropolis acceptance rule

$$
A(x\to y)=\min(1,e^{-[S(y)-S(x)]})
$$

satisfies detailed balance.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The transition probability for accepted moves is

$$
T(x\to y)=q(x\to y)A(x\to y)
$$

for $x\ne y$. Because $q$ is symmetric, it is enough to show

$$
p(x)A(x\to y)=p(y)A(y\to x).
$$

If $S(y)>S(x)$, then

$$
A(x\to y)=e^{-[S(y)-S(x)]},
\qquad
A(y\to x)=1.
$$

Thus

$$
p(x)A(x\to y)
\propto e^{-S(x)}e^{-S(y)+S(x)}=e^{-S(y)}
\propto p(y)A(y\to x).
$$

If $S(y)<S(x)$, the same argument holds with $x$ and $y$ exchanged. Therefore detailed balance is satisfied.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Effective sample size

Suppose an observable measured along a Markov chain has integrated autocorrelation time $\tau_{\rm int}=8$. Roughly how many independent samples are equivalent to a chain of length $N=16000$?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use

$$
N_{\rm eff}\approx \frac{N}{2\tau_{\rm int}}.
$$

With $N=16000$ and $\tau_{\rm int}=8$,

$$
N_{\rm eff}\approx \frac{16000}{16}=1000.
$$

So the chain has the statistical power of roughly $1000$ independent samples for that observable.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Reweighting identity

Let

$$
\langle O\rangle_1
=\frac{\int dx\,O(x)e^{-S_1(x)}}{\int dx\,e^{-S_1(x)}}.
$$

Define an ensemble with weight $e^{-S_0(x)}$ and $w(x)=e^{-[S_1(x)-S_0(x)]}$. Show that

$$
\langle O\rangle_1
=\frac{\langle Ow\rangle_0}{\langle w\rangle_0}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Let

$$
Z_0=\int dx\,e^{-S_0(x)}.
$$

Then

$$
\langle Ow\rangle_0
=\frac1{Z_0}\int dx\,O(x)e^{-[S_1(x)-S_0(x)]}e^{-S_0(x)}
=\frac1{Z_0}\int dx\,O(x)e^{-S_1(x)}.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\langle w\rangle_0
=\frac1{Z_0}\int dx\,e^{-S_1(x)}.
$$

Taking the ratio cancels $Z_0$ and gives

$$
\frac{\langle Ow\rangle_0}{\langle w\rangle_0}
=
\frac{\int dx\,O(x)e^{-S_1(x)}}{\int dx\,e^{-S_1(x)}}
=\langle O\rangle_1.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5: Average phase and the sign problem

Suppose the average phase in a complex-weight system behaves as

$$
\langle e^{i\theta}\rangle_{|w|}=e^{-V\Delta f}.
$$

Explain why the number of samples required for fixed relative accuracy grows exponentially with $V$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The phase $e^{i\theta}$ has magnitude $1$, so its variance is generically order one unless special cancellations occur. The standard error of its sample mean is therefore of order

$$
\frac1{\sqrt N}.
$$

The signal is

$$
e^{-V\Delta f}.
$$

For fixed relative error, one needs

$$
\frac{1/\sqrt N}{e^{-V\Delta f}}
$$

to stay fixed. Thus

$$
\sqrt N\sim e^{V\Delta f},
$$

or

$$
N\sim e^{2V\Delta f}.
$$

The required sample size grows exponentially with volume. That is the practical core of the sign problem.

</details>
