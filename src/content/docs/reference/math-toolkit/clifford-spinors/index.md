Clifford Algebras and Spinors is the chapter in the Mathematical Toolkit volume where the metric becomes algebra. Vectors multiply, their anticommutator remembers the quadratic form, and spinors appear as the spaces on which this multiplication acts.

The chapter exists because fermions are not optional decorative machinery. Relativistic QFT needs spinorial representations for electrons, quarks, gauginos, neutrinos, supersymmetry generators, spin fields, Dirac operators, spin sums, anomalies, and modern amplitude variables.

The central slogan is

$$
\text{Clifford algebra} + \text{representation theory}
\longrightarrow
\text{spinors and gamma matrices}.
$$

## Prerequisites

You should know [Mathematical Conventions](/math-toolkit/conventions/), especially the mostly-minus metric and the local Clifford convention

$$
\{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\}=2\eta^{\mu\nu}\mathbf 1.
$$

You should also know [SU(2) and Angular Momentum](/math-toolkit/groups-representations/su2-and-angular-momentum/) and [SL(2,C) and the Lorentz Group](/math-toolkit/groups-representations/sl2c-and-lorentz-group/). The first explains spin-$j$ representations; the second explains why Lorentz spinors naturally come in left-handed and right-handed Weyl representations.

A useful checkpoint after the first page is to derive the Clifford-style identity

$$
(\mathbf n\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma)^2=|\mathbf n|^2\mathbf 1.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use

$$
\sigma_i\sigma_j=\delta_{ij}\mathbf 1+i\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k.
$$

Then

$$
(\mathbf n\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma)^2
=n_in_j\sigma_i\sigma_j
=n_in_j\delta_{ij}\mathbf 1+i n_in_j\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k.
$$

The second term vanishes because $n_in_j$ is symmetric in $i,j$, while $\epsilon_{ijk}$ is antisymmetric. Therefore

$$
(\mathbf n\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma)^2=|\mathbf n|^2\mathbf 1.
$$

This is the Pauli-matrix prototype of the Clifford relation.

</details>

A useful checkpoint after the spin-group page is to explain why a $2\pi$ rotation acts as $-\mathbf 1$ on a spinor but as $+\mathbf 1$ on an ordinary vector.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In the spin-one-half representation,

$$
U(\mathbf n,\theta)=\exp\left(-\frac{i\theta}{2}\mathbf n\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma\right)
=
\cos\frac\theta2\,\mathbf 1-i\sin\frac\theta2\,\mathbf n\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma.
$$

At $\theta=2\pi$ this gives

$$
U(\mathbf n,2\pi)=-\mathbf 1.
$$

The induced action on vectors is by conjugation,

$$
U(\mathbf a\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma)U^\dagger=(R\mathbf a)\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma.
$$

Both $U$ and $-U$ give the same $R\in SO(3)$. This is the two-to-one cover $SU(2)\to SO(3)$.

</details>


## Landmarks

The first landmark is the **Clifford relation**. Given a vector space $V$ with quadratic form $g$, the Clifford algebra is generated by vectors subject to

$$
uv+vu=2g(u,v)\mathbf 1.
$$

For an orthonormal basis $e_a$ this becomes

$$
e_ae_b+e_be_a=2g_{ab}\mathbf 1.
$$

Gamma matrices are a representation of this algebra:

$$
\gamma^a\gamma^b+\gamma^b\gamma^a=2g^{ab}\mathbf 1.
$$

The second landmark is the **spin group**. Orthogonal transformations act on vectors. Spin groups are double covers of the connected orthogonal groups and act naturally on spinors. In Lorentzian $3+1$ dimensions,

$$
Spin^+(1,3)\simeq SL(2,\mathbb C),
$$

which double-covers the proper orthochronous Lorentz group.

The third landmark is a **spinor module**. A spinor is not a vector with hidden components. It is an element of a representation space for the Clifford algebra or spin group. Matrix columns become spinors only after the transformation law is specified.

The fourth landmark is the **Pauli algebra**:

$$
\sigma_i\sigma_j=\delta_{ij}\mathbf 1+i\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k.
$$

This one formula contains the spin-$1/2$ commutator algebra, the anticommutator Clifford relation in Euclidean three-space, projectors onto spin along an axis, and the trace identities behind many spin sums.

The fifth landmark is the **Weyl sigma-matrix pair**. In mostly-minus conventions,

$$
\sigma^\mu=(\mathbf 1,\sigma^i),
\qquad
\bar\sigma^\mu=(\mathbf 1,-\sigma^i),
$$

with

$$
\sigma^\mu\bar\sigma^\nu+\sigma^\nu\bar\sigma^\mu=2\eta^{\mu\nu}\mathbf 1.
$$

These matrices connect dotted and undotted Weyl spinor indices and encode the $SL(2,\mathbb C)$ construction of Lorentz vectors.

The sixth landmark is **chirality**. In four-dimensional Lorentzian signature, Dirac spinors split into left-handed and right-handed Weyl spinors. Chirality is the eigenvalue of $\gamma^5$; helicity is the projection of spin along momentum. They coincide for massless on-shell particles, but not as abstract concepts.

The seventh landmark is **spinor bilinears**. Expressions such as

$$
\bar\psi\psi,
\qquad
\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\psi,
\qquad
\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\gamma^5\psi
$$

are not arbitrary decorations. They are Lorentz-covariant ways to build scalars, currents, axial currents, and tensor operators from spinors.

The eighth landmark is **Fierz rearrangement**. Gamma matrices form a complete basis for matrices on spinor space. Fierz identities are just this completeness used carefully, with Grassmann signs included. They are the algebraic reason four-fermion operators can be written in multiple equivalent-looking bases.

## Common confusions

**A Clifford algebra is not a list of gamma matrices.** Gamma matrices are a representation of the algebra. The abstract algebra is basis-independent; a matrix choice is a convention-dependent realization.

**A spinor is not a strange vector.** Vectors and spinors are different representations of the spin group. A $2\pi$ rotation acts trivially on ordinary vectors but as $-1$ on spin-$1/2$ spinors.

**The Pauli matrices are doing two jobs.** They are generators of $SU(2)$ after a factor of $1/2$, and they are also a Clifford-algebra basis for Euclidean three-space through their anticommutator. Forgetting which job is being used leads to factors of $2$.

**Chirality and helicity are not synonyms.** Chirality is a representation-theoretic label in four dimensions. Helicity is an eigenvalue of spin projected along momentum for a particle state. Only for massless on-shell states do they line up cleanly.

**Majorana conditions are signature- and dimension-dependent.** You cannot impose a Majorana reality condition merely because it would be convenient. Whether such a condition exists depends on the spacetime signature, dimension, and representation convention.

**Euclidean spinors are not obtained by replacing $t$ with $-i\tau$ everywhere.** Wick rotation changes the Clifford algebra, reality conditions, adjoints, and sometimes the meaning of chirality. Scalar formulas are a poor guide to spinorial continuation.

## Boundaries

This chapter gives the spinor and Clifford-algebra technology needed for QFT calculations. It is not a full course on spin geometry, real Clifford-algebra classification, index theory, or the representation theory of all $Spin(p,q)$ groups.

The physics of quantized fermion fields belongs to Foundations of QFT. This chapter explains the algebra of spinors, gamma matrices, bilinears, and identities; it does not replace the canonical or path-integral construction of fermionic fields.

The geometry of spin structures, spin bundles, Dirac operators on curved manifolds, and index theorems belongs mainly to the Geometry of Fields and Topology chapters. This chapter supplies the local algebraic language those pages use.

Supersymmetry uses spinors as supercharges and Grassmann coordinates, but superspace and supermultiplets are not developed here. Spinor-helicity variables are introduced only as a preview; the full amplitude technology belongs in scattering and amplitudes pages.

## Where to go next

Begin with [Pauli Matrices](/math-toolkit/clifford-spinors/pauli-matrices/). It fixes the concrete $2\times2$ identities that make the later Clifford and Weyl-spinor pages painless.

If you came here from representation theory, keep [SL(2,C) and the Lorentz Group](/math-toolkit/groups-representations/sl2c-and-lorentz-group/) nearby. It explains how Weyl spinors arise from Lorentz symmetry before gamma matrices enter.

If your goal is fermion QFT, the most important outputs of this chapter are gamma-matrix conventions, the Dirac adjoint, chiral projectors, spin sums, and bilinear transformation rules. Those are the algebraic ingredients behind the Dirac equation, fermion propagators, and Yukawa or gauge couplings.

If your goal is anomalies, topology, or curved backgrounds, read this chapter as the local algebraic layer. Spin structures, Dirac operators, characteristic classes, and index theorems will add global geometry later.
