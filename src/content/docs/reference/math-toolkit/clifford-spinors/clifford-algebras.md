## Summary

A Clifford algebra is the associative algebra generated by vectors whose symmetric product is fixed by a metric. If $V$ is a vector space with symmetric bilinear form $g$, then the Clifford algebra is generated by $v\in V$ subject to

$$
uv+vu=2g(u,v)\mathbf 1.
$$

Equivalently, each vector squares to its length:

$$
v^2=g(v,v)\mathbf 1.
$$

This one relation is the algebraic source of gamma matrices, Dirac operators, spin groups, and spinors. It says that vectors do not commute, but their failure to anticommute is exactly controlled by the quadratic form.

For QFT, the most important representation is the gamma-matrix relation

$$
\{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\}=2\eta^{\mu\nu}\mathbf 1.
$$

It immediately implies

$$
(p\!\!\!/)^2=p^2\mathbf 1,
$$

which is why the Dirac equation is a square root of the Klein–Gordon equation. That slogan is a little too cute for serious work, but it points in the right direction: the Clifford algebra is how the metric enters fermion kinematics.

## Core idea

The Clifford algebra is a controlled compromise between the tensor algebra and the exterior algebra.

The tensor algebra $T(V)$ lets vectors multiply freely:

$$
T(V)=\mathbb K\oplus V\oplus (V\otimes V)\oplus (V\otimes V\otimes V)\oplus\cdots.
$$

No relation is imposed. The exterior algebra $\Lambda^\bullet V$ imposes total antisymmetry, so every vector squares to zero:

$$
v\wedge v=0.
$$

The Clifford algebra instead imposes

$$
v^2=g(v,v)\mathbf 1.
$$

So null vectors still square to zero, but non-null vectors do not. The metric has been promoted from a number-valued pairing to an algebraic multiplication rule.

This is why Clifford algebras appear whenever a first-order operator should square to a second-order metric operator. If

$$
D\!\!\!/ = \gamma^\mu D_\mu,
$$

then the leading second-order part of $(D\!\!\!/)^2$ is controlled by

$$
\gamma^\mu\gamma^\nu D_\mu D_\nu.
$$

The symmetric part of the gamma product gives the metric contraction. The antisymmetric part carries curvature, spin, and field-strength information. That is the local algebraic seed of the Dirac operator, spin connection, Pauli term, and index-theorem technology.

## Setup and conventions

Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$. Unless stated otherwise, $g$ is a nondegenerate symmetric bilinear form. Over $\mathbb R$, the signature matters. We write

$$
\operatorname{Cl}_{p,q}(\mathbb R)
$$

for the real Clifford algebra with $p$ orthonormal generators squaring to $+1$ and $q$ orthonormal generators squaring to $-1$:

$$
e_i^2=+1,
\qquad
f_j^2=-1.
$$

With the mostly-minus spacetime metric

$$
\eta=\operatorname{diag}(+1,-1,-1,-1),
$$

four-dimensional Minkowski space has real signature $(1,3)$ in this convention. Some mathematics and physics sources reverse the order in the notation $\operatorname{Cl}_{p,q}$. Whenever the real signature is important, check whether the author means $p$ plus signs or $p$ minus signs. This is one of those tiny convention gremlins that has eaten entire afternoons.

Over $\mathbb C$, all nondegenerate quadratic forms of the same dimension are equivalent. Thus the complex Clifford algebra depends only on

$$
n=\dim_\mathbb C V,
$$

not on a signature. This is why complex gamma matrices in four-dimensional Lorentzian QFT are much less sensitive to the difference between mostly-minus and mostly-plus signatures than real Clifford modules are.

We use $\mathbf 1$ for the algebra unit. When $e_a$ is an orthonormal basis, the defining relation is

$$
e_ae_b+e_be_a=2g_{ab}\mathbf 1.
$$

For Lorentz gamma matrices, a representation sends

$$
e^\mu\mapsto \gamma^\mu,
$$

with

$$
\gamma^\mu\gamma^\nu+\gamma^\nu\gamma^\mu=2\eta^{\mu\nu}\mathbf 1.
$$

## The quotient construction

The clean definition starts from the tensor algebra. Let $Q(v)=g(v,v)$. The Clifford algebra is

$$
\operatorname{Cl}(V,g)=T(V)\big/\langle v\otimes v-Q(v)\mathbf 1: v\in V\rangle.
$$

Inside the quotient, we usually suppress the tensor-product symbol and write products by juxtaposition. Thus

$$
v\otimes w\quad\leadsto\quad vw.
$$

The quotient relation gives

$$
v^2=Q(v)\mathbf 1.
$$

The anticommutator form follows by polarizing. Since

$$
(u+v)^2=u^2+uv+vu+v^2,
$$

and also

$$
(u+v)^2=Q(u+v)\mathbf 1,
$$

we get

$$
uv+vu=\bigl(Q(u+v)-Q(u)-Q(v)\bigr)\mathbf 1.
$$

For a symmetric bilinear form in characteristic not equal to two,

$$
Q(u+v)-Q(u)-Q(v)=2g(u,v),
$$

and therefore

$$
uv+vu=2g(u,v)\mathbf 1.
$$

Conversely, setting $u=v$ in the anticommutator gives $v^2=g(v,v)\mathbf 1$. The two definitions are equivalent in the setting used in QFT.

## Universal property

The quotient definition is concrete, but the universal property explains why Clifford algebras are unavoidable.

Suppose $A$ is an associative algebra with unit and $\phi:V\to A$ is a linear map satisfying

$$
\phi(v)^2=Q(v)\mathbf 1_A
$$

for every $v\in V$. Then there is a unique algebra homomorphism

$$
\Phi:\operatorname{Cl}(V,g)\to A
$$

such that

$$
\Phi(v)=\phi(v)
$$

for every $v\in V$.

In plain language: whenever some algebra contains objects that square to the quadratic form, the Clifford algebra is the unique algebra through which that construction factors.

Gamma matrices are exactly this. Choosing matrices $\gamma(v)$ such that

$$
\gamma(v)^2=g(v,v)\mathbf 1
$$

is the same as choosing a representation

$$
\gamma:\operatorname{Cl}(V,g)\to\operatorname{End}(S),
$$

where $S$ is the spinor module. Matrix identities involving gamma matrices are therefore not random matrix miracles; they are representation-level shadows of the Clifford relation.

## Basis and dimension

Let $\dim V=n$ and choose an orthogonal basis $e_1,\ldots,e_n$ with

$$
e_a^2=\pm\mathbf 1,
\qquad
 e_ae_b=-e_be_a\quad(a\ne b).
$$

Every element of $\operatorname{Cl}(V,g)$ can be written as a linear combination of ordered monomials

$$
e_{a_1}e_{a_2}\cdots e_{a_k},
\qquad
1\le a_1<a_2<\cdots<a_k\le n,
$$

including the empty product $\mathbf 1$. There are

$$
\binom n0+\binom n1+\cdots+\binom nn=2^n
$$

such monomials. Hence

$$
\dim \operatorname{Cl}(V,g)=2^n.
$$

The proof is the same sorting game one plays with fermionic creation operators: use anticommutation to put indices in increasing order, then use $e_a^2=\pm1$ to remove repeated indices. The nontrivial part is linear independence; one way to prove it is to represent the Clifford algebra on the exterior algebra $\Lambda^\bullet V$ by a combination of wedge and contraction operators.

The ordered-monomial basis is useful, but it is not invariantly meaningful by itself. It depends on a choice of orthogonal basis. The invariant statement is that the Clifford algebra has a natural filtration whose associated graded algebra is the exterior algebra.

## Exterior-algebra shadow

The Clifford algebra is not the exterior algebra, but it has the same vector-space size. A useful linear identification is obtained by antisymmetrizing products:

$$
u_1\wedge\cdots\wedge u_k
\longmapsto
\frac{1}{k!}\sum_{\pi\in S_k}\operatorname{sgn}(\pi)
 u_{\pi(1)}u_{\pi(2)}\cdots u_{\pi(k)}.
$$

Under this identification, the Clifford product of two vectors splits into an exterior part and a metric part:

$$
uv=u\wedge v+g(u,v)\mathbf 1.
$$

This formula is one of the best ways to remember what the algebra is doing. The wedge part remembers oriented area; the metric part contracts the two vectors.

For a vector $v$ and a $k$-vector $\omega$, Clifford multiplication decomposes schematically as

$$
v\omega=v\wedge\omega+\iota_{v^\sharp}\omega,
$$

up to the standard convention for raising an index with $g$. Here $\iota$ denotes contraction. Thus Clifford multiplication combines creation and annihilation operations on the exterior algebra. This is the algebraic reason spinors often feel like “square roots” of differential forms in Euclidean geometry, though one should not identify spinors with forms without specifying a representation and additional structure.

The exterior-algebra shadow is also a good sanity check for dimensions. The Clifford algebra has scalar, vector, bivector, trivector, and higher-grade components just as $\Lambda^\bullet V$ does as a vector space. But the Clifford product mixes grades because contractions can lower degree.

## Even and odd parts

The Clifford algebra has a canonical $\mathbb Z_2$ grading:

$$
\operatorname{Cl}(V,g)=\operatorname{Cl}^0(V,g)\oplus\operatorname{Cl}^1(V,g),
$$

where $\operatorname{Cl}^0$ is spanned by products of an even number of vectors and $\operatorname{Cl}^1$ is spanned by products of an odd number of vectors. The parity is well-defined even though the full integer grade is not preserved by Clifford multiplication.

The even part is a subalgebra:

$$
\operatorname{Cl}^0(V,g)\operatorname{Cl}^0(V,g)\subset\operatorname{Cl}^0(V,g).
$$

The odd part is not a subalgebra, since odd times odd is even.

The even subalgebra is where rotations live. Products of two unit vectors implement rotations or boosts, while single unit vectors implement reflections. This is the algebraic origin of the distinction between Spin and Pin groups. The next page, [Spin Groups](/math-toolkit/clifford-spinors/spin-groups/), develops this construction.

The parity automorphism, also called the grade involution, is the algebra automorphism $\alpha$ defined on vectors by

$$
\alpha(v)=-v.
$$

Thus $\alpha$ acts by $+1$ on even elements and by $-1$ on odd elements. This map is essential in the clean Clifford formula for reflections and Pin actions.

## Reversion and Clifford conjugation

There are two other standard anti-involutions that show up in spin groups, adjoints, and bilinears.

The **reversion** operation reverses the order of vector products:

$$
(v_1v_2\cdots v_k)^\sim=v_k\cdots v_2v_1.
$$

It is an anti-automorphism:

$$
(ab)^\sim=b^\sim a^\sim.
$$

The **Clifford conjugation** combines reversion with the grade involution:

$$
\bar a=\alpha(a^\sim).
$$

Different communities use different symbols for these operations. The important point is not the typography; it is that Clifford algebras contain natural order-reversing operations. They let one define norms for spin-group elements and adjoint-like operations in representations.

For example, if $v$ is a non-null vector, then

$$
v^{-1}=\frac{v}{g(v,v)}.
$$

This follows immediately from $v^2=g(v,v)\mathbf 1$. Null vectors, by contrast, satisfy $v^2=0$ and are not invertible. That nilpotent behavior is not a bug. It is part of why lightlike momenta and spinor-helicity variables have special algebraic structure.

## Low-dimensional examples

The first few real Clifford algebras are worth knowing because they anchor the conventions.

| Quadratic space | Generator relations | Real Clifford algebra | Comment |
|---|---|---|---|
| one positive line | $e^2=+1$ | $\mathbb R\oplus\mathbb R$ | idempotent decomposition |
| one negative line | $e^2=-1$ | $\mathbb C$ | $e$ behaves like $i$ |
| positive plane | $e_1^2=e_2^2=+1$ | $\operatorname{Mat}(2,\mathbb R)$ | first real matrix example |
| negative plane | $f_1^2=f_2^2=-1$ | $\mathbb H$ | quaternionic example |
| Euclidean three-space | $e_i^2=+1$ | $\operatorname{Mat}(2,\mathbb C)$ as a real algebra | Pauli matrices represent it |
| mostly-minus spacetime | signature $(1,3)$ | $\operatorname{Mat}(2,\mathbb H)$ | real algebra behind Lorentzian spinors |
| mostly-plus spacetime | signature $(3,1)$ | $\operatorname{Mat}(4,\mathbb R)$ | real signature is different |

The last two rows are a useful warning. The real Clifford algebras for $(1,3)$ and $(3,1)$ are not isomorphic. But after complexification, both become

$$
\operatorname{Cl}_{1,3}(\mathbb R)\otimes\mathbb C
\simeq
\operatorname{Cl}_{3,1}(\mathbb R)\otimes\mathbb C
\simeq
\operatorname{Mat}(4,\mathbb C).
$$

Most perturbative four-dimensional QFT calculations use complex $4\times4$ gamma matrices, so they see this common complex algebra. Real structures, Majorana conditions, Euclidean continuation, and spin geometry do see the difference.

## Complex Clifford algebras

Over $\mathbb C$, the classification is simple. If $n=2m$, then

$$
\operatorname{Cl}_n(\mathbb C)\simeq\operatorname{Mat}(2^m,\mathbb C).
$$

If $n=2m+1$, then

$$
\operatorname{Cl}_n(\mathbb C)
\simeq
\operatorname{Mat}(2^m,\mathbb C)
\oplus
\operatorname{Mat}(2^m,\mathbb C).
$$

In even complex dimension, there is a unique irreducible module for the full Clifford algebra, of complex dimension $2^m$. In four spacetime dimensions this gives the familiar Dirac spinor dimension

$$
2^{4/2}=4.
$$

The even subalgebra in even dimension is not simple; its irreducible modules are the chiral or Weyl spinor modules. This is the local algebraic reason a four-component Dirac spinor can split into two two-component Weyl spinors.

One way to see the split is through the volume element. In four-dimensional Lorentzian QFT one defines

$$
\gamma^5=i\gamma^0\gamma^1\gamma^2\gamma^3,
$$

with the convention fixed on the [Mathematical Conventions](/math-toolkit/conventions/) page. The projectors

$$
P_L=\frac{1-\gamma^5}{2},
\qquad
P_R=\frac{1+\gamma^5}{2}
$$

select left- and right-handed Weyl components. The signs in these projectors are convention-dependent, but the algebraic idea is not: chirality comes from the even-dimensional Clifford volume element.

## Gamma matrices as representations

A gamma-matrix representation is a homomorphism

$$
\rho:\operatorname{Cl}(V,g)\to\operatorname{End}(S).
$$

The images of basis vectors are the gamma matrices:

$$
\rho(e_a)=\gamma_a.
$$

The defining Clifford relation becomes

$$
\gamma_a\gamma_b+\gamma_b\gamma_a=2g_{ab}\mathbf 1.
$$

Two different gamma-matrix sets can represent the same Clifford algebra. In a fixed irreducible complex representation of an even-dimensional complex Clifford algebra, any two choices are related by a similarity transformation:

$$
\gamma_a' = M\gamma_a M^{-1}.
$$

That means the physics cannot depend on a particular gamma-matrix basis. It may depend on choices of adjoint, charge conjugation, chirality matrix, and reality condition, but not on whether one uses the chiral, Dirac, or Majorana basis for the same local algebra.

In mostly-minus Lorentzian conventions, gamma matrices satisfy

$$
(\gamma^0)^2=+\mathbf 1,
\qquad
(\gamma^i)^2=-\mathbf 1.
$$

Therefore

$$
(p\!\!\!/)^2
=\gamma^\mu p_\mu\gamma^\nu p_\nu
=\frac12\{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\}p_\mu p_\nu
=\eta^{\mu\nu}p_\mu p_\nu\mathbf 1
=p^2\mathbf 1.
$$

The antisymmetric gamma part drops out because $p_\mu p_\nu$ is symmetric. This one-line calculation is the local algebra behind the free Dirac equation.

## Bivectors and infinitesimal rotations

The degree-two elements of the Clifford algebra are called bivectors. In an orthonormal basis, the basic bivectors are

$$
e_ae_b
\qquad (a\ne b).
$$

Under the commutator bracket, bivectors reproduce the Lie algebra of the orthogonal group. In a gamma-matrix representation, define

$$
\Sigma_{ab}=\frac14[\gamma_a,\gamma_b].
$$

Then

$$
[\Sigma_{ab},\gamma_c]=g_{bc}\gamma_a-g_{ac}\gamma_b.
$$

This is exactly the infinitesimal action of an antisymmetric generator on a vector index. The same matrices $\Sigma_{ab}$ act on spinors, while the commutator above shows how they rotate the gamma matrices as vectors.

This is the first hint that spinors transform under a double cover of the orthogonal group rather than under the orthogonal group itself. The full construction belongs to the next page.

## Common pitfalls

**A Clifford algebra is not a gamma-matrix basis.** The abstract algebra is defined by a universal property. Gamma matrices are one representation. Confusing the two is like confusing a group with one matrix representation of that group.

**The exterior-algebra identification is not an algebra isomorphism.** As vector spaces, $\operatorname{Cl}(V,g)$ and $\Lambda^\bullet V$ have the same dimension. Their products are different. The Clifford product contains contractions.

**Signature matters over the real numbers.** The real algebras $\operatorname{Cl}_{1,3}(\mathbb R)$ and $\operatorname{Cl}_{3,1}(\mathbb R)$ are different. Complex gamma-matrix calculations often hide this difference.

**The notation $\operatorname{Cl}_{p,q}$ is not universal.** Some authors put the negative signs first. Always check the convention before importing a real-classification table or a Majorana condition.

**Null vectors are special.** If $v$ is null, then $v^2=0$. Such a vector is not invertible in the Clifford algebra. This matters for lightlike momenta, Weyl spinors, and spinor-helicity factorizations.

**Spinors are not introduced by declaring columns to be spinors.** A column vector becomes a spinor only after specifying a Clifford or spin-group representation on the vector space of columns.

## Research status

The algebraic construction of Clifford algebras is completely standard. What remains convention-sensitive in QFT is not the definition but its implementation: real forms, Euclidean continuation, adjoints, charge conjugation matrices, chirality conventions, dimensional regularization, and Majorana conditions.

Active modern uses include spinor-helicity variables, fermionic topological phases, spin-TQFTs, anomalies on non-spin manifolds, pin structures for time-reversal systems, and geometric index theory. The local algebra on this page is settled; the global and physical contexts keep producing interesting edge cases.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Polarize the square relation

Assume $v^2=g(v,v)\mathbf 1$ for all $v\in V$. Show that

$$
uv+vu=2g(u,v)\mathbf 1.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Apply the square relation to $u+v$:

$$
(u+v)^2=g(u+v,u+v)\mathbf 1.
$$

Expanding the left side gives

$$
u^2+uv+vu+v^2.
$$

Using $u^2=g(u,u)\mathbf 1$ and $v^2=g(v,v)\mathbf 1$, we get

$$
uv+vu=\bigl(g(u+v,u+v)-g(u,u)-g(v,v)\bigr)\mathbf 1.
$$

Since $g$ is symmetric and bilinear,

$$
g(u+v,u+v)=g(u,u)+2g(u,v)+g(v,v).
$$

Therefore

$$
uv+vu=2g(u,v)\mathbf 1.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Count the dimension

Let $e_1,\ldots,e_n$ be an orthogonal basis with $e_a^2=\pm1$. Show that the ordered monomials

$$
e_{a_1}\cdots e_{a_k},
\qquad
1\le a_1<\cdots<a_k\le n,
$$

span the Clifford algebra. How many are there?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Any product of basis vectors can be reordered using

$$
e_ae_b=-e_be_a\qquad(a\ne b).
$$

Whenever the same basis vector appears twice, use

$$
e_a^2=\pm\mathbf 1
$$

to remove the repeated pair. Repeating this process turns any product into a scalar multiple of an ordered product with no repeated indices.

There is one ordered product for every subset of $\{1,\ldots,n\}$. Hence the number is

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n\binom nk=2^n.
$$

This proves spanning. Linear independence requires a little more work, for example by using the standard representation of Clifford multiplication on $\Lambda^\bullet V$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Square slash momentum

Let

$$
p\!\!\!/\equiv\gamma^\mu p_\mu
$$

and assume

$$
\{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\}=2\eta^{\mu\nu}\mathbf 1.
$$

Show that

$$
(p\!\!\!/)^2=p^2\mathbf 1.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
(p\!\!\!/)^2=\gamma^\mu\gamma^\nu p_\mu p_\nu.
$$

Since $p_\mu p_\nu$ is symmetric in $\mu,\nu$, only the symmetric part of $\gamma^\mu\gamma^\nu$ contributes:

$$
\gamma^\mu\gamma^\nu p_\mu p_\nu
=\frac12\{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\}p_\mu p_\nu.
$$

Using the Clifford relation,

$$
(p\!\!\!/)^2
=\eta^{\mu\nu}p_\mu p_\nu\mathbf 1
=p^2\mathbf 1.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: The Pauli pseudoscalar

In Euclidean three-space, let $e_i$ be Clifford generators with $e_i^2=1$ and $e_ie_j=-e_je_i$ for $i\ne j$. Define

$$
I=e_1e_2e_3.
$$

Show that $I^2=-1$. Under the Pauli representation $e_i\mapsto\sigma_i$, what does $I$ map to?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

First,

$$
I^2=e_1e_2e_3e_1e_2e_3.
$$

Move the second $e_1$ left past $e_3$ and $e_2$, producing two minus signs, so no net sign:

$$
I^2=e_1e_1e_2e_3e_2e_3=e_2e_3e_2e_3.
$$

Now move the second $e_2$ past $e_3$, producing one minus sign:

$$
e_2e_3e_2e_3=-e_2e_2e_3e_3=-1.
$$

Under the Pauli representation,

$$
I\mapsto\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3.
$$

Since $\sigma_1\sigma_2=i\sigma_3$, this becomes

$$
\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3=i\sigma_3^2=i\mathbf 1.
$$

So the real Clifford pseudoscalar is represented by the ordinary complex number $i$ in the Pauli representation.

</details>
