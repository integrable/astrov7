## Summary

Spin groups are the groups that act honestly on spinors and project down to ordinary rotations or Lorentz transformations on vectors. They live inside the even part of a Clifford algebra.

The construction starts with the Clifford reflection formula. If $v$ is a non-null vector, then

$$
R_v(x)=-vxv^{-1}
$$

is the reflection of a vector $x\in V$ across the hyperplane orthogonal to $v$. Products of reflections generate orthogonal transformations. Products of an even number of unit vectors form the spin group.

In the familiar cases,

$$
\operatorname{Spin}(3)\simeq SU(2),
\qquad
\operatorname{Spin}^+(1,3)\simeq SL(2,\mathbb C).
$$

The first double-covers $SO(3)$; the second double-covers the proper orthochronous Lorentz group $SO^+(1,3)$. This is why spin-one-half particles transform under $SU(2)$ or $SL(2,\mathbb C)$ even though ordinary vectors transform under $SO(3)$ or $SO^+(1,3)$.

$$
uv+vu=2g(u,v)\mathbf 1,

$$
v^{-1}=\frac{v}{g(v,v)}.
$$

You should also know [Pauli Matrices](/math-toolkit/clifford-spinors/pauli-matrices/), [SU(2) and Angular Momentum](/math-toolkit/groups-representations/su2-and-angular-momentum/), and [SL(2,C) and the Lorentz Group](/math-toolkit/groups-representations/sl2c-and-lorentz-group/). ## Core idea

The orthogonal group is generated by reflections. The Clifford algebra gives a compact algebraic formula for reflections. Therefore products of Clifford vectors give orthogonal transformations.

The surprise is that the product itself contains more information than the orthogonal transformation it induces. The two Clifford elements

$$
s
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
-s
$$

produce the same transformation on vectors, because conjugation by $s$ and by $-s$ is identical. But they act differently on spinors. In particular, a $2\pi$ rotation is trivial on vectors and equals $-\mathbf 1$ on spinors.

This is the basic double-cover phenomenon:

$$
1\longrightarrow \{\pm1\}
\longrightarrow \operatorname{Spin}(p,q)
\longrightarrow SO(p,q)
\longrightarrow 1,
$$

with the appropriate connected component inserted when needed.

Spin is therefore not a mysterious new kind of vector. It is what happens when one represents rotations before quotienting by the sign that vectors cannot see.

## Setup and conventions

Let $(V,g)$ be a finite-dimensional real vector space with nondegenerate symmetric bilinear form. We write $\operatorname{Cl}(V,g)$ for its Clifford algebra. For real signature,

$$
\operatorname{Cl}_{p,q}(\mathbb R)
$$

means $p$ basis vectors square to $+1$ and $q$ basis vectors square to $-1$.

For four-dimensional spacetime with mostly-minus metric,

$$
\eta=\operatorname{diag}(+1,-1,-1,-1),
$$

the relevant real signature is $(1,3)$. The connected spin group used for ordinary proper orthochronous Lorentz transformations is

$$
\operatorname{Spin}^+(1,3)\simeq SL(2,\mathbb C).
$$

This page focuses on the local algebraic construction. Global questions, such as whether a manifold admits a spin structure, are not merely local Clifford-algebra questions. They require topology.

## Reflections from Clifford multiplication

Let $v\in V$ be non-null, so $g(v,v)\ne0$. Then

$$
v^{-1}=\frac{v}{g(v,v)}.
$$

Define

$$
R_v(x)=-vxv^{-1}
$$

for $x\in V$. We claim that $R_v$ is the reflection that flips the component of $x$ parallel to $v$ and leaves the orthogonal component fixed.

Write

$$
x=x_\parallel+x_\perp,
$$

where

$$
x_\parallel=\frac{g(x,v)}{g(v,v)}v,
\qquad
 g(x_\perp,v)=0.
$$

For the parallel part,

$$
R_v(v)=-vvv^{-1}=-v.
$$

For the perpendicular part, $g(x_\perp,v)=0$, so the Clifford relation gives

$$
vx_\perp=-x_\perp v.
$$

Therefore

$$
R_v(x_\perp)=-vx_\perp v^{-1}=x_\perp vv^{-1}=x_\perp.
$$

Thus

$$
R_v(x)=x-2\frac{g(x,v)}{g(v,v)}v.
$$

This is the usual reflection formula. The minus sign in $R_v(x)=-vxv^{-1}$ is not decorative; without it the vector parallel to $v$ would not flip correctly.

A more uniform formula uses the grade involution $\alpha$, defined by $\alpha(v)=-v$ for vectors. Then a single reflection is

$$
R_v(x)=\alpha(v)xv^{-1}.
$$

This form generalizes cleanly to products of vectors.

## Pin and Spin groups

The **Pin group** is generated by non-null unit vectors inside the Clifford algebra. It covers the full orthogonal group, including reflections.

The **Spin group** is the even part of this construction: it is generated by products of an even number of non-null unit vectors. In positive-definite signature, one can say simply

$$
\operatorname{Spin}(n)
=
\{v_1v_2\cdots v_{2k}: |v_i|=1\}\subset\operatorname{Cl}^0_n.
$$

In indefinite signature, one must allow unit vectors with square $+1$ or $-1$, and one must be more careful about connected components. For most local QFT purposes, the important object in four-dimensional Lorentzian signature is the connected component

$$
\operatorname{Spin}^+(1,3),
$$

which covers $SO^+(1,3)$.

If

$$
s=v_1v_2\cdots v_k,
$$

then the induced action on vectors is

$$
\rho(s)(x)=\alpha(s)xs^{-1}.
$$

For even $s$, $\alpha(s)=s$, so the spin action on vectors is simply

$$
\rho(s)(x)=sxs^{-1}.
$$

The element $s$ acts on spinors by left multiplication or by the chosen spin representation. The vector $x$ sees only $\rho(s)$.

## The double cover

The map

$$
\rho:\operatorname{Spin}(p,q)\to SO(p,q)
$$

sends a spin element to its conjugation action on $V$:

$$
\rho(s)(x)=sxs^{-1}.
$$

The two elements $s$ and $-s$ give the same vector transformation:

$$
(-s)x(-s)^{-1}=sxs^{-1}.
$$

For the connected spin groups used in standard QFT, the kernel is

$$
\ker\rho=\{+1,-1\}.
$$

Thus the spin group is a double cover of the corresponding special orthogonal group.

This statement is not just group-theory trivia. Quantum states are rays, but quantum amplitudes compare phases. A path in the rotation group that returns a vector frame to itself can lift to a path in the spin group that ends at $-1$. For a spinor,

$$
\psi\mapsto -\psi
$$

after a $2\pi$ rotation. Only after a $4\pi$ rotation does it return to itself as a vector in the spinor representation.

## Example: ordinary rotations

For Euclidean three-space,

$$
\operatorname{Spin}(3)\simeq SU(2),
$$

and the map to $SO(3)$ is the familiar double cover.

Let $\mathbf n$ be a unit vector and define

$$
U(\mathbf n,\theta)
=
\exp\left(-\frac{i\theta}{2}\mathbf n\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma\right).
$$

Then $U\in SU(2)$ acts on a vector $\mathbf a$ encoded as a Hermitian matrix

$$
A=\mathbf a\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma
$$

by

$$
A\mapsto UAU^\dagger.
$$

There is a unique rotated vector $R\mathbf a$ such that

$$
UAU^\dagger=(R\mathbf a)\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma.
$$

Both $U$ and $-U$ give the same $R$. At $\theta=2\pi$,

$$
U(\mathbf n,2\pi)=-\mathbf 1,
$$

but $R$ is the identity rotation on ordinary vectors.

This is the cleanest model of what “spin one half” means: it is not that spinors rotate halfway. It is that the group acting on spinors is the double cover of the rotation group.

## Example: Lorentz transformations

In mostly-minus four-dimensional spacetime,

$$
\operatorname{Spin}^+(1,3)\simeq SL(2,\mathbb C).
$$

The group $SL(2,\mathbb C)$ acts on Hermitian $2\times2$ matrices

$$
X=x_\mu\sigma^\mu
$$

by

$$
X\mapsto MXM^\dagger,
\qquad
M\in SL(2,\mathbb C).
$$

Because

$$
\det X=x^2,
$$

this action preserves the Minkowski norm. Therefore it defines a Lorentz transformation. Again, $M$ and $-M$ define the same Lorentz transformation.

This is why four-dimensional Weyl spinors transform as

$$
\psi_\alpha\mapsto M_\alpha{}^\beta\psi_\beta
$$

under $SL(2,\mathbb C)$, while vectors transform under the induced Lorentz transformation. The dotted representation transforms with the complex-conjugate matrix. The detailed dotted-undotted index conventions belong to the later Weyl, Dirac, and Majorana Spinors page; the group-theoretic fact is already here.

## The Lie algebra from bivectors

The Lie algebra of the spin group is made of bivectors. In a gamma-matrix representation, define

$$
\Sigma_{ab}=\frac14[\gamma_a,\gamma_b].
$$

Using the Clifford relation, one finds

$$
[\Sigma_{ab},\gamma_c]=g_{bc}\gamma_a-g_{ac}\gamma_b.
$$

This says that $\Sigma_{ab}$ acts on the vector-index object $\gamma_c$ exactly as an infinitesimal orthogonal transformation should.

The commutator of the $\Sigma_{ab}$ closes as

$$
[\Sigma_{ab},\Sigma_{cd}]
=
 g_{bc}\Sigma_{ad}
-g_{ac}\Sigma_{bd}
-g_{bd}\Sigma_{ac}
+g_{ad}\Sigma_{bc},
$$

up to the sign conventions implied by the placement of indices. This is the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(p,q)$ in the spinor representation.

A finite spin transformation is represented by an exponential of bivectors. Locally one writes

$$
S(\omega)=
\exp\left(\frac12\omega^{ab}\Sigma_{ab}\right),
$$

where $\omega^{ab}=-\omega^{ba}$. Depending on whether one uses Hermitian or anti-Hermitian generators, factors of $i$ move between $\omega^{ab}$ and the generators. Later gamma-matrix pages will fix the detailed QFT convention for Lorentz transformations of Dirac spinors.

The invariant content is that the spinor transformation $S$ satisfies

$$
S^{-1}\gamma^aS=\Lambda^a{}_b\gamma^b
$$

or the equivalent convention with $S$ and $S^{-1}$ interchanged. The side on which $S$ appears is convention-dependent; the covariance statement is not.

## Spinors as representations

A spinor module $S$ is a representation space for the Clifford algebra or, at minimum, for the spin group. A spin element $s\in\operatorname{Spin}(p,q)$ acts on

$$
\psi\in S
$$

by

$$
\psi\mapsto s\cdot\psi.
$$

The same $s$ acts on vectors by conjugation:

$$
x\mapsto sxs^{-1}.
$$

Thus the same abstract element relates two transformation laws:

$$
\text{spinors: } \psi\mapsto s\psi,
\qquad
\text{vectors: } x\mapsto sxs^{-1}.
$$

This distinction is the reason bilinears such as

$$
\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\psi
$$

can transform as vectors even though $\psi$ itself does not. One spinor transformation acts on $\psi$, the inverse transformation acts on $\bar\psi$, and the gamma matrix transforms by the induced vector action.

## Spin structures and the global warning

Locally, every orthonormal frame can be lifted to a spin frame. Globally, this may fail. A manifold admits a spin structure only when a certain topological obstruction vanishes. For an oriented Riemannian manifold, this obstruction is the second Stiefel–Whitney class

$$
w_2(TM).
$$

If

$$
w_2(TM)\ne0,
$$

then there is no globally defined spinor bundle in the usual sense. If $w_2(TM)=0$, there may still be more than one spin structure.

This matters in QFT because a fermionic path integral on a spacetime manifold is not specified by the metric alone. It also requires spin-structure data, or a suitable replacement such as a spin-$c$ structure in theories where fermions are tied to gauge fields.

This page does not develop spin geometry, but it is worth planting the warning early: Clifford algebras are local; fermions in QFT are global.

## Pin groups and discrete spacetime symmetries

Spin groups use even products of unit vectors and cover orientation-preserving transformations. Pin groups use both odd and even products and cover the full orthogonal group, including reflections.

This distinction matters for parity, time reversal, and QFT on nonorientable manifolds. There is not just one Pin group in indefinite signature. Different sign conventions lead to different Pin structures, often denoted $Pin^+$ and $Pin^-$ in Euclidean-signature discussions. These distinctions are not cosmetic; they affect fermion signs and anomalies.

For ordinary perturbative QFT in flat Minkowski spacetime, one can often postpone Pin subtleties. For anomalies, topological phases, Euclidean manifolds, time-reversal systems, and fermionic TQFT, postponement stops working.

## Common pitfalls

**Spin groups are not the same as orthogonal groups.** They map onto orthogonal groups with kernel $\{\pm1\}$. A vector cannot see the sign; a spinor can.

**Spin is not only about three-dimensional rotations.** The slogan $\operatorname{Spin}(3)=SU(2)$ is important, but Lorentzian QFT mostly uses $\operatorname{Spin}^+(1,3)=SL(2,\mathbb C)$.

**A $2\pi$ rotation is not the identity on spinors.** It maps $\psi$ to $-\psi$. A $4\pi$ rotation is the identity in the spinor representation.

**Pin and Spin are different.** Spin covers orientation-preserving transformations. Pin covers transformations including reflections. Parity and time reversal are Pin-sensitive.

**Local gamma matrices do not guarantee global spinors.** A manifold needs spin-structure data before one can define spinor fields globally.

**The sign conventions are genuinely treacherous.** Metric signature, $\operatorname{Cl}_{p,q}$ notation, Hermitian versus anti-Hermitian generators, and $S^{-1}\gamma S$ versus $S\gamma S^{-1}$ conventions all move signs around.

## Research status

The construction of spin groups and their double-cover maps is established mathematics. In everyday perturbative QFT, the main tasks are convention management and representation bookkeeping.

The active edges appear when spinors meet global topology: spin structures, Pin structures, spin-$c$ structures, anomalies, fermionic phases of matter, TQFTs, nonorientable manifolds, and QFTs with extended operators. In those settings, the double cover is not a small detail. It is part of the data of the theory.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Verify the reflection formula

Let $v$ be non-null and define

$$
R_v(x)=-vxv^{-1}.
$$

Show that

$$
R_v(x)=x-2\frac{g(x,v)}{g(v,v)}v.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Decompose

$$
x=x_\parallel+x_\perp,
$$

where

$$
x_\parallel=\frac{g(x,v)}{g(v,v)}v,
\qquad
 g(x_\perp,v)=0.
$$

For the parallel part,

$$
R_v(x_\parallel)=-x_\parallel.
$$

For the perpendicular part, the Clifford relation gives $vx_\perp=-x_\perp v$, so

$$
R_v(x_\perp)=-vx_\perp v^{-1}=x_\perp vv^{-1}=x_\perp.
$$

Therefore

$$
R_v(x)=-x_\parallel+x_\perp
=x-2x_\parallel
=x-2\frac{g(x,v)}{g(v,v)}v.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: The sign invisible to vectors

Let $s\in\operatorname{Spin}(p,q)$ and let $\rho(s)(x)=sxs^{-1}$. Show that $s$ and $-s$ induce the same vector transformation.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
\rho(-s)(x)=(-s)x(-s)^{-1}.
$$

Since

$$
(-s)^{-1}=-s^{-1},
$$

we get

$$
\rho(-s)(x)=(-s)x(-s^{-1})=sxs^{-1}=\rho(s)(x).
$$

Thus the map from the spin group to the orthogonal group has at least the sign ambiguity $s\sim -s$. For the connected spin groups used here, this is exactly the kernel.

</details>

### Exercise 3: A two-dimensional rotor

In Euclidean two-space, let $e_1^2=e_2^2=1$ and $e_1e_2=-e_2e_1$. Define

$$
B=e_1e_2,
\qquad
s(\theta)=\exp\left(-\frac\theta2 B\right).
$$

Show that $B^2=-1$ and that $s(2\pi)=-1$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

First,

$$
B^2=e_1e_2e_1e_2=-e_1e_1e_2e_2=-1.
$$

Therefore the exponential behaves like an ordinary complex exponential:

$$
s(\theta)=\cos\frac\theta2-B\sin\frac\theta2.
$$

At $\theta=2\pi$,

$$
s(2\pi)=\cos\pi-B\sin\pi=-1.
$$

Thus a full $2\pi$ vector rotation lifts to the nontrivial central element of the spin group.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Bivectors rotate gamma matrices

Let

$$
\Sigma_{ab}=\frac14[\gamma_a,\gamma_b]
$$

with

$$
\{\gamma_a,\gamma_b\}=2g_{ab}\mathbf 1.
$$

Show that

$$
[\Sigma_{ab},\gamma_c]=g_{bc}\gamma_a-g_{ac}\gamma_b.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $a\ne b$, the antisymmetric generator is equivalent to

$$
\Sigma_{ab}=\frac12\gamma_a\gamma_b,
$$

with the same final formula extending by antisymmetry. Then

$$
[\Sigma_{ab},\gamma_c]
=\frac12(\gamma_a\gamma_b\gamma_c-\gamma_c\gamma_a\gamma_b).
$$

Move $\gamma_c$ left through $\gamma_b$ in the first term:

$$
\gamma_b\gamma_c=-\gamma_c\gamma_b+2g_{bc}\mathbf 1.
$$

Thus

$$
\gamma_a\gamma_b\gamma_c
=-\gamma_a\gamma_c\gamma_b+2g_{bc}\gamma_a.
$$

Now move $\gamma_c$ left through $\gamma_a$:

$$
\gamma_a\gamma_c=-\gamma_c\gamma_a+2g_{ac}\mathbf 1.
$$

So

$$
-\gamma_a\gamma_c\gamma_b
=\gamma_c\gamma_a\gamma_b-2g_{ac}\gamma_b.
$$

Putting this into the commutator gives

$$
[\Sigma_{ab},\gamma_c]
=\frac12(2g_{bc}\gamma_a-2g_{ac}\gamma_b)
=g_{bc}\gamma_a-g_{ac}\gamma_b.
$$

</details>
