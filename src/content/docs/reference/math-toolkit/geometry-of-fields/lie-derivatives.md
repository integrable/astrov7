## Summary

A Lie derivative measures how a geometric object changes when dragged along the flow of a vector field. A vector field is an infinitesimal motion of points. Its flow $\Phi_t$ moves each point a small amount. The Lie derivative asks: after moving the object by this flow and comparing it back at the original point, what is the first-order change?

For a differential form $\omega$, the definition is

$$
\mathcal L_X\omega
=
\left.\frac{d}{dt}\right|_{t=0}\Phi_t^*\omega.
$$

Here $\Phi_t^*$ is a pullback, so both $\Phi_t^*\omega$ and $\omega$ live at the same point and can be subtracted. This “pull back before subtracting” step is the whole geometric trick.

For forms, the practical identity is Cartan’s formula:

$$
\mathcal L_X=d\iota_X+\iota_X d,
$$

where $\iota_X$ is contraction with the vector field $X$. This says that the infinitesimal change of a form is the sum of two operations: differentiate then contract, and contract then differentiate.

In QFT, Lie derivatives are how spacetime symmetries enter geometry. Translations, rotations, boosts, dilatations, conformal transformations, time evolution, diffeomorphisms, and many background-field variations are all expressed by Lie derivatives or their gauge-covariant variants.

## Core idea

The ordinary derivative of a function compares values at nearby points:

$$
\frac{f(x+t)-f(x)}{t}.
$$

That works because values of $f$ are numbers, and numbers at different points can be subtracted. Tensor fields are different. A one-form at $p$ lives in $T_p^*M$, while a one-form at $q$ lives in $T_q^*M$. Without extra structure, those are different vector spaces. Subtracting them directly is meaningless.

The Lie derivative solves this using the flow of a vector field. Let $X$ generate a local flow

$$
\Phi_t:M\to M,
\qquad
\Phi_0=\operatorname{id},
\qquad
\left.\frac{d}{dt}\right|_{t=0}\Phi_t(p)=X_p.
$$

To compare a covariant tensor field at nearby points, pull it back by $\Phi_t$ so that it lives at the original point. Then differentiate with respect to $t$.

For a covariant tensor field $T$,

$$
\mathcal L_XT
=
\left.\frac{d}{dt}\right|_{t=0}\Phi_t^*T.
$$

For vector fields and other contravariant objects, the definition is equivalent but uses pushforwards. The resulting formulas are compatible with products, contractions, and tensor algebra.

The slogan is:

$$
\mathcal L_X = \text{infinitesimal pullback along the flow of }X.
$$

This derivative needs no metric, no connection, and no coordinates. That is why it is the natural language for diffeomorphisms.

## Flows of vector fields

A vector field $X$ assigns to each point $p\in M$ a tangent vector

$$
X_p\in T_pM.
$$

A flow is a family of maps $\Phi_t$ satisfying

$$
\Phi_0(p)=p,
\qquad
\Phi_{t+s}(p)=\Phi_t(\Phi_s(p))
$$

where both sides are defined, and

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\Phi_t(p)=X_{\Phi_t(p)}.
$$

In local coordinates, if $X=X^\mu(x)\partial_\mu$, the flow solves the ordinary differential equation

$$
\frac{dx^\mu(t)}{dt}=X^\mu(x(t)),
\qquad
x^\mu(0)=x^\mu_0.
$$

For small $t$,

$$
x^\mu(t)=x^\mu_0+tX^\mu(x_0)+O(t^2).
$$

Examples:

| Vector field | Flow | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| $\partial_x$ on $\mathbb R$ | $x\mapsto x+t$ | translation |
| $x\partial_y-y\partial_x$ on $\mathbb R^2$ | rotation by angle $t$ | planar rotation |
| $x^\mu\partial_\mu$ on $\mathbb R^d$ | $x^\mu\mapsto e^t x^\mu$ | scale transformation |
| $\partial_t$ on spacetime | time translation | Hamiltonian evolution, when it is a symmetry |

The flow is active: it moves points. A coordinate change is passive: it relabels points. Lie derivatives are often written in coordinates, but their geometric meaning is active dragging along a vector field.

## Functions

For a function $f\in C^\infty(M)$,

$$
\mathcal L_X f
=
X(f).
$$

In local coordinates,

$$
\mathcal L_X f=X^\mu\partial_\mu f.
$$

This is the directional derivative of $f$ along $X$. If $\mathcal L_X f=0$, then $f$ is constant along the flow of $X$.

For example, on $\mathbb R^2$ let

$$
X=x\partial_y-y\partial_x.
$$

This generates rotations. The function

$$
f=x^2+y^2
$$

satisfies

$$
\mathcal L_X f
=x(2y)-y(2x)=0,
$$

so it is rotationally invariant.

## Vector fields and the Lie bracket

For vector fields, the Lie derivative is the Lie bracket:

$$
\mathcal L_XY=[X,Y].
$$

In local coordinates,

$$
[X,Y]
=
\left(X^\nu\partial_\nu Y^\mu-Y^\nu\partial_\nu X^\mu\right)\partial_\mu.
$$

The bracket measures the failure of the flows of $X$ and $Y$ to commute. If one flows a little along $X$, then $Y$, then backward along $X$, then backward along $Y$, the leading displacement is governed by $[X,Y]$.

The bracket satisfies

$$
[X,Y]=-[Y,X]
$$

and the Jacobi identity

$$
[X,[Y,Z]]+[Y,[Z,X]]+[Z,[X,Y]]=0.
$$

The Jacobi identity makes vector fields into an infinite-dimensional Lie algebra.

For spacetime symmetries, this bracket is the geometric origin of commutation relations. Translations, rotations, boosts, and conformal vector fields close under Lie bracket when they form a symmetry algebra.

## One-forms and covariant tensors

Let $\alpha=\alpha_\mu dx^\mu$ be a one-form. Its Lie derivative is

$$
(\mathcal L_X\alpha)_\mu
=
X^\nu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu
+
\alpha_\nu\partial_\mu X^\nu.
$$

The first term differentiates the components along the vector field. The second term accounts for the change of the basis one-forms $dx^\mu$ under the flow.

For a covariant tensor

$$
T=T_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_k}dx^{\mu_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes dx^{\mu_k},
$$

the coordinate formula is

$$
(\mathcal L_XT)_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_k}
=
X^\nu\partial_\nu T_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_k}
+
\sum_{a=1}^k
T_{\mu_1\cdots \nu\cdots\mu_k}\partial_{\mu_a}X^\nu.
$$

For a mixed tensor, contravariant indices contribute with the opposite sign. For example, for a vector field $Y^\mu$,

$$
(\mathcal L_XY)^\mu
=X^\nu\partial_\nu Y^\mu-Y^\nu\partial_\nu X^\mu.
$$

This is why the Lie derivative knows the tensor type. It is not just “take $X^\nu\partial_\nu$ of every component.” That would miss the basis-change terms.

## Interior product

The interior product, or contraction, with a vector field $X$ is an operation

$$
\iota_X:\Omega^p(M)\to\Omega^{p-1}(M).
$$

For a $p$-form $\omega$, it is defined by inserting $X$ into the first slot:

$$
(\iota_X\omega)(Y_1,\ldots,Y_{p-1})
=
\omega(X,Y_1,\ldots,Y_{p-1}).
$$

For a one-form $\alpha=\alpha_\mu dx^\mu$,

$$
\iota_X \alpha=\alpha_\mu X^\mu.
$$

For a two-form

$$
F=\frac12F_{\mu\nu}dx^\mu\wedge dx^\nu,
$$

one finds

$$
\iota_X F=X^\mu F_{\mu\nu}dx^\nu.
$$

The interior product has degree $-1$ and satisfies the graded rule

$$
\iota_X(\alpha\wedge\beta)
=(\iota_X \alpha)\wedge\beta+(-1)^p\alpha\wedge\iota_X\beta,
\qquad
\alpha\in\Omega^p(M).
$$

The sign comes from moving $X$ past a $p$-form slot. And yes, this is where a lot of sign gremlins live.

## Cartan formula

For differential forms, the Lie derivative has the beautiful expression

$$
\mathcal L_X=d\iota_X+\iota_X d.
$$

This is often called Cartan’s formula, or Cartan’s magic formula. It is “magic” because it converts a definition involving flows into a purely algebraic-differential identity.

Check it first on functions. If $f$ is a zero-form, then $\iota_Xf=0$, so

$$
(d\iota_X+\iota_X d)f=\iota_X(df)=df(X)=X(f)=\mathcal L_Xf.
$$

For a one-form $\alpha$, write

$$
\alpha=\alpha_\mu dx^\mu.
$$

Then

$$
\iota_X \alpha=X^\mu\alpha_\mu.
$$

Also

$$
d\alpha=\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu dx^\nu\wedge dx^\mu.
$$

Using contraction,

$$
\iota_X d\alpha
=X^\nu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu dx^\mu
-X^\mu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu dx^\nu.
$$

Meanwhile

$$
d\iota_X \alpha
=d(X^\mu\alpha_\mu)
=(\partial_\nu X^\mu)\alpha_\mu dx^\nu
+X^\mu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu dx^\nu.
$$

The last term cancels the second term in $\iota_X d\alpha$, leaving

$$
(d\iota_X+\iota_X d)\alpha
=
X^\nu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu dx^\mu
+
\alpha_\mu\partial_\nu X^\mu dx^\nu.
$$

Relabeling dummy indices gives

$$
(\mathcal L_X\alpha)_\mu
=X^\nu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu+
\alpha_\nu\partial_\mu X^\nu,
$$

which is the coordinate formula above.

Because both sides are graded derivations and agree on functions and one-forms, they agree on all forms.

## Basic identities

The Lie derivative of forms satisfies

$$
[\mathcal L_X,d]=0.
$$

This follows immediately from Cartan’s formula and $d^2=0$:

$$
\mathcal L_Xd=(d\iota_X+\iota_X d)d=d\iota_X d,
$$

while

$$
d\mathcal L_X=d(d\iota_X+\iota_X d)=d\iota_X d.
$$

It also satisfies

$$
[\mathcal L_X,\iota_Y]=\iota_{[X,Y]},
$$

and

$$
[\mathcal L_X,\mathcal L_Y]=\mathcal L_{[X,Y]}.
$$

These identities encode compatibility between flows, contractions, and exterior differentiation.

The Lie derivative is a derivation of degree zero:

$$
\mathcal L_X(\alpha\wedge\beta)
=(\mathcal L_X\alpha)\wedge\beta+
\alpha\wedge\mathcal L_X\beta.
$$

Unlike $d$, it does not introduce a sign in the product rule because it has degree zero.

## Divergence and volume forms

Let $\mu$ be a volume form on a $d$-dimensional manifold. The divergence of a vector field $X$ with respect to $\mu$ is defined by

$$
\mathcal L_X\mu=(\operatorname{div}_\mu X)\mu.
$$

In local coordinates with

$$
\mu=\rho(x)dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^d,
$$

one finds

$$
\operatorname{div}_\mu X
=
\frac1{\rho}\partial_\mu(\rho X^\mu).
$$

If $\mu=\operatorname{vol}_g=\sqrt{|g|}d^d x$ is the metric volume form, then

$$
\operatorname{div}_g X
=
\frac1{\sqrt{|g|}}\partial_\mu(\sqrt{|g|}X^\mu).
$$

This formula is often written as

$$
\nabla_\mu X^\mu
=
\frac1{\sqrt{|g|}}\partial_\mu(\sqrt{|g|}X^\mu).
$$

The left-hand side uses the Levi-Civita connection; the right-hand side shows that the divergence is also the infinitesimal change of the volume form.

If $\mathcal L_X\mu=0$, the flow of $X$ preserves volume. Hamiltonian vector fields preserve the symplectic volume form; Killing vector fields preserve the metric volume form.

## Metrics and Killing vector fields

A vector field $X$ is a Killing vector field for a metric $g$ if

$$
\mathcal L_Xg=0.
$$

In coordinates,

$$
(\mathcal L_Xg)_{\mu\nu}
=X^\rho\partial_\rho g_{\mu\nu}
+g_{\rho\nu}\partial_\mu X^\rho
+g_{\mu\rho}\partial_\nu X^\rho.
$$

Using the Levi-Civita connection, this becomes the Killing equation

$$
\nabla_\mu X_\nu+
\nabla_\nu X_\mu=0.
$$

Killing vector fields generate isometries. In QFT on a fixed background, isometries are candidates for spacetime symmetries. If the action and state respect the symmetry, a Killing vector can be used to define a conserved current or charge.

For example, in flat Minkowski space, translations and Lorentz transformations are generated by Killing vector fields. The corresponding conserved charges are energy-momentum and angular momentum.

Conformal Killing vector fields obey the weaker condition

$$
\mathcal L_Xg=2\sigma g
$$

for some function $\sigma$. These generate conformal transformations rather than isometries.

## Symplectic forms and Hamiltonian vector fields

Let $\Omega$ be a symplectic form, so

$$
d\Omega=0
$$

and $\Omega$ is nondegenerate. A vector field $X$ preserves the symplectic form if

$$
\mathcal L_X\Omega=0.
$$

Cartan’s formula gives

$$
\mathcal L_X\Omega=d\iota_X \Omega+\iota_X d\Omega=d\iota_X \Omega.
$$

Thus $X$ is symplectic precisely when $\iota_X \Omega$ is closed:

$$
d(\iota_X \Omega)=0.
$$

If there exists a function $H$ such that

$$
\iota_X \Omega=dH,
$$

then $X$ is the Hamiltonian vector field generated by $H$, up to the sign convention chosen for Hamiltonian vector fields. The local distinction between closed and exact is once again important: locally a symplectic vector field is Hamiltonian, but global obstructions can appear.

This is the geometric bridge to the phase-space pages on [Symplectic Forms](/math-toolkit/calculus-of-variations-and-phase-space/symplectic-forms/) and [Poisson Brackets](/math-toolkit/calculus-of-variations-and-phase-space/poisson-brackets/).

## Gauge potentials and Cartan formula

Let $A$ be an abelian gauge potential, treated as a one-form, and let

$$
F=dA.
$$

Cartan’s formula gives

$$
\mathcal L_XA=\iota_X dA+d(\iota_X A)
=\iota_X F+d(\iota_X A).
$$

This identity is extremely useful. It says that the change of $A$ under a spacetime flow is the sum of a field-strength term and an exact term. The exact term has the form of an abelian gauge transformation.

If $X$ generates a symmetry and $\mathcal L_XA=0$, then

$$
\iota_X F=-d(\iota_X A).
$$

This is the differential-form origin of conserved quantities for charged particles in symmetric electromagnetic backgrounds.

For nonabelian gauge fields, the same idea becomes gauge-covariant only after adding the connection to the derivative. Schematically one replaces ordinary expressions by covariant ones, for example

$$
\mathcal L_X A
=
\iota_X F+D(\iota_X A),
$$

with $D$ the gauge-covariant exterior derivative. The displayed formula is correct in a local trivialization with the usual sign conventions, but the invariant interpretation belongs to the later pages on bundles, connections, and curvature.

## Diffeomorphism variations in field theory

In classical field theory, an infinitesimal diffeomorphism generated by $X$ changes tensor fields by Lie derivatives. For example,

$$
\delta_X g_{\mu\nu}=\mathcal L_Xg_{\mu\nu},
$$

and for a scalar field,

$$
\delta_X\phi=\mathcal L_X\phi=X^\mu\partial_\mu\phi.
$$

Some authors use the opposite sign,

$$
\delta_X\phi=-\mathcal L_X\phi,
$$

depending on whether the diffeomorphism is treated actively or passively, and whether the variation compares pulled-back or pushed-forward fields. The sign is convention-dependent; the geometric operation is not.

When an action is invariant under diffeomorphisms, the variation has the form

$$
\delta_X S=0
$$

up to boundary terms. After integrating by parts, this gives identities involving the stress tensor and equations of motion. In a metric theory, variation with respect to the metric defines the stress tensor, and invariance under diffeomorphisms leads to covariant conservation equations such as

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0
$$

when the equations of motion hold and there are no external sources or anomalies.

The Lie derivative is therefore not just a geometric nicety. It is the infinitesimal language behind stress tensors, Ward identities, background-field variations, and spacetime symmetry currents.

## Gauge-covariant and spinorial refinements

The ordinary Lie derivative acts naturally on tensors and differential forms. Spinors are not ordinary tensors, and charged fields live in associated vector bundles. Therefore, a plain Lie derivative is not always the right operation.

For a charged scalar field in a gauge background, one often wants a gauge-covariant infinitesimal translation:

$$
X^\mu D_\mu\phi
$$

rather than $X^\mu\partial_\mu\phi$. For gauge fields, one often combines a diffeomorphism with a compensating gauge transformation. For spinors, one uses a spinorial Lie derivative, often called the Kosmann Lie derivative, when the vector field is compatible with the spin structure and metric data.

These refinements are not optional technicalities in advanced applications. Supersymmetry on curved manifolds, localization, anomalies, and gauge-gravity systems all require knowing which Lie derivative is being used. This page gives the ordinary base layer; later pages add the relevant bundles, connections, and spin structures.

## Common pitfalls

**Taking only the directional derivative of components.** For tensors and forms, $X^\mu\partial_\mu$ is only part of the Lie derivative. Basis-change terms are essential.

**Comparing tensors at different points directly.** A one-form at $p$ and a one-form at $\Phi_t(p)$ live in different cotangent spaces. The pullback in the definition is what makes subtraction meaningful.

**Confusing Lie derivatives with covariant derivatives.** A covariant derivative $\nabla_X$ depends on a connection. A Lie derivative $\mathcal L_X$ depends on a vector field flow and needs no connection.

**Assuming a symmetry means every component is constant.** A tensor can be invariant under a flow even when its components change in a coordinate system. The invariant condition is $\mathcal L_XT=0$.

**Forgetting active/passive sign conventions.** Some physics formulas use $\delta=-\mathcal L_X$ for the variation under an infinitesimal coordinate transformation. Always check the convention.

**Using the ordinary Lie derivative on spinors.** Spinors need extra structure. Use the appropriate spinorial Lie derivative when the object is a spinor field.

**Treating gauge transformations as diffeomorphisms.** A Lie derivative handles spacetime dragging. Gauge transformations change internal trivializations. Gauge-covariant Lie derivatives combine the two in a controlled way.

## Worked example: rotation on the plane

Let

$$
X=x\partial_y-y\partial_x
$$

on $\mathbb R^2$. This generates rotations. Consider the one-form

$$
\alpha=-y\,dx+x\,dy.
$$

We compute $\mathcal L_X\alpha$ using Cartan’s formula.

First,

$$
\iota_X \alpha
=(-y)X^x+xX^y
=(-y)(-y)+x(x)=x^2+y^2.
$$

Next,

$$
d\alpha=d(-y\,dx+x\,dy)=2dx\wedge dy.
$$

Then

$$
\iota_X d\alpha
=2\iota_X(dx\wedge dy)
=2(X^xdy-X^ydx)
=2(-y\,dy-x\,dx).
$$

Also

$$
d\iota_X \alpha=d(x^2+y^2)=2x\,dx+2y\,dy.
$$

Adding,

$$
\mathcal L_X\alpha=d\iota_X \alpha+\iota_X d\alpha=0.
$$

So $\alpha$ is rotationally invariant. This is exactly what one expects: in polar coordinates, $\alpha=r^2d\theta$, and rotations change $\theta$ by a constant while leaving $d\theta$ and $r$ unchanged.

## Worked example: divergence from a volume form

Let

$$
\mu=dx\wedge dy
$$

on $\mathbb R^2$, and let

$$
X=P(x,y)\partial_x+Q(x,y)\partial_y.
$$

Since $d\mu=0$, Cartan’s formula gives

$$
\mathcal L_X\mu=d\iota_X \mu.
$$

Now

$$
\iota_X(dx\wedge dy)=Pdy-Qdx.
$$

Therefore

$$
d\iota_X \mu=d(Pdy-Qdx)
=(\partial_xP+\partial_yQ)dx\wedge dy.
$$

Thus

$$
\mathcal L_X\mu=(\partial_xP+\partial_yQ)\mu.
$$

The coefficient is the ordinary divergence. In curved coordinates or with a nontrivial volume form, the same definition gives the correct Jacobian-weighted divergence.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Lie derivative of a one-form

Let $X=X^\mu\partial_\mu$ and $\alpha=\alpha_\mu dx^\mu$. Starting from Cartan’s formula, show that

$$
(\mathcal L_X\alpha)_\mu
=X^\nu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu+\alpha_\nu\partial_\mu X^\nu.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

First,

$$
\iota_X \alpha=X^\nu\alpha_\nu.
$$

Therefore

$$
d\iota_X \alpha
=\partial_\mu(X^\nu\alpha_\nu)dx^\mu
=(\partial_\mu X^\nu)\alpha_\nu dx^\mu+X^\nu\partial_\mu\alpha_\nu dx^\mu.
$$

Next,

$$
d\alpha=\partial_\rho\alpha_\mu dx^\rho\wedge dx^\mu.
$$

Contracting with $X$ gives

$$

\iota_X d\alpha
=X^\rho\partial_\rho\alpha_\mu dx^\mu
-X^\mu\partial_\rho\alpha_\mu dx^\rho.
$$

Relabel the dummy indices in the second term as $\rho\to\mu$ and $\mu\to\nu$:

$$
-X^\nu\partial_\mu\alpha_\nu dx^\mu.
$$

Adding $d\iota_X \alpha$ cancels this term and leaves

$$
\mathcal L_X\alpha
=X^\nu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu dx^\mu
+
\alpha_\nu\partial_\mu X^\nu dx^\mu.
$$

Thus

$$
(\mathcal L_X\alpha)_\mu
=X^\nu\partial_\nu\alpha_\mu+\alpha_\nu\partial_\mu X^\nu.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Bracket of two vector fields

On $\mathbb R^2$, let

$$
X=\partial_x,
\qquad
Y=x\partial_y.
$$

Compute $[X,Y]$ and interpret the result.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Using

$$
[X,Y]^\mu=X^\nu\partial_\nu Y^\mu-Y^\nu\partial_\nu X^\mu,
$$

we have $X^x=1$, $X^y=0$, $Y^x=0$, and $Y^y=x$. Since $X$ has constant components, the second term vanishes. The first term gives

$$
[X,Y]^y=\partial_x(x)=1,
\qquad
[X,Y]^x=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
[X,Y]=\partial_y.
$$

The interpretation is that translating in $x$ changes the strength of the vector field $x\partial_y$. The flows therefore fail to commute by a $y$-translation.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Killing equation in flat space

Let $g=dx^2+dy^2$ on $\mathbb R^2$, and let

$$
X=x\partial_y-y\partial_x.
$$

Show that $\mathcal L_Xg=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In Cartesian coordinates, $g_{ij}=\delta_{ij}$ is constant. Therefore

$$
(\mathcal L_Xg)_{ij}
=\partial_iX_j+
\partial_jX_i.
$$

Here

$$
X^x=-y,
\qquad
X^y=x,
$$

and lowering indices does nothing because $g_{ij}=\delta_{ij}$. Thus

$$
\partial_xX_x=\partial_x(-y)=0,
\qquad
\partial_yX_y=\partial_yx=0,
$$

and

$$
\partial_xX_y+
\partial_yX_x
=\partial_xx+
\partial_y(-y)=1-1=0.
$$

All components vanish, so

$$
\mathcal L_Xg=0.
$$

The vector field generates rotations, which are isometries of the Euclidean metric.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Cartan formula for a gauge potential

Let $A$ be an abelian gauge potential and $F=dA$. Show that

$$
\mathcal L_XA=\iota_X F+d(\iota_X A).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Apply Cartan’s formula directly to the one-form $A$:

$$
\mathcal L_XA=d\iota_X A+\iota_X dA.
$$

Since $F=dA$, this becomes

$$
\mathcal L_XA=d(\iota_X A)+\iota_X F.
$$

Reordering the two terms gives

$$
\mathcal L_XA=\iota_X F+d(\iota_X A).
$$

The second term is exact, so in abelian gauge theory it has the form of a gauge transformation.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Lie derivative commutes with the exterior derivative

Use Cartan’s formula to show that

$$
\mathcal L_Xd\omega=d\mathcal L_X\omega
$$

for any differential form $\omega$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Using Cartan’s formula,

$$
\mathcal L_Xd\omega
=(d\iota_X+\iota_X d)d\omega
=d\iota_X d\omega+\iota_X d^2\omega.
$$

Since $d^2=0$,

$$
\mathcal L_Xd\omega=d\iota_X d\omega.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
d\mathcal L_X\omega
=d(d\iota_X+\iota_X d)\omega
=d^2\iota_X\omega+d\iota_X d\omega.
$$

Again $d^2=0$, so

$$
d\mathcal L_X\omega=d\iota_X d\omega.
$$

Therefore

$$
\mathcal L_Xd\omega=d\mathcal L_X\omega.
$$

</details>

## Research status

For smooth manifolds, Lie derivatives and Cartan’s formula are settled standard material. In QFT, the subtle points are not the ordinary formula but the extra structures attached to fields: gauge bundles, spin bundles, anomalies, boundaries, defects, and infinite-dimensional spaces of fields.

Whenever a page says “vary by a diffeomorphism,” “drag the background,” “preserve the metric,” “preserve the symplectic form,” or “generate a spacetime symmetry,” a Lie derivative or one of its covariant refinements is usually hiding underneath.
