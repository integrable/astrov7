## Summary

The numerical bootstrap begins by rewriting crossing symmetry as a vector equation. For an identical scalar $\phi$, the four-point function has an OPE expansion

$$
\mathcal G(u,v)=\sum_{\mathcal O\in\phi\times\phi}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v).
$$

Crossing under exchanging points $1$ and $3$ says

$$
v^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(u,v)=u^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(v,u).
$$

Substituting the conformal block expansion gives

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O\in\phi\times\phi}
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2F_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)=0,
$$

where

$$
F_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
=v^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
-u^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(v,u).
$$

This is still an equation between functions. The numerical bootstrap turns it into a vector equation by applying finitely many derivatives at the crossing-symmetric point. Each operator contributes a vector

$$
\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}
$$

in a finite-dimensional derivative space. The crossing equation becomes

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}=0.
$$

This page explains how that vector equation arises, what its entries mean, why positivity matters, and how it generalizes to global symmetries and mixed correlators.

Read [What the Numerical Bootstrap Computes](/cft-bootstrap/numerical-bootstrap/what-the-numerical-bootstrap-computes/), [Crossing Symmetry](/cft-bootstrap/crossing-bootstrap-logic/crossing-symmetry/), [Scalar Conformal Blocks](/cft-bootstrap/conformal-blocks/scalar-conformal-blocks/), and [Identical Scalar Bootstrap](/cft-bootstrap/crossing-bootstrap-logic/identical-scalar-bootstrap/) first.

## Core idea

Crossing symmetry is an equality of functions. Computers prefer finite lists of numbers. The bridge is to evaluate derivatives of the crossing equation at a special point, usually the crossing-symmetric point.

The conceptual chain is

$$
\text{crossing equation for functions}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{infinite vector equation of derivatives}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{finite truncated vector equation}.
$$

Each primary operator contributes a conformal block vector. Its coefficient is the square of an OPE coefficient. The identity contributes a fixed vector. The bootstrap asks whether a positive linear combination of allowed block vectors can cancel the identity vector.

The geometric picture is useful:

$$
-\vec F_{\mathbf1}
\quad\text{must lie in the cone generated by allowed }\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}.
$$

If it does not, the assumed spectrum is impossible.

This is the reason linear functionals appear. A linear functional is a hyperplane that separates the identity vector from the allowed cone.

## Setup and conventions

For identical scalar primaries $\phi$ of dimension $\Delta_\phi$, write

$$
\langle\phi(x_1)\phi(x_2)\phi(x_3)\phi(x_4)\rangle
=\frac{1}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\phi}x_{34}^{2\Delta_\phi}}\mathcal G(u,v),
$$

with

$$
u=\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2},
\qquad
v=\frac{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2}.
$$

It is often convenient to introduce variables $z,\bar z$ by

$$
u=z\bar z,
\qquad
v=(1-z)(1-\bar z).
$$

For Euclidean configurations, $z$ and $\bar z$ are complex conjugates or both real in a conformal frame. The crossing-symmetric point is

$$
z=\bar z=\frac12,
\qquad
u=v=\frac14.
$$

The conformal block expansion is

$$
\mathcal G(u,v)=\sum_{\mathcal O}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v),
$$

where the sum runs over primary operators in the $\phi\times\phi$ OPE. For identical bosonic scalars, only even spin $\ell$ appears in this OPE.

## Deriving the crossing equation

The same four-point function can be expanded in the $12\to34$ channel or the $32\to14$ channel. Exchanging $x_1$ and $x_3$ sends

$$
u\leftrightarrow v
$$

and changes the prefactor. The reduced correlator obeys

$$
v^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(u,v)=u^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(v,u).
$$

Move everything to one side:

$$
v^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(u,v)-u^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(v,u)=0.
$$

Now substitute the conformal block decomposition:

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2
\left[
v^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
-u^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(v,u)
\right]=0.
$$

Define

$$
F_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
=v^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
-u^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(v,u).
$$

Then crossing becomes

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2F_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)=0.
$$

This is the scalar crossing equation in the form most directly connected to numerical bootstrap.

## Separating the identity

The identity operator has

$$
\Delta=0,
\qquad
\ell=0,
\qquad
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathbf1}^2=1.
$$

Its conformal block is conventionally

$$
G_{0,0}(u,v)=1.
$$

Therefore the identity contribution to the crossing function is

$$
F_{\mathbf1}(u,v)
=v^{\Delta_\phi}-u^{\Delta_\phi}.
$$

Crossing can be written as

$$
F_{\mathbf1}(u,v)
+\sum_{\mathcal O\ne\mathbf1}
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2F_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)=0.
$$

This form is important because the identity contribution is fixed, while the non-identity terms have unknown nonnegative coefficients.

Thus the bootstrap question becomes:

$$
- F_{\mathbf1}
\quad\text{can it be expressed as a positive sum of allowed }F_{\Delta,\ell}\text{?}
$$

After truncating to derivatives, this becomes a finite-dimensional convex geometry question.

## From functions to vectors

Choose a list of derivative operators evaluated at the crossing-symmetric point. For example,

$$
\partial_z^m\partial_{\bar z}^n\big|_{z=\bar z=1/2}
$$

for selected nonnegative integers $m,n$.

Apply these derivatives to each crossing function $F_{\Delta,\ell}$. This produces a vector

$$
\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}
=
\left(
\partial_z^m\partial_{\bar z}^nF_{\Delta,\ell}\big|_{z=\bar z=1/2}
\right)_{(m,n)\in\mathcal D},
$$

where $\mathcal D$ is the chosen derivative set.

The functional crossing equation becomes the vector equation

$$
\vec F_{\mathbf1}
+\sum_{\mathcal O\ne\mathbf1}
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}=0.
$$

This is the equation that appears in the simplest numerical bootstrap. The vector dimension is the number of derivatives kept.

If the derivative set is enlarged, the vector equation captures more information from the original functional equation. The full crossing equation corresponds morally to keeping all derivatives, subject to convergence and analyticity subtleties.

## Why the symmetric point?

The crossing-symmetric point

$$
z=\bar z=\frac12
$$

is fixed by exchanging channels. Expanding around it treats the competing OPE channels symmetrically.

There are practical reasons too:

1. Conformal blocks have good convergence properties near this point in radial variables.
2. The crossing equation has definite symmetry properties under channel exchange.
3. Derivatives at this point give a natural finite-dimensional approximation.
4. Many bootstrap algorithms and block approximations are optimized for this expansion.

One can formulate bootstrap equations in other variables or at other points, but the symmetric-point derivative basis is the standard starting point.

A useful mental image is Taylor expansion:

$$
F(u,v)=0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\partial^k F\big|_{\rm symmetric}=0
\quad\text{for all }k.
$$

The numerical bootstrap keeps only finitely many of these conditions.

## Parity under crossing

The crossing function

$$
F_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
=v^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
-u^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(v,u)
$$

is antisymmetric under exchanging $u$ and $v$:

$$
F_{\Delta,\ell}(v,u)=-F_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v).
$$

Therefore only derivatives with the appropriate parity under the exchange carry independent information. In practical variables, one often chooses coordinates adapted to the crossing transformation and keeps derivatives with the correct parity.

This reduces redundancy. It also improves numerical conditioning.

The details depend on the variable choice. The invariant lesson is that the vector components should span independent crossing constraints, not duplicate the same equation in disguised form.

## Positivity and cones

In a unitary identical-scalar setup,

$$
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\ge0.
$$

Thus the non-identity side of crossing is a positive linear combination of block vectors:

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O\ne\mathbf1}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}.
$$

The set of all positive linear combinations of allowed block vectors is a convex cone. Crossing requires

$$
-\vec F_{\mathbf1}\in \operatorname{Cone}\{\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}\text{ allowed}\}.
$$

If this membership fails, the spectrum is excluded.

This cone picture is the geometric heart of the bootstrap. Linear functionals are dual vectors. A functional $\alpha$ separates a point from a cone if

$$
\alpha(\vec F_{\Delta,\ell})\ge0
$$

on the cone but

$$
\alpha(-\vec F_{\mathbf1})<0.
$$

Equivalently, one often writes the sign convention with $\alpha(\vec F_{\mathbf1})>0$.

## A simple gap problem

Suppose we want to test whether the first scalar after the identity has dimension at least $\Delta_{\rm gap}$. The assumed allowed spectrum is:

- scalar operators with $\Delta\ge\Delta_{\rm gap}$;
- spin-$\ell$ operators with $\ell>0$ satisfying the unitarity bound;
- only even spins for identical scalar external operators;
- the stress tensor at $\Delta=d$, $\ell=2$, if one includes it explicitly or allows it through the spin-two sector.

A functional excluding this gap must satisfy positivity for all these allowed operators:

$$
\alpha(\vec F_{\Delta,0})\ge0
\qquad
\Delta\ge\Delta_{\rm gap},
$$

and

$$
\alpha(\vec F_{\Delta,\ell})\ge0
\qquad
\Delta\ge \ell+d-2,\quad \ell=2,4,6,\ldots .
$$

If such a functional exists with the right identity sign, then the gap is too large. Therefore crossing forces a scalar below $\Delta_{\rm gap}$.

Scanning $\Delta_{\rm gap}$ produces an upper bound on the first scalar dimension.

## Global symmetry vector equations

With global symmetry, the four-point function decomposes into representation channels. For example, in an $O(N)$ model,

$$
\phi_i\times\phi_j
$$

decomposes into singlet, traceless symmetric, and antisymmetric sectors:

$$
S,\qquad T,\qquad A.
$$

Crossing is no longer one scalar equation. It becomes a vector equation in the space of independent tensor structures:

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O\in S}\lambda_{\mathcal O}^2\vec F^{S}_{\Delta,\ell}
+\sum_{\mathcal O\in T}\lambda_{\mathcal O}^2\vec F^{T}_{\Delta,\ell}
+\sum_{\mathcal O\in A}\lambda_{\mathcal O}^2\vec F^{A}_{\Delta,\ell}=0.
$$

Each representation sector has its own spin selection rules and unitarity constraints. For instance, the conserved $O(N)$ current is in the antisymmetric sector with

$$
\Delta_J=d-1,
\qquad
\ell=1.
$$

The stress tensor is in the singlet sector with

$$
\Delta_T=d,
\qquad
\ell=2.
$$

Global symmetry therefore enriches the vector equation but does not change the basic logic: crossing is a sum of positive OPE data times known block vectors.

## Mixed correlator systems

Mixed correlator systems include several external operators, such as $\sigma$ and $\epsilon$ in the 3D Ising bootstrap. One studies correlators like

$$
\langle\sigma\sigma\sigma\sigma\rangle,
\qquad
\langle\sigma\sigma\epsilon\epsilon\rangle,
\qquad
\langle\epsilon\epsilon\epsilon\epsilon\rangle.
$$

Now OPE coefficients appear in matrices. For example, an even operator $\mathcal O$ may couple both to $\sigma\sigma$ and to $\epsilon\epsilon$:

$$
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\mathcal O},
\qquad
\lambda_{\epsilon\epsilon\mathcal O}.
$$

The contribution is built from an outer product

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\mathcal O}\\
\lambda_{\epsilon\epsilon\mathcal O}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\mathcal O}&
\lambda_{\epsilon\epsilon\mathcal O}
\end{pmatrix},
$$

which is positive semidefinite.

This is why mixed-correlator bootstrap leads naturally to semidefinite programming rather than ordinary linear programming. The vector equation becomes a matrix-valued positivity problem.

## Spinning operators

For spinning external operators, crossing involves tensor structures. A four-point function can have several independent tensor structures before any OPE decomposition is performed. Conformal blocks become matrices or collections of functions relating external tensor structures to exchanged representations.

The schematic form remains recognizable:

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O}\text{OPE data}\times\text{block vector or block matrix}=0.
$$

But the OPE data are not always simple squares. They may be vectors of three-point coefficients subject to positivity constraints. Conservation equations, parity, global symmetry, and permutation symmetry all affect the block vectors.

The scalar case is the clean training ground. Modern bootstrap applications often require the full spinning version.

## Finite truncation

The exact vector equation would include infinitely many derivatives and infinitely many spins. Numerically one uses cutoffs. A typical truncation includes derivatives with total order up to some $\Lambda$:

$$
m+n\le\Lambda
$$

subject to crossing parity constraints.

One also treats spin in a finite or asymptotic way. Low spins are included explicitly, while large spin may be bounded using analytic estimates or numerical positivity methods.

At finite truncation, exclusion is only as strong as the chosen search space. Increasing $\Lambda$ usually strengthens bounds. A trustworthy result should show stability as the truncation is improved.

This is why plots often label bounds by $\Lambda$. The label is not decoration; it is part of the result.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not confuse the function equation with the vector equation.** The vector equation is a derivative-truncated representation of crossing, not a different physical principle.

**Do not forget the identity term.** The identity contribution is fixed and is usually the vector that the positive cone must cancel.

**Do not assume positivity without unitarity.** The cone picture depends on nonnegative OPE squares or positive semidefinite OPE matrices.

**Do not use identical-scalar spin selection for mixed correlators.** Mixed external operators can allow odd spins and different representation sectors.

**Do not ignore global symmetry tensors.** With global symmetry, crossing is a vector equation in tensor-structure space as well as derivative space.

**Do not treat finite derivative order as exact crossing.** It is a controlled approximation whose convergence must be checked.

**Do not assume every functional convention has the same signs.** Some papers put the identity on the other side or reverse the sign of $F_{\Delta,\ell}$. The exclusion logic is invariant.

## Research status

The vector-equation formulation of crossing is established and standard. It is the entry point to numerical bootstrap algorithms based on linear and semidefinite optimization.

Active research improves how these equations are generated and solved for complicated systems: spinning external operators, defects and boundaries, supersymmetric multiplets, gauge-theory operators, mixed correlators with many sectors, nonunitary theories, and algorithms that search high-dimensional spaces more efficiently.

### Exercise 2

What is the identity contribution $F_{\mathbf1}(u,v)$ in the convention of this page?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The identity block is

$$
G_{0,0}(u,v)=1,
$$

and its OPE coefficient is fixed to one in the unit-normalized scalar convention. Thus

$$
F_{\mathbf1}(u,v)
=v^{\Delta_\phi}-u^{\Delta_\phi}.
$$

### Exercise 3

Explain the cone interpretation of the vector equation.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The vector crossing equation is

$$
\vec F_{\mathbf1}+\sum_{\mathcal O\ne\mathbf1}\lambda_{\mathcal O}^2\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}=0.
$$

In a unitary identical-scalar setup,

$$
\lambda_{\mathcal O}^2\ge0.
$$

Therefore the non-identity sum is a positive linear combination of allowed block vectors. The set of all such positive linear combinations is a convex cone. Crossing requires

$$
-\vec F_{\mathbf1}
$$

to lie inside that cone. If a linear functional separates $-\vec F_{\mathbf1}$ from the cone, the assumed spectrum is excluded.

</details>

### Exercise 4

Why do mixed correlators lead to matrix positivity?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In a mixed system with external operators such as $\sigma$ and $\epsilon$, a single exchanged operator can couple to several pairs of external operators:

$$
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\mathcal O},
\qquad
\lambda_{\epsilon\epsilon\mathcal O}.
$$

Its contribution involves products of these coefficients. These products form an outer-product matrix:

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\mathcal O}\\
\lambda_{\epsilon\epsilon\mathcal O}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\mathcal O}&
\lambda_{\epsilon\epsilon\mathcal O}
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

An outer product of a real vector with itself is positive semidefinite. Therefore the positivity condition is matrix positivity rather than positivity of a single number.

</details>

### Exercise 5

Why is increasing the derivative cutoff expected to strengthen a bound?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A larger derivative cutoff gives a larger space of linear functionals. Since exclusion is achieved by finding a functional with certain positivity properties, enlarging the search space can find all previous functionals plus possibly new stronger ones. Therefore bounds usually become stronger or stabilize as the cutoff increases. If a feature is unstable under increasing cutoff, it should be treated cautiously.

</details>
