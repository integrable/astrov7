## Summary

The extremal functional method is the bridge between **excluding theories** and **guessing the data of a theory on the boundary of exclusion**.

In a unitary four-point bootstrap problem, crossing can often be written schematically as

$$
T=\sum_{a} p_a V_a,
\qquad p_a\ge 0.
$$

Here $T$ is the target vector fixed by the identity operator and kinematics, $V_a$ are conformal-block vectors for allowed exchanged primaries, and $p_a$ are positive OPE-squared coefficients or positive semidefinite OPE matrices. A linear functional $\alpha$ excludes a proposed assumption if it separates $T$ from the positive cone generated by the $V_a$.

At an optimal bound, the separating functional becomes tangent to the cone. If

$$
\alpha(T)=0,
\qquad
\alpha(V_a)\ge 0,
$$

then any exchanged operator that participates in a boundary solution with positive coefficient must satisfy

$$
\alpha(V_a)=0.
$$

The zeros of the extremal functional are therefore candidate operator dimensions and spins. Solving the truncated crossing equation on this zero set gives candidate OPE coefficients. That is the core idea.

The method is powerful, but it is not magic. A spectrum extracted from a finite derivative order is evidence for a CFT, not by itself a proof that the CFT exists. The real craft is to combine extremal spectra with stability checks, mixed correlators, Ward identities, known symmetry sectors, and independent physics.

## Core idea

The bootstrap has two complementary modes.

The first mode is **exclusion**. We assume a spectrum pattern, a set of gaps, or a trial value of external dimensions. Then we search for a functional proving that the corresponding crossing equation has no positive solution.

The second mode is **reconstruction**. We tune the assumptions until exclusion just fails. Near this boundary, the optimal functional is no longer just a certificate of impossibility. It is a microscope. Its zero set points to the spectrum of a candidate theory that would saturate the bound.

In finite-dimensional convex geometry this is ordinary support-hyperplane logic. Suppose $C$ is a cone and $T$ lies on its boundary. A supporting functional $\alpha$ satisfies

$$
\alpha(T)=0,
\qquad
\alpha(C)\ge 0.
$$

If $T$ is decomposed using vectors in $C$,

$$
T=\sum_i p_i V_i,
\qquad p_i>0,
$$

then

$$
0=\alpha(T)=\sum_i p_i\alpha(V_i).
$$

Since each term is nonnegative, every active vector must obey $\alpha(V_i)=0$.

That one-line argument is the heart of the extremal functional method. In the conformal bootstrap, the cone is generated by conformal-block vectors, and the active vectors are the primary operators appearing in the relevant OPE channel.

## Setup and conventions

We use the conventions of [Bootstrap Equations](/cft-bootstrap/crossing-bootstrap-logic/bootstrap-equations/). For identical scalar external operators $\phi$, the crossing equation is schematically

$$
V_{\mathbf 1}+\sum_{\mathcal O\in\phi\times\phi}p_{\mathcal O}V_{\Delta,\ell}=0,
\qquad
p_{\mathcal O}=\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\ge 0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
T\equiv -V_{\mathbf 1}
=\sum_{\mathcal O}p_{\mathcal O}V_{\Delta,\ell}.
$$

A bootstrap computation does not work with the full infinite-dimensional function space. It projects the equation to a finite-dimensional space of derivatives at a crossing-symmetric point, or to some equivalent finite basis. A linear functional is then a vector of derivative coefficients,

$$
\alpha[f]
=\sum_{m+n\le \Lambda}a_{mn}\,\partial_z^m\partial_{\bar z}^n f(z,\bar z)\Big|_{z=\bar z=1/2},
$$

with parity restrictions or other basis choices depending on the problem. The truncation parameter $\Lambda$ is often called the derivative order.

:::note[Sign conventions]
Different bootstrap papers put the identity on different sides of the equation. Some normalize functionals by $\alpha(V_{\mathbf 1})=1$; others use $\alpha(T)=-1$ or a closely related condition. The geometry is invariant: an exclusion functional separates the target vector from the positive cone of allowed conformal-block vectors.
:::

For a single scalar curve at fixed spin, it is useful to view

$$
f_\ell(\Delta)=\alpha(V_{\Delta,\ell})
$$

as a function of the exchanged dimension. Positivity means

$$
f_\ell(\Delta)\ge 0
$$

for every $\Delta$ above the relevant unitarity bound and any imposed gap. Active operators in an extremal solution appear at zeros of $f_\ell$.

## From a bound to a spectrum

Consider a common task: fix $\Delta_\phi$ and ask how large the lowest scalar singlet dimension $\Delta_S$ can be. One imposes a trial gap,

$$
\Delta_{S,0}\ge \Delta_{\mathrm{gap}},
$$

and searches for a functional excluding all spectra satisfying that gap. If such a functional exists, the gap is too high. Lower the gap until exclusion becomes impossible. The maximal allowed gap is a numerical upper bound.

Near the optimal gap, the extremal functional has a characteristic zero pattern:

$$
\alpha(V_{\Delta_{S,0},0})=0,
\qquad
\alpha(V_{\Delta_{S,1},0})=0,
\qquad
\alpha(V_{\Delta_T,2})=0,
\quad \ldots
$$

The first zero often sits at the assumed gap. Later zeros estimate higher scalar, spin-two, spin-four, and higher-spin dimensions. The identity is already accounted for in the target $T$.

If a zero is isolated in the interior of an allowed continuum, nonnegativity usually forces it to be a double zero:

$$
\alpha(V_{\Delta_*,\ell})=0,
\qquad
\partial_\Delta\alpha(V_{\Delta,\ell})\big|_{\Delta=\Delta_*}=0.
$$

This is the same reason a nonnegative smooth function touching zero at an interior point has vanishing first derivative. Boundary zeros, such as a zero exactly at an imposed gap or a unitarity bound, need not be double zeros.

The final step is to solve for OPE coefficients. If the zeros suggest a finite set of exchanged primaries $\{\mathcal O_i\}$, we solve the truncated linear system

$$
T\approx \sum_i p_i V_i,
\qquad p_i\ge 0.
$$

In practice, the number of candidate operators and the number of equations must be balanced. One does not trust a single solve blindly. One checks stability as $\Lambda$ increases, as the zero-finding tolerance changes, and as additional channels or correlators are included.

## Why zeros know about operators

The zero condition has a simple physical interpretation. The functional is designed to be nonnegative on every operator allowed by the assumptions. If crossing is saturated, all positive OPE-weighted contributions must conspire to reproduce the target. A strictly positive contribution would push the functional value positive, so every operator with nonzero OPE coefficient must lie in the functional kernel.

This is why the method is most useful on the boundary of an allowed region. Deep inside an allowed region there is no unique supporting functional, and there may be many spectra compatible with the same external data. Far outside the allowed region a functional only says “no”; its zeros need not describe a nearby theory.

The method is also why kinks in bounds are so tantalizing. A sharp kink often suggests that the boundary switches from one extremal spectrum to another, or that a physically important CFT lies where several constraints become simultaneously tight. Tantalizing, yes. A theorem, no. A kink is a geometric feature of a finite or limiting bootstrap problem; it becomes physical only after additional evidence identifies a CFT there.

## OPE coefficients and normalization

The zeros give candidate dimensions and spins. OPE coefficients require a second step.

Suppose the candidate exchanged primaries are $\mathcal O_i$ with vectors $V_i$. A truncated crossing solve gives

$$
T=\sum_i p_i V_i+\text{residual},
\qquad
p_i=\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O_i}^2.
$$

A good extremal solution should have small residual in the chosen derivative basis and positive $p_i$. If the residual is large, or if important coefficients are negative, the zero list is incomplete or the extraction is unstable.

The normalization of $p_i$ is tied to the normalization of two-point functions and conformal blocks. If scalar primaries are unit-normalized,

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}},
$$

then $p_i=\lambda_{\phi\phi i}^2$ for identical real scalar external operators. If the two-point metric is not diagonal or not unit-normalized, then the positive object is really

$$
\lambda_{\phi\phi i}\,G^{ij}\,\lambda_{\phi\phi j}.
$$

For mixed correlators, $p_i$ is replaced by a positive semidefinite matrix of OPE coefficients. The extremal condition becomes a kernel condition for a matrix-valued polynomial, not merely a zero of a scalar function.

## Mixed correlators and matrix kernels

For several external operators, crossing equations have the form

$$
T=\sum_{\mathcal O} \mathbf \Lambda_{\mathcal O}^{\mathrm T}\,\boldsymbol V_{\mathcal O}\,\mathbf \Lambda_{\mathcal O},
$$

where $\mathbf \Lambda_{\mathcal O}$ is a vector of OPE coefficients involving different external pairs, and $\boldsymbol V_{\mathcal O}$ is a matrix of conformal-block vectors. A functional acts entrywise and produces a numerical matrix

$$
\alpha(\boldsymbol V_{\mathcal O}).
$$

Positivity means

$$
\alpha(\boldsymbol V_{\mathcal O})\succeq 0.
$$

An active exchanged operator must have an OPE vector lying in the kernel:

$$
\alpha(\boldsymbol V_{\mathcal O})\,\mathbf \Lambda_{\mathcal O}=0.
$$

Thus the mixed-correlator extremal functional gives more than dimensions. It can estimate ratios of OPE coefficients from null vectors of these matrices. This is one reason mixed-correlator bootstrap can produce islands: the same exchanged operator must satisfy several crossing equations at once, and its OPE vector must be compatible across them.

This extra structure is also why extraction becomes more delicate. One must track degeneracies, representation labels, normalization choices, and matrix conditioning. A beautiful island can still hide a numerically grumpy linear algebra problem. The bootstrap gremlins are rarely gone; they just wear nicer coordinates.

## What the method outputs

A responsible extremal-functional analysis separates several layers of output.

| Output | Meaning | Main caveat |
|---|---|---|
| Zero locations | Candidate exchanged dimensions and spins. | Spurious zeros can appear at finite derivative order. |
| Kernel vectors | Candidate OPE-ratio data in mixed systems. | Degenerate operators and basis choices can obscure interpretation. |
| Linear solve coefficients | Candidate OPE-squared coefficients or OPE matrices. | Sensitive to missing operators and normalization. |
| Stability under increasing $\Lambda$ | Evidence that the candidate data approximate a limiting solution. | Slow convergence can mimic instability, especially near accumulation regions. |
| Agreement with Ward identities or known data | Physics validation. | Agreement checks consistency but does not prove uniqueness. |

The safest language is “the extremal spectrum suggests,” “the extracted data are consistent with,” or “the finite-$\Lambda$ solution appears to converge to.” Reserve “proves” for certified exclusion statements, and even then state the assumptions.

## A small example in words

Imagine the identical-scalar bootstrap for a $\mathbb Z_2$-odd scalar $\sigma$. The OPE $\sigma\times\sigma$ contains even operators, including the identity, the leading even scalar $\epsilon$, the stress tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$, and higher operators.

A gap bound for the first even scalar asks whether the theory can have

$$
\Delta_\epsilon\ge \Delta_{\mathrm{gap}}.
$$

At the maximal allowed $\Delta_{\mathrm{gap}}$, an extremal functional often has zeros near

$$
\Delta_\epsilon,
\quad
\Delta_T=d,
\quad
\Delta_{\epsilon'},
\quad
\Delta_{\ell=4},
\quad\ldots
$$

The stress-tensor zero is especially meaningful if the assumptions require a local CFT with a conserved stress tensor. Its OPE coefficient is tied to $C_T$ by a Ward identity. If the extracted stress-tensor coefficient is wildly inconsistent with the Ward normalization, something is wrong with the extraction, the normalization, or the assumptions.

## Checks that make extraction credible

The most important checks are boring and therefore excellent.

First, increase the derivative order. Candidate low-lying dimensions should stabilize. High-lying dimensions are expected to drift more.

Second, change the basis. A spectrum that appears only in one derivative basis and evaporates in radial coordinates is not yet physics.

Third, include known protected or conserved operators. Stress tensors, global currents, and symmetry selection rules are not optional decorations.

Fourth, verify positivity of extracted coefficients. A few tiny negative numbers may reflect numerical conditioning; large negative OPE-squared coefficients are a flare gun.

Fifth, compare with independent methods when available: exact 2D data, perturbation theory, large-$N$ expansions, Monte Carlo, lattice simulations, supersymmetric localization, or known Ward identities.

Sixth, track assumptions. The extremal spectrum is the spectrum of a candidate theory saturating **your** assumptions, not of reality in the abstract.

## Common pitfalls

**Confusing an excluded point with a spectrum.** A functional deep in the excluded region is a certificate of nonexistence under assumptions. Its zeros are not automatically a nearby CFT.

**Reading every zero as a physical operator.** Finite-dimensional approximations can produce spurious zeros. Low-lying stable zeros are much more meaningful than late noisy zeros.

**Forgetting degeneracy.** If two primaries have the same spin and nearly the same dimension, a scalar zero may represent a combined contribution. Mixed correlators may separate them; a single correlator may not.

**Ignoring imposed gaps.** A zero at an imposed gap can be a boundary artifact. It may indicate the operator saturating the assumption, but it is less diagnostic than a stable interior zero.

**Mistaking unit-normalized and physical stress tensors.** The stress tensor’s OPE coefficient depends on the normalization convention for $T_{\mu\nu}$ and $C_T$. Ward identities fix the physical combination, not every convention-dependent symbol.

**Treating finite precision as exact geometry.** Semidefinite programs can be ill-conditioned. Precision settings, rational approximations, and solver tolerances matter.

## Research status

The extremal functional method is an established part of the numerical bootstrap toolkit. It helped turn bootstrap bounds from “allowed versus excluded” plots into approximate spectra and OPE data, and it remains a useful diagnostic of candidate CFTs near sharp boundaries or islands.

The limitations are equally important. At finite derivative order, an extremal spectrum is not a rigorous construction of a CFT. It is a candidate solution to a truncated crossing problem. The method is strongest when its extracted data converge with increasing truncation and agree with independent constraints.

Modern high-precision bootstrap work often combines extremal-functional ideas with mixed correlators, semidefinite programming, navigator functions, cutting-surface methods, skydive-type searches, and rigorous numerical certification. The conceptual lesson remains the same: boundary functionals are not merely walls; they can also be windows.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Active operators lie at zeros

Let $T=\sum_i p_iV_i$ with $p_i>0$. Suppose a functional $\alpha$ satisfies

$$
\alpha(T)=0,
\qquad
\alpha(V_i)\ge 0
$$

for every $i$. Show that $\alpha(V_i)=0$ for every active operator.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Apply $\alpha$ to the decomposition:

$$
0=\alpha(T)=\sum_i p_i\alpha(V_i).
$$

Each coefficient $p_i$ is strictly positive and each number $\alpha(V_i)$ is nonnegative. A sum of nonnegative terms can vanish only if every term vanishes. Hence

$$
\alpha(V_i)=0
$$

for each active vector.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Why interior zeros are often double zeros

Let $f(\Delta)$ be smooth and nonnegative on an open interval. If $f(\Delta_*)=0$ for an interior point $\Delta_*$, show that $f'(\Delta_*)=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Since $f(\Delta)\ge0$ and $f(\Delta_*)=0$, the point $\Delta_*$ is a local minimum. A differentiable function has zero first derivative at an interior local minimum. Therefore

$$
f'(\Delta_*)=0.
$$

In bootstrap language, a nonnegative functional curve $\alpha(V_{\Delta,\ell})$ that touches zero at an interior exchanged dimension usually has a double zero. Zeros at imposed boundaries, such as a gap threshold, need not be double.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Matrix version of the zero condition

Suppose a mixed-correlator crossing equation contains a positive contribution

$$
\mathbf \Lambda^{\mathrm T}\boldsymbol V\mathbf \Lambda.
$$

A functional $\alpha$ gives a positive semidefinite matrix $M=\alpha(\boldsymbol V)\succeq0$. If the active contribution must vanish under $\alpha$, show that $M\mathbf \Lambda=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The contribution to the functional is

$$
\mathbf \Lambda^{\mathrm T}M\mathbf \Lambda.
$$

Because $M\succeq0$, this quadratic form is nonnegative. If it vanishes, then $M^{1/2}\mathbf \Lambda=0$, where $M^{1/2}$ is the positive semidefinite square root. Multiplying by $M^{1/2}$ again gives

$$
M\mathbf \Lambda=0.
$$

Thus the active OPE vector lies in the kernel of the functional matrix.

</details>
