## Summary

A conformal-bootstrap statement is never just “crossing says no.” It is always

$$
\text{crossing} + \text{unitarity} + \text{symmetry} + \text{assumptions}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{allowed or excluded data}.
$$

The assumptions are not decorative. They decide what operators are allowed to appear, which OPE coefficients must be positive, which sectors are present, and where spectral gaps begin. A **gap assumption** says that after listing some low-lying operators in a sector, no other primary in that sector appears below a chosen dimension.

A typical statement has the form

$$
\Delta_{\mathcal O}\ge \Delta_{\mathrm{gap}}^{(R,\ell)}
\qquad
\text{for all unlisted primaries in sector }(R,\ell),
$$

where $R$ is a global-symmetry representation and $\ell$ is spin. If the assumed gap is too large, crossing may become impossible. If it is too small, the bootstrap may become too weak to say anything sharp.

This page explains how to state assumptions cleanly, how gaps enter bootstrap equations, why assumptions must be audited, and how to read conditional numerical results without overclaiming. The moral is simple: a bootstrap bound is only as meaningful as the assumptions written above it.

## Core idea

The bootstrap starts from equations such as

$$
\vec F_{\mathbf 1}(p)+
\sum_{\mathcal O\in\mathcal S}
\lambda_{\mathcal O}^2\vec F_{\mathcal O}(p)=0,
\qquad
\lambda_{\mathcal O}^2\ge0,
$$

where $p$ denotes external data such as $\Delta_\phi$, $\Delta_\sigma$, $\Delta_\epsilon$, OPE-ratio data, or global-symmetry parameters. The set $\mathcal S$ is not automatic. It is chosen by the physical problem.

For example, in an identical real-scalar problem, Bose symmetry says that only even-spin primaries appear in $\phi\times\phi$. In a $\mathbb Z_2$-symmetric Ising-like problem, $\sigma\times\sigma$ contains $\mathbb Z_2$-even operators, while $\sigma\times\epsilon$ contains $\mathbb Z_2$-odd operators. In an $O(N)$ problem, exchanged operators are organized into singlet, traceless-symmetric, and antisymmetric representations. These are assumptions about the operator algebra.

A spectral gap is a further restriction on $\mathcal S$. Schematically,

$$
\mathcal S_{\,\text{assumed}}
=
\mathcal S_{\text{listed}}
\cup
\left\{(\Delta,\ell,R):\Delta\ge\Delta_{\mathrm{gap}}^{(\ell,R)}\right\}.
$$

Here $\mathcal S_{\text{listed}}$ contains operators whose existence, spin, representation, or dimension is being fixed or scanned. The continuum part says what else is allowed.

The key point is that bootstrap logic is conditional:

$$
\text{no solution with these assumptions}
\ne
\text{no CFT exists}.
$$

It means only that no CFT exists with that exact list of symmetry, unitarity, and gap assumptions.

## Setup and conventions

We use the CFT conventions of [Conventions and Notation](/cft-bootstrap/conventions/). In Euclidean signature,

$$
x_{ij}^2=(x_i-x_j)^2,
$$

and scalar four-point functions are written in terms of cross-ratios

$$
U=z\bar z,
\qquad
V=(1-z)(1-\bar z).
$$

For a unit-normalized real scalar primary,

$$
\langle \phi(x)\phi(0)\rangle=\frac{1}{(x^2)^{\Delta_\phi}},
$$

the identical-scalar bootstrap equation can be written as

$$
\vec F_{\mathbf 1}
+
\sum_{\mathcal O\ne\mathbf 1}
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}=0.
$$

The symbol $\vec F_{\Delta,\ell}$ denotes the vector of crossing functions after choosing a finite derivative basis or a functional space. In an exact analytic discussion, it denotes the full function of cross-ratios. In a numerical discussion, it denotes its projection into a finite-dimensional search space.

For mixed correlators, the scalar coefficient $\lambda_{\mathcal O}^2$ is replaced by a positive semidefinite matrix or outer product of OPE-coefficient vectors. The same language of sectors and gaps still applies.

## What a gap means

A **gap** is a lower bound on the dimension of unlisted primaries in a specific sector. It is not the dimension of the first operator unless one has already specified which lower operators are being listed separately.

For example, saying

$$
\Delta_{S,0}\ge 3
$$

is ambiguous until the sector is defined. It might mean

| Statement | Meaning |
|---|---|
| No scalar singlet below $3$ except the identity | The first nontrivial scalar singlet has dimension at least $3$. |
| No second scalar singlet below $3$ | One scalar singlet, usually called $\epsilon$, is allowed below $3$, but the next one is not. |
| No relevant scalar singlet other than a listed one | A physical assumption about relevant deformations. |
| No scalar singlet below $3$ in an even sector | A symmetry-refined assumption, not a spin-only assumption. |

These statements are different. Confusing them is one of the fastest ways to misread a bootstrap plot.

A clean gap statement should specify four pieces of information:

| Ingredient | Question |
|---|---|
| Sector | Which spin, global representation, parity, charge, or tensor structure is being constrained? |
| Listed exceptions | Which known or scanned operators are allowed below the gap? |
| Threshold | What value of $\Delta_{\mathrm{gap}}$ is assumed? |
| Scope | Is the gap exact, a scan variable, a numerical trial, or a physical hypothesis from another method? |

A good page, paper, or computation should make all four visible.

## Common kinds of assumptions

Bootstrap assumptions come in several layers. Some are structural, some are physical, and some are numerical.

### Structural assumptions

Structural assumptions define the theory class:

| Assumption | Example | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Spacetime dimension | $d=3$ | Determines the conformal algebra and blocks. |
| Unitarity | Reflection positivity | Gives positive OPE weights and unitarity bounds. |
| Locality | OPE associativity and crossing | Makes different OPE channels agree. |
| Global symmetry | $\mathbb Z_2$, $O(N)$, supersymmetry | Splits operator sectors and crossing equations. |
| External operators | $\sigma$, $\epsilon$, $\phi_i$ | Chooses which correlators are bootstrapped. |
| Two-point normalization | Unit-normalized primaries | Fixes the meaning of OPE coefficients. |

These are not optional once the problem is chosen. They are part of the definition of the bootstrap setup.

### Spectral assumptions

Spectral assumptions restrict the operator dimensions:

| Assumption | Example | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Scalar gap | $\Delta_{S,0}'\ge d$ | Exclude extra relevant singlets. |
| Spin gap | no spin-$\ell$ operator below a threshold | Isolate sparse spectra or large-$N$-like behavior. |
| Charge-sector gap | no charged scalar below a threshold | Constrain global-symmetry sectors. |
| Twist gap | $\tau=\Delta-\ell\ge\tau_{\mathrm{gap}}$ | Useful in analytic and Lorentzian bootstrap. |
| Unique low operator | exactly one scalar in a sector below a threshold | Turn broad allowed regions into smaller regions. |

A gap can be scanned. For example, in the identical scalar bootstrap, one asks: for fixed $\Delta_\phi$, how large can the gap to the first scalar in $\phi\times\phi$ be? The answer is an upper bound on the possible scalar gap.

### OPE and Ward assumptions

Some assumptions involve coefficients rather than dimensions. Examples include:

$$
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}=\lambda_{\sigma\epsilon\sigma}
$$

when operator orderings and normalizations imply the equality, or Ward-identity relations that fix $\lambda_{\mathcal O\mathcal O T}$ in terms of $\Delta_\mathcal O$ and $C_T$.

These assumptions are powerful but convention-sensitive. A wrong two-point normalization changes the numerical value of an OPE coefficient. A wrong stress-tensor normalization changes the relation between $C_T$ and $\lambda_{\mathcal O\mathcal O T}$. The physics is invariant; the printed number is not.

### Numerical assumptions

Numerical bootstrap computations also require computational choices:

| Choice | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Derivative order | How many derivatives of crossing are used. |
| Spin cutoff or tail treatment | How the infinite spin sum is represented. |
| Block approximation | How conformal blocks are approximated. |
| Precision | Arithmetic precision and solver tolerances. |
| Feasibility threshold | Criterion for declaring a point allowed or excluded. |

These are not physical assumptions, but they affect the displayed finite-computation region. A mature numerical claim separates physical assumptions from numerical implementation choices.

## How gaps enter exclusion proofs

Suppose we test a proposed external parameter point $p$ and an assumed spectrum set $\mathcal S_{\text{assumed}}$. A linear functional $\alpha$ excludes the point if

$$
\alpha(\vec F_{\mathbf 1}(p))>0,
$$

and

$$
\alpha(\vec F_{\Delta,\ell,R}(p))\ge0
\qquad
\text{for every allowed }(\Delta,\ell,R)\in\mathcal S_{\text{assumed}}.
$$

Then applying $\alpha$ to the crossing equation gives

$$
0
=
\alpha(\vec F_{\mathbf 1})+
\sum_{\mathcal O}p_{\mathcal O}\alpha(\vec F_{\mathcal O})
>
0,
$$

where $p_{\mathcal O}\ge0$. This contradiction proves that the assumed spectrum cannot solve crossing.

A gap changes the set of inequalities that $\alpha$ must satisfy. A larger gap removes some vectors from the cone. Removing vectors can make separation easier. That is why stronger gap assumptions tend to produce stronger bounds.

Geometrically,

$$
-\vec F_{\mathbf 1}
\in
\operatorname{Cone}\{\vec F_{\Delta,\ell,R}: (\Delta,\ell,R)\in\mathcal S_{\text{assumed}}\}
$$

means the point is allowed by this bootstrap test. A separating functional proves that $-\vec F_{\mathbf 1}$ lies outside the cone.

## Monotonicity of assumptions

Let $A$ be a set of assumptions and let $A'$ be a stronger set of assumptions. “Stronger” means every spectrum allowed by $A'$ is also allowed by $A$.

Then the exact allowed sets obey

$$
\mathcal R(A')\subseteq \mathcal R(A).
$$

This has two useful consequences.

First, if a point is excluded under weak assumptions, it remains excluded under stronger assumptions.

Second, if a point is allowed under strong assumptions, it is also allowed under weaker assumptions.

The converses are false. A point allowed under weak assumptions may be excluded after adding a gap. A point excluded under strong assumptions may become allowed after relaxing a gap.

This monotonicity is the conceptual reason gap scans are useful. They let us ask how strong an assumption must be before crossing becomes impossible.

## Examples

### Identical real scalar

In a unitary CFT with one real scalar primary $\phi$, Bose symmetry allows only even-spin primaries in $\phi\times\phi$ when the exchanged operators are symmetric under $1\leftrightarrow2$. A common scalar-gap question is:

> Given $\Delta_\phi$, how large can the dimension of the first scalar primary in $\phi\times\phi$ be?

This gives an upper-bound curve. Kinks on such curves often signal interesting CFTs, but a kink is not by itself a theorem that a CFT exists there.

### Ising-like mixed correlators

For a $\mathbb Z_2$-symmetric theory with odd scalar $\sigma$ and even scalar $\epsilon$, the OPEs have schematic sectors

$$
\sigma\times\sigma\sim \mathbb Z_2\text{-even},
\qquad
\sigma\times\epsilon\sim \mathbb Z_2\text{-odd},
\qquad
\epsilon\times\epsilon\sim \mathbb Z_2\text{-even}.
$$

To isolate the three-dimensional Ising CFT, one often assumes that $\sigma$ is the only relevant odd scalar and $\epsilon$ is the only relevant even scalar. In compact notation,

$$
\Delta_{\sigma'}\ge d,
\qquad
\Delta_{\epsilon'}\ge d,
$$

where primes mean “next scalar in the same symmetry sector.” These are not consequences of crossing alone. They are physical assumptions defining the target island.

### Global-symmetry sectors

For an $O(N)$ vector $\phi_i$, the OPE decomposes as

$$
\phi_i\times\phi_j
\sim
S^+\oplus T^+\oplus A^-.
$$

Here $S$ is singlet, $T$ is traceless symmetric, $A$ is antisymmetric, and the superscript indicates spin parity in the identical-vector OPE. A gap in the singlet sector is not a gap in the traceless-symmetric sector. Each representation needs its own assumption.

### Conserved currents and stress tensors

A continuous global symmetry implies a conserved spin-one current with

$$
\Delta_J=d-1.
$$

Every local CFT has a conserved stress tensor with

$$
\Delta_T=d.
$$

One should not impose a gap that accidentally removes these protected operators. Instead, they should be listed explicitly and then gaps should apply to the next operators in the same sector.

## Assumption audit

Before trusting a bootstrap conclusion, ask the following questions.

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Which external operators were included? | A four-point system only sees sectors reachable from those OPEs. |
| Which global symmetry was assumed? | Symmetry changes the crossing equations and positivity conditions. |
| Which operators were listed below gaps? | A “gap” without exceptions is usually ambiguous. |
| Are gaps relevant, marginal, spin-specific, or representation-specific? | Different sector labels define different problems. |
| Are OPE coefficients fixed, scanned, or bounded? | Coefficient assumptions can be as strong as dimension assumptions. |
| Is the computation exact, finite-derivative, or approximate? | Numerical allowed regions depend on search strength. |
| Are there independent checks? | Perturbation theory, Monte Carlo, large-$N$, supersymmetry, or exact results can justify assumptions. |

This audit is not bureaucracy. It is how one prevents a beautiful plot from saying less than it appears to say.

## Common pitfalls

**Mistaking a conditional exclusion for an absolute no-go.** If a point is excluded under a gap assumption, the conclusion is that the gap assumption and the point are incompatible with crossing and unitarity. A theory with extra low-lying operators may still exist.

**Calling a finite-derivative allowed point a CFT.** Numerical allowed means “not excluded by this finite search.” It is evidence, not construction.

**Forgetting listed operators.** The statement $\Delta_{\epsilon'}\ge3$ is not the same as $\Delta_\epsilon\ge3$. The prime matters.

**Using gaps from one sector in another.** A singlet scalar gap says nothing about charged scalars unless the equations or symmetry connect them.

**Treating numerical choices as physics.** Derivative order, solver tolerances, and rational approximations are implementation choices. They should be reported, but not confused with assumptions about the theory.

**Assuming kinks require islands.** A kink in a bound may indicate a physical CFT, a change in extremal solution type, or a numerical artifact. Additional assumptions and correlators are usually needed to isolate a small island.

## Research status

The logic of assumptions and gaps is settled: bootstrap bounds are conditional on the assumed theory class. The main practical difficulty is choosing assumptions that are strong enough to isolate a target CFT but weak enough to be physically justified.

In modern numerical work, this choice is increasingly intertwined with algorithmic questions. Mixed-correlator systems, navigator functions, skydive-type methods, surface cutting, improved block software, and higher-precision semidefinite programming have made it possible to explore much higher-dimensional assumption spaces. That power makes careful assumption auditing more important, not less.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: monotonicity

Let $A'$ be a stronger set of spectral assumptions than $A$. Show that the exact allowed region for $A'$ is contained in the exact allowed region for $A$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Let $\mathcal C(A)$ be the positive cone generated by all crossing vectors allowed by assumptions $A$. If $A'$ is stronger, then every spectrum allowed by $A'$ is also allowed by $A$, so

$$
\mathcal C(A')\subseteq \mathcal C(A).
$$

A point is allowed when $-\vec F_{\mathbf 1}$ lies in the corresponding cone. Therefore membership in $\mathcal C(A')$ implies membership in $\mathcal C(A)$. Hence

$$
\mathcal R(A')\subseteq \mathcal R(A).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: the prime in a gap

In a $\mathbb Z_2$-symmetric CFT, suppose $\epsilon$ is the lowest even scalar. Explain the difference between $\Delta_\epsilon\ge3$ and $\Delta_{\epsilon'}\ge3$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The statement $\Delta_\epsilon\ge3$ says the lowest nontrivial even scalar has dimension at least $3$. In a three-dimensional Ising-like theory this would remove the relevant even scalar usually called $\epsilon$, so it would not describe the Ising CFT.

The statement $\Delta_{\epsilon'}\ge3$ lists $\epsilon$ as an allowed low-lying operator and then imposes a gap on the next even scalar. This is the assumption that there is only one relevant even scalar.

</details>

### Exercise 3: protected operators

Why should a bootstrap setup with a continuous global symmetry not impose a spin-one gap above $d-1$ without listing the current?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A continuous global symmetry implies a conserved current $J_\mu$. Conservation fixes its dimension to

$$
\Delta_J=d-1.
$$

If the setup imposes a spin-one gap above $d-1$ without listing $J_\mu$ as an exception, the assumptions exclude the current required by the symmetry. The bootstrap problem would no longer describe a CFT with that continuous global symmetry.

</details>
