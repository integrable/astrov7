## Summary

The conformal algebra is the infinitesimal form of the global conformal group. In $d$ dimensions it is generated by

$$
P_\mu,
\qquad
M_{\mu\nu}=-M_{\nu\mu},
\qquad
D,
\qquad
K_\mu,
$$

where $P_\mu$ generates translations, $M_{\mu\nu}$ generates rotations or Lorentz transformations, $D$ generates dilatations, and $K_\mu$ generates special conformal transformations.

In the radial-quantization convention used on this page, the key commutators are

$$
[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu,
\qquad
[D,K_\mu]=-K_\mu,
\qquad
[K_\mu,P_\nu]=2\delta_{\mu\nu}D-2M_{\mu\nu}
$$

in Euclidean signature. The first two equations say that $P_\mu$ raises scaling dimension by one and $K_\mu$ lowers it by one. This is the algebraic origin of primary operators, descendants, conformal multiplets, unitarity bounds, and conformal blocks.

:::note[One algebra, two jobs]
The conformal algebra is both geometric and spectral. Geometrically, it differentiates conformal transformations. Spectrally, it organizes the Hilbert space on the cylinder into conformal multiplets.
:::

This page uses Euclidean notation for most formulas. Lorentzian changes replace $\delta_{\mu\nu}$ by $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ and rotations by Lorentz transformations. Factors of $i$ depend on whether generators are taken to be Hermitian charges or real vector fields; this page states its convention explicitly below.

## Core idea

The conformal algebra has two complementary forms.

The geometric form comes from infinitesimal coordinate transformations. In flat Euclidean space, the conformal Killing equation has the solution

$$
\xi^\mu(x)=a^\mu+\omega^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu+\lambda x^\mu+2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2.
$$

The four terms are translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations.

The operator form comes from the charges that implement these transformations on local operators and states. In radial quantization, a primary state obeys

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0,
\qquad
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta |\mathcal O\rangle,
$$

and its descendants are

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

The algebra tells us how these descendants transform and which combinations can become null in special cases.

## Setup and conventions

There are two common sign conventions. The first uses real vector fields acting on coordinates. The second uses charges acting on operators and states. They differ by signs, and sometimes by factors of $i$.

This page uses the charge convention adapted to radial quantization and bootstrap representation theory:

$$
[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu,
\qquad
[D,K_\mu]=-K_\mu.
$$

With this choice, $P_\mu$ raises scaling dimension and $K_\mu$ lowers scaling dimension.

For comparison, the corresponding infinitesimal coordinate vector fields are often written as

$$
\begin{aligned}
p_\mu &= \partial_\mu,\\
m_{\mu\nu} &= x_\mu\partial_\nu-x_\nu\partial_\mu,\\
d &= x\cdot\partial,\\
k_\mu &= 2x_\mu x\cdot\partial-x^2\partial_\mu.
\end{aligned}
$$

These vector fields generate passive coordinate changes. The charge action on operators is the opposite active action, with possible spin and scaling-dimension terms. Many sign puzzles in CFT are just this active-versus-passive distinction in disguise.

## The commutation relations

In Euclidean signature, the conformal algebra in the radial-quantization convention is

$$
\begin{aligned}
[M_{\mu\nu},M_{\rho\sigma}]
&=
\delta_{\nu\rho}M_{\mu\sigma}
-\delta_{\mu\rho}M_{\nu\sigma}
-\delta_{\nu\sigma}M_{\mu\rho}
+\delta_{\mu\sigma}M_{\nu\rho},\\
[M_{\mu\nu},P_\rho]
&=
\delta_{\nu\rho}P_\mu-\delta_{\mu\rho}P_\nu,\\
[M_{\mu\nu},K_\rho]
&=
\delta_{\nu\rho}K_\mu-\delta_{\mu\rho}K_\nu,\\
[D,P_\mu]&=P_\mu,\\
[D,K_\mu]&=-K_\mu,\\
[K_\mu,P_\nu]&=2\delta_{\mu\nu}D-2M_{\mu\nu}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The remaining commutators vanish:

$$
[P_\mu,P_\nu]=0,
\qquad
[K_\mu,K_\nu]=0,
\qquad
[D,M_{\mu\nu}]=0.
$$

In Lorentzian signature, replace $\delta_{\mu\nu}$ by $\eta_{\mu\nu}$, and interpret $M_{\mu\nu}$ as Lorentz generators. If Hermitian generators are used, insert the conventional factors of $i$ consistently.

### What the relations mean

The rotation subalgebra is the first line. It says that $M_{\mu\nu}$ generate $\mathfrak{so}(d)$ in Euclidean signature or $\mathfrak{so}(d-1,1)$ in Lorentzian signature.

The next two lines say that $P_\mu$ and $K_\mu$ transform as vectors under rotations or Lorentz transformations.

The two commutators with $D$ say that translations and special conformal transformations have opposite scaling weights:

$$
P_\mu: +1,
\qquad
K_\mu: -1.
$$

The mixed commutator

$$
[K_\mu,P_\nu]=2\delta_{\mu\nu}D-2M_{\mu\nu}
$$

is the heart of the algebra. It says that lowering after raising returns the dilatation and spin quantum numbers. This is exactly what one needs to compute descendant norms.

## Differential operators on scalar primaries

Let $\mathcal O(x)$ be a scalar primary of scaling dimension $\Delta$. Under an infinitesimal conformal transformation generated by $\xi^\mu(x)$,

$$
\delta_\xi \mathcal O(x)
=
-\xi^\mu(x)\partial_\mu\mathcal O(x)
-\Delta\,\sigma(x)\mathcal O(x),
$$

where

$$
\sigma(x)=\frac{1}{d}\partial_\mu\xi^\mu(x).
$$

For the four elementary generators, this gives the scalar-primary differential representation

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal P_\mu &= -\partial_\mu,\\
\mathcal M_{\mu\nu} &= x_\mu\partial_\nu-x_\nu\partial_\mu,\\
\mathcal D &= -(x\cdot\partial+\Delta),\\
\mathcal K_\mu &= -\left(2x_\mu x\cdot\partial-x^2\partial_\mu+2\Delta x_\mu\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

These are differential operators implementing the active variation of the operator insertion. They are not the same objects as the coordinate vector fields $p_\mu,m_{\mu\nu},d,k_\mu$; they include the scaling term and the active-action sign.

For a spinning primary, there is an additional spin term. If $\Sigma_{\mu\nu}$ are the spin matrices in the representation of $\mathcal O$, then

$$
\mathcal M_{\mu\nu}
=x_\mu\partial_\nu-x_\nu\partial_\mu+\Sigma_{\mu\nu},
$$

and

$$
\mathcal K_\mu
=-\left(2x_\mu x\cdot\partial-x^2\partial_\mu+2\Delta x_\mu+2x^\nu\Sigma_{\mu\nu}\right).
$$

The spin term in $\mathcal K_\mu$ is chosen to match the same rotation convention. The invariant content is that spinning primaries transform with the local rotation part of the Jacobian.

## Primary states and descendants

In radial quantization, a local operator inserted at the origin creates a state:

$$
|\mathcal O\rangle=\mathcal O(0)|0\rangle.
$$

A primary operator is characterized by

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0.
$$

It also has definite scaling dimension and spin:

$$
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta |\mathcal O\rangle,
$$

and

$$
M_{\mu\nu}|\mathcal O\rangle
\quad\text{acts in the spin representation of }\mathcal O.
$$

Descendants are obtained by acting with translations:

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

The commutator

$$
[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu
$$

implies

$$
D\,P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle
=(\Delta+n)P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle
$$

up to spin mixing among indices. Thus level $n$ descendants have scaling dimension $\Delta+n$.

This is the algebraic skeleton of a conformal multiplet:

$$
\text{primary}
\xrightarrow{P}
\text{level 1 descendants}
\xrightarrow{P}
\text{level 2 descendants}
\xrightarrow{P}
\cdots.
$$

## Why special conformal generators matter

A common first impression is that $K_\mu$ looks optional. Translations, rotations, and scale transformations already sound like plenty. But without $K_\mu$, there is no finite-dimensional conformal algebra and no primary-descendant lowest-weight structure.

The role of $K_\mu$ is analogous to a lowering operator. It detects whether a state is a primary, computes descendant norms, and enforces shortening conditions. In radial quantization, the adjoint relation is

$$
P_\mu^\dagger=K_\mu.
$$

Therefore the norm of a level-one descendant is controlled by

$$
\langle \mathcal O|K_\nu P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

For a scalar primary, $M_{\mu\nu}|\mathcal O\rangle=0$, so

$$
\langle \mathcal O|K_\nu P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=2\delta_{\mu\nu}\Delta\langle\mathcal O|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Positivity then requires $\Delta\geq 0$. Stronger unitarity bounds come from higher-level descendants and from spinning representations. The details belong to [Unitarity Bounds](/cft-bootstrap/operators-states-radial-quantization/unitarity-bounds/), but the mechanism is already visible here.

## The conformal algebra as an orthogonal algebra

The commutation relations above are equivalent to the orthogonal algebra

$$
\mathfrak{so}(d+1,1)
$$

in Euclidean signature and

$$
\mathfrak{so}(d,2)
$$

in Lorentzian signature.

One way to see this is to introduce embedding-space generators $J_{AB}=-J_{BA}$ preserving a metric with two extra directions. They obey

$$
[J_{AB},J_{CD}]
=
G_{BC}J_{AD}-G_{AC}J_{BD}-G_{BD}J_{AC}+G_{AD}J_{BC}.
$$

The conformal generators can be assembled from these $J_{AB}$ as linear combinations. Schematically,

$$
M_{\mu\nu}\leftrightarrow J_{\mu\nu},
\qquad
D\leftrightarrow J_{+-},
\qquad
P_\mu,K_\mu\leftrightarrow J_{\mu +}\pm J_{\mu -},
$$

with signs depending on the lightcone convention for the embedding metric. This is the infinitesimal version of the projective null-cone construction from [Conformal Group in d Dimensions](/cft-bootstrap/conformal-symmetry/conformal-group-in-d-dimensions/).

## The quadratic Casimir

The quadratic conformal Casimir is the invariant operator built from the generators. In the present convention, a common normalization is

$$
C_2
=
D^2
-\frac12\left(P_\mu K^\mu+K_\mu P^\mu\right)
+\frac12 M_{\mu\nu}M^{\mu\nu}.
$$

On a primary of scaling dimension $\Delta$ and spin $\ell$ in the symmetric traceless representation, the eigenvalue is

$$
C_2(\Delta,\ell)
=
\Delta(\Delta-d)+\ell(\ell+d-2).
$$

This formula is one of the main bridges from symmetry to conformal blocks. When a four-point function is decomposed into exchanged conformal multiplets, each exchanged primary labels a Casimir eigenvalue. The Casimir equation for conformal blocks is just the statement that a block carries a definite conformal representation.

:::tip[Casimir intuition]
The conformal block is not merely “a special function.” It is the contribution of one irreducible conformal multiplet, and the Casimir is the representation-theoretic label that makes this precise.
:::

## Conserved currents as shortened multiplets

Some conformal multiplets are shorter than generic multiplets. The shortening happens when a descendant is null or conserved.

For a spin-one primary current $J_\mu$ of dimension $\Delta=d-1$, conservation says

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu=0.
$$

For the stress tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$, the dimension is

$$
\Delta_T=d,
$$

and conservation says

$$
\partial^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

In the conformal multiplet language, these conservation equations remove descendants. Algebraically, they occur exactly at unitarity bounds for the corresponding spin.

This is why conservation laws, unitarity bounds, and representation shortening are not three separate facts. They are three views of the same conformal-algebra mechanism.

## Relation to correlation functions

For a correlation function of local operators,

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle,
$$

conformal symmetry means that the sum of generator actions on all insertions vanishes:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n \mathcal G_i\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle=0,
$$

where $\mathcal G_i$ is the appropriate differential operator for the generator acting on the $i$th insertion.

For example, scalar two-point functions are fixed by translation, rotation, scale, and special conformal invariance to be

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=
\frac{\delta_{ij}}{x^{2\Delta_i}}
$$

when the operators are orthonormal primaries of equal dimension. Three-point functions are fixed up to OPE coefficients. Four-point functions depend on cross-ratios, and their conformal-block decompositions are organized by irreducible representations of the same algebra.

The algebra is therefore the engine behind a large fraction of the CFT curriculum:

$$
\text{conformal algebra}
\longrightarrow
\text{multiplets}
\longrightarrow
\text{correlator forms}
\longrightarrow
\text{blocks}
\longrightarrow
\text{crossing equations}.
$$

## Common pitfalls

Do not mix vector-field and charge conventions without changing signs. Coordinate vector fields often satisfy $[d,p_\mu]=-p_\mu$, while radial-quantization charges are usually written so that $[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu$. Both are correct in their own conventions.

Do not forget that $K_\mu$ is a lowering operator in radial quantization. Calling it “special” can make it sound ornamental, but it is essential for defining primaries and proving unitarity bounds.

Do not assume all descendants are independent. Conservation equations and null states remove descendants. This is the representation-theoretic origin of short multiplets.

Do not use scalar differential operators for spinning operators. Spinning primaries transform with spin matrices, and their $K_\mu$ action contains spin-dependent terms.

Do not confuse the global conformal algebra with the Virasoro algebra. In two dimensions, the global holomorphic generators $L_{-1},L_0,L_1$ form an $\mathfrak{sl}(2)$ subalgebra, but the full local quantum symmetry is much larger when the stress tensor exists and appropriate assumptions hold.

## Research status

The finite conformal algebra and its representation-theoretic interpretation are settled. They are part of the standard language of CFT in any dimension.

Active work uses this algebra in increasingly refined settings: spinning correlator bases, mixed-correlator bootstrap systems, defect and boundary conformal algebras, superconformal representation theory, Lorentzian inversion formulas, Regge and lightcone limits, celestial conformal bases, and computational block generation.

The caution is that the algebra alone does not define a CFT. A CFT also needs a spectrum, OPE coefficients, unitarity or reflection positivity when assumed, and crossing symmetry. The algebra supplies the kinematic grammar; the bootstrap asks which grammatically valid sentences form a consistent theory.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Scaling dimension of descendants

Assume

$$
[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu,
\qquad
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Show that $P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle$ has dimension $\Delta+1$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
D P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=
P_\mu D|\mathcal O\rangle+[D,P_\mu]|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Using the assumptions,

$$
D P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=\Delta P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle+P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=(\Delta+1)P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

So the level-one descendant has dimension $\Delta+1$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Level-one scalar norm

Let $|\mathcal O\rangle$ be a scalar primary, so

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0,
\qquad
M_{\mu\nu}|\mathcal O\rangle=0,
\qquad
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Using $P_\mu^\dagger=K_\mu$, compute

$$
\langle P_\nu\mathcal O|P_\mu\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

By radial quantization,

$$
\langle P_\nu\mathcal O|P_\mu\mathcal O\rangle
=
\langle \mathcal O|K_\nu P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Since $K_\nu|\mathcal O\rangle=0$,

$$
K_\nu P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=[K_\nu,P_\mu]|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

The algebra gives

$$
[K_\nu,P_\mu]=2\delta_{\mu\nu}D-2M_{\nu\mu}.
$$

For a scalar primary, $M_{\nu\mu}|\mathcal O\rangle=0$, hence

$$
\langle P_\nu\mathcal O|P_\mu\mathcal O\rangle
=2\delta_{\mu\nu}\Delta\langle \mathcal O|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Positivity requires $\Delta\geq 0$. This is only the first and weakest scalar positivity condition; the familiar nonidentity scalar bound $\Delta\geq (d-2)/2$ comes from higher-level descendants.

</details>

### Exercise 3: The Casimir eigenvalue for a scalar primary

Using

$$
C_2
=
D^2
-\frac12(P_\mu K^\mu+K_\mu P^\mu)
+\frac12M_{\mu\nu}M^{\mu\nu},
$$

show that a scalar primary has

$$
C_2=\Delta(\Delta-d).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For a scalar primary,

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0,
\qquad
M_{\mu\nu}|\mathcal O\rangle=0,
\qquad
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

The term $P_\mu K^\mu$ annihilates the primary because $K^\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0$. The term $K_\mu P^\mu$ becomes a commutator:

$$
K_\mu P^\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=[K_\mu,P^\mu]|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Summing over $\mu$,

$$
[K_\mu,P^\mu]
=2\delta_\mu{}^\mu D-2M_\mu{}^\mu
=2dD,
$$

because $M_\mu{}^\mu=0$. Therefore

$$
C_2|\mathcal O\rangle
=\left(D^2-dD\right)|\mathcal O\rangle
=\Delta(\Delta-d)|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Global two-dimensional subalgebra

In two-dimensional CFT, the global holomorphic conformal generators are $L_{-1}$, $L_0$, and $L_1$, with

$$
[L_m,L_n]=(m-n)L_{m+n}
$$

for $m,n=-1,0,1$. Show that these three generators close among themselves.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Check the possible sums:

$$
[-1,0]\to -1,
\qquad
[0,1]\to 1,
\qquad
[-1,1]\to 0.
$$

More explicitly,

$$
[L_0,L_{-1}]=L_{-1},
\qquad
[L_0,L_1]=-L_1,
\qquad
[L_1,L_{-1}]=2L_0.
$$

No generator outside $\{L_{-1},L_0,L_1\}$ appears. Thus they form an $\mathfrak{sl}(2)$ subalgebra, the holomorphic part of the global conformal algebra in two dimensions.

</details>
