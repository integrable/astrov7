## Summary

Finite conformal transformations are the actual coordinate maps obtained by exponentiating the conformal generators. In flat Euclidean space they are built from four elementary moves:

$$
x^\mu\mapsto x^\mu+a^\mu,
\qquad
x^\mu\mapsto R^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu,
\qquad
x^\mu\mapsto \lambda x^\mu,
\qquad
x^\mu\mapsto \frac{x^\mu}{x^2}.
$$

These are translations, rotations, dilatations, and inversion. A special conformal transformation is the composition inversion–translation–inversion. With the convention used on this page,

$$
x'^\mu
=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

The map is conformal because its Jacobian is a position-dependent scale times a rotation or reflection:

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\rho}
\frac{\partial x'^\nu}{\partial x^\sigma}\delta_{\mu\nu}
=
\Omega(x)^2\delta_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

For a scalar primary of scaling dimension $\Delta$, correlation functions transform as

$$
\left\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1')\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n')\right\rangle
=
\prod_{i=1}^n \Omega(x_i)^{-\Delta_i}
\left\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)\right\rangle.
$$

This formula is one of the main reasons finite transformations matter: it lets us move operator insertions to convenient conformal frames without losing normalization data.

:::note[Finite maps are global moves]
Infinitesimal generators tell us the algebra. Finite transformations tell us how points, distances, cross-ratios, and primary operators actually move. Bootstrap equations use both: the algebra organizes representations, while finite covariance fixes the allowed form of correlators.
:::

This page uses Euclidean notation unless otherwise stated. Lorentzian CFT uses the same local formulas with $\delta_{\mu\nu}$ replaced by $\eta_{\mu\nu}$, but the global causal and singularity structure becomes more delicate.

## Core idea

A conformal transformation is a map that preserves angles but may rescale lengths by a position-dependent factor. If

$$
x'^\mu=f^\mu(x),
$$

then the defining local condition is

$$
\frac{\partial f^\mu}{\partial x^\rho}
\frac{\partial f^\nu}{\partial x^\sigma}\delta_{\mu\nu}
=
\Omega(x)^2\delta_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

The number $\Omega(x)>0$ is the local scale factor. Infinitesimally this condition becomes the conformal Killing equation, studied in [Conformal Killing Equation](/cft-bootstrap/conformal-symmetry/conformal-killing-equation/). Finite transformations solve the same geometric condition without assuming the displacement is small.

For $d>2$, the answer is rigid. Apart from global issues and singular points, the finite conformal transformations are generated by

$$
\text{translations},
\qquad
\text{rotations},
\qquad
\text{dilatations},
\qquad
\text{special conformal transformations}.
$$

The inversion is not continuously connected to the identity, but it is the simplest way to write special conformal transformations and to understand the conformal compactification.

## Setup and conventions

We work on flat Euclidean $\mathbb R^d$ with

$$
x^2=x\cdot x=\delta_{\mu\nu}x^\mu x^\nu.
$$

A finite conformal map is written as

$$
x\mapsto x'=f(x).
$$

The local conformal factor $\Omega(x)$ is defined by

$$
ds'^2=dx'\cdot dx'=\Omega(x)^2 dx\cdot dx.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
J^T J=\Omega(x)^2\mathbf 1,
\qquad
J^\mu{}_{\nu}=\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\nu}.
$$

This page uses the special-conformal parameter $b^\mu$ in the convention

$$
x'^\mu
=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

The infinitesimal form is therefore

$$
\delta x^\mu=2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2+O(b^2).
$$

Some references use $b\mapsto -b$. This changes the sign in the numerator and denominator but not the underlying group element.

## The elementary finite transformations

### Translations

A translation is

$$
x'^\mu=x^\mu+a^\mu.
$$

The Jacobian is the identity, so

$$
\Omega(x)=1.
$$

Translations preserve all distances, not just angles:

$$
(x_i'-x_j')^2=(x_i-x_j)^2.
$$

They are generated infinitesimally by $P_\mu$.

### Rotations

A Euclidean rotation is

$$
x'^\mu=R^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu,
\qquad
R^T R=\mathbf 1.
$$

Again

$$
\Omega(x)=1.
$$

Rotations preserve distances and angles exactly. They are generated by $M_{\mu\nu}$. If reflections are included, one obtains the full orthogonal group $O(d)$ rather than the connected rotation group $SO(d)$.

### Dilatations

A dilatation is

$$
x'^\mu=\lambda x^\mu,
\qquad
\lambda>0.
$$

The conformal factor is constant:

$$
\Omega(x)=\lambda.
$$

Dilatations preserve angles but rescale all lengths:

$$
(x_i'-x_j')^2=\lambda^2(x_i-x_j)^2.
$$

They are generated by $D$.

### Inversion

The inversion is

$$
x'^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2}.
$$

It is undefined at $x=0$ and sends the origin to infinity. Conversely, infinity is sent to the origin. This is the first place where the conformal compactification of $\mathbb R^d$ stops being optional bookkeeping and becomes the natural stage.

The derivative is

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\nu}
=
\frac{1}{x^2}\left(\delta^\mu{}_{\nu}-2\frac{x^\mu x_\nu}{x^2}\right).
$$

The matrix in parentheses is a reflection through the hyperplane perpendicular to $x^\mu$. Therefore

$$
ds'^2=\frac{ds^2}{x^4},
\qquad
\Omega(x)=\frac{1}{x^2}.
$$

A useful identity is

$$
\left(\frac{x_i}{x_i^2}-\frac{x_j}{x_j^2}\right)^2
=
\frac{x_{ij}^2}{x_i^2x_j^2},
\qquad
x_{ij}=x_i-x_j.
$$

This identity is the quick check that the scalar two-point function

$$
\langle \mathcal O(x)\mathcal O(0)\rangle=\frac{C_{\mathcal O}}{(x^2)^\Delta}
$$

has the correct conformal covariance.

## Special conformal transformations

A special conformal transformation is obtained by inverting, translating, and inverting again. With the sign convention of this page,

$$
K_b=I\circ T_{-b}\circ I.
$$

Starting from $x$, first invert to $x/x^2$, translate by $-b$, then invert again. The result is

$$
x'^\mu
=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

The denominator

$$
\alpha(x)=1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2
$$

controls the conformal factor:

$$
\Omega(x)=\frac{1}{\alpha(x)}.
$$

Thus

$$
ds'^2=\frac{ds^2}{\alpha(x)^2}.
$$

For two points, the squared distance transforms as

$$
x_{ij}'{}^2
=
\frac{x_{ij}^2}{\alpha(x_i)\alpha(x_j)}.
$$

This formula is a workhorse. It immediately shows why two-point and three-point functions have the standard power-law form, and why four-point functions can depend only on cross-ratios.

### Infinitesimal limit

For small $b^\mu$,

$$
\frac{1}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}
=
1+2b\cdot x+O(b^2),
$$

so

$$
x'^\mu
=x^\mu+2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2+O(b^2).
$$

The corresponding vector field is

$$
\xi^\mu_b(x)=2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2.
$$

This is the special-conformal term in the general solution of the conformal Killing equation.

## How primary operators transform

Let $x'=f(x)$ be a conformal transformation with scale factor $\Omega(x)$. A scalar primary operator of dimension $\Delta$ has correlation-function covariance

$$
\left\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1')\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n')\right\rangle
=
\prod_{i=1}^n \Omega(x_i)^{-\Delta_i}
\left\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)\right\rangle.
$$

For a dilatation $x'=\lambda x$, this gives

$$
\langle \mathcal O(\lambda x)\mathcal O(0)\rangle
=
\lambda^{-2\Delta}\langle \mathcal O(x)\mathcal O(0)\rangle,
$$

which is exactly the expected scaling law.

For spinning primaries, the local orthogonal matrix in the Jacobian also acts on spin indices. Since

$$
J^\mu{}_{\nu}=\Omega(x)R^\mu{}_{\nu}(x),
\qquad
R(x)\in O(d),
$$

a primary in representation $\rho$ transforms with the representation matrix $\rho(R(x))$, in addition to the factor $\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}$. Schematically,

$$
\left\langle \mathcal O_{a_1}(x_1')\cdots\right\rangle
=
\Omega(x_1)^{-\Delta}
\rho(R(x_1))_{a_1}{}^{b_1}
\cdots
\left\langle \mathcal O_{b_1}(x_1)\cdots\right\rangle.
$$

The scalar case is easier because $\rho(R)=1$.

### Primary versus descendant behavior

The simple finite transformation law is part of the definition of a primary. Descendants do not transform by a pure scale-and-spin factor; derivatives of $\Omega(x)$ and $R(x)$ mix descendants inside the same conformal multiplet. This is why primary operators, not arbitrary local operators, are the natural building blocks of CFT data.

## What finite transformations preserve

Finite conformal transformations do not preserve ordinary distances. They preserve angles and certain dimensionless combinations.

For four points, define the cross-ratios

$$
u
=
\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2},
\qquad
v
=
\frac{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2}.
$$

Under any finite conformal transformation,

$$
x_{ij}'{}^2=\Omega(x_i)\Omega(x_j)x_{ij}^2
$$

for maps written with the relevant local factors. Each point appears equally often in the numerator and denominator of $u$ and $v$, so all scale factors cancel:

$$
u'=u,
\qquad
v'=v.
$$

This is why scalar four-point functions take the form

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\mathcal O_3(x_3)\mathcal O_4(x_4)\rangle
=
\text{fixed powers of }x_{ij}^2
\times
G(u,v),
$$

where the dynamical information begins in the function $G(u,v)$.

:::tip[Conformal frames]
For $d>2$, the conformal group is large enough to move three generic points to convenient fixed locations, such as $0$, a unit vector, and $\infty$. Four-point functions therefore depend on two invariant variables. This simple counting is the seed of conformal blocks and crossing equations.
:::

## The two-dimensional exception

In two Euclidean dimensions, local conformal transformations are much larger. Writing

$$
z=x^1+ix^2,
\qquad
\bar z=x^1-ix^2,
$$

any holomorphic map

$$
z\mapsto f(z)
$$

is locally conformal where $f'(z)\neq 0$. This infinite-dimensional local symmetry is the origin of Virasoro symmetry after quantization.

The global conformal transformations of the Riemann sphere are still finite-dimensional:

$$
z\mapsto \frac{az+b}{cz+d},
\qquad
ad-bc\neq 0.
$$

Modulo an overall rescaling of $a,b,c,d$, this is the group $PSL(2,\mathbb C)$ in Euclidean two-dimensional CFT. The 2D story is therefore not that finite transformations disappear. It is that the local conformal group becomes vastly larger than the global one.

## Common pitfalls

**Treating inversion as an ordinary rotation.** Inversion is conformal but not an isometry. Its Jacobian is a reflection times a scale factor $1/x^2$, and it exchanges the origin with infinity.

**Forgetting the denominator of a special conformal transformation.** The special conformal map is rational, not polynomial. Its denominator is exactly what makes the map finite and conformal.

**Confusing the sign of the special-conformal parameter.** Both

$$
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\frac{x^\mu+b^\mu x^2}{1+2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}
$$

appear in the literature. They differ by $b\mapsto -b$.

**Using Euclidean formulas blindly in Lorentzian signature.** Algebraically one replaces $\delta_{\mu\nu}$ by $\eta_{\mu\nu}$, but finite Lorentzian transformations can move points across light cones or through singular loci. Lorentzian CFT requires causal ordering and analytic-continuation care.

**Applying the primary transformation law to descendants.** Descendants mix with one another under finite conformal transformations. Only primaries transform by a pure local scale and spin rotation.

## Research status

The finite conformal maps of flat space are settled classical geometry. For ordinary CFT, the important convention choices are signs, Euclidean versus Lorentzian signature, and whether one works on $\mathbb R^d$, its conformal compactification, or a curved conformal manifold.

Active research uses these maps in more sophisticated settings: Lorentzian inversion formulas, celestial CFT, conformal collider bounds, defect CFT kinematics, conformal frame algorithms for spinning correlators, and embedding-space or twistor-space methods. The basic finite transformations remain the same, but their analytic domains and representation-theoretic packaging become richer.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Check that inversion is conformal

For

$$
x'^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2},
$$

show that

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\rho}
\frac{\partial x'^\nu}{\partial x^\sigma}\delta_{\mu\nu}
=
\frac{1}{x^4}\delta_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate:

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\rho}
=
\frac{1}{x^2}\left(\delta^\mu{}_{\rho}-2\frac{x^\mu x_\rho}{x^2}\right).
$$

Define

$$
R^\mu{}_{\rho}=\delta^\mu{}_{\rho}-2\frac{x^\mu x_\rho}{x^2}.
$$

This is a reflection, so

$$
R^\mu{}_{\rho}R^\nu{}_{\sigma}\delta_{\mu\nu}=\delta_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\rho}
\frac{\partial x'^\nu}{\partial x^\sigma}\delta_{\mu\nu}
=
\frac{1}{x^4}\delta_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

Thus $\Omega(x)=1/x^2$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Derive the special conformal formula

Starting from

$$
K_b=I\circ T_{-b}\circ I,
$$

derive

$$
x'^\mu
=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

After the first inversion,

$$
y^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2}.
$$

After the translation by $-b$,

$$
z^\mu=y^\mu-b^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2}-b^\mu.
$$

The last inversion gives

$$
x'^\mu=\frac{z^\mu}{z^2}.
$$

Compute

$$
z^2
=
\left(\frac{x}{x^2}-b\right)^2
=
\frac{1}{x^2}-\frac{2b\cdot x}{x^2}+b^2
=
\frac{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}{x^2}.
$$

Therefore

$$
x'^\mu
=
\frac{x^\mu/x^2-b^\mu}{(1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2)/x^2}
=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Show that cross-ratios are invariant

Assume squared distances transform as

$$
x_{ij}'{}^2=\Omega_i\Omega_j x_{ij}^2.
$$

Show that

$$
u
=
\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2},
\qquad
v
=
\frac{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2}
$$

are invariant.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $u$,

$$
u'
=
\frac{(\Omega_1\Omega_2x_{12}^2)(\Omega_3\Omega_4x_{34}^2)}{(\Omega_1\Omega_3x_{13}^2)(\Omega_2\Omega_4x_{24}^2)}.
$$

All four $\Omega_i$ cancel, leaving

$$
u'=u.
$$

The same cancellation gives

$$
v'
=
\frac{(\Omega_1\Omega_4x_{14}^2)(\Omega_2\Omega_3x_{23}^2)}{(\Omega_1\Omega_3x_{13}^2)(\Omega_2\Omega_4x_{24}^2)}=v.
$$

</details>
