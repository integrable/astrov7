## Summary

A conformal transformation is a coordinate transformation that preserves angles but not necessarily distances. In flat Euclidean space, $x\mapsto x'=f(x)$ is conformal when

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\rho}{\partial x^\mu}
\frac{\partial x'^\sigma}{\partial x^\nu}
\delta_{\rho\sigma}
=
\Omega(x)^2\delta_{\mu\nu}.
$$

Equivalently, at each point the Jacobian is a scale factor times an orthogonal matrix:

$$
J(x)=\Omega(x)R(x),
\qquad
R(x)^T R(x)=1.
$$

This page explains that definition, derives the infinitesimal conformal Killing equation, lists the basic finite transformations, and records how primary operators transform. These are the kinematic facts behind almost every later formula in CFT.

:::note[Main idea]
A conformal transformation is not an arbitrary change of coordinates. It is a coordinate transformation whose local linearization is a rotation or reflection times a scale. No shear is allowed.
:::

## Core idea

The geometric content of conformality is local. A smooth map $f$ takes a small displacement $dx$ near $x$ to

$$
dx'^\mu=J^\mu{}_{\nu}(x)dx^\nu,
\qquad
J^\mu{}_{\nu}(x)=\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\nu}.
$$

The map is conformal when the squared length transforms as

$$
ds'^2=\Omega(x)^2 ds^2.
$$

This condition preserves the angle between two infinitesimal vectors $u$ and $v$. If $u\cdot v=0$, then

$$
(Ju)\cdot(Jv)=u^T J^T J v=\Omega(x)^2 u\cdot v=0.
$$

More generally,

$$
\frac{(Ju)\cdot(Jv)}{|Ju|\,|Jv|}
=
\frac{u\cdot v}{|u|\,|v|}.
$$

So conformal transformations preserve infinitesimal angles. They may stretch different points by different factors, but they cannot shear a small circle into an ellipse.

## Setup and conventions

This page mostly uses flat Euclidean signature in $d$ dimensions. Coordinates are $x^\mu$, with

$$
ds^2=\delta_{\mu\nu}dx^\mu dx^\nu.
$$

A finite transformation is written as

$$
x'^\mu=f^\mu(x).
$$

The conformal factor $\Omega(x)$ is defined by

$$
ds'^2=\Omega(x)^2 ds^2.
$$

In Lorentzian signature, replace $\delta_{\mu\nu}$ by $\eta_{\mu\nu}$. The same local equation reads

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\rho}{\partial x^\mu}
\frac{\partial x'^\sigma}{\partial x^\nu}
\eta_{\rho\sigma}
=
\Omega(x)^2\eta_{\mu\nu}.
$$

Lorentzian conformal transformations preserve the light cone, because $ds^2=0$ implies $ds'^2=0$.

:::caution[Conventions for special conformal transformations]
This page uses

$$
x'=\frac{x-b x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

Some books use $b\to -b$. The physics is identical, but signs in the infinitesimal generator $K_\mu$ change accordingly.
:::

## The finite building blocks

In flat Euclidean space with $d\geq 3$, the global conformal transformations are generated by translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations. A convenient way to generate the last class is to also use inversion.

### Translations

A translation is

$$
x'^\mu=x^\mu+a^\mu.
$$

The Jacobian is $J=1$, so

$$
\Omega(x)=1.
$$

Translations preserve all distances and angles.

### Rotations and reflections

An orthogonal transformation is

$$
x'^\mu=R^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu,
\qquad
R^T R=1.
$$

Again

$$
\Omega(x)=1.
$$

For the connected conformal group one normally keeps rotations with $\det R=1$. Reflections are useful discrete transformations but are not connected to the identity.

### Dilatations

A dilatation is

$$
x'^\mu=\lambda x^\mu,
\qquad
\lambda>0.
$$

Then

$$
\Omega(x)=\lambda.
$$

Dilatations preserve angles but rescale all distances by the same constant factor.

### Inversion

Inversion is

$$
x'^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2},
\qquad
x^2=x^\mu x_\mu.
$$

It is singular at the origin and sends the origin to infinity. This is not a bug; it is a sign that global conformal transformations are naturally defined on the conformal compactification of flat space.

A short computation gives

$$
dx'^2=\frac{dx^2}{(x^2)^2},
$$

so

$$
\Omega(x)=\frac{1}{x^2}.
$$

Inversion is conformal but not continuously connected to the identity. It is nevertheless the most economical way to understand special conformal transformations.

### Special conformal transformations

A special conformal transformation is inversion, then translation by $-b$, then inversion again:

$$
\text{SCT}_b=I\circ T_{-b}\circ I.
$$

With the convention of this page,

$$
x'^\mu
=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

The conformal factor is

$$
\Omega(x)=\frac{1}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

For infinitesimal $b$, this becomes

$$
x'^\mu=x^\mu+2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2+O(b^2).
$$

The associated vector field is therefore

$$
\xi_b^\mu(x)=2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2.
$$

Special conformal transformations look strange at first because they move points nonlinearly. A useful physical picture is: translate the inverted point, then come back to ordinary coordinates. This is why they are as fundamental as translations once conformal compactification is allowed.

## The infinitesimal equation

Let

$$
x'^\mu=x^\mu+\xi^\mu(x)
$$

be an infinitesimal transformation. To first order,

$$
J^\mu{}_{\nu}=\delta^\mu{}_{\nu}+\partial_\nu\xi^\mu.
$$

The conformal condition says

$$
J^T J=\Omega^2 I.
$$

Write

$$
\Omega(x)=1+\sigma(x)+O(\xi^2).
$$

Then the conformal condition becomes

$$
\partial_\mu\xi_\nu+\partial_\nu\xi_\mu=2\sigma(x)\delta_{\mu\nu}.
$$

Taking the trace gives

$$
\sigma(x)=\frac{1}{d}\partial_\rho\xi^\rho.
$$

Therefore

$$
\partial_\mu\xi_\nu+\partial_\nu\xi_\mu
=
\frac{2}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial_\rho\xi^\rho.
$$

This is the conformal Killing equation in flat Euclidean space.

For $d\geq 3$, its global smooth solutions on flat space are

$$
\xi^\mu(x)
=
a^\mu
+\omega^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu
+\lambda x^\mu
+2(b\cdot x)x^\mu
-b^\mu x^2,
$$

with

$$
\omega_{\mu\nu}=-\omega_{\nu\mu}.
$$

These four terms are, respectively, translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations.

Counting parameters gives

$$
d+\frac{d(d-1)}{2}+1+d
=
\frac{(d+1)(d+2)}{2},
$$

which is the dimension of $SO(d+1,1)$.

## The two-dimensional exception

In two Euclidean dimensions, use complex coordinates

$$
z=x^1+ix^2,
\qquad
\bar z=x^1-ix^2.
$$

The metric is proportional to

$$
ds^2=dz\,d\bar z.
$$

Any holomorphic map

$$
z'=f(z),
\qquad
\bar z'=\bar f(\bar z)
$$

is locally conformal wherever $f'(z)\neq 0$, because

$$
ds'^2=f'(z)\bar f'(\bar z)\,dz\,d\bar z.
$$

Thus the local conformal transformations in two dimensions are infinite-dimensional. Globally well-defined conformal maps of the Riemann sphere are much more restricted: they are Möbius transformations,

$$
z'=\frac{az+b}{cz+d},
\qquad
ad-bc\neq 0.
$$

The infinite-dimensional local symmetry becomes the Virasoro story in quantum two-dimensional CFT. This is why two-dimensional CFT is special, and why one should not automatically transfer every two-dimensional statement to $d\geq 3$.

## How distances transform

For the global conformal transformations above, squared distances transform as

$$
(x'_i-x'_j)^2
=
\Omega(x_i)\Omega(x_j)(x_i-x_j)^2.
$$

For translations and rotations this is immediate. For a dilatation, $\Omega=\lambda$. For inversion,

$$
\left(\frac{x_i}{x_i^2}-\frac{x_j}{x_j^2}\right)^2
=
\frac{(x_i-x_j)^2}{x_i^2x_j^2},
$$

which is the same formula with $\Omega(x)=1/x^2$.

This formula explains why cross-ratios are conformally invariant. For four points,

$$
u=\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2},
\qquad
v=\frac{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2},
$$

where

$$
x_{ij}=x_i-x_j.
$$

Every factor $\Omega(x_i)$ cancels. The existence of these invariants is why conformal symmetry fixes two-point and three-point functions but leaves nontrivial functions in four-point correlators.

## How primary operators transform

A scalar primary operator of scaling dimension $\Delta$ transforms under a finite conformal transformation as

$$
\mathcal O'(x')=\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}\mathcal O(x).
$$

Equivalently, correlation functions obey

$$
\left\langle
\mathcal O_1'(x'_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n'(x'_n)
\right\rangle
=
\prod_{i=1}^n \Omega(x_i)^{-\Delta_i}
\left\langle
\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)
\right\rangle.
$$

For a spinning primary, the Jacobian also contains a local orthogonal transformation

$$
R^\mu{}_{\nu}(x)=\Omega(x)^{-1}\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\nu},
$$

and the spin indices transform in the corresponding representation:

$$
\mathcal O'_a(x')
=
\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}
D[R(x)]_a{}^b
\mathcal O_b(x).
$$

Here $a,b$ stand for whatever tensor or spinor indices the operator carries.

The word “primary” matters. Descendant operators, such as derivatives of primaries, acquire extra terms because derivatives can act on the conformal factor and on $R(x)$.

## Why this fixes two-point functions

Suppose $\mathcal O_1$ and $\mathcal O_2$ are scalar primaries in a Euclidean CFT. Translation invariance says

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\rangle=G(x_{12}).
$$

Rotation invariance says $G$ depends only on $x_{12}^2$. Dilatation covariance gives

$$
G(\lambda x)=\lambda^{-\Delta_1-\Delta_2}G(x),
$$

so

$$
G(x)=\frac{C_{12}}{|x|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}.
$$

Special conformal covariance further forces the correlator to vanish unless the two dimensions match, assuming the operators are scalar primaries in a diagonal basis. Thus

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=
\frac{C_{ij}\delta_{\Delta_i\Delta_j}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}.
$$

In a unitary theory one can usually choose an orthonormal basis of scalar primaries with

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=
\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}.
$$

This short derivation is the first taste of why conformal symmetry is so powerful: it turns symmetry covariance into exact functional form.

## Common pitfalls

Do not call every scale transformation conformal. A global dilatation is one conformal transformation, but conformal symmetry also includes special conformal transformations. The latter are what distinguish full conformal covariance from ordinary scale covariance.

Do not say a conformal map preserves finite angles between arbitrary curves by magic. The definition is infinitesimal: it preserves angles between tangent vectors at each point. Finite curve angles follow when the curves are smooth and the map is regular at the intersection point.

Do not forget singularities. Inversion is singular at $x=0$, and special conformal transformations can have singular loci where

$$
1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2=0.
$$

The clean global object is compactified flat space.

Do not mix active and passive language carelessly. The formula

$$
\mathcal O'(x')=\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}\mathcal O(x)
$$

uses a common passive/covariant convention. Other pages may write an equivalent active action of a unitary operator on $\mathcal O(x)$, with $x$ and $x'$ interchanged. Check the convention before comparing signs.

Do not treat the two-dimensional infinite-dimensional local conformal group as if it exists in the same way for $d\geq 3$. The conformal Killing equation is much more rigid in higher dimensions.

Do not ignore spin. Scalars only see the conformal factor. Vectors, tensors, and spinors also see the local rotation or Lorentz transformation extracted from the Jacobian.

## Research status

The flat-space theory of conformal transformations is settled. The conformal Killing equation, the finite transformation list, and the primary transformation rules are standard textbook material.

The subtleties are not in these local formulas but in where and how they are used. Lorentzian CFT requires care with causal domains, operator orderings, and analytic continuation. Curved backgrounds require distinguishing diffeomorphisms from Weyl transformations and tracking Weyl anomalies. Defect and boundary CFTs preserve only subgroups of the bulk conformal group. Supersymmetric CFTs extend the conformal algebra. These are later layers, not replacements for the flat-space foundation.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Derive the conformal Killing equation

Let

$$
x'^\mu=x^\mu+\xi^\mu(x)
$$

and assume $\xi$ is infinitesimal. Starting from

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\rho}{\partial x^\mu}
\frac{\partial x'^\sigma}{\partial x^\nu}
\delta_{\rho\sigma}
=\Omega(x)^2\delta_{\mu\nu},
$$

derive

$$
\partial_\mu\xi_\nu+\partial_\nu\xi_\mu
=
\frac{2}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial_\rho\xi^\rho.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

To first order,

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\rho}{\partial x^\mu}
=\delta^\rho{}_\mu+\partial_\mu\xi^\rho.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac{\partial x'^\rho}{\partial x^\mu}
\frac{\partial x'^\sigma}{\partial x^\nu}
\delta_{\rho\sigma}
=
\delta_{\mu\nu}+\partial_\mu\xi_\nu+\partial_\nu\xi_\mu+O(\xi^2).
$$

Write

$$
\Omega(x)=1+\sigma(x)+O(\xi^2),
$$

so

$$
\Omega(x)^2\delta_{\mu\nu}
=\delta_{\mu\nu}+2\sigma(x)\delta_{\mu\nu}+O(\xi^2).
$$

Equating first-order terms gives

$$
\partial_\mu\xi_\nu+\partial_\nu\xi_\mu=2\sigma\delta_{\mu\nu}.
$$

Taking the trace gives

$$
2\partial_\rho\xi^\rho=2d\sigma,
$$

hence

$$
\sigma=\frac{1}{d}\partial_\rho\xi^\rho.
$$

Substituting back gives the conformal Killing equation.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Check inversion of distances

For inversion,

$$
x'^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2},
$$

show that

$$
(x'_i-x'_j)^2=\frac{(x_i-x_j)^2}{x_i^2x_j^2}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
x'_i-x'_j
=
\frac{x_i}{x_i^2}-\frac{x_j}{x_j^2}
=
\frac{x_j^2x_i-x_i^2x_j}{x_i^2x_j^2}.
$$

Squaring the numerator gives

$$
(x_j^2x_i-x_i^2x_j)^2
=x_j^4x_i^2+x_i^4x_j^2-2x_i^2x_j^2(x_i\cdot x_j).
$$

Factor out $x_i^2x_j^2$:

$$
(x_j^2x_i-x_i^2x_j)^2
=x_i^2x_j^2(x_i^2+x_j^2-2x_i\cdot x_j)
=x_i^2x_j^2(x_i-x_j)^2.
$$

Dividing by $(x_i^2x_j^2)^2$ gives

$$
(x'_i-x'_j)^2=\frac{(x_i-x_j)^2}{x_i^2x_j^2}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Extract the infinitesimal special conformal vector field

Use

$$
x'^\mu
=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}
$$

and keep only terms linear in $b$. Find $\xi_b^\mu=x'^\mu-x^\mu$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

To first order in $b$,

$$
\frac{1}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}
=1+2b\cdot x+O(b^2).
$$

Therefore

$$
x'^\mu
=(x^\mu-b^\mu x^2)(1+2b\cdot x)+O(b^2).
$$

Dropping quadratic terms gives

$$
x'^\mu
=x^\mu+2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2+O(b^2).
$$

Thus

$$
\xi_b^\mu(x)=2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Why scalar two-point functions need equal dimensions

Assume scalar primaries $\mathcal O_1$ and $\mathcal O_2$ have a nonzero two-point function. Translation, rotation, and dilatation covariance allow

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x)\mathcal O_2(0)\rangle
=\frac{C}{|x|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}.
$$

Use inversion to show that conformal covariance requires $\Delta_1=\Delta_2$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under inversion,

$$
x' = \frac{x}{x^2},
\qquad
\Omega(x)=\frac{1}{x^2}.
$$

The origin is sent to infinity, so it is cleaner to place the two operators at $x_1$ and $x_2$. The dilation-invariant candidate is

$$
G(x_1,x_2)=\frac{C}{|x_{12}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}.
$$

Conformal covariance under inversion demands

$$
G(x'_1,x'_2)
=
\Omega(x_1)^{-\Delta_1}\Omega(x_2)^{-\Delta_2}G(x_1,x_2).
$$

Using Exercise 2,

$$
|x'_{12}|=\frac{|x_{12}|}{|x_1||x_2|}.
$$

Therefore the left-hand side is

$$
G(x'_1,x'_2)
=
\frac{C |x_1|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}|x_2|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}{|x_{12}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}.
$$

The right-hand side is

$$
(x_1^2)^{\Delta_1}(x_2^2)^{\Delta_2}
\frac{C}{|x_{12}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}
=
\frac{C |x_1|^{2\Delta_1}|x_2|^{2\Delta_2}}{|x_{12}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}.
$$

Equality for arbitrary $x_1,x_2$ requires

$$
\Delta_1+\Delta_2=2\Delta_1,
\qquad
\Delta_1+\Delta_2=2\Delta_2,
$$

so

$$
\Delta_1=\Delta_2.
$$

</details>
