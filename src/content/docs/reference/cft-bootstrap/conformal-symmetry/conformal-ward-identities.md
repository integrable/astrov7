## Summary

A Ward identity is the equation saying that a symmetry of the theory is also a symmetry of its correlation functions. For conformal symmetry, the basic separated-point identity for primary operators is

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n \mathcal D_i[\xi]\,
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle=0,
$$

where $\xi^\mu(x)$ is a conformal Killing vector and $\mathcal D_i[\xi]$ is the differential operator that implements the infinitesimal conformal transformation on the $i$th operator.

For scalar primaries of dimensions $\Delta_i$,

$$
\mathcal D_i[\xi]
=
\xi^\mu(x_i)\partial_{i\mu}+\Delta_i\sigma(x_i),
\qquad
\sigma(x)\equiv \frac{1}{d}\partial_\mu\xi^\mu(x).
$$

For spinning primaries, one adds the spin-rotation term

$$
\frac{1}{2}\omega_{\mu\nu}(x_i)\Sigma_i^{\mu\nu},
\qquad
\omega_{\mu\nu}=\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\xi_\nu-\partial_\nu\xi_\mu),
$$

where $\Sigma_i^{\mu\nu}$ acts on the spin indices of $\mathcal O_i$.

The stress tensor is the local operator that generates these identities. In flat space and away from insertions, conformal invariance says

$$
\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0,
\qquad
T^\mu{}_{\mu}=0,
$$

up to anomalies and improvement subtleties. At insertion points, these equations acquire contact terms. Those contact terms are not annoying decoration; they are exactly what makes the stress tensor act on local operators.

:::note[Ward identities are symmetry with bookkeeping]
The elegant global equation $\sum_i\mathcal D_iG=0$ is the separated-point version. The full local statement includes stress-tensor insertions and contact terms at the operator positions.
:::

## Core idea

Noether's theorem says that a continuous spacetime symmetry has a conserved current. For conformal transformations, the current associated with a vector field $\xi^\mu(x)$ is

$$
J_\xi^\mu=T^{\mu\nu}\xi_\nu.
$$

At separated points,

$$
\partial_\mu J_\xi^\mu
=
(\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu})\xi_\nu
+\frac{1}{2}T^{\mu\nu}(\partial_\mu\xi_\nu+\partial_\nu\xi_\mu).
$$

If $T^{\mu\nu}$ is conserved, symmetric, and traceless, and if $\xi^\mu$ obeys the conformal Killing equation,

$$
\partial_\mu\xi_\nu+\partial_\nu\xi_\mu
=
2\sigma(x)\delta_{\mu\nu},
\qquad
\sigma(x)=\frac{1}{d}\partial\cdot\xi,
$$

then

$$
\partial_\mu J_\xi^\mu=0
$$

away from insertions.

The Ward identity is what happens when one inserts this current in a correlator and integrates it over a closed surface. Because the current is conserved away from operator insertions, the surface can be deformed. When it crosses an operator, the charge acts on that operator. This gives the global differential identity for the correlator.

In one line:

$$
\text{conservation of }T_{\mu\nu}
+\text{tracelessness}
+\text{operator contact terms}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{conformal Ward identities}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

This page uses flat Euclidean CFT conventions in $d$ dimensions unless stated otherwise. The stress tensor is symmetric and normalized consistently with the CFT convention page. We write

$$
G(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\langle\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle.
$$

For a conformal Killing vector $\xi^\mu$, define

$$
\sigma(x)=\frac{1}{d}\partial_\mu\xi^\mu(x),
\qquad
\omega_{\mu\nu}(x)=\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\xi_\nu-\partial_\nu\xi_\mu).
$$

The infinitesimal action on a primary operator is written as

$$
\delta_\xi\mathcal O(x)=-\mathcal D_\xi\mathcal O(x),
$$

with

$$
\mathcal D_\xi
=
\xi^\mu\partial_\mu+\Delta\sigma
+\frac{1}{2}\omega_{\mu\nu}\Sigma^{\mu\nu}
$$

for a primary of dimension $\Delta$ and spin representation matrices $\Sigma^{\mu\nu}$. With this sign convention, symmetry invariance gives

$$
\sum_i \mathcal D_i[\xi]G=0.
$$

Other references may put the minus sign into the definition of $\mathcal D_\xi$. The physics is the same; the useful test is whether translations give

$$
\sum_i\partial_{i\mu}G=0.
$$

## Global Ward identities for primary correlators

The global conformal transformations in $d\geq 3$ are generated by conformal Killing vectors of the form

$$
\xi^\mu(x)
=
a^\mu+\omega^\mu{}_\nu x^\nu+\lambda x^\mu+2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2.
$$

Each term gives a Ward identity.

For translations, $\xi^\mu=a^\mu$, so $\sigma=0$ and

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n\partial_{i\mu}G=0.
$$

This says that the correlator depends only on relative positions.

For rotations, $\xi^\mu=\omega^\mu{}_\nu x^\nu$, and the scalar-primary identity is

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n
\left(x_i^\mu\partial_i^\nu-x_i^\nu\partial_i^\mu\right)G=0.
$$

For spinning operators, the spin matrices must be added:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n
\left(x_i^\mu\partial_i^\nu-x_i^\nu\partial_i^\mu+\Sigma_i^{\mu\nu}\right)G=0.
$$

For dilatations, $\xi^\mu=\lambda x^\mu$, so $\sigma=\lambda$. Removing the common infinitesimal factor gives

$$
\left(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\cdot\partial_i+\sum_{i=1}^n\Delta_i\right)G=0.
$$

For special conformal transformations, $\xi^\mu=2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2$. For scalar primaries,

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n
\left(
2x_i^\mu x_i\cdot\partial_i
-x_i^2\partial_i^\mu
+2\Delta_i x_i^\mu
\right)G=0.
$$

For spinning operators, add

$$
2x_{i\nu}\Sigma_i^{\mu\nu}
$$

inside the sum.

These four identities are often the fastest way to derive the conformal form of two-point and three-point functions. They are also the reason four-point functions can depend only on conformal cross-ratios.

## Stress-tensor insertions and contact terms

The differential Ward identities above are the clean separated-point form. The local form involves $T_{\mu\nu}(x)$.

For scalar insertions, translation invariance is encoded by the contact-term identity

$$
\partial_\mu\langle T^{\mu\nu}(x)\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle
=
-\sum_{i=1}^n\delta^{(d)}(x-x_i)\partial_i^\nu G.
$$

This formula should be read distributionally: it is true after integrating against a smooth test function. Away from all $x_i$, it reduces to

$$
\partial_\mu\langle T^{\mu\nu}(x)\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle=0.
$$

Likewise, scale covariance of scalar primaries gives the trace contact term

$$
\langle T^\mu{}_{\mu}(x)\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle
=
-\sum_{i=1}^n\Delta_i\delta^{(d)}(x-x_i)G
$$

in flat space, ignoring possible Weyl anomalies and improvement terms. At separated points,

$$
\langle T^\mu{}_{\mu}(x)\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle=0.
$$

These contact terms are how the stress tensor knows where the local operators are. If one deletes them, the integrated Ward identities lose the operator variations.

## Integrated charges

Let $S$ be a closed codimension-one surface that encloses some operator insertions but not others. Define

$$
Q_\xi(S)=\int_S dS_\mu\,\xi_\nu T^{\mu\nu}.
$$

For a surface $S_i$ enclosing only $x_i$, the Ward identity says schematically

$$
\langle Q_\xi(S_i)\mathcal O_i(x_i)\prod_{j\neq i}\mathcal O_j(x_j)\rangle
=
\mathcal D_i[\xi]G.
$$

For a surface enclosing all insertions in a vacuum correlator, the total charge annihilates the vacuum, so

$$
\sum_i\mathcal D_i[\xi]G=0.
$$

This is the CFT version of contour deformation. In two-dimensional CFT the word “contour” is literal. In higher dimensions the contour is a sphere or another closed codimension-one surface.

## Worked example: the scalar two-point function

Consider two scalar primaries $\mathcal O_1$ and $\mathcal O_2$ of dimensions $\Delta_1$ and $\Delta_2$. Translation and rotation Ward identities imply

$$
\langle\mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\rangle=f(r),
\qquad
r=|x_{12}|.
$$

The dilatation Ward identity gives

$$
\left(r\frac{d}{dr}+\Delta_1+\Delta_2\right)f(r)=0,
$$

so

$$
f(r)=\frac{C_{12}}{r^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}.
$$

The special conformal Ward identity then requires $C_{12}=0$ unless

$$
\Delta_1=\Delta_2.
$$

Therefore, after diagonalizing operators with the same spin and dimension,

$$
\langle\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}
$$

for scalar primaries in a common unit-normalization convention.

This small derivation is the template for much of CFT kinematics: translations and rotations reduce coordinate dependence; dilatations fix homogeneity; special conformal transformations impose the final rigidity.

## Three-point functions

The same Ward-identity logic fixes the coordinate dependence of three scalar primaries. Translation and rotation invariance imply that

$$
\big\langle
\mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\mathcal O_3(x_3)
\big\rangle
$$

can depend only on

$$
|x_{12}|,
\qquad
|x_{23}|,
\qquad
|x_{31}|.
$$

Assume the form

$$
G_3=
\frac{C_{123}}{|x_{12}|^a|x_{23}|^b|x_{31}|^c}.
$$

Dilatations and inversion covariance give the three linear equations

$$
a+c=2\Delta_1,
\qquad
a+b=2\Delta_2,
\qquad
b+c=2\Delta_3.
$$

Thus

$$
a=\Delta_1+\Delta_2-\Delta_3,
\qquad
b=\Delta_2+\Delta_3-\Delta_1,
\qquad
c=\Delta_3+\Delta_1-\Delta_2.
$$

The scalar-primary three-point function is therefore

$$
\left\langle
\mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\mathcal O_3(x_3)
\right\rangle
=
\frac{C_{123}}
{|x_{12}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2-\Delta_3}
 |x_{23}|^{\Delta_2+\Delta_3-\Delta_1}
 |x_{31}|^{\Delta_3+\Delta_1-\Delta_2}}.
$$

The number $C_{123}$ is not fixed by conformal symmetry. Once two-point functions have been normalized, these constants are dynamical CFT data. This is one of the key lessons of Ward identities: conformal symmetry fixes the kinematic shape, but the theory supplies the spectrum and OPE coefficients.

For spinning operators, three-point functions contain finitely many conformally allowed tensor structures. Ward identities determine the allowed structures; the coefficients multiplying those structures are again dynamical CFT data.

## Four-point functions and cross-ratios

Four points contain genuine conformal invariants. For four scalar primaries, the standard cross-ratios are

$$
u=\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2},
\qquad
v=\frac{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2}.
$$

It is also common to use $z,\bar z$ defined by

$$
u=z\bar z,
\qquad
v=(1-z)(1-\bar z).
$$

For identical scalar primaries $\phi$ of dimension $\Delta_\phi$, the Ward identities imply

$$
\left\langle
\phi(x_1)\phi(x_2)\phi(x_3)\phi(x_4)
\right\rangle
=
\frac{1}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\phi}x_{34}^{2\Delta_\phi}}
\,\mathcal G(u,v),
$$

where $\mathcal G(u,v)$ is not fixed by conformal symmetry alone. Other prefactor conventions are common, especially for non-identical scalars, but all of them leave a reduced function of the conformal cross-ratios.

This is where the bootstrap begins. Ward identities reduce the problem to functions of cross-ratios. The OPE expands those functions into conformal blocks. Crossing symmetry then says that different OPE channels must reconstruct the same reduced correlator.

## Two-dimensional Ward identity

In two-dimensional CFT, the holomorphic stress tensor $T(z)$ gives an especially compact local Ward identity. For primary fields $\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)$ of weights $(h_i,\bar h_i)$,

$$
T(z)\phi_i(w,\bar w)
\sim
\frac{h_i\phi_i(w,\bar w)}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{\partial_w\phi_i(w,\bar w)}{z-w}
+\cdots .
$$

Inserting this OPE into a correlator gives

$$
\langle T(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)\rangle
=
\sum_i
\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z-z_i)^2}
+\frac{1}{z-z_i}\partial_{z_i}
\right)
\langle\prod_i\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)\rangle.
$$

There is an analogous antiholomorphic identity with $\bar T(\bar z)$, $\bar h_i$, and $\partial_{\bar z_i}$.

This formula is the prototype for many later pages. Laurent-expanding $T(z)$ gives the Virasoro generators, the $T(z)T(w)$ OPE gives the central charge, and contour integrals of $T(z)$ generate local conformal transformations.

## Separated-point identities versus anomalies

The flat-space statement

$$
T^\mu{}_{\mu}=0
$$

is true at separated points in a conformal theory after choosing an improved stress tensor, when such a choice is available. This does not mean the trace is always zero as an operator identity on every background.

On curved backgrounds and in even spacetime dimensions, a CFT can have a Weyl anomaly. In two dimensions, for example,

$$
\langle T^\mu{}_{\mu}\rangle
=-\frac{c}{12}R
$$

in one common convention, where $R$ is the scalar curvature and $c$ is the central charge. Different sign conventions for curvature and stress-tensor normalization move signs and factors, so curved-space anomaly formulas should always be compared with the convention page.

The practical rule is:

$$
\text{global flat-space Ward identities}
\neq
\text{all local curved-space Weyl identities}.
$$

The former are the main kinematic input for this chapter. The latter belong partly to the trace-anomaly and curved-space CFT pages.

## Why Ward identities matter for bootstrap

The conformal bootstrap usually starts from correlation functions of primary operators, but Ward identities are quietly everywhere.

They imply the transformation rules of primaries. They constrain which tensor structures are allowed in correlators. They fix how conserved currents and the stress tensor appear in OPEs. They relate the normalization of the stress-tensor two-point function to the coefficient of $T_{\mu\nu}$ in the OPE of a scalar with itself. They explain why conserved currents lead to shortening conditions in the operator spectrum.

A bootstrap equation is not itself a Ward identity. It is a crossing equation obtained by applying the OPE in different channels. But the objects entering that equation — conformal blocks, allowed spins, conserved-current contributions, and unitarity bounds — all rely on representation theory and Ward identities.

## Common pitfalls

**Dropping contact terms too early.** At separated points $\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0$ and $T^\mu{}_{\mu}=0$ look simple. The full Ward identity lives as a distribution and includes delta functions at operator insertions.

**Confusing stress-tensor conservation with conformal invariance.** Conservation follows from translations. Conformal invariance also needs the trace condition, up to improvement and anomalies.

**Using scalar formulas for spinning operators.** Spinning primaries acquire a local rotation term. Omitting $\Sigma^{\mu\nu}$ gives wrong rotation and special conformal Ward identities.

**Mixing active and passive signs.** Some references define $\delta\mathcal O=+\mathcal D\mathcal O$ instead of $-\mathcal D\mathcal O$. Always check the translation Ward identity to see where the sign went.

**Forgetting anomalies.** Flat-space separated-point formulas do not erase Weyl anomalies on curved backgrounds or central terms in two-dimensional stress-tensor OPEs.

**Treating two-dimensional formulas as universal.** The holomorphic identity with $T(z)$ is special to two dimensions. Higher-dimensional CFT has stress-tensor Ward identities too, but not holomorphic contour algebra.

## Research status

- **Established:** Global and local conformal Ward identities, including stress-tensor contact terms, are standard consequences of symmetry and locality in CFT.
- **Convention-dependent:** Signs and factors depend on stress-tensor normalization, Euclidean versus Lorentzian signature, curvature conventions, and whether one uses active or passive transformations.
- **Active:** Ward identities for spinning operators, defects, boundaries, curved backgrounds, supersymmetric multiplets, energy correlators, and automated bootstrap systems remain active technical tools.
- **Subtle:** Trace identities involving anomalies or improvement terms require more care than the flat-space separated-point equations suggest.

## Exercises

### Exercise: Translation Ward identity

Use the scalar global Ward identity with $\xi^\mu=a^\mu$ to show that an $n$-point function depends only on relative positions.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For translations,

$$
\sigma=0,
\qquad
\mathcal D_i=a^\mu\partial_{i\mu}.
$$

The Ward identity is

$$
a^\mu\sum_i\partial_{i\mu}G=0.
$$

Since $a^\mu$ is arbitrary,

$$
\sum_i\partial_{i\mu}G=0.
$$

This says that $G$ is invariant under shifting all points by the same vector. Therefore it can depend only on differences such as $x_i-x_j$.

</details>

### Exercise: Dilatation Ward identity for a two-point function

Assume two scalar primaries have a two-point function $G(x_1,x_2)=f(|x_{12}|)$. Use the dilatation Ward identity to determine the radial dependence.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For dilatations,

$$
\left(x_1\cdot\partial_1+x_2\cdot\partial_2+\Delta_1+\Delta_2\right)G=0.
$$

Since $G=f(r)$ with $r=|x_{12}|$,

$$
(x_1\cdot\partial_1+x_2\cdot\partial_2)f(r)=r\frac{df}{dr}.
$$

Thus

$$
\left(r\frac{d}{dr}+\Delta_1+\Delta_2\right)f(r)=0,
$$

whose solution is

$$
f(r)=\frac{C}{r^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2}}.
$$

Special conformal invariance then further requires $C=0$ unless $\Delta_1=\Delta_2$.

</details>

### Exercise: Two-dimensional stress-tensor contour

Assume the OPE

$$
T(z)\phi(w,\bar w)
\sim
\frac{h\phi(w,\bar w)}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{\partial_w\phi(w,\bar w)}{z-w}.
$$

Show that

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_w dz\,\epsilon(z)T(z)
$$

acts on $\phi$ as

$$
\epsilon(w)\partial_w\phi+h(\partial_w\epsilon)\phi.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Insert the OPE into the contour integral:

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_w dz\,\epsilon(z)
\left[
\frac{h\phi(w,\bar w)}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{\partial_w\phi(w,\bar w)}{z-w}
\right].
$$

The simple pole gives

$$
\epsilon(w)\partial_w\phi.
$$

For the double pole, Cauchy's formula gives

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_w dz\,\frac{\epsilon(z)}{(z-w)^2}
=\partial_w\epsilon(w),
$$

so the double pole contributes

$$
h(\partial_w\epsilon)\phi.
$$

Adding both pieces gives

$$
\epsilon\partial_w\phi+h(\partial_w\epsilon)\phi,
$$

which is the holomorphic infinitesimal transformation law of a primary field.

</details>
