Conformal Symmetry is the chapter where the word “conformal” becomes operational. The previous chapter explained why conformal field theories matter; this chapter explains what transformations are being imposed, what algebra they generate, how local operators transform, and how Ward identities turn symmetry into equations for correlation functions.

The guiding condition is that a coordinate transformation preserves angles. In flat Euclidean space this means

$$
\frac{\partial x'^{\mu}}{\partial x^{\rho}}
\frac{\partial x'^{\nu}}{\partial x^{\sigma}}
\delta_{\mu\nu}
=
\Omega(x)^2\delta_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

Thus a conformal transformation is locally a rotation followed by a scale transformation. The scale factor may depend on position, but the shape of an infinitesimal angle is not distorted.

For CFT, this geometric statement is only the start. Once local operators are required to transform covariantly, conformal symmetry fixes two-point and three-point functions, organizes operators into multiplets, supplies the quadratic Casimir used to define conformal blocks, and gives the Ward identities that control stress-tensor insertions.

## Prerequisites

You should know the CFT-specific [Conventions and Notation](/cft-bootstrap/conventions/) and the basic entry chapter [What Is a CFT?](/cft-bootstrap/what-is-a-cft/). ## Landmarks

The first landmark is the metric definition of a conformal map. A diffeomorphism $x\mapsto x'=f(x)$ is conformal when

$$
f^*g=\Omega(x)^2 g.
$$

In flat Euclidean coordinates this becomes the Jacobian equation

$$
J(x)^T J(x)=\Omega(x)^2\mathbf 1,
\qquad
J^\mu{}_{\rho}(x)=\frac{\partial x'^\mu}{\partial x^\rho}.
$$

At each point, the Jacobian is a scale times an orthogonal matrix. This is the precise meaning of “angle-preserving.”

The second landmark is the list of elementary global conformal transformations:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{translations:}\quad &x'^\mu=x^\mu+a^\mu,\\
\text{rotations:}\quad &x'^\mu=R^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu,\\
\text{dilatations:}\quad &x'^\mu=\lambda x^\mu,\\
\text{inversion:}\quad &x'^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2},\\
\text{special conformal transformations:}\quad
&x'^\mu=\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The last line is not an arbitrary trick. It is inversion, translation, and inversion again. Inversions exchange the origin and infinity; special conformal transformations are translations seen through that exchange.

The third landmark is the infinitesimal conformal Killing equation. For a vector field $\xi^\mu(x)$ generating $x^\mu\mapsto x^\mu+\xi^\mu(x)$, angle preservation gives

$$
\partial_\mu\xi_\nu+\partial_\nu\xi_\mu
=
\frac{2}{d}(\partial\cdot\xi)\delta_{\mu\nu}
$$

in flat Euclidean signature. For $d\geq 3$, its solutions are only

$$
\xi^\mu(x)=a^\mu+\omega^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu+\lambda x^\mu+2(b\cdot x)x^\mu-b^\mu x^2.
$$

These four terms generate translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations.

The fourth landmark is the conformal group. In Euclidean signature and $d\geq 3$, the connected global conformal group is locally

$$
SO(d+1,1).
$$

In Lorentzian signature with $d$ spacetime dimensions, it is locally

$$
SO(d,2).
$$

The phrase “locally” matters because one often works with covering groups, quotients by centers, or compactified spaces depending on the operator, spin structure, and global question.

The fifth landmark is primary-operator covariance. For a scalar primary operator of scaling dimension $\Delta$,

$$
\mathcal O'(x')=\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}\mathcal O(x).
$$

For spinning primaries, the local rotation part of the Jacobian also acts on spin indices. This one formula explains why two-point and three-point functions are so rigid.

The sixth landmark is the Ward-identity bridge. Geometry becomes physics through conserved currents. The stress tensor generates spacetime transformations, and conformal Ward identities encode the consequences of conformal symmetry for correlators. In flat space, the tracelessness condition

$$
T^\mu{}_{\mu}=0
$$

is the local signal of conformal invariance, up to anomalies, improvements, and contact terms that must be handled honestly.

## Common confusions

Do not confuse a conformal transformation with an arbitrary position-dependent rescaling. A conformal coordinate transformation is a diffeomorphism whose pullback rescales the metric. A Weyl transformation rescales the metric at fixed coordinates. They are related, but not identical.

Do not confuse scale invariance with the full conformal group. Dilatations are only one generator. Special conformal transformations are the extra ingredient that makes CFT far more rigid than a merely scale-invariant theory.

Do not assume inversion is optional decoration. In $d\geq 3$, inversion is the simple discrete operation that produces special conformal transformations from translations. It is also the operation that makes the point at infinity hard to ignore.

Do not use the two-dimensional local story too early. In two dimensions, holomorphic maps give infinitely many local conformal transformations, and after quantization the Virasoro algebra appears. In $d\geq 3$, the global conformal algebra is finite-dimensional. The distinction is not cosmetic; it changes the subject.

Do not forget spin. Scalar formulas are the cleanest entry point, but spinning operators transform with a local rotation matrix. Conserved currents, the stress tensor, spinning correlators, and conformal blocks all depend on this extra structure.

Do not erase contact terms from Ward identities. Separated-point formulas are elegant, but symmetry variations of local operator insertions usually produce contact terms. Those terms are essential when deriving OPEs, charges, anomalies, and conservation laws.

## Boundaries

This chapter is the home of conformal symmetry as kinematics: transformations, groups, algebras, generators, Casimirs, and Ward identities. It does not classify all conformal representations; that is the job of [Operators, States, and Radial Quantization](/cft-bootstrap/operators-states-radial-quantization/). It does not derive all correlator forms; that is the job of [Correlation Functions](/cft-bootstrap/correlation-functions/). It does not build conformal blocks; that belongs to [Conformal Blocks](/cft-bootstrap/conformal-blocks/).

Two-dimensional local conformal symmetry is introduced here only enough to prevent confusion. The full Virasoro story belongs to [Two-Dimensional CFT](/cft-bootstrap/two-dimensional-cft/) and [Virasoro Symmetry and Central Charge](/cft-bootstrap/virasoro-central-charge/).

The differential geometry of conformal maps on general manifolds belongs mostly to the mathematical toolkit. This chapter uses curved-space language only when it clarifies flat-space CFT, Ward identities, anomalies, or radial quantization.

## Where to go next

After this chapter, read [Operators, States, and Radial Quantization](/cft-bootstrap/operators-states-radial-quantization/). That chapter turns the algebra into Hilbert-space structure: primaries, descendants, conformal multiplets, the state-operator correspondence, and unitarity bounds.

Then read [Correlation Functions](/cft-bootstrap/correlation-functions/) and [Operator Product Expansion](/cft-bootstrap/operator-product-expansion/). Conformal symmetry fixes the kinematic shape of correlators; the OPE supplies the dynamical coefficients; crossing symmetry later tests whether the data are mutually consistent.

If your goal is the numerical bootstrap, keep track of the Casimir and Ward-identity pages. They are the conceptual ancestors of conformal blocks, crossing equations, and the positivity conditions used by bootstrap algorithms.

- S. Rychkov, *EPFL Lectures on Conformal Field Theory in D ≥ 3 Dimensions*, Springer, 2017. Compact modern source for higher-dimensional conformal symmetry, operators, and correlation functions.
- D. Simmons-Duffin, “TASI Lectures on the Conformal Bootstrap,” arXiv:1602.07982. A clear bridge from conformal symmetry to modern bootstrap equations.
- D. Poland, S. Rychkov, and A. Vichi, “The Conformal Bootstrap: Theory, Numerical Techniques, and Applications,” *Reviews of Modern Physics* **91** (2019), arXiv:1805.04405. Review of conformal kinematics, blocks, unitarity constraints, and numerical bootstrap applications.
