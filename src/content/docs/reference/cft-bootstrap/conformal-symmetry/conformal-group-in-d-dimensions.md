## Summary

The global conformal group is the finite-dimensional group of angle-preserving transformations that act globally on the conformal compactification of flat space. For Euclidean CFT in $d\geq 3$, its connected component is locally

$$
SO(d+1,1).
$$

For Lorentzian CFT in $d$ spacetime dimensions, the corresponding connected group is locally

$$
SO(d,2).
$$

The word “locally” is doing real work. The Lie algebra is unambiguous, but the global group can be a quotient, a connected component, or a covering group depending on whether one keeps orientation reversal, time reversal, spinors, and the universal cover of Lorentzian time.

A useful way to remember the result is this:

$$
\text{conformal transformations of flat space}
=
\text{linear rotations of one higher-signature null cone, modulo scale}.
$$

This page explains why translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations close into $SO(d+1,1)$ or $SO(d,2)$, why the point at infinity belongs in the story, and how the special cases $d=1$ and $d=2$ fit with the general pattern.

## Prerequisites

You should know the definition of a [Conformal Transformation](/cft-bootstrap/conformal-symmetry/conformal-transformations/) and the CFT-specific [Conventions and Notation](/cft-bootstrap/conventions/). It is helpful to know the distinction between Euclidean and Lorentzian CFT from [Euclidean and Lorentzian CFT](/cft-bootstrap/what-is-a-cft/euclidean-and-lorentzian-cft/).

The only group theory assumed here is the idea of an orthogonal group preserving a bilinear form. The next page, [Conformal Algebra](/cft-bootstrap/conformal-symmetry/conformal-algebra/), gives the infinitesimal commutators.

## Core idea

A conformal transformation is locally a scale times a rotation or Lorentz transformation. In $d\geq 3$, this local requirement is so strong that the global conformal transformations of flat space are generated by only four types of transformations:

$$
\begin{array}{ccl}
\text{translations} &:& x^\mu\mapsto x^\mu+a^\mu,\\
\text{rotations or Lorentz transformations} &:& x^\mu\mapsto \Lambda^\mu{}_{\nu}x^\nu,\\
\text{dilatations} &:& x^\mu\mapsto \lambda x^\mu,\\
\text{special conformal transformations} &:&
 x^\mu\mapsto \dfrac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
\end{array}
$$

Counting parameters gives

$$
d+\frac{d(d-1)}{2}+1+d
=
\frac{(d+1)(d+2)}{2}.
$$

This is exactly the dimension of $SO(d+1,1)$ in Euclidean signature and $SO(d,2)$ in Lorentzian signature. The equality of dimensions is not proof by itself, but it is the first strong hint that the conformal group is secretly an orthogonal group in two extra dimensions.

:::note[The point at infinity is not optional]
Translations are smooth on $\mathbb R^d$, but inversion sends the origin to infinity. Since special conformal transformations are inversion–translation–inversion, the natural home of the conformal group is not bare $\mathbb R^d$ but its conformal compactification.
:::

## Setup and conventions

This page uses $d$ for the number of dimensions of the CFT. In Euclidean signature,

$$
x^2=\delta_{\mu\nu}x^\mu x^\nu.
$$

In Lorentzian signature, the site-wide convention is mostly minus,

$$
\eta_{\mu\nu}=\operatorname{diag}(+,-,\ldots,-),
\qquad
x^2=\eta_{\mu\nu}x^\mu x^\nu.
$$

The finite special conformal transformation convention is

$$
x'^\mu=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

Some references use $b\to -b$. The conformal group is the same; only the sign convention for the generator $K_\mu$ changes.

## The Euclidean global conformal group

In Euclidean signature, the metric is positive definite on $\mathbb R^d$:

$$
ds^2=\delta_{\mu\nu}dx^\mu dx^\nu.
$$

The elementary conformal transformations have conformal factors

$$
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{transformation} & \Omega(x) \\
\hline
x' = x+a & 1 \\
x' = Rx,
\; R^T R=1 & 1 \\
x' = \lambda x & \lambda \\
x' = x/x^2 & 1/x^2 \\
x' = \dfrac{x-bx^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2} & \dfrac{1}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}
\end{array}
$$

For $d\geq 3$, these transformations generate the finite global conformal group. Its connected orientation-preserving component is locally $SO(d+1,1)$.

More precisely, the full conformal group of the round $d$-sphere is

$$
O(d+1,1)/\{\pm 1\},
$$

while the connected component of the identity is usually denoted

$$
SO^+(d+1,1)/\{\pm 1\}
$$

when the quotient by the central element is relevant. Most CFT formulas only use the Lie algebra, so one often simply says “the Euclidean conformal group is $SO(d+1,1)$.” That shorthand is fine locally, but global questions may care about the quotient or cover.

### Why a sphere appears

Euclidean flat space $\mathbb R^d$ can be compactified by adding one point at infinity:

$$
\mathbb R^d\cup\{\infty\}\simeq S^d.
$$

The flat metric is not the round sphere metric, but the two are conformally related after stereographic projection. In coordinates $x^\mu$ on $\mathbb R^d$, the round sphere metric takes the form

$$
ds^2_{S^d}
=
\frac{4\,dx^2}{(1+x^2)^2}.
$$

Thus the flat metric and the sphere metric differ by a Weyl factor. Since CFT is designed to care about angles and local scale weights rather than absolute length scales, $S^d$ is the natural compact global stage for Euclidean conformal transformations.

### What inversion does globally

The inversion

$$
I:x^\mu\mapsto \frac{x^\mu}{x^2}
$$

is singular at $x=0$ if one insists on $\mathbb R^d$. On $S^d$, it simply exchanges the origin and the point at infinity. This is why the special conformal transformation

$$
K_b=I\circ T_{-b}\circ I
$$

is a perfectly natural global conformal transformation even though its flat-coordinate expression can have denominators.

The moral is practical: singularities in a flat-coordinate formula often mean “the transformation moved a point through infinity,” not “the conformal group broke.”

## The projective null-cone construction

The cleanest way to see $SO(d+1,1)$ is to embed $\mathbb R^d$ into a projective null cone in $\mathbb R^{d+1,1}$.

Introduce coordinates

$$
X^A=(X^+,X^-,X^\mu),
\qquad \mu=1,\ldots,d,
$$

with quadratic form

$$
X^2=-X^+X^-+\delta_{\mu\nu}X^\mu X^\nu.
$$

The null cone is

$$
X^2=0.
$$

The projective null cone identifies points related by nonzero rescalings:

$$
X^A\sim \alpha X^A,
\qquad \alpha\neq 0.
$$

Flat Euclidean space appears as the patch $X^+\neq 0$. A convenient section is

$$
X^A(x)=(1,x^2,x^\mu).
$$

Indeed,

$$
X(x)^2=-1\cdot x^2+x^\mu x_\mu=0.
$$

The group $SO(d+1,1)$ acts linearly on $X^A$ and preserves the null cone. Since rays are identified, this linear action becomes a nonlinear action on the coordinate $x^\mu$. The nonlinear conformal maps of $\mathbb R^d$ are therefore shadows of ordinary linear transformations in $\mathbb R^{d+1,1}$.

:::tip[Why embedding space is useful]
The embedding-space picture is not just group-theory decoration. It is the starting point for efficient formulas for spinning correlators, conformal blocks, and tensor structures. The price is one extra redundancy: $X^A\sim \alpha X^A$.
:::

## The Lorentzian conformal group

In Lorentzian signature, flat space has metric

$$
ds^2=\eta_{\mu\nu}dx^\mu dx^\nu,
\qquad
\eta_{\mu\nu}=\operatorname{diag}(+,-,\ldots,-).
$$

The conformal group preserves the light cone. If a displacement is null, $dx^2=0$, then after a conformal transformation

$$
dx'^2=\Omega(x)^2 dx^2=0.
$$

The connected Lorentzian conformal group in $d$ spacetime dimensions is locally

$$
SO(d,2).
$$

The two extra dimensions again come from the projective null-cone construction, but now the embedding space has signature $(d,2)$. The Lorentzian compactification is more subtle than the Euclidean one. Roughly, compactified Minkowski space is related to

$$
S^{d-1}\times S^1
$$

with an additional $\mathbb Z_2$ identification. In quantum theory one often passes to a universal cover to avoid closed timelike curves in the compactified time direction.

This is why Lorentzian CFT discussions often distinguish carefully between:

| Object | What it controls |
|---|---|
| $\mathfrak{so}(d,2)$ | The local conformal algebra. |
| $SO(d,2)$ | The finite connected group before spin and cover subtleties. |
| The universal cover of $SO(d,2)$ | The natural home for radial quantization and unitary representations with unrestricted scaling dimensions. |
| Causal domains such as diamonds and Rindler wedges | The physically relevant regions for modular flow, Lorentzian OPE limits, and lightcone bootstrap arguments. |

For many Euclidean bootstrap computations, the local algebra and Euclidean correlators are enough. For causality, analyticity, chaos bounds, thermal CFT, conformal Regge theory, and Lorentzian inversion formulas, these global Lorentzian distinctions become load-bearing.

## How the generators fit together

The finite-dimensional conformal group is generated by

$$
\{P_\mu,M_{\mu\nu},D,K_\mu\}.
$$

Their geometric meanings are:

| Generator | Finite transformation | Number of generators |
|---|---|---:|
| $P_\mu$ | translations | $d$ |
| $M_{\mu\nu}=-M_{\nu\mu}$ | rotations or Lorentz transformations | $d(d-1)/2$ |
| $D$ | dilatations | $1$ |
| $K_\mu$ | special conformal transformations | $d$ |

The total number is

$$
\frac{(d+1)(d+2)}{2},
$$

which agrees with

$$
\dim SO(d+1,1)=\dim SO(d,2)=\frac{(d+2)(d+1)}{2}.
$$

The roles of $P_\mu$ and $K_\mu$ are exchanged by inversion. Schematically,

$$
I P_\mu I = K_\mu,
\qquad
I K_\mu I = P_\mu,
\qquad
I D I = -D.
$$

This is the first sign of the radial-quantization adjoint relation $P_\mu^\dagger=K_\mu$, which becomes central in the derivation of unitarity bounds.

## Special dimensions

### One dimension

In one Euclidean dimension, every smooth monotone map preserves “angles” in the empty sense, so the local angle-preserving definition is degenerate. The finite global conformal group relevant to one-dimensional CFT or conformal quantum mechanics is usually taken to be the group of fractional linear transformations,

$$
x\mapsto \frac{ax+b}{cx+d},
\qquad ad-bc=1,
$$

namely $SL(2,\mathbb R)$ up to quotient. Its algebra is generated by

$$
P,\quad D,\quad K.
$$

This is the same three-generator pattern one obtains by setting $d=1$ in the finite conformal algebra.

### Two Euclidean dimensions

Two dimensions are special because local conformal transformations are holomorphic or antiholomorphic maps:

$$
z\mapsto f(z),
\qquad
\bar z\mapsto \bar f(\bar z).
$$

Locally this gives infinitely many conformal transformations. Globally on the Riemann sphere, however, nonsingular one-to-one conformal maps are Möbius transformations,

$$
z\mapsto \frac{az+b}{cz+d},
\qquad ad-bc\neq 0,
$$

with group

$$
PSL(2,\mathbb C).
$$

As a real Lie group,

$$
PSL(2,\mathbb C)\simeq SO^+(3,1).
$$

This matches the $SO(d+1,1)$ pattern for $d=2$ at the global level. The infinite-dimensional local enhancement belongs to [Two-Dimensional CFT](/cft-bootstrap/two-dimensional-cft/) and [Virasoro Symmetry and Central Charge](/cft-bootstrap/virasoro-central-charge/).

### Higher dimensions

For $d\geq 3$, there is no infinite-dimensional local conformal freedom in flat space. The conformal Killing equation has only the finite family generated by $P_\mu$, $M_{\mu\nu}$, $D$, and $K_\mu$.

This is one of the reasons higher-dimensional CFT is simultaneously rigid and difficult. Conformal symmetry strongly constrains correlators, but it does not solve the theory by itself. The missing dynamical information is the CFT data and the bootstrap consistency constraints.

## Relation to radial quantization

Remove the origin from Euclidean flat space and write

$$
r=e^\tau,
\qquad
x^\mu=r n^\mu,
\qquad n^\mu n_\mu=1.
$$

Then

$$
ds^2=dr^2+r^2d\Omega_{d-1}^2
=e^{2\tau}\left(d\tau^2+d\Omega_{d-1}^2\right).
$$

So

$$
\mathbb R^d\setminus\{0\}
$$

is conformal to the cylinder

$$
\mathbb R_\tau\times S^{d-1}.
$$

Dilatations of flat space become translations in cylinder time $\tau$. This is why the dilatation generator $D$ becomes the Hamiltonian in radial quantization. The conformal group then acts as the symmetry group organizing states on $S^{d-1}$.

This statement is simple, but it powers much of CFT:

$$
\text{local operators at the origin}
\longleftrightarrow
\text{states on }S^{d-1}.
$$

The detailed state-operator correspondence belongs to [State-Operator Correspondence](/cft-bootstrap/operators-states-radial-quantization/state-operator-correspondence/), but the group-theoretic seed is already here.

## Common pitfalls

Do not identify the conformal group of bare $\mathbb R^d$ with only maps that are nonsingular in one coordinate patch. Special conformal transformations can have denominators, and inversion is singular at the origin, but these are smooth operations on the conformal compactification.

Do not ignore the difference between Euclidean and Lorentzian real forms. Euclidean $d$-dimensional CFT uses $SO(d+1,1)$ locally. Lorentzian $d$-dimensional CFT uses $SO(d,2)$ locally. They are related by analytic continuation, but their global and causal structures are different.

Do not confuse the finite global conformal group in two dimensions with the infinite-dimensional local conformal group. On the sphere, the global group is $PSL(2,\mathbb C)$. Locally, holomorphic functions generate infinitely many transformations, and quantum mechanically the Virasoro algebra appears.

Do not treat “$SO(d+1,1)$” as a complete global statement. For spinors, projective actions, orientation, time orientation, and radial quantization, one may need a spin group, a quotient, a connected component, or a universal cover.

Do not mistake the embedding-space redundancy for a new physical dimension. The projective null cone is a representation tool. The physical CFT still lives in $d$ dimensions.

## Research status

The classification of global flat-space conformal transformations in $d\geq 3$ is standard. The identification of the Euclidean and Lorentzian conformal algebras with $\mathfrak{so}(d+1,1)$ and $\mathfrak{so}(d,2)$ is settled textbook material.

The active subtleties are mostly not about the local group. They concern where and how the group acts: Lorentzian domains, defects and boundaries, nontrivial manifolds, global quotients, spin structures, reflection positivity, modular flow, celestial limits, and the distinction between formal analytic continuation and genuine Lorentzian operator ordering.

For bootstrap applications, the group is the kinematic input. The hard dynamical question is not “what is the conformal group?” but “which spectra and OPE coefficients define a unitary associative operator algebra compatible with this group?”

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Count the generators

Show that translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations in $d$ dimensions have the same total number of generators as $SO(d+1,1)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Translations give $d$ generators. Rotations give the antisymmetric generators $M_{\mu\nu}$, so there are

$$
\frac{d(d-1)}{2}
$$

of them. Dilatations give one generator. Special conformal transformations give another $d$ generators. Thus

$$
d+\frac{d(d-1)}{2}+1+d
=
\frac{d^2-d+4d+2}{2}
=
\frac{(d+1)(d+2)}{2}.
$$

The orthogonal group in $d+2$ dimensions has

$$
\frac{(d+2)(d+1)}{2}
$$

generators, independent of signature. Hence the counts agree.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Check the null-cone section

Using the embedding-space metric

$$
X^2=-X^+X^-+\delta_{\mu\nu}X^\mu X^\nu,
$$

show that

$$
X^A(x)=(1,x^2,x^\mu)
$$

is null.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Substitute the section into the quadratic form:

$$
X(x)^2
=-(1)(x^2)+\delta_{\mu\nu}x^\mu x^\nu
=-x^2+x^2
=0.
$$

Therefore $X^A(x)$ lies on the null cone. Since the physical point is the ray $X\sim \alpha X$, choosing $X^+=1$ is a gauge choice for the projective redundancy.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why special conformal transformations need infinity

Consider

$$
x'^\mu=
\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

For Euclidean $b^2>0$, find the locus where the denominator vanishes. What does this mean geometrically?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The denominator is

$$
1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2
=b^2\left(x-\frac{b}{b^2}\right)^2.
$$

To see this, expand:

$$
b^2\left(x-\frac{b}{b^2}\right)^2
=b^2x^2-2b\cdot x+\frac{b^2}{b^2}
=b^2x^2-2b\cdot x+1.
$$

Thus the denominator vanishes at

$$
x^\mu=\frac{b^\mu}{b^2}.
$$

In the flat coordinate patch, that point is sent to infinity. On the conformal compactification, this is not a pathology; it means the transformation moves an ordinary point through the point at infinity.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Sphere metric from stereographic projection

The round metric on $S^d$ in stereographic coordinates is

$$
ds^2_{S^d}=\frac{4\,dx^2}{(1+x^2)^2}.
$$

Explain why this makes $S^d$ the conformal compactification of $\mathbb R^d$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The metric $ds^2_{S^d}$ differs from the flat metric $dx^2$ by a position-dependent Weyl factor:

$$
ds^2_{S^d}=\Omega(x)^2 dx^2,
\qquad
\Omega(x)=\frac{2}{1+x^2}.
$$

Thus stereographic coordinates identify the sphere minus one point with $\mathbb R^d$ in a way that preserves angles. The missing point is the point at infinity. Adding it compactifies $\mathbb R^d$ to $S^d$.

</details>
