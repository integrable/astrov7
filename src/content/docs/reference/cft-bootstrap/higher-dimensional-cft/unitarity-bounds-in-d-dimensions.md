## Summary

**Unitarity bounds** are lower bounds on scaling dimensions imposed by positive norm in radial quantization. In a unitary CFT, every physical state must have nonnegative norm. Since descendants are created by acting with translation generators $P_\mu$ on primary states, positivity of descendant norms restricts the allowed dimensions of primaries.

For scalar primaries in $d>2$,

$$
\Delta\ge \frac{d-2}{2},
$$

except for the identity operator, which has

$$
\Delta=0.
$$

For symmetric traceless spin-$\ell$ primaries with $\ell\ge1$,

$$
\Delta\ge \ell+d-2.
$$

Saturation has physical meaning. A spin-one primary at

$$
\Delta=d-1
$$

is conserved:

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu=0.
$$

A spin-two primary at

$$
\Delta=d
$$

is a conserved stress tensor when it has the correct Ward identity:

$$
\partial^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

Thus unitarity bounds are not merely inequalities. They explain why conserved currents and stress tensors are protected short multiplets, and they are among the basic assumptions behind numerical conformal bootstrap bounds.

$$
\text{local primary operator}\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad\text{primary state on }S^{d-1}.
$$

The page uses Euclidean conventions and assumes reflection positivity, which becomes Hilbert-space unitarity after radial quantization.

## Core idea

In radial quantization, the dilatation generator $D$ is the Hamiltonian on the cylinder $\mathbb R\times S^{d-1}$. A primary state satisfies

$$
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta|\mathcal O\rangle,
\qquad
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0.
$$

Descendants are generated by translations:

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

The Hermitian conjugation relation is

$$
P_\mu^\dagger=K_\mu.
$$

Therefore descendant norms are computed using the conformal algebra. If a descendant has negative norm, the representation cannot appear in a unitary CFT. Requiring all descendant norms to be nonnegative gives inequalities for $\Delta$.

At a bound, a descendant has zero norm. In a unitary theory, a zero-norm descendant decouples and becomes a null state. This is called **shortening**.

The slogan is

$$
\text{unitarity}+	ext{conformal algebra}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{dimension bounds and short multiplets}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

The Euclidean conformal algebra contains

$$
[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu,
\qquad
[D,K_\mu]=-K_\mu,
$$

and

$$
[K_\mu,P_\nu]=2\delta_{\mu\nu}D-2M_{\mu\nu},
$$

up to choices of factors of $i$ in Hermitian-generator conventions. The representation-theoretic content is that $P_\mu$ raises scaling dimension by one and $K_\mu$ lowers it by one.

A primary state is annihilated by all $K_\mu$:

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0.
$$

It also transforms in an irreducible representation of the rotation group $SO(d)$. The simplest spinful representations are symmetric traceless tensors of rank $\ell$:

$$
\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}.
$$

This page focuses on scalar and symmetric traceless tensor primaries. Spinors, forms, and mixed-symmetry tensors have analogous but representation-dependent bounds.

## Scalar bound

For a scalar primary $|\phi\rangle$, the level-one descendants are

$$
P_\mu|\phi\rangle.
$$

Their norm is proportional to $\Delta$:

$$
\langle \phi|K_\mu P_\nu|\phi\rangle
=2\Delta\delta_{\mu\nu}\langle\phi|\phi\rangle.
$$

This only implies

$$
\Delta\ge0.
$$

The stronger scalar bound comes from the level-two descendant

$$
P^2|\phi\rangle=P_\mu P^\mu|\phi\rangle.
$$

Its norm is proportional to

$$
\Delta\left(\Delta-\frac{d-2}{2}\right).
$$

For a non-identity scalar primary, positivity gives

$$
\Delta\ge\frac{d-2}{2}.
$$

The identity operator is the exception. It has

$$
\Delta_{\mathbf 1}=0,
$$

but its level-one descendants vanish:

$$
P_\mu|\mathbf 1\rangle=0.
$$

Thus the identity is a trivial short multiplet rather than a counterexample.

## Saturating the scalar bound

If a scalar primary saturates the bound,

$$
\Delta=\frac{d-2}{2},
$$

then the level-two descendant is null:

$$
P^2|\phi\rangle=0.
$$

In position space this is the equation

$$
\partial^2\phi=0.
$$

Thus a scalar saturating the bound behaves like a free massless scalar field. In a unitary local CFT, the presence of such an operator typically implies a decoupled free scalar sector, under standard assumptions.

This is a useful lesson: saturating a unitarity bound is not generic. It means a shortening relation has appeared, and shortening usually reflects conservation, free equations of motion, or protected structure.

For an interacting scalar order parameter such as the spin operator of the three-dimensional Ising CFT, one has

$$
\Delta_\sigma>\frac12
$$

in $d=3$, not equality. The strict inequality is the representation-theory shadow of interaction.

## Symmetric traceless spin bound

Let $\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}$ be a symmetric traceless spin-$\ell$ primary with $\ell\ge1$. The first level of descendants decomposes into several $SO(d)$ representations. One of them is the divergence descendant:

$$
P^{\mu_1}|\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}\rangle.
$$

This descendant has spin $\ell-1$. Its norm is proportional to

$$
\Delta-(\ell+d-2).
$$

Positivity gives

$$
\Delta\ge\ell+d-2,
\qquad
\ell\ge1.
$$

At saturation, the divergence descendant is null:

$$
\partial^{\mu_1}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0.
$$

Thus saturation means conservation.

For the first few spins:

| Spin | Bound | Saturation means |
|---:|---:|---|
| $1$ | $\Delta\ge d-1$ | conserved global-symmetry current |
| $2$ | $\Delta\ge d$ | conserved stress tensor, if Ward identity is correct |
| $\ell>2$ | $\Delta\ge \ell+d-2$ | conserved higher-spin current |

In interacting unitary CFTs with a unique stress tensor, conserved currents of spin greater than two are highly restrictive and typically imply free or generalized-free structure.

## Spinor and mixed-representation bounds

Spinors and mixed-symmetry tensors have their own bounds. For a spin-one-half primary in $d>2$, the standard bound is

$$
\Delta\ge\frac{d-1}{2}.
$$

At saturation, the Dirac equation appears as a null descendant:

$$
\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu\psi=0,
$$

with the gamma-matrix convention determined by the dimension and signature.

For $p$-form currents, mixed-symmetry tensors, and general $SO(d)$ Young diagrams, the bounds depend on the representation. The logic is the same: decompose level-one descendants into irreducible rotation representations and require the norm in each channel to be nonnegative.

This page emphasizes scalar and symmetric traceless tensor bounds because those are the most common in scalar conformal bootstrap. General spinning and mixed-symmetry bootstrap studies need the full representation-theory catalogue.

## Short multiplets

A conformal multiplet is called **long** when no descendant is null. It is called **short** when a descendant is null and is quotiented out.

Examples:

| Primary | Bound saturation | Null descendant | Physical meaning |
|---|---:|---|---|
| identity $\mathbf 1$ | $\Delta=0$ | $P_\mu|\mathbf 1\rangle=0$ | no nontrivial descendants |
| scalar $\phi$ | $\Delta=(d-2)/2$ | $P^2|\phi\rangle=0$ | free equation of motion |
| vector $J_\mu$ | $\Delta=d-1$ | $P^\mu|J_\mu\rangle=0$ | current conservation |
| spin two $T_{\mu\nu}$ | $\Delta=d$ | $P^\mu|T_{\mu\nu}\rangle=0$ | stress-tensor conservation |
| spin $\ell>2$ | $\Delta=\ell+d-2$ | divergence vanishes | higher-spin conservation |

Shortening removes states from the conformal family. In conformal blocks, this changes the representation and can produce special limits or conserved-current blocks.

This is the higher-dimensional analogue of null-state decoupling in two-dimensional Virasoro representations, but the algebra is finite-dimensional rather than Virasoro.

## Conserved currents and protected dimensions

The current and stress-tensor dimensions are protected because they are tied to symmetry:

$$
\Delta_J=d-1,
\qquad
\Delta_T=d.
$$

These values do not receive anomalous corrections along an exactly conformal theory. An interacting fixed point can have large anomalous dimensions for generic operators, but the conserved current and stress tensor remain at the unitarity bound.

For a global symmetry current,

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu^a=0.
$$

For the stress tensor,

$$
\partial^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

Their OPE coefficients with other operators are constrained by Ward identities. For example, the coefficient of $T$ in $\phi\times\phi$ is fixed by $\Delta_\phi$ and $C_T$, while the coefficient of $J^a$ in a charged operator OPE is fixed by the representation matrices and $C_J$.

Thus unitarity bounds are not isolated representation theory. They are directly visible in CFT data.

## Relevance for the bootstrap

Numerical bootstrap studies use unitarity bounds constantly. A four-point function is decomposed into conformal blocks labeled by dimensions and spins:

$$
(\Delta,\ell).
$$

In a unitary CFT, allowed exchanged operators must obey

$$
\Delta\ge\frac{d-2}{2}
\quad\text{for scalars,}
$$

and

$$
\Delta\ge\ell+d-2
\quad\text{for symmetric traceless spin }\ell\ge1.
$$

Reflection positivity also implies positivity of squared OPE coefficients in identical-scalar bootstrap equations:

$$
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\ge0.
$$

Together, these facts turn crossing into a convex positivity problem. This is the technical reason unitarity is so powerful in numerical bootstrap.

In nonunitary bootstrap problems, such as the Yang–Lee model or some statistical systems, these positivity assumptions fail. One can still write crossing equations, but the allowed-space geometry changes dramatically.

## Higher-spin currents and free theories

A conserved spin-$\ell$ current with $\ell>2$ has

$$
\Delta=\ell+d-2,
\qquad
\partial^{\mu_1}J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0.
$$

Such currents are rare in interacting unitary CFTs. Under standard assumptions in $d>2$, the existence of one exactly conserved higher-spin current implies an infinite tower of higher-spin currents and forces the theory to be free or very close to generalized free structure.

This is why bootstrap studies of interacting CFTs often impose a gap above the stress tensor in the spin-two sector or above the conserved current in spin-one sectors, and do not expect conserved spin-$\ell>2$ operators.

Approximate higher-spin currents can appear in large-$N$ theories. Their dimensions are close to the bound but not exactly at it:

$$
\Delta_\ell=\ell+d-2+\gamma_\ell,
\qquad
\gamma_\ell\ll1.
$$

The small anomalous dimension $\gamma_\ell$ measures weak breaking of higher-spin symmetry.

## Nonunitary theories

Nonunitary CFTs can violate unitarity bounds. This does not make them mathematically useless. Many nonunitary CFTs describe statistical systems, critical phenomena with complex weights, or analytic continuations in dimension or parameters.

For example, a nonunitary model can have scalar dimensions below

$$
\frac{d-2}{2}.
$$

What fails is the Hilbert-space positivity assumption used to derive the bound. Correlation functions may still be conformally covariant and crossing-symmetric, but OPE coefficients need not obey positivity.

Therefore, when a bootstrap paper says “we impose unitarity,” it is not a decorative assumption. It is a major structural input that excludes large classes of nonunitary solutions.

## Two-dimensional contrast

In two dimensions, the global conformal algebra gives bounds analogous to higher-dimensional ones. But unitary 2D CFT often has the stronger Virasoro algebra, and representation theory is organized by left and right weights

$$
h,\qquad \bar h.
$$

Unitarity requires

$$
h\ge0,
\qquad
\bar h\ge0
$$

for ordinary unitary Virasoro representations, with additional constraints in minimal models and extended chiral algebras.

The higher-dimensional formulas

$$
\Delta\ge\frac{d-2}{2},
\qquad
\Delta\ge\ell+d-2
$$

should therefore not be imported blindly into Virasoro representation theory. The logic is shared—positive descendant norms—but the symmetry algebra and representation labels differ.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not apply unitarity bounds to nonunitary theories.** They rely on positive norm. Nonunitary CFTs can and do violate them.

**Do not forget the identity exception to the scalar bound.** The identity has $\Delta=0$ because its descendants vanish, not because it is an ordinary scalar above the bound.

**Do not confuse a vector primary with a conserved current.** A vector primary is conserved only if it saturates $\Delta=d-1$.

**Do not call every spin-two operator the stress tensor.** A generic spin-two primary can have $\Delta>d$. The stress tensor is the conserved spin-two operator with the correct Ward identity.

**Do not expect conserved higher-spin currents in generic interacting CFTs.** Exact higher-spin conservation in $d>2$ is extraordinarily restrictive.

**Do not use symmetric traceless bounds for mixed-symmetry tensors without checking the representation.** General $SO(d)$ representations have their own bounds.

**Do not confuse shortening with a perturbative equation of motion.** A null descendant is an operator statement in the CFT representation, not merely a classical field equation.

## Research status

The basic unitarity bounds for scalar, spinor, symmetric traceless, and general conformal representations are established. Their interpretation as positive-norm constraints and shortening conditions is standard.

Active work concerns applications and refinements: mixed-symmetry representations, defect CFT, superconformal shortening, noninteger dimension, Lorentzian positivity, nonlocal theories, numerical-bootstrap assumptions, and near-conserved higher-spin currents in large-$N$ CFTs.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Use the conformal algebra to show that the level-one scalar descendant has norm proportional to $\Delta$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For a scalar primary $|\phi\rangle$,

$$
K_\mu|\phi\rangle=0,
\qquad
D|\phi\rangle=\Delta|\phi\rangle.
$$

The norm matrix of the level-one descendants is

$$
\langle P_\mu\phi|P_\nu\phi\rangle
=\langle \phi|K_\mu P_\nu|\phi\rangle.
$$

Using

$$
[K_\mu,P_\nu]=2\delta_{\mu\nu}D-2M_{\mu\nu},
$$

and the fact that $M_{\mu\nu}$ annihilates a scalar primary at the origin, we get

$$
\langle \phi|K_\mu P_\nu|\phi\rangle
=2\Delta\delta_{\mu\nu}\langle\phi|\phi\rangle.
$$

Positivity at level one gives $\Delta\ge0$. The stronger scalar bound comes from level two.

</details>

### Exercise 2

What happens when a scalar saturates

$$
\Delta=\frac{d-2}{2}?
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

At this value, the level-two descendant

$$
P^2|\phi\rangle
$$

has zero norm. In a unitary theory, zero-norm states decouple, so

$$
P^2|\phi\rangle=0.
$$

In position space this becomes

$$
\partial^2\phi=0.
$$

Thus the scalar satisfies a free-field equation of motion. Under standard assumptions, this indicates a free scalar sector.

</details>

### Exercise 3

Use the spin-$\ell$ unitarity bound to find the dimensions of conserved spin-one and spin-two currents.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For symmetric traceless spin $\ell\ge1$,

$$
\Delta\ge\ell+d-2.
$$

For spin one,

$$
\Delta\ge1+d-2=d-1.
$$

A conserved current saturates this:

$$
\Delta_J=d-1.
$$

For spin two,

$$
\Delta\ge2+d-2=d.
$$

A conserved stress tensor saturates this:

$$
\Delta_T=d.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4

Why do unitarity bounds matter for numerical bootstrap?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In a unitary CFT, exchanged operators in a four-point function must have dimensions above the appropriate unitarity bound. For identical scalar correlators, reflection positivity also implies

$$
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\ge0.
$$

These two facts make the crossing equation a positivity problem over a restricted space of possible operator dimensions and spins. Numerical bootstrap algorithms exploit precisely this structure to rule out spectra or bound CFT data.

Without unitarity, the positivity assumptions fail and the numerical problem has a different character.

</details>
