## Summary

In dimensions greater than two, conformal symmetry is powerful but finite-dimensional. A conformal transformation is a coordinate transformation that preserves angles, or equivalently changes the flat metric only by a local scale factor:

$$
\delta_{\rho\sigma}\frac{\partial x'^\rho}{\partial x^\mu}\frac{\partial x'^\sigma}{\partial x^\nu}
=\Omega(x)^2\delta_{\mu\nu}.
$$

For $d>2$, the solutions are generated by translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations. The Euclidean conformal group is locally

$$
SO(d+1,1),
$$

and its algebra is generated by

$$
P_\mu,\quad M_{\mu\nu},\quad D,\quad K_\mu.
$$

This is the main structural difference from two-dimensional CFT. In two dimensions, local conformal transformations are arbitrary holomorphic maps and lead to Virasoro symmetry. In $d>2$, conformal symmetry is finite, but it still fixes two-point and three-point functions up to finite CFT data, organizes local operators into conformal multiplets, and supplies the kinematic backbone of the conformal bootstrap.

This page explains the finite conformal group, the conformal Killing equation, the generators and algebra, the compactification picture, the primary-descendant representation structure, and the practical consequences for higher-dimensional CFT.

## Core idea

In any dimension, a conformal transformation preserves angles. Infinitesimally, write

$$
x^\mu\mapsto x^\mu+\epsilon^\mu(x).
$$

The conformal Killing equation in flat Euclidean space is

$$
\partial_\mu\epsilon_\nu+\partial_\nu\epsilon_\mu
=\frac{2}{d}(\partial\cdot\epsilon)\delta_{\mu\nu}.
$$

For $d=2$, this equation has infinitely many local solutions. For $d>2$, differentiating the equation further forces $\epsilon^\mu(x)$ to be at most quadratic in $x$:

$$
\epsilon^\mu(x)
=a^\mu+\omega^\mu{}_\nu x^\nu+\lambda x^\mu+\left(b^\mu x^2-2x^\mu b\cdot x\right).
$$

These four terms are translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations. That finite list is the higher-dimensional conformal group.

The slogan is

$$
\text{angle preservation in }d>2
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
P_\mu,\ M_{\mu\nu},\ D,\ K_\mu\text{ only}.
$$

This finite symmetry is less miraculous than Virasoro symmetry, but it is still strong enough to make CFT a nonperturbative framework.

## Setup and conventions

We work first in Euclidean flat space $\mathbb R^d$ with metric

$$
ds^2=\delta_{\mu\nu}dx^\mu dx^\nu.
$$

The conformal algebra generators are

| Generator | Transformation | Parameters |
|---|---|---:|
| $P_\mu$ | translations | $d$ |
| $M_{\mu\nu}=-M_{\nu\mu}$ | rotations | $d(d-1)/2$ |
| $D$ | dilatations | $1$ |
| $K_\mu$ | special conformal transformations | $d$ |

The total number of generators is

$$
d+\frac{d(d-1)}{2}+1+d=\frac{(d+1)(d+2)}{2},
$$

which is the dimension of $SO(d+1,1)$.

In Lorentzian signature, the connected conformal group is locally

$$
SO(d,2),
$$

with $d$ now the spacetime dimension. The algebraic formulas are obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric $\delta_{\mu\nu}$ with the Lorentzian metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}$. In this page, Euclidean formulas are used unless explicitly stated.

## Finite transformations

The finite conformal transformations of flat $\mathbb R^d$ for $d>2$ are generated by the following maps.

Translations:

$$
x'^\mu=x^\mu+a^\mu.
$$

Rotations:

$$
x'^\mu=R^\mu{}_\nu x^\nu,
\qquad
R\in SO(d).
$$

Dilatations:

$$
x'^\mu=\lambda x^\mu,
\qquad
\lambda>0.
$$

Inversion:

$$
x'^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2}.
$$

Special conformal transformations can be built as inversion, translation, inversion:

$$
x'^\mu=\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

The denominator can vanish at finite $x$, which is a clue that conformal transformations are most naturally defined on the conformal compactification rather than on all of $\mathbb R^d$ without extra points.

The inversion is discrete and not connected to the identity, but it is extremely useful. It exchanges translations and special conformal transformations:

$$
K_\mu=I P_\mu I,
$$

schematically, where $I$ denotes inversion.

## Infinitesimal generators

As differential operators acting on coordinates, a convenient Euclidean representation is

$$
P_\mu=-i\partial_\mu,
$$

$$
M_{\mu\nu}=i(x_\mu\partial_\nu-x_\nu\partial_\mu),
$$

$$
D=-ix^\mu\partial_\mu,
$$

and

$$
K_\mu=-i\left(2x_\mu x^\nu\partial_\nu-x^2\partial_\mu\right),
$$

for scalar functions. For fields with spin, $M_{\mu\nu}$ also includes a spin generator acting on tensor or spinor indices.

The important structural commutators are

$$
[D,P_\mu]=iP_\mu,
\qquad
[D,K_\mu]=-iK_\mu,
$$

and

$$
[K_\mu,P_\nu]=2i(\delta_{\mu\nu}D-M_{\mu\nu}),
$$

with rotations acting in the expected way:

$$
[M_{\mu\nu},P_\rho]
=i(\delta_{\mu\rho}P_\nu-\delta_{\nu\rho}P_\mu),
$$

and similarly for $K_\rho$. The signs depend on whether one includes factors of $i$ in the generator definitions. The invariant content is the grading by $D$: translations raise dimension and special conformal generators lower it in radial quantization.

## The SO(d+1,1) form

The conformal algebra can be packaged as the Lie algebra of $SO(d+1,1)$. Introduce embedding-space coordinates

$$
X^A,
\qquad
A=0,1,\ldots,d+1,
$$

with metric of signature $(d+1,1)$. The conformal compactification is described by the projective null cone

$$
X^2=0,
\qquad
X^A\sim\lambda X^A,
\qquad
\lambda\ne0.
$$

The group $SO(d+1,1)$ acts linearly on $X^A$ and therefore nonlinearly on physical coordinates $x^\mu$ after choosing a section of the projective cone.

This embedding-space viewpoint is not only aesthetic. It makes conformal covariance transparent and is one of the standard tools for constructing spinning correlators and conformal blocks in higher dimensions.

For a first pass, remember the following:

$$
\text{nonlinear conformal transformations on }x^\mu
\quad=\quad
\text{linear rotations on the projective light cone}.
$$

## Conformal compactification

Conformal transformations can send finite points to infinity. Therefore the natural home of Euclidean conformal symmetry is not exactly $\mathbb R^d$, but its conformal compactification.

For Euclidean CFT, the compactified space is topologically

$$
S^d.
$$

One way to see this is through stereographic projection:

$$
\mathbb R^d\cup\{\infty\}\simeq S^d.
$$

The conformal group acts globally on the sphere. In radial quantization, this compactification is closely related to the cylinder

$$
\mathbb R\times S^{d-1},
$$

obtained by writing

$$
r=e^\tau.
$$

Then dilatations on the plane become translations along cylinder time $\tau$. The generator $D$ becomes the Hamiltonian on the cylinder.

This is the representation-theoretic reason scaling dimensions behave like energies:

$$
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta_\mathcal O|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

## Primary operators

A local operator at the origin is called a primary if it is annihilated by the special conformal generators:

$$
[K_\mu,\mathcal O(0)]=0.
$$

It also has definite scaling dimension and spin:

$$
[D,\mathcal O(0)]=i\Delta\mathcal O(0),
$$

and transforms in a representation of rotations $SO(d)$.

Descendants are obtained by acting with translations:

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}\mathcal O(0).
$$

In position space, these are derivatives of the primary and its descendants:

$$
\partial_{\mu_1}\cdots\partial_{\mu_n}\mathcal O(0).
$$

The conformal multiplet is therefore

$$
\mathcal O,\quad \partial_\mu\mathcal O,\quad \partial_\mu\partial_\nu\mathcal O,\quad\ldots .
$$

In $d>2$, these are multiplets of the finite conformal algebra, not Virasoro towers. This is one of the main bookkeeping differences from two-dimensional CFT.

## Transformation of primary fields

A scalar primary of dimension $\Delta$ transforms under a finite conformal map $x\mapsto x'$ as

$$
\mathcal O'(x')=\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}\mathcal O(x),
$$

where

$$
ds'^2=\Omega(x)^2ds^2.
$$

Equivalently, in correlation functions,

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x'_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x'_n)\rangle
=\prod_i \Omega(x_i)^{-\Delta_i}
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle
$$

for scalar primaries, up to the active versus passive convention used for fields.

For spinning primaries, there is an additional local rotation matrix acting on tensor or spinor indices. This is why higher-dimensional spinning correlators involve tensor structures. A vector primary, for example, does not merely pick up a scalar conformal factor; its index is rotated by the Jacobian after removing the local scale.

The scalar case teaches the core idea. The spinning case is where real higher-dimensional bootstrap work becomes technical.

## Consequences for two- and three-point functions

Conformal symmetry fixes scalar two-point functions to be diagonal in scaling dimension:

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=\frac{C_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}},
\qquad
\Delta_i=\Delta_j.
$$

With an orthonormal operator basis, one usually writes

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}.
$$

Scalar three-point functions are fixed up to an OPE coefficient:

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\mathcal O_3(x_3)\rangle
=\frac{C_{123}}{|x_{12}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2-\Delta_3}|x_{23}|^{\Delta_2+\Delta_3-\Delta_1}|x_{13}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_3-\Delta_2}}.
$$

Four-point functions are not fixed. They depend on cross-ratios. For four scalar insertions, the two independent cross-ratios can be written

$$
u=\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2},
\qquad
v=\frac{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2}.
$$

The dynamical problem of CFT begins with these functions of cross-ratios. The bootstrap asks which such functions are compatible with the OPE, crossing, and unitarity.

## Higher-dimensional versus two-dimensional CFT

The contrast with two dimensions is worth making explicit.

| Feature | $d=2$ | $d>2$ |
|---|---|---|
| Local conformal maps | Infinite-dimensional holomorphic maps | Finite-dimensional maps generated by $P,M,D,K$ |
| Chiral factorization | Often central | Not generic |
| Stress-tensor algebra | Virasoro with central charge $c$ | Finite conformal algebra; stress tensor is an operator, not a Virasoro generator tower |
| Exactly solved models | Many rational examples | Rare; usually perturbative, large-$N$, supersymmetric, holographic, or bootstrapped |
| Spinning data | Often organized by holomorphic weights $(h,\bar h)$ | Organized by $SO(d)$ or Lorentz representations |
| Bootstrap emphasis | Exact and algebraic in many 2D cases | Numerical, analytic large-spin, perturbative, and structural |

This table should not be read as saying that higher-dimensional CFT is weaker or less interesting. It is harder because the symmetry is less overwhelming. It is also where many physically central critical points live.

## Scale versus conformal invariance

A scale-invariant theory is invariant under

$$
x^\mu\mapsto\lambda x^\mu.
$$

A conformal theory is invariant under the larger group generated by translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations.

In many unitary relativistic QFTs with a discrete spectrum and reasonable stress-tensor properties, scale invariance is expected to imply conformal invariance. But the statement is subtle. There are assumptions, counterexamples in weakened settings, and dimension-dependent caveats.

For this chapter, the working assumption is that we are studying CFTs: theories with conformal symmetry, not merely scale symmetry. Pages on RG fixed points and scale-versus-conformal enhancement explain the question separately.

A good practical rule is:

$$
\text{RG fixed point}\quad\text{often}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\text{CFT},
$$

but when precision matters, state the assumptions under which the enhancement is being used.

## Lorentzian continuation

Much of CFT kinematics is cleanest in Euclidean signature. Bootstrap equations for Euclidean four-point functions, radial quantization, and reflection positivity are often formulated there.

Lorentzian CFT has additional structure:

- causal ordering;
- Wightman functions;
- commutators and lightcone singularities;
- Regge limits;
- averaged null energy and collider bounds;
- chaos and causality constraints.

The conformal group becomes locally

$$
SO(d,2).
$$

The same algebraic generators appear, but the reality conditions and causal interpretation change. A Euclidean formula should not be analytically continued blindly across branch cuts. Lorentzian continuation is a controlled procedure, not a typography change.

## Why this matters for bootstrap

The conformal bootstrap starts with a simple claim: the OPE is associative. For a four-point function, applying the OPE in different pairings must give the same answer. Conformal symmetry supplies the kinematic functions, called conformal blocks, and the unknown CFT data appear as dimensions and OPE coefficients.

For identical scalar fields, the schematic equation is

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2 G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
=
\sum_{\mathcal O}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2 G_{\Delta,\ell}(v,u),
$$

with positive coefficients in a unitary theory.

Every symbol here uses the finite conformal symmetry explained on this page:

| Bootstrap ingredient | Conformal-symmetry origin |
|---|---|
| $\Delta$ | Eigenvalue of dilatations. |
| $\ell$ | Spin representation under rotations. |
| $G_{\Delta,\ell}$ | Contribution of one conformal multiplet. |
| OPE coefficients | Three-point data allowed by conformal symmetry. |
| Positivity | Reflection positivity and Hilbert-space norms. |
| Crossing | Associativity of the local operator algebra. |

Thus higher-dimensional conformal symmetry is the kinematic grammar of modern bootstrap calculations.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not import Virasoro intuition into $d>2$ without checking it.** Higher-dimensional conformal symmetry is finite-dimensional. There are no generic holomorphic currents $T(z)$ or Virasoro descendants.

**Do not confuse rotations with the full conformal group.** Local operators transform under rotations, but conformal multiplets are built using $P_\mu$, $D$, and $K_\mu$ as well.

**Do not treat $K_\mu$ as optional.** The primary condition $K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0$ is what makes conformal representation theory a highest-weight theory.

**Do not forget spin structures.** Scalar formulas are the entry point. Spinning operators require tensor structures and local rotations under conformal maps.

**Do not assume every scale-invariant model is automatically a CFT.** The enhancement is expected in many important settings, but it is not a definition-free theorem in complete generality.

**Do not analytically continue Euclidean formulas casually.** Lorentzian correlators have ordering, branch cuts, causal support, and positivity conditions that are invisible in a purely Euclidean expression.

**Do not confuse CFT data with a Lagrangian.** Many higher-dimensional CFTs are defined more sharply by their operator spectrum and OPE coefficients than by a known microscopic action.

## Research status

The finite conformal group and its action on local operators in $d>2$ are established. The representation theory, conformal Ward identities, and kinematic form of scalar correlation functions are standard.

Active work concerns deeper consequences and broader settings: proving scale-to-conformal enhancement under optimal assumptions, developing efficient spinning correlator technology, understanding Lorentzian causality constraints, classifying allowed CFT data, and applying bootstrap methods to strongly coupled gauge theories, critical matter systems, and holographic CFTs.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Count the number of generators of the conformal group in $d>2$ and show that it equals the dimension of $SO(d+1,1)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The generators are translations, rotations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations:

$$
d+\frac{d(d-1)}{2}+1+d.
$$

This gives

$$
2d+1+\frac{d(d-1)}{2}
=\frac{4d+2+d^2-d}{2}
=\frac{d^2+3d+2}{2}
=\frac{(d+1)(d+2)}{2}.
$$

The group $SO(d+1,1)$ acts on a vector space of dimension $d+2$, and the dimension of $SO(N)$ or $SO(p,q)$ is

$$
\frac{N(N-1)}{2}.
$$

With $N=d+2$, this is

$$
\frac{(d+2)(d+1)}{2},
$$

which matches.

</details>

### Exercise 2

Show that a special conformal transformation can be generated by inversion, translation, and inversion.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Let inversion be

$$
I:x^\mu\mapsto \frac{x^\mu}{x^2}.
$$

Apply inversion, then translation by $-b^\mu$, then inversion again. After the first inversion,

$$
y^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2}.
$$

After translation,

$$
z^\mu=y^\mu-b^\mu=\frac{x^\mu}{x^2}-b^\mu.
$$

Now invert again:

$$
x'^\mu=\frac{z^\mu}{z^2}.
$$

Compute

$$
z^2=\frac{1}{x^2}-\frac{2b\cdot x}{x^2}+b^2
=\frac{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}{x^2}.
$$

Therefore

$$
x'^\mu
=\frac{x^\mu/x^2-b^\mu}{(1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2)/x^2}
=\frac{x^\mu-b^\mu x^2}{1-2b\cdot x+b^2x^2}.
$$

This is the standard special conformal transformation in the convention used on this page.

</details>

### Exercise 3

Use scale invariance and translation invariance to determine the power of a scalar two-point function.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Translation invariance implies

$$
\langle \mathcal O(x)\mathcal O(0)\rangle=f(|x|).
$$

If $\mathcal O$ has dimension $\Delta$, then under $x\mapsto\lambda x$ the two operators contribute a factor $\lambda^{-2\Delta}$. Therefore

$$
f(\lambda |x|)=\lambda^{-2\Delta}f(|x|).
$$

The solution is

$$
f(|x|)=\frac{C}{|x|^{2\Delta}}.
$$

Conformal invariance further fixes two-point functions of nonidentical primaries to vanish unless their dimensions and spin representations are compatible.

</details>

### Exercise 4

Explain why $K_\mu$ lowers scaling dimension in radial quantization.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The conformal algebra contains

$$
[D,K_\mu]=-iK_\mu
$$

in the convention with Hermitian generators. Ignoring the convention-dependent factor of $i$, this says that acting with $K_\mu$ lowers the eigenvalue of $D$ by one unit.

In radial quantization, $D$ is the Hamiltonian on the cylinder. Therefore $K_\mu$ acts like a lowering operator. A primary state is a lowest-energy state in its conformal multiplet and is annihilated by all $K_\mu$:

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0.
$$

Descendants are generated by $P_\mu$, which raises the scaling dimension.

</details>
