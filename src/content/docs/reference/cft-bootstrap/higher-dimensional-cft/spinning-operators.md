## Summary

A **spinning operator** is a local operator that transforms nontrivially under rotations. In a Euclidean CFT in $d$ dimensions, a primary operator is labeled by a scaling dimension $\Delta$ and a representation of $SO(d)$. The simplest spinning primaries are symmetric traceless tensors,

$$
\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}(x),
\qquad
\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=\mathcal O_{(\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell)},
\qquad
\delta^{\mu_1\mu_2}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_\ell}=0.
$$

They are often called spin-$\ell$ operators. Important examples include global symmetry currents $J_\mu$, the stress tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$, fermion bilinears, and the spinful operators exchanged in scalar four-point functions.

Spinning operators are unavoidable in higher-dimensional CFT. Even if one studies only scalar external operators, the OPE of two scalars contains spinful primaries:

$$
\phi(x)\phi(0)\sim
\sum_{\mathcal O_{\Delta,\ell}}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}\,C_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}(x,\partial)\mathcal O^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}(0).
$$

This page explains the representation labels, the polarization-vector notation, how spinning primaries transform, how tensor structures enter correlation functions, and why conserved currents and stress tensors are special short multiplets.

## Core idea

In two-dimensional CFT, spin is often encoded by holomorphic and antiholomorphic weights:

$$
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
J=h-\bar h.
$$

In $d>2$, there is no generic holomorphic factorization. A local operator instead carries a representation of the rotation group $SO(d)$. The most common bootstrap representation is a symmetric traceless tensor of rank $\ell$, abbreviated STT:

$$
\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}.
$$

The conformal multiplet is generated by derivatives:

$$
\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell},\quad
\partial_\nu\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell},\quad
\partial_{\nu_1}\partial_{\nu_2}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell},\quad\ldots .
$$

Thus a higher-dimensional primary is labeled schematically by

$$
(\Delta,\rho),
$$

where $\rho$ is an irreducible representation of $SO(d)$. For identical scalar four-point functions, only symmetric traceless exchange usually appears, but mixed-correlator and spinning-correlator bootstrap systems require more general representations.

The slogan is

$$
\text{higher-dimensional primary}=
\text{dimension}+\text{rotation representation}+\text{descendant tower}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

Let $\mathcal O_A(x)$ be a primary operator, where $A$ is an index in a finite-dimensional representation $\rho$ of $SO(d)$. Under a conformal transformation $x\mapsto x'$, the flat metric changes by

$$
ds'^2=\Omega(x)^2ds^2.
$$

A scalar primary transforms as

$$
\mathcal O'(x')=\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}\mathcal O(x).
$$

A spinning primary also transforms by a local rotation matrix:

$$
\mathcal O'_A(x')
=\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}R_A{}^B(x)\mathcal O_B(x).
$$

Here $R(x)$ is the $SO(d)$ rotation obtained from the Jacobian after removing the local scale factor. For tensors, it acts on each index. For spinors, it acts through the spin representation of the local rotation.

For symmetric traceless tensors, one may write

$$
\mathcal O'_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}(x')
=\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}
R_{\mu_1}{}^{\nu_1}(x)\cdots R_{\mu_\ell}{}^{\nu_\ell}(x)
\mathcal O_{\nu_1\cdots\nu_\ell}(x).
$$

This is the higher-dimensional replacement for the simple holomorphic Jacobian factors of two-dimensional CFT.

## Symmetric traceless tensors

The rank-$\ell$ symmetric traceless tensor representation is the most common spin representation in bootstrap applications. It is built from tensors satisfying

$$
\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=\mathcal O_{(\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell)},
$$

and

$$
\delta^{\mu_i\mu_j}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0.
$$

For small $\ell$:

| Spin | Operator type | Examples |
|---:|---|---|
| $0$ | scalar | order parameters, energy operators, singlet scalars |
| $1$ | vector | global symmetry currents when conserved |
| $2$ | symmetric traceless tensor | stress tensor when conserved |
| $\ell\ge3$ | higher-spin STT | generic exchanged operators; conserved only in special theories |

A generic spin-$\ell$ primary has dimension $\Delta$. If it saturates the unitarity bound for $\ell\ge1$,

$$
\Delta=\ell+d-2,
$$

then it is conserved:

$$
\partial^{\mu_1}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0.
$$

For $\ell=1$, this is an ordinary conserved current with $\Delta=d-1$. For $\ell=2$, this is the stress tensor with $\Delta=d$.

## Polarization-vector notation

Carrying many tensor indices is cumbersome. For symmetric traceless tensors, introduce an auxiliary polarization vector $z^\mu$ and define

$$
\mathcal O(x,z)=z^{\mu_1}\cdots z^{\mu_\ell}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}(x).
$$

Tracelessness is imposed by taking

$$
z^2=0.
$$

This works because traces are proportional to powers of $z^2$. The original tensor can be recovered by expanding in $z$ and projecting back to the symmetric traceless part.

In this notation, a two-point function can be written compactly. Define

$$
I_{\mu\nu}(x)=\delta_{\mu\nu}-2\frac{x_\mu x_\nu}{x^2}.
$$

Then the two-point function of a spin-$\ell$ primary is

$$
\langle \mathcal O(x,z_1)\mathcal O(0,z_2)\rangle
=\frac{C_{\mathcal O}\left(z_1\cdot I(x)\cdot z_2\right)^\ell}{(x^2)^\Delta}.
$$

With unit-normalized operators, one chooses $C_{\mathcal O}=1$ or a related convention-dependent normalization. The tensor $I_{\mu\nu}(x)$ is the inversion tensor and supplies the correct rotation of indices between the two points.

## Why tensor structures appear

For scalars, conformal symmetry fixes a three-point function up to one number:

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1\mathcal O_2\mathcal O_3\rangle
\propto C_{123}.
$$

For spinning operators, there can be several independent conformally invariant tensor structures. The general form is

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1\mathcal O_2\mathcal O_3\rangle
=\sum_a \lambda_{123}^{(a)}\,\mathcal T_a(x_i,z_i),
$$

where $\mathcal T_a$ are independent tensor structures and $\lambda_{123}^{(a)}$ are OPE coefficients.

This is a major source of complexity in higher-dimensional CFT. A spinning correlator is not specified by a single coefficient unless symmetry or the choice of operators makes the tensor-structure space one-dimensional.

For example, correlators involving currents and stress tensors can have several parity-even and, in special dimensions, parity-odd structures. Conservation Ward identities reduce the number of independent coefficients but rarely remove all structure.

The bootstrap consequence is direct: spinning bootstrap equations are vector- or matrix-valued not only because of global symmetries, but also because of tensor structures.

## Scalar-scalar-spinning three-point functions

A very important special case is the three-point function of two scalar primaries and one symmetric traceless spin-$\ell$ primary:

$$
\langle \phi_1(x_1)\phi_2(x_2)\mathcal O_{\Delta,\ell}(x_3,z)\rangle.
$$

Conformal symmetry fixes the position dependence up to one coefficient. In a standard form,

$$
\langle \phi_1(x_1)\phi_2(x_2)\mathcal O_{\Delta,\ell}(x_3,z)\rangle
=\frac{\lambda_{12\mathcal O}\,\left(z\cdot V_{3,12}\right)^\ell}
{(x_{12}^2)^{(\Delta_1+\Delta_2-\Delta+\ell)/2}
(x_{23}^2)^{(\Delta_2+\Delta-\Delta_1+\ell)/2}
(x_{13}^2)^{(\Delta_1+\Delta-\Delta_2+\ell)/2}},
$$

where

$$
V_{3,12}^\mu=\frac{x_{31}^\mu}{x_{31}^2}-\frac{x_{32}^\mu}{x_{32}^2}.
$$

For identical scalars, exchange symmetry imposes a selection rule:

$$
\phi\times\phi\quad\text{contains only even }\ell
$$

for symmetric traceless spin-$\ell$ primaries in a bosonic theory. This even-spin selection rule is one reason identical-scalar bootstrap equations are comparatively clean.

## Conservation and shortening preview

A generic vector primary has dimension $\Delta$. In a unitary CFT, a spin-one symmetric traceless primary obeys

$$
\Delta\ge d-1.
$$

If

$$
\Delta=d-1,
$$

then the descendant

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu
$$

has zero norm and decouples. The operator is conserved:

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu=0.
$$

Similarly, a spin-two primary satisfies

$$
\Delta\ge d,
$$

and saturation gives the stress tensor conservation equation

$$
\partial^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

This is the higher-dimensional analogue of null-state shortening, but it is governed by the finite conformal algebra rather than the Virasoro algebra.

Conserved higher-spin currents with $\ell>2$ are even more restrictive. In a unitary CFT with a unique stress tensor and ordinary assumptions, the existence of an exactly conserved higher-spin current typically forces the theory to be free or generalized free in a strong sense. Therefore, interacting CFTs normally have no conserved currents with spin greater than two.

## Spinors and mixed-symmetry operators

Not every spinning operator is a symmetric traceless tensor. In $d\ge3$, operators may transform in spinor, antisymmetric tensor, or mixed-symmetry representations.

Examples include:

| Representation type | Example | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| spinor | $\psi_\alpha$ | Fermionic CFTs and supersymmetric theories. |
| antisymmetric tensor | $B_{\mu\nu}$ | Higher-form currents and form-valued operators. |
| mixed symmetry | $\mathcal O_{[\mu\nu]\rho\cdots}$ | Appears in general spinning OPEs and defects. |
| symmetric traceless | $\mathcal O_{(\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell)}$ traceless | Most common in scalar four-point bootstrap. |

Mixed-symmetry representations are described by Young diagrams of $SO(d)$, with trace constraints. They matter in general spinning correlators, in theories with fermions, in four-dimensional CFT with Lorentz representations $(j,\bar j)$, and in defect CFT where the preserved rotation group is smaller.

This page emphasizes symmetric traceless tensors because they are the basic workhorse of the scalar bootstrap. The general representation-theoretic language is the same: choose a primary representation, build descendants with $P_\mu$, impose unitarity, and decompose correlators into allowed tensor structures.

## Embedding-space preview

The embedding-space formalism packages conformal symmetry linearly. Physical points $x^\mu$ are lifted to null rays

$$
X^2=0,
\qquad
X\sim\lambda X
$$

in $\mathbb R^{d+1,1}$. Spinning operators are represented with polarization variables in embedding space, and conformally covariant tensor structures are built from simple inner products.

The benefit is not just beauty. Embedding space gives an algorithmic way to construct correlation functions with spin. It helps separate conformal covariance from representation-theoretic constraints such as transversality, gauge redundancy in polarization variables, tracelessness, and permutation symmetry.

For a first pass, one does not need embedding space to understand spinning operators. But most modern formulas for spinning conformal blocks, weight-shifting operators, and conformal collider structures use either embedding space or an equivalent index-free formalism.

## Parity-odd structures

In some dimensions, there are tensor structures involving the Levi-Civita tensor. These are called parity-odd structures. For example, in three dimensions, one may build invariants using

$$
\epsilon_{\mu\nu\rho}.
$$

Parity-odd structures appear in CFTs that do not preserve parity, such as Chern–Simons–matter theories. They also appear in current and stress-tensor three-point functions in three dimensions.

The existence of parity-odd structures depends on dimension and operator spins. The practical rule is simple:

$$
\text{spinning correlator data}=	ext{parity-even coefficients}+\text{possible parity-odd coefficients}.
$$

When setting up a bootstrap problem, one must specify whether parity is assumed. If parity is imposed, parity-odd structures are absent. If parity is not imposed, they may have to be included.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not use two-dimensional spin notation in higher dimensions without translation.** In $d>2$, spin is a representation of $SO(d)$ or the Lorentz group, not just $h-\bar h$.

**Do not assume every spinful operator is symmetric traceless.** STT operators dominate scalar bootstrap examples, but spinors, forms, and mixed-symmetry tensors are real and important.

**Do not confuse tensor structures with different operators.** One triple of operators can admit several independent three-point tensor structures, each with its own coefficient.

**Do not forget local rotations under conformal maps.** Spinning primaries transform with both a conformal scale factor and a rotation matrix acting on indices.

**Do not treat conservation as generic.** A vector primary is conserved only when it saturates the spin-one unitarity bound. A spin-two primary is the stress tensor only if it is conserved and has the correct Ward identity.

**Do not ignore parity assumptions.** In three dimensions especially, parity-odd tensor structures are common in theories with Chern–Simons interactions.

**Do not assume scalar correlator formulas generalize by adding indices casually.** Spinning correlators require a basis of conformally invariant tensor structures and careful treatment of redundancies.

## Research status

The representation theory of spinning primary operators under the finite conformal group is established. Tensor structures for many correlators, especially in embedding-space and weight-shifting formalisms, are standard tools.

Active work focuses on making spinning technology more efficient and less error-prone: spinning numerical bootstrap systems, defects and boundaries, Lorentzian inversion formulas with spin, conformal collider constraints, parity-odd structures, mixed-symmetry exchange, and applications to gauge theories and supersymmetric CFTs.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Show that a rank-$\ell$ symmetric traceless tensor can be encoded by a polynomial $\mathcal O(x,z)$ with $z^2=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Define

$$
\mathcal O(x,z)=z^{\mu_1}\cdots z^{\mu_\ell}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}(x).
$$

If $\mathcal O$ has a trace, then some term contains $\delta_{\mu_i\mu_j}z^{\mu_i}z^{\mu_j}=z^2$. Setting $z^2=0$ removes all trace contributions. Therefore the polynomial on the null cone depends only on the symmetric traceless part of the tensor.

Conversely, expanding a homogeneous degree-$\ell$ polynomial in $z$ and projecting to the part modulo $z^2$ recovers the symmetric traceless tensor.

</details>

### Exercise 2

For identical scalar operators $\phi$, explain why only even-spin symmetric traceless operators appear in the $\phi\times\phi$ OPE.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The three-point function

$$
\langle \phi(x_1)\phi(x_2)\mathcal O_{\Delta,\ell}(x_3,z)\rangle
$$

contains the factor

$$
(z\cdot V_{3,12})^\ell.
$$

Exchanging $x_1$ and $x_2$ sends

$$
V_{3,12}\mapsto -V_{3,12}.
$$

Therefore the three-point function gains a factor $(-1)^\ell$. Since the two scalars are identical bosonic operators, the correlator must be symmetric under exchange. Hence $(-1)^\ell=1$, so $\ell$ must be even.

</details>

### Exercise 3

Write the two-point function of a unit-normalized vector primary of dimension $\Delta$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A vector primary is the $\ell=1$ case of the symmetric traceless formula. With unit normalization,

$$
\langle V_\mu(x)V_\nu(0)\rangle
=\frac{I_{\mu\nu}(x)}{(x^2)^\Delta},
$$

where

$$
I_{\mu\nu}(x)=\delta_{\mu\nu}-2\frac{x_\mu x_\nu}{x^2}.
$$

The inversion tensor carries the vector index at $0$ to the vector index at $x$ in a conformally covariant way.

</details>

### Exercise 4

Why does conservation imply that a spin-one current has dimension $d-1$ in a unitary CFT?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For a spin-one primary in a unitary CFT, the unitarity bound is

$$
\Delta\ge d-1.
$$

The divergence descendant

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu
$$

has norm proportional to $\Delta-(d-1)$. If $\Delta>d-1$, this descendant is a genuine nonzero descendant. If $\Delta=d-1$, the descendant has zero norm and decouples, giving

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu=0.
$$

Thus a conserved spin-one primary saturates the bound and has dimension $d-1$.

</details>
