## Summary

This page fixes the CFT-specific conventions used throughout the CFT and Bootstrap volume. The default language is **Euclidean CFT in $d$ dimensions**, with local operators inserted at points $x\in\mathbb R^d$ and correlation functions written without time ordering.

The default data of a local CFT is written schematically as

$$
\text{CFT data}
=\{\mathcal O_i,\Delta_i,\rho_i,\lambda_{ijk}\},
$$

where $\mathcal O_i$ are primary operators, $\Delta_i$ are scaling dimensions, $\rho_i$ are spin or global-symmetry representations, and $\lambda_{ijk}$ are OPE coefficients in the normalization fixed below. Descendants are generated by acting with translations on primaries.

For a real scalar primary normalized by

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}},
$$

the scalar three-point coefficient and the leading scalar OPE coefficient are the same number:

$$
\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)
\supset
\lambda_{ijk}|x|^{\Delta_k-\Delta_i-\Delta_j}\mathcal O_k(0).
$$

This identification is convenient, but it is not automatic. It relies on the two-point normalization, on an operator basis, and on the absence of unresolved degeneracies.

:::note[Default CFT convention]
Unless a page says otherwise, this volume uses Euclidean signature, scalar two-point functions normalized to one, conformal blocks normalized by their leading OPE behavior, and crossing equations written with all identity and exchanged-primary contributions included in the same channel sum.
:::

## Prerequisites

You should know the site-wide [Conventions and Normalizations](/foundations/conventions-and-normalizations/), especially natural units, Lorentzian mostly-minus signature, Euclidean continuation, Fourier conventions, and stress-tensor sign care. You should also be comfortable with local operators, correlation functions, Noether currents, stress tensors, and the basic idea of an RG fixed point.

This page is a convention reference, not a first introduction. The meaning of conformal transformations, primaries, descendants, radial quantization, conformal blocks, and crossing is developed in the later chapters.

## Core idea

CFT conventions are unusually load-bearing because the same physical datum can appear as a two-point normalization, a three-point coefficient, an OPE coefficient, a block coefficient, or a bootstrap variable. Changing one normalization moves factors elsewhere.

The safest workflow is:

1. normalize two-point functions;
2. define primary operators and their representation labels;
3. define OPE coefficients using the chosen two-point basis;
4. choose conformal-block normalization;
5. write crossing equations;
6. only then compare numbers with another paper or code.

Most apparent disagreements in CFT formulas are not physics. They are basis choices, tensor-structure choices, factors of $2$, or different block normalizations in disguise.

## Setup and conventions

The default spacetime for this volume is flat Euclidean space $\mathbb R^d$ with metric $\delta_{\mu\nu}$. We write

$$
x^2=\delta_{\mu\nu}x^\mu x^\nu,
\qquad
|x|=\sqrt{x^2},
\qquad
x_{ij}^\mu=x_i^\mu-x_j^\mu,
\qquad
x_{ij}=|x_i-x_j|.
$$

The total number of Euclidean coordinates is denoted by $d$. In Lorentzian discussions, the site-wide mostly-minus convention is used unless stated otherwise:

$$
\eta_{\mu\nu}=\operatorname{diag}(+,-,\ldots,-).
$$

For Euclidean CFT on $\mathbb R^d$, the connected conformal group is locally

$$
SO(d+1,1).
$$

For Lorentzian CFT in $d$ spacetime dimensions, it is locally

$$
SO(d,2).
$$

Pages usually work with the Lie algebra and the identity component, avoiding global subtleties unless compactification, spin, defects, or projective representations matter.

The generators are denoted

$$
P_\mu,
\qquad
M_{\mu\nu},
\qquad
D,
\qquad
K_\mu,
$$

for translations, rotations or Lorentz transformations, dilatations, and special conformal transformations. In radial quantization, $D$ is the Hamiltonian on the cylinder.

## Operators and CFT data

A local primary operator is written $\mathcal O_i(x)$. Its scaling dimension is $\Delta_i$. Its spin representation under the Euclidean rotation group $SO(d)$ is denoted $\rho_i$. For symmetric traceless tensors, we often write the spin as an integer $\ell$.

A scalar primary obeys

$$
\mathcal O(x)\mapsto \Omega(x')^{\Delta}\mathcal O(x'),
\qquad x'=f^{-1}(x),
$$

under a conformal map $x\mapsto f(x)$ with local scale factor $\Omega$. Spinning primaries also transform with the corresponding rotation matrix in their spin representation.

A descendant is obtained by acting with translations:

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}\mathcal O(0)
\quad\leftrightarrow\quad
\partial_{\mu_1}\cdots\partial_{\mu_n}\mathcal O(0).
$$

The phrase “sum over operators” in a conformal-block expansion means “sum over primary conformal multiplets” unless descendants are explicitly mentioned.

### Identity operator

The identity operator is denoted $\mathbf 1$ and normalized by

$$
\langle \mathbf 1\rangle=1.
$$

It has

$$
\Delta_{\mathbf 1}=0,
\qquad
\ell_{\mathbf 1}=0.
$$

For an orthonormal scalar basis, the identity contribution to the OPE is

$$
\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)
\supset
\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}\mathbf 1.
$$

This term is often the first check that two-point and OPE conventions are mutually consistent.

## Euclidean and Lorentzian correlation functions

Euclidean correlators are written as expectation values

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle.
$$

They are ordinary Euclidean-ordered Schwinger functions away from coincident points. Lorentzian correlators require an ordering prescription: Wightman, time-ordered, retarded, advanced, out-of-time-ordered, or thermal. Those are not interchangeable.

When a CFT page says “correlator” without qualification, it means a Euclidean correlator at separated points. Contact terms, anomalies, curved backgrounds, and Lorentzian orderings are stated explicitly when relevant.

Reflection positivity is the Euclidean counterpart of unitarity. In the bootstrap, it is what ultimately gives positive Hilbert-space norms and nonnegative coefficients such as $\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2$ in suitable identical-scalar four-point functions.

## Two-point functions

For real scalar primaries, the default orthonormal two-point convention is

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}.
$$

If two scalar primaries have different dimensions, their two-point function vanishes. If several scalar primaries have the same dimension and quantum numbers, the two-point matrix must be diagonalized before the simple $\delta_{ij}$ convention applies.

For complex operators, we write

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j^\dagger(0)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}.
$$

For a spin-one primary $J_\mu$ of dimension $\Delta$, the standard tensor structure is built from

$$
I_{\mu\nu}(x)=\delta_{\mu\nu}-2\frac{x_\mu x_\nu}{x^2}.
$$

A commonly used normalization is

$$
\langle J_\mu(x)J_\nu(0)\rangle
=\frac{C_J}{|x|^{2\Delta}}I_{\mu\nu}(x),
$$

with $\Delta=d-1$ for a conserved current. The value of $C_J$ is convention-dependent unless the current normalization is fixed by a charge convention.

For the stress tensor,

$$
\langle T_{\mu\nu}(x)T_{\rho\sigma}(0)\rangle
=\frac{C_T}{|x|^{2d}}\mathcal I_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(x),
$$

where

$$
\mathcal I_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}(x)
=\frac12\left(I_{\mu\rho}I_{\nu\sigma}+I_{\mu\sigma}I_{\nu\rho}\right)
-\frac1d\delta_{\mu\nu}\delta_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

This equation defines the $C_T$ convention used in higher-dimensional pages unless a page explicitly rescales it.

## Three-point functions

For scalar primaries, conformal symmetry fixes the separated-point three-point function up to one coefficient:

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\mathcal O_3(x_3)\rangle
=\frac{\lambda_{123}}
{x_{12}^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2-\Delta_3}
 x_{23}^{\Delta_2+\Delta_3-\Delta_1}
 x_{13}^{\Delta_1+\Delta_3-\Delta_2}}.
$$

With the scalar two-point normalization above, $\lambda_{123}$ is the scalar OPE coefficient. For spinning operators, there can be several independent tensor structures. Then we write

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1\mathcal O_2\mathcal O_3\rangle
=\sum_a \lambda_{123}^{(a)}\,\mathcal T_a(x_i),
$$

where $a$ labels tensor structures. A page using spinning correlators must state its tensor-structure basis.

The OPE coefficients obey selection rules from spin, internal symmetries, parity, charge conjugation, supersymmetry, and other symmetries. For example, in a theory with a $\mathbb Z_2$ symmetry, an odd number of $\mathbb Z_2$-odd scalar operators has vanishing three-point coefficient.

## Operator product expansion

The OPE of two scalar primaries is written schematically as

$$
\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)
=\sum_k \lambda_{ijk}\,C_{ijk}(x,\partial)\mathcal O_k(0),
$$

where $C_{ijk}(x,\partial)$ is a differential operator fixed by conformal symmetry once the exchanged primary and external operators are specified.

For a scalar exchanged primary, the leading term is

$$
\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)
\supset
\lambda_{ijk}|x|^{\Delta_k-\Delta_i-\Delta_j}\mathcal O_k(0).
$$

Descendant terms are not optional decoration. They are what make a whole conformal multiplet contribute to a correlator and are packaged by conformal blocks.

The OPE is an operator statement inside correlation functions and within a convergence domain. In radial quantization, convergence is controlled by the nearest other operator insertion. This is one of the reasons CFT OPEs are sharper than purely asymptotic short-distance expansions in generic QFT.

## Four-point functions and cross-ratios

For four points in $d\geq 2$, conformal invariance leaves two independent cross-ratios. We use

$$
u=\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2},
\qquad
v=\frac{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}{x_{13}^2x_{24}^2}.
$$

Here $v$ is a cross-ratio, not a velocity. Pages that use velocities write them as words or with explicit context.

For four identical scalar primaries $\phi$ of dimension $\Delta_\phi$, the reduced correlator is defined by

$$
\langle \phi(x_1)\phi(x_2)\phi(x_3)\phi(x_4)\rangle
=\frac{1}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\phi}x_{34}^{2\Delta_\phi}}\mathcal G(u,v).
$$

In terms of complex variables $z,\bar z$, when such a parametrization is useful,

$$
u=z\bar z,
\qquad
v=(1-z)(1-\bar z).
$$

In Euclidean signature, $\bar z$ is the complex conjugate of $z$ in the usual conformal frame. In Lorentzian signature, $z$ and $\bar z$ are often analytically continued as independent variables.

## Conformal blocks

For identical scalar external operators, the $12\to34$ OPE channel decomposition is written

$$
\mathcal G(u,v)
=\sum_{\mathcal O}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v),
$$

where the sum runs over primary operators $\mathcal O$ appearing in the $\phi\times\phi$ OPE. The identity operator is included with

$$
G_{0,0}(u,v)=1,
\qquad
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathbf 1}=1.
$$

The scalar conformal blocks $G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)$ are normalized by their leading OPE behavior in the $u\to0$ channel. Concretely, they begin as

$$
G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
\sim
u^{\Delta/2}\times\text{a spin-dependent angular structure}
$$

up to conventions for the angular polynomial. Pages that use explicit block formulas state the exact normalization, especially in two dimensions, in noninteger dimension, or for spinning external operators.

For non-identical scalars, spinning operators, global symmetries, or multiple tensor structures, the block decomposition becomes a vector or matrix equation. Then the coefficients are products of OPE coefficients in specified tensor-structure bases, not simply $\lambda^2$.

## Crossing equations

For four identical scalars, crossing under $x_1\leftrightarrow x_3$ gives

$$
v^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(u,v)
=u^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(v,u).
$$

Using the block decomposition, this becomes

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O\in\phi\times\phi}
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2
\left[
 v^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
-u^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(v,u)
\right]
=0.
$$

This is the canonical starting point for the identical-scalar bootstrap. In a unitary theory with Hermitian $\phi$, the coefficients satisfy

$$
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2\geq0
$$

for exchanged primaries in this channel, after an appropriate real basis is chosen.

For global symmetries, mixed correlators, conserved currents, stress tensors, supersymmetry, defects, or boundaries, crossing is a coupled system. The same principle remains: different OPE decompositions of the same correlator must agree.

## Unitarity bounds and conserved currents

In unitary CFTs with $d\geq3$, symmetric traceless primary operators obey the standard bounds

$$
\Delta\geq\frac{d-2}{2}\qquad (\ell=0,\ \mathcal O\neq\mathbf 1),
$$

and

$$
\Delta\geq \ell+d-2\qquad (\ell\geq1).
$$

Saturation of the spin bound for $\ell\geq1$ corresponds to a conserved current:

$$
\partial^{\mu_1}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0,
\qquad
\Delta=\ell+d-2.
$$

For example,

$$
\Delta_J=d-1
$$

for a conserved spin-one current, and

$$
\Delta_T=d
$$

for the stress tensor.

Two-dimensional CFT has its own representation-theoretic unitarity conditions in terms of left and right weights and, when Virasoro symmetry is used, the central charge and highest-weight representations.

## Stress tensor and central charges

The stress tensor is denoted $T_{\mu\nu}$. In flat Euclidean CFT at separated points,

$$
T^\mu_{\ \mu}=0,
\qquad
\partial^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0,
$$

up to contact terms, anomalies, improvement subtleties, and curved-background terms.

The higher-dimensional stress-tensor two-point normalization is $C_T$, defined above. It is positive in unitary theories. Its numerical value depends on convention, so comparisons across papers must check the precise two-point tensor structure and free-field normalization.

In two-dimensional CFT, the holomorphic stress tensor has the OPE

$$
T(z)T(0)
\sim
\frac{c}{2z^4}+\frac{2T(0)}{z^2}+\frac{\partial T(0)}{z},
$$

where $c$ is the Virasoro central charge. The antiholomorphic stress tensor $\bar T(\bar z)$ has central charge $\bar c$. For ordinary parity-invariant unitary CFTs, $c=\bar c$.

## Two-dimensional notation

In two Euclidean dimensions, we use

$$
z=x^1+ix^2,
\qquad
\bar z=x^1-ix^2,
$$

and

$$
\partial=\partial_z=\frac12(\partial_1-i\partial_2),
\qquad
\bar\partial=\partial_{\bar z}=\frac12(\partial_1+i\partial_2).
$$

A two-dimensional primary has left and right weights $(h,\bar h)$, with

$$
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h.
$$

The holomorphic stress tensor acts on a primary field by

$$
T(z)\mathcal O(w,\bar w)
\sim
\frac{h\,\mathcal O(w,\bar w)}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{\partial_w\mathcal O(w,\bar w)}{z-w}.
$$

The mode expansion convention is

$$
T(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\frac{L_n}{z^{n+2}},
\qquad
L_n=\oint_0\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,z^{n+1}T(z).
$$

The Virasoro algebra is

$$
[L_m,L_n]
=(m-n)L_{m+n}+\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

Antiholomorphic modes $\bar L_n$ obey the same formula with $\bar c$ and commute with the holomorphic modes in an ordinary local theory.

## Global symmetries and sectors

Internal global symmetries are denoted by $G$. Operators carry representations $R_i$ of $G$, and OPE coefficients are invariant tensors in

$$
R_i\otimes R_j\otimes R_k.
$$

When a four-point function is decomposed into symmetry sectors, crossing becomes a vector equation. For example, in $O(N)$ models, the product of two fundamental scalars decomposes into singlet, traceless-symmetric, and antisymmetric channels. Bootstrap equations and positivity conditions are then imposed sector by sector.

Discrete symmetries are treated the same way: they impose selection rules. A $\mathbb Z_2$-odd scalar $\sigma$ and a $\mathbb Z_2$-even scalar $\epsilon$ obey

$$
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\sigma}=0,
\qquad
\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}\neq0\ \text{in general}.
$$

This notation is common in Ising-CFT pages.

## Numerical bootstrap notation

A linear functional is usually denoted $\alpha$. It acts on functions of cross-ratios or on vectors of such functions. In an identical-scalar bootstrap equation, one often defines

$$
F_{\Delta,\ell}^{\Delta_\phi}(u,v)
= v^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(u,v)
-u^{\Delta_\phi}G_{\Delta,\ell}(v,u),
$$

so crossing becomes

$$
\sum_{\mathcal O}\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2
F_{\Delta,\ell}^{\Delta_\phi}(u,v)=0.
$$

A functional excluding a proposed spectrum typically satisfies positivity conditions such as

$$
\alpha\!
\left(F_{\Delta,\ell}^{\Delta_\phi}\right)\geq0
$$

for all allowed exchanged operators, together with a sign condition on the identity contribution. Exact inequalities depend on the convention used to move the identity block to one side of the equation. Numerical pages must state that choice.

When a page quotes numerical bounds or islands, it should specify the correlators used, assumptions on gaps or symmetries, derivative order or truncation parameters, block convention, and software when relevant. Current software and algorithm names belong on computational pages, not in the definition of CFT.

## Common pitfalls

**Comparing OPE coefficients without comparing two-point normalization.** Rescaling an operator rescales its three-point coefficients. A number like $\lambda_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}$ is meaningful only after the two-point normalizations of $\sigma$ and $\epsilon$ have been fixed.

**Forgetting tensor structures.** Scalar correlators hide a lot of bookkeeping. Spinning correlators can have several independent tensor structures, and each structure has its own coefficient and crossing equation.

**Dropping the identity block.** The identity contribution is usually the simplest term and the easiest to lose when rearranging crossing equations. Its sign is especially important in numerical bootstrap positivity arguments.

**Treating Euclidean and Lorentzian correlators as the same object.** Euclidean correlators are analytic data. Lorentzian physics requires continuation with a specified ordering, branch, and $i\epsilon$ prescription.

**Assuming every scale-invariant theory is automatically a CFT.** Many physical fixed points of interest are conformal, but the implication from scale invariance to conformal invariance depends on assumptions. State those assumptions when the distinction matters.

**Using $c$, $C_T$, and central charge interchangeably.** In two dimensions, $c$ is the Virasoro central charge. In higher dimensions, $C_T$ is a stress-tensor two-point normalization; anomaly coefficients and sphere free energies are related but distinct pieces of data.

## Research status

The conventions on this page are textbook-standard, but not universal. Different papers normalize stress tensors, currents, generators, conformal blocks, and OPE coefficients differently. The page therefore fixes a local convention for this volume rather than claiming that other conventions are wrong.

The active part is mostly computational and comparative. Modern numerical bootstrap calculations depend on block conventions, derivative bases, semidefinite-programming setup, precision choices, and assumptions about gaps or sectors. Those choices must be recorded on pages that quote bounds, islands, or spectra.

Lorentzian CFT, defect CFT, supersymmetric CFT, and logarithmic or nonunitary CFTs require extra conventions beyond this page. Their chapters extend this notation locally.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Scaling check for the scalar three-point function

Check that

$$
\langle \mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\mathcal O_3(x_3)\rangle
=\frac{\lambda_{123}}
{x_{12}^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2-\Delta_3}
 x_{23}^{\Delta_2+\Delta_3-\Delta_1}
 x_{13}^{\Delta_1+\Delta_3-\Delta_2}}
$$

has the correct scaling under $x_i\mapsto \Lambda x_i$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under $x_i\mapsto \Lambda x_i$, every distance $x_{ij}$ gains a factor of $\Lambda$. The total power in the denominator is

$$
(\Delta_1+\Delta_2-\Delta_3)
+(\Delta_2+\Delta_3-\Delta_1)
+(\Delta_1+\Delta_3-\Delta_2)
=\Delta_1+\Delta_2+\Delta_3.
$$

Therefore the correlator scales as

$$
\Lambda^{-(\Delta_1+\Delta_2+\Delta_3)},
$$

which is exactly the product of the three primary scaling factors.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Identity coefficient in the OPE

Assume

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}.
$$

Use the OPE to determine the identity contribution to $\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The identity operator has expectation value $\langle\mathbf 1\rangle=1$. The identity term in the OPE must reproduce the two-point function after taking the vacuum expectation value.

Thus

$$
\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)
\supset
\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}\mathbf 1.
$$

Taking the expectation value gives exactly

$$
\langle \mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{|x|^{2\Delta_i}}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Identical-scalar crossing equation

Starting from

$$
\langle \phi_1\phi_2\phi_3\phi_4\rangle
=\frac{1}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\phi}x_{34}^{2\Delta_\phi}}\mathcal G(u,v),
$$

where all four operators are the same scalar $\phi$, show that exchange symmetry implies

$$
v^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(u,v)
=u^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(v,u).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Exchange $x_1\leftrightarrow x_3$. The same correlator can be written as

$$
\langle \phi_1\phi_2\phi_3\phi_4\rangle
=\frac{1}{x_{32}^{2\Delta_\phi}x_{14}^{2\Delta_\phi}}\mathcal G(v,u).
$$

Equating this with the original expression gives

$$
\frac{1}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\phi}x_{34}^{2\Delta_\phi}}\mathcal G(u,v)
=
\frac{1}{x_{23}^{2\Delta_\phi}x_{14}^{2\Delta_\phi}}\mathcal G(v,u).
$$

Multiplying by $x_{12}^{2\Delta_\phi}x_{34}^{2\Delta_\phi}$ gives

$$
\mathcal G(u,v)
=\left(\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}\right)^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(v,u).
$$

Since

$$
\frac{x_{12}^2x_{34}^2}{x_{14}^2x_{23}^2}=\frac{u}{v},
$$

we get

$$
v^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(u,v)
=u^{\Delta_\phi}\mathcal G(v,u).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Operator rescaling

Let $\mathcal O\mapsto a\mathcal O$ for a nonzero real constant $a$. How do the two-point coefficient and the coefficient $\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}$ change?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The two-point function scales as

$$
\langle a\mathcal O(x)a\mathcal O(0)\rangle
=a^2\langle\mathcal O(x)\mathcal O(0)\rangle.
$$

The three-point function $\langle\phi\phi\mathcal O\rangle$ scales by one power of $a$, so

$$
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}\mapsto a\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}
$$

if $\phi$ is not rescaled. This is why OPE coefficients cannot be compared before fixing two-point normalizations.

</details>
