## Summary

Two-dimensional conformal blocks are special because the global conformal group factorizes:

$$
SO(3,1)\simeq PSL(2,\mathbb C),
\qquad
\mathfrak{so}(3,1)_\mathbb C\simeq \mathfrak{sl}(2)_L\oplus\mathfrak{sl}(2)_R.
$$

A primary is labeled by two weights,

$$
(h,\bar h),
\qquad
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h.
$$

For the finite global conformal group, the conformal block in a four-point function factorizes into a holomorphic block and an antiholomorphic block:

$$
G_{h,\bar h}(z,\bar z)
=
\mathcal F_h(z)\,\bar{\mathcal F}_{\bar h}(\bar z).
$$

For scalar external operators, a common convention is

$$
\mathcal F_h^{12,34}(z)
=
z^h\,{}_2F_1(h-h_{12},h+h_{34};2h;z),
$$

and similarly for $\bar h$. Here $h_{ij}=h_i-h_j$. The antiholomorphic block uses $\bar h_i$ in the same way.

This page is about **global** two-dimensional blocks. Full two-dimensional CFT has the much larger Virasoro symmetry. Virasoro blocks are not the same as global blocks: a Virasoro block sums the descendants generated by all $L_{-n}$ with $n\ge1$, while a global block only sums the descendants generated by $L_{-1}$ and $\bar L_{-1}$. The difference is small in notation and enormous in physics.

## Core idea

In $d>2$, the conformal group is finite-dimensional and conformal blocks are naturally functions of two cross-ratios. In $d=2$, Euclidean coordinates combine into complex coordinates,

$$
z=x^1+i x^2,
\qquad
\bar z=x^1-i x^2,
$$

and the global conformal transformations are Möbius transformations,

$$
z\mapsto \frac{az+b}{cz+d},
\qquad
\bar z\mapsto \frac{\bar a\bar z+\bar b}{\bar c\bar z+\bar d}.
$$

The left and right global algebras commute. Therefore the descendant sum for a global block splits into two independent one-dimensional sums: one over $L_{-1}$ descendants and one over $\bar L_{-1}$ descendants. That is why the answer is a product of hypergeometric functions.

This is one of the great conveniences of two dimensions:

$$
\text{2D global block}
=
\text{holomorphic }SL(2)\text{ block}
\times
\text{antiholomorphic }SL(2)\text{ block}.
$$

The trap is that two-dimensional CFT usually has more symmetry than this. The local conformal transformations generate the Virasoro algebra, and Virasoro descendants do not fit inside a single global block. A Virasoro block is a larger object.

## Setup and conventions

A primary operator $\mathcal O(z,\bar z)$ with weights $(h,\bar h)$ transforms under a local holomorphic change of coordinates as

$$
\mathcal O(z,\bar z)
\mapsto
\left(\frac{dw}{dz}\right)^{-h}
\left(\frac{d\bar w}{d\bar z}\right)^{-\bar h}
\mathcal O(w,\bar w),
$$

or equivalently for correlation functions with the inverse Jacobian factors, depending on active/passive convention. The scaling dimension and spin are

$$
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h.
$$

For mutually local bosonic scalar operators, $s=0$. More generally, locality imposes integrality conditions on spin differences in operator products. Chiral fields can have $\bar h=0$, and antichiral fields can have $h=0$.

For four operators, define

$$
z=\frac{z_{12}z_{34}}{z_{13}z_{24}},
\qquad
\bar z=\frac{\bar z_{12}\bar z_{34}}{\bar z_{13}\bar z_{24}}.
$$

In Euclidean signature, $\bar z$ is the complex conjugate of $z$. In Lorentzian signature, $z$ and $\bar z$ are often treated as independent real variables after analytic continuation.

## Four-point functions in two dimensions

For primary operators $\mathcal O_i$ of weights $(h_i,\bar h_i)$, the holomorphic part of a four-point function can be stripped of a standard kinematic prefactor. One convenient convention is

$$
\langle\mathcal O_1\mathcal O_2\mathcal O_3\mathcal O_4\rangle
=
\mathcal K(z_i,\bar z_i)\,\mathcal G(z,\bar z),
$$

where $\mathcal K$ carries the required scaling weights at each point. The nontrivial dynamical information sits in $\mathcal G(z,\bar z)$.

The $12\to34$ OPE expansion has the form

$$
\mathcal G(z,\bar z)
=
\sum_{\mathcal O}
\lambda_{12\mathcal O}\lambda_{34\mathcal O}
G_{h,\bar h}^{12,34}(z,\bar z),
$$

where the exchanged primary $\mathcal O$ has weights $(h,\bar h)$. For global blocks,

$$
G_{h,\bar h}^{12,34}(z,\bar z)
=
\mathcal F_h^{12,34}(z)
\mathcal F_{\bar h}^{12,34}(\bar z).
$$

Here

$$
\mathcal F_h^{12,34}(z)
=
z^h\,{}_2F_1(h-h_{12},h+h_{34};2h;z),
$$

and

$$
\mathcal F_{\bar h}^{12,34}(\bar z)
=
\bar z^{\bar h}\,{}_2F_1(\bar h-\bar h_{12},\bar h+\bar h_{34};2\bar h;\bar z).
$$

Different references move simple powers of $z$ and $1-z$ between the prefactor $\mathcal K$ and the reduced function $\mathcal G$. The invariant content is the same: the holomorphic global descendant sum is a hypergeometric function.

## Derivation from the global Casimir

The holomorphic global conformal generators are

$$
L_{-1}=-\partial_z,
\qquad
L_0=-z\partial_z-h,
\qquad
L_1=-z^2\partial_z-2hz,
$$

up to active/passive sign conventions. They generate an $\mathfrak{sl}(2)$ algebra. The antiholomorphic generators $\bar L_{-1},\bar L_0,\bar L_1$ act on $\bar z$ and commute with the holomorphic ones.

A holomorphic global block is an eigenfunction of the pairwise $SL(2)$ Casimir in the $12$ channel. After stripping the kinematic prefactor, the equation reduces to a hypergeometric differential equation. The solution with OPE behavior

$$
\mathcal F_h(z)\sim z^h
\qquad
z\to0
$$

is

$$
\mathcal F_h^{12,34}(z)
=
z^h\,{}_2F_1(h-h_{12},h+h_{34};2h;z).
$$

The antiholomorphic equation is identical with $h\to\bar h$ and $z\to\bar z$.

The product solution follows because the full global Casimir is a sum of left and right Casimirs. The exchanged primary has eigenvalues

$$
C_L=h(h-1),
\qquad
C_R=\bar h(\bar h-1),
$$

and the full data are equivalently $\Delta=h+\bar h$ and $s=h-\bar h$.

## Relation to higher-dimensional scalar blocks

For scalar external operators in general dimension, a symmetric-traceless exchanged primary has

$$
h=\frac{\Delta+\ell}{2},
\qquad
\bar h=\frac{\Delta-\ell}{2}
$$

in two dimensions. With a common higher-dimensional normalization, the $d=2$ scalar block can be written as a sum of two products,

$$
g_{\Delta,\ell}(z,\bar z)
\propto
k_{\Delta+\ell}(z)k_{\Delta-\ell}(\bar z)
+
k_{\Delta+\ell}(\bar z)k_{\Delta-\ell}(z),
$$

where

$$
k_\beta(x)=x^{\beta/2}
{}_2F_1\left(\frac{\beta-\Delta_{12}}{2},\frac{\beta+\Delta_{34}}{2};\beta;x\right).
$$

The symmetrized form reflects the fact that a real spin-$\ell$ representation in two Euclidean dimensions can be built from left- and right-moving weights. If one works directly with complex primaries of definite $(h,\bar h)$, it is usually cleaner to use the factorized form $\mathcal F_h(z)\mathcal F_{\bar h}(\bar z)$.

:::caution[Convention warning]
Some sources define $k_\beta$ with $\Delta_{12}$ and $\Delta_{34}$ signs reversed, because they strip a different four-point prefactor or order the operators differently. Always check the OPE limit $z\to0$: the block should behave like $z^h\bar z^{\bar h}$ in the chosen channel.
:::

## Global blocks versus Virasoro blocks

The global conformal algebra in two dimensions is generated by

$$
L_{-1},L_0,L_1,
\qquad
\bar L_{-1},\bar L_0,\bar L_1.
$$

The full local conformal algebra is the Virasoro algebra,

$$
[L_m,L_n]
=
(m-n)L_{m+n}
+
\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0},
$$

and similarly for $\bar L_n$. A Virasoro primary has descendants generated by all negative modes,

$$
L_{-n_1}\cdots L_{-n_k}\bar L_{-m_1}\cdots \bar L_{-m_j}\mid h,\bar h\rangle,
\qquad
n_i,m_i\ge1.
$$

A global block only includes descendants generated by $L_{-1}$ and $\bar L_{-1}$. Therefore a Virasoro block contains infinitely many global conformal families. Schematically,

$$
\mathcal V_{h,\bar h}^{(c)}(z,\bar z)
=
\sum_{\text{global families inside the Virasoro family}}
A_{n,\bar n}(c,h_i,h)
G_{h+n,\bar h+\bar n}(z,\bar z).
$$

This distinction matters immediately in minimal models, Liouville theory, and any exact 2D CFT calculation where the stress tensor and its descendants are not optional. Global blocks are still useful: they are simple, they connect smoothly to higher-dimensional bootstrap notation, and they appear naturally in large-$c$ or perturbative decompositions.

## Identity exchange

The global identity block is simple:

$$
h=\bar h=0.
$$

For scalar external operators, it contributes only when the relevant two-point functions are allowed. In a four-point function of identical scalars normalized by $\langle\phi\phi\rangle=1$, the identity contribution in the $12\to34$ channel is the leading constant term in $\mathcal G(z,\bar z)$.

The Virasoro identity block is much richer. It includes not only the identity state but also stress-tensor descendants such as $T$, $\bar T$, composites of stress tensors, and their descendants. In large-$c$ holographic CFTs, the Virasoro identity block captures universal gravitational contributions in three-dimensional AdS gravity. That physics is not present in the global identity block alone.

## Crossing in two dimensions

For identical scalar primaries of dimension $\Delta_\phi=2h_\phi$, one common reduced four-point function satisfies

$$
\mathcal G(z,\bar z)
=
\left(\frac{z\bar z}{(1-z)(1-\bar z)}\right)^{\Delta_\phi}
\mathcal G(1-z,1-\bar z).
$$

The global block expansion gives

$$
\mathcal G(z,\bar z)
=
\sum_{\mathcal O}
\lambda_{\phi\phi\mathcal O}^2
\mathcal F_h(z)\mathcal F_{\bar h}(\bar z).
$$

Unitarity implies nonnegative squared OPE coefficients when the exchanged operators are normalized appropriately. Thus the two-dimensional global bootstrap can be formulated in almost the same language as the higher-dimensional scalar bootstrap. The main difference is that the natural representation labels are $(h,\bar h)$ rather than $(\Delta,\ell)$.

If the theory has Virasoro symmetry and one chooses a Virasoro-block expansion, crossing becomes a constraint on Virasoro primaries and Virasoro blocks instead. The two approaches are compatible, but they organize the same correlator differently.

## Common pitfalls

**Calling every 2D block a Virasoro block.** The hypergeometric product above is a global block. Virasoro blocks depend on the central charge $c$ and include descendants generated by all Virasoro modes.

**Forgetting spin.** In two dimensions, spin is $s=h-\bar h$. A local scalar has $h=\bar h$, but chiral fields, currents, stress tensors, and fermions do not.

**Confusing Euclidean conjugation with Lorentzian independence.** In Euclidean signature, $\bar z=z^*$. In Lorentzian kinematics, analytic continuation often treats $z$ and $\bar z$ as independent variables.

**Trusting formulas without checking the prefactor.** Hypergeometric parameters depend on how the four-point function is stripped. The OPE limit is the quickest sanity check.

**Ignoring null states.** In minimal models and other degenerate Virasoro representations, null states impose differential equations and truncate fusion rules. A generic global-block formula does not by itself know that a null state has been quotiented out.

## Research status

Global two-dimensional blocks are completely standard and elementary compared with higher-dimensional spinning blocks. Virasoro blocks are more powerful and more technically involved. They are central in exact 2D CFT, Liouville theory, minimal models, holographic CFT$_2$, and many modern bootstrap problems.

Active topics include efficient numerical evaluation of Virasoro blocks, semiclassical and heavy-light limits, crossing kernels, modular constraints, logarithmic CFT, irrational CFTs, and the relation between Virasoro-block expansions and bulk gravitational physics in AdS$_3$.

## Exercises

### Exercise: Dimension and spin from weights

An operator has weights $(h,\bar h)=(3/2,1/2)$. What are its scaling dimension and spin?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The dimension and spin are

$$
\Delta=h+\bar h=2,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h=1.
$$

So the operator has scaling dimension $2$ and spin $1$.

</details>

### Exercise: OPE limit of the holomorphic block

Show that

$$
\mathcal F_h^{12,34}(z)
=
z^h{}_2F_1(h-h_{12},h+h_{34};2h;z)
$$

has the expected leading behavior as $z\to0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The hypergeometric function satisfies

$$
{}_2F_1(a,b;c;z)=1+O(z)
$$

near $z=0$. Therefore

$$
\mathcal F_h^{12,34}(z)=z^h(1+O(z)).
$$

This is the expected contribution of a holomorphic primary of weight $h$, followed by its $L_{-1}$ descendants.

</details>

### Exercise: Global descendants inside a Virasoro family

Which Virasoro descendants of a primary are included in the global conformal block?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The global block includes descendants generated only by the global lowering operators $L_{-1}$ and $\bar L_{-1}$:

$$
L_{-1}^n\bar L_{-1}^{\bar n}\mid h,\bar h\rangle.
$$

A full Virasoro family also includes descendants involving $L_{-2},L_{-3},\ldots$ and their antiholomorphic analogues. Those are not included in a single global block. Instead, from the global point of view, they appear as additional global primaries and descendants inside the same Virasoro family.

</details>

### Exercise: Identical scalar crossing

For identical scalar external operators of dimension $\Delta_\phi$, explain why crossing exchanges $z$ with $1-z$ and $\bar z$ with $1-\bar z$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The cross-ratio

$$
z=\frac{z_{12}z_{34}}{z_{13}z_{24}}
$$

changes under permutations of the four points. Exchanging operators $1$ and $3$, which relates the $12\to34$ and $32\to14$ OPE channels, maps the holomorphic cross-ratio to $1-z$. The antiholomorphic cross-ratio transforms in the same way, giving $\bar z\to1-\bar z$. The prefactor stripped from the four-point function then supplies the explicit power multiplying $\mathcal G(1-z,1-\bar z)$.

</details>
