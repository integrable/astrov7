## Summary

Two-dimensional CFT has two closely related but different notions of conformal block.

A **global conformal block** uses only the finite-dimensional global conformal algebra

$$
\mathfrak{sl}(2)_L\oplus\mathfrak{sl}(2)_R,
$$

with generators $L_{-1},L_0,L_1$ and $\bar L_{-1},\bar L_0,\bar L_1$. It sums the descendants obtained by ordinary derivatives of a global primary. For a scalar four-point function, a global block factorizes into holomorphic and antiholomorphic hypergeometric functions.

A **Virasoro conformal block** uses the full local conformal algebra

$$
[L_m,L_n]=(m-n)L_{m+n}+\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0},
$$

and the corresponding antiholomorphic copy. It sums all descendants generated by $L_{-n}$ and $\bar L_{-n}$ with $n\ge1$. It depends on the central charge $c$, not just on dimensions and spins.

The practical distinction is this:

$$
\text{global block}
=
\text{one global multiplet},
\qquad
\text{Virasoro block}
=
\text{one Virasoro multiplet}.
$$

Since a Virasoro multiplet contains infinitely many global quasi-primaries, a Virasoro block generally expands as an infinite sum of global blocks.

## Core idea

In $d>2$, the conformal group is finite-dimensional. Once one says “the conformal block of the primary $\mathcal O$,” there is essentially one natural object: the contribution of the conformal multiplet of $\mathcal O$.

In two dimensions, the word “conformal” is overloaded. The finite global group is only the beginning. Locally, every holomorphic map is conformal, and quantization produces the Virasoro algebra. Therefore one must ask:

```txt
Do we mean a block for the global algebra, or a block for the Virasoro algebra?
```

The global block is easier and often useful. It is the direct two-dimensional analogue of higher-dimensional blocks. The Virasoro block is more intrinsic to local two-dimensional CFT. It knows about the stress tensor, the central charge, null vectors, minimal models, semiclassical gravity in $AdS_3$, and modular bootstrap.

A rough slogan is:

$$
\text{global blocks know finite conformal kinematics},
\qquad
\text{Virasoro blocks know local stress-tensor symmetry}.
$$

This slogan has limits. Virasoro blocks are still kinematic objects once the central charge and external/internal weights are fixed. They are not themselves dynamical OPE coefficients. But they incorporate far more symmetry than global blocks.

## Setup and conventions

We work in Euclidean two-dimensional CFT with complex coordinates

$$
z=x^1+i x^2,
\qquad
\bar z=x^1-i x^2.
$$

The holomorphic stress tensor has modes

$$
T(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\frac{L_n}{z^{n+2}},
$$

with Virasoro algebra

$$
[L_m,L_n]=(m-n)L_{m+n}+\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

The modes $L_{-1},L_0,L_1$ form an $\mathfrak{sl}(2)$ subalgebra because the central term vanishes for $m,n\in\{-1,0,1\}$. The antiholomorphic modes $\bar L_n$ obey the same algebra with central charge $\bar c$. For parity-invariant compact CFTs one often has $c=\bar c$, but the distinction can matter in chiral theories and anomalous systems.

A highest-weight state $|h\rangle$ obeys

$$
L_0|h\rangle=h|h\rangle,
\qquad
L_n|h\rangle=0\quad n>0.
$$

The corresponding local operator is a Virasoro primary. A global primary is only required to be annihilated by $L_1$ and $\bar L_1$. Thus every Virasoro primary is a global primary, but not every global primary inside a module is a Virasoro primary.

The full primary has weights $(h,\bar h)$, scaling dimension and spin

$$
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h.
$$

## Global blocks

A holomorphic global family built on $|h\rangle$ contains the states

$$
|h\rangle,
\quad
L_{-1}|h\rangle,
\quad
L_{-1}^2|h\rangle,
\quad
\ldots.
$$

Together with the antiholomorphic descendants, this gives a global conformal multiplet. In a four-point function, the corresponding global block takes the factorized form

$$
G_{h,\bar h}^{12,34}(z,\bar z)
=
\mathcal F_h^{12,34}(z)\,\mathcal F_{\bar h}^{12,34}(\bar z),
$$

where a common holomorphic convention is

$$
\mathcal F_h^{12,34}(z)
=
z^h\,{}_2F_1(h-h_{12},h+h_{34};2h;z).
$$

Here $h_{ij}=h_i-h_j$. The antiholomorphic block is the same expression with barred weights and $\bar z$.

If a reduced four-point function is expanded in global blocks, then the sum runs over all global primaries appearing in the OPE. In a generic two-dimensional CFT, this includes Virasoro primaries and also Virasoro descendants that happen to be global primaries. These latter states are usually called **quasi-primaries**.

Thus the global-block expansion is schematically

$$
\mathcal G(z,\bar z)
=
\sum_{\text{global primaries }q}
 a_q\,G_{h_q,\bar h_q}^{\text{global}}(z,\bar z).
$$

The coefficients $a_q$ are not arbitrary if the global primaries $q$ sit inside the same Virasoro module. Virasoro symmetry relates them.

## Virasoro blocks

A holomorphic Virasoro family built on $|h\rangle$ contains all states

$$
L_{-n_1}L_{-n_2}\cdots L_{-n_k}|h\rangle,
\qquad
n_i\ge1.
$$

The level is

$$
N=n_1+n_2+\cdots+n_k,
$$

so the state has $L_0$ eigenvalue $h+N$. The number of states at level $N$ is the partition number $p(N)$ before null-state quotients. This grows quickly.

A holomorphic Virasoro block is the contribution of this entire Verma module to a four-point function. For external holomorphic weights $h_i$ and exchanged weight $h_p$, write it schematically as

$$
\mathcal F(c,h_i,h_p;z).
$$

The full nonchiral contribution is often written

$$
\mathcal V_{p}(z,\bar z)
=
\mathcal F(c,h_i,h_p;z)
\bar{\mathcal F}(\bar c,\bar h_i,\bar h_p;\bar z),
$$

and the four-point function has the Virasoro-block expansion

$$
\mathcal G(z,\bar z)
=
\sum_{p\in\text{Virasoro primaries}}
C_{12p}C_{34p}\,\mathcal V_p(z,\bar z).
$$

The central charge enters because norms and inner products of descendants involve the Virasoro commutator. For example,

$$
\langle h|L_2L_{-2}|h\rangle
=
\langle h|\left(4L_0+\frac c2\right)|h\rangle
=
4h+\frac c2.
$$

This is already enough to see that a Virasoro block cannot be the same thing as a global block. Global blocks do not depend on $c$.

## Virasoro blocks as global-block sums

Inside a Verma module, some descendant states are global primaries. These are the Virasoro quasi-primaries. Each quasi-primary generates its own global $SL(2)$ family under repeated action of $L_{-1}$.

Therefore a Virasoro module decomposes into global modules:

$$
\mathcal V_h^{\text{Vir}}
=
\bigoplus_{q\in\text{quasi-primaries in }\mathcal V_h}
\mathcal G_{h+N_q}^{\text{global}}.
$$

Consequently, a Virasoro block decomposes as

$$
\mathcal F(c,h_i,h;z)
=
\sum_{q\in\mathcal V_h}
\alpha_q(c,h_i,h)\,\mathcal F_{h+N_q}^{\text{global}}(z),
$$

where the sum is over quasi-primaries in the holomorphic Verma module. The coefficients $\alpha_q$ are fixed by Virasoro representation theory and by the external weights.

At level $0$, the highest-weight state itself is a global primary. At level $1$, $L_{-1}|h\rangle$ is a global descendant, not a new global primary. At level $2$, for generic $h$, the combination

$$
\left(L_{-2}-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}L_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle
$$

is annihilated by $L_1$ and is therefore a global primary. It begins a new global block of holomorphic weight $h+2$ inside the same Virasoro block.

This is the simplest place where the distinction becomes concrete: a Virasoro block contains the original global block plus further global blocks built on quasi-primaries made from stress-tensor descendants.

## The identity block

The identity exchange is where many mistakes reveal themselves.

For the global algebra, the identity operator has no nonzero $L_{-1}$ descendants:

$$
L_{-1}|0\rangle=0.
$$

So the **global identity block** is just the identity contribution, often normalized to $1$ in the reduced correlator.

For the Virasoro algebra, the vacuum module contains the stress tensor:

$$
L_{-2}|0\rangle
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
T(z).
$$

It also contains its descendants and multi-stress-tensor states such as

$$
L_{-2}^2|0\rangle,
\qquad
L_{-3}|0\rangle,
\qquad
L_{-4}|0\rangle,
\qquad
\ldots.
$$

Thus the **Virasoro identity block** is not just $1$. It contains the stress tensor and the entire vacuum Virasoro module. In holographic CFTs, this object is central because it resums universal gravitational exchanges in $AdS_3$.

:::caution[Do not double count the stress tensor]
If you expand a two-dimensional correlator in Virasoro blocks, the stress tensor contribution is already included in the Virasoro vacuum block. Do not add a separate global stress-tensor block unless you have intentionally changed from a Virasoro-block expansion to a global-block expansion.
:::

## Which expansion should you use?

The correct expansion depends on the question.

| Goal | Natural block |
|---|---|
| Compare with higher-dimensional bootstrap notation | Global blocks |
| Use only finite conformal symmetry | Global blocks |
| Analyze local 2D CFT with stress-tensor symmetry | Virasoro blocks |
| Study minimal models and null-vector equations | Virasoro blocks |
| Use the vacuum module in 2D gravity or large-$c$ limits | Virasoro blocks |
| Build numerical bootstrap equations with only global symmetry | Global blocks |
| Build 2D bootstrap equations exploiting full local conformal symmetry | Virasoro blocks |

Global blocks are not wrong in two dimensions. Any Virasoro block can be expanded into global blocks, and any correlator can be decomposed into global blocks. The issue is efficiency and interpretation. If the theory has Virasoro symmetry, the global coefficients are highly constrained. Virasoro blocks package those constraints automatically.

## Minimal models and null states

In a generic Verma module, descendant states are independent. At special values of $c$ and $h$, some descendants have zero norm and generate null submodules. In a unitary theory, null states are quotiented out; in correlation functions, they lead to differential equations.

This is the mechanism behind the BPZ solution of minimal models. If an external field is degenerate, its null descendant vanishes inside correlators. A four-point function involving that field then obeys a finite-order differential equation. The conformal blocks are the independent solutions of that equation.

From the global-block viewpoint, this is hidden. From the Virasoro-block viewpoint, it is native: null states are statements about the Virasoro module.

For example, the Ising spin field has a degenerate Virasoro representation. Its four-point functions are much more constrained than generic global conformal symmetry would imply. This is why the two-dimensional Ising model can be solved exactly using conformal symmetry alone, while a generic higher-dimensional CFT cannot.

## Large central charge and semiclassical blocks

The central charge controls the size of stress-tensor fluctuations. In many applications one studies limits such as

$$
c\to\infty,
\qquad
\frac{h_i}{c}\quad\text{fixed or small},
\qquad
\frac{h_p}{c}\quad\text{fixed or small}.
$$

Virasoro blocks can exponentiate in semiclassical regimes:

$$
\mathcal F(c,h_i,h_p;z)
\sim
\exp\left[-\frac c6 f\left(\frac{h_i}{c},\frac{h_p}{c};z\right)\right],
$$

with the precise sign and normalization depending on conventions. Heavy-light Virasoro blocks, where some external dimensions scale like $c$ and others remain fixed, are especially important in $AdS_3/CFT_2$.

Global blocks do not capture this resummation by themselves. A single global stress-tensor block is only the first term in a much larger Virasoro vacuum-block effect.

## Common pitfalls

| Pitfall | Repair |
|---|---|
| Calling the hypergeometric $SL(2)$ block “the 2D conformal block” without qualification. | Say “global block” when only $L_{-1},L_0,L_1$ are being used. |
| Treating the Virasoro identity block as just $1$. | Remember that the vacuum module contains $T$, its descendants, and multi-stress-tensor states. |
| Adding a separate stress-tensor block on top of a Virasoro vacuum block. | Use either a Virasoro-block expansion or a global-block expansion, not both at once. |
| Thinking that Virasoro blocks are dynamical OPE coefficients. | Blocks are fixed by symmetry once $c$ and weights are specified; OPE coefficients are dynamical data. |
| Forgetting antiholomorphic data. | A physical local operator usually has both $(h,\bar h)$; the full block is holomorphic times antiholomorphic only after choosing the appropriate chiral decomposition. |
| Ignoring null states. | In minimal models and degenerate representations, quotient by null states or impose the corresponding differential equations. |
| Assuming large-$c$ Virasoro physics is visible in a single global block. | Large-$c$ Virasoro effects often resum infinitely many global stress-tensor-family contributions. |

## Research status

The conceptual distinction between global and Virasoro blocks is settled. The active work is mostly computational and applicational.

Virasoro blocks can be computed by recursion relations, Coulomb-gas methods in special theories, monodromy methods in semiclassical regimes, and numerical series expansions. The optimal method depends on the central charge, the external and internal weights, the desired channel, and whether the theory is rational, irrational, heavy-light, logarithmic, or supersymmetric.

In modern applications, Virasoro blocks appear in two-dimensional bootstrap, modular bootstrap, entanglement and twist-field calculations, Liouville theory, semiclassical $AdS_3$ gravity, black-hole microphysics, and chaotic Lorentzian limits. The basic definitions are stable, but efficient and reliable evaluation across parameter space is still a live technical subject.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Global versus Virasoro primary

Show that every Virasoro primary is a global primary, but not conversely.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A Virasoro primary state obeys

$$
L_n|h\rangle=0
\qquad n>0.
$$

In particular, it obeys $L_1|h\rangle=0$. Since $L_1$ is one of the global $SL(2)$ generators, the state is a global primary.

The converse fails because a global primary only needs to be annihilated by $L_1$. A state can obey $L_1|\psi\rangle=0$ while failing to obey $L_n|\psi\rangle=0$ for some $n\ge2$. Such states are quasi-primaries. They are global primaries but not Virasoro primaries.

</details>

### Exercise 2: A level-two quasi-primary

Let $|h\rangle$ be a Virasoro highest-weight state. Show that, for $h\neq -1/2$,

$$
|\Lambda\rangle
=
\left(L_{-2}-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}L_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle
$$

is annihilated by $L_1$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use

$$
[L_1,L_{-2}]=3L_{-1},
\qquad
[L_1,L_{-1}]=2L_0.
$$

Since $L_1|h\rangle=0$,

$$
L_1L_{-2}|h\rangle=3L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Also,

$$
L_1L_{-1}^2|h\rangle
=
2L_0L_{-1}|h\rangle+2L_{-1}L_0|h\rangle.
$$

Using $L_0L_{-1}|h\rangle=(h+1)L_{-1}|h\rangle$ and $L_0|h\rangle=h|h\rangle$, this becomes

$$
L_1L_{-1}^2|h\rangle
=
2(2h+1)L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Therefore

$$
L_1|\Lambda\rangle
=
\left(3-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}2(2h+1)\right)L_{-1}|h\rangle=0.
$$

Thus $|\Lambda\rangle$ is a global primary inside the Virasoro module. It is not a new Virasoro primary in the original CFT spectrum; it is a descendant quasi-primary.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why the vacuum Virasoro block contains the stress tensor

Explain why the global identity block is trivial but the Virasoro identity block is not.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For the global algebra, the identity state is invariant under translations, so

$$
L_{-1}|0\rangle=0.
$$

The global identity family therefore contains only the identity.

For the Virasoro algebra, the vacuum obeys

$$
L_n|0\rangle=0
\qquad n\ge -1,
$$

but $L_{-2}|0\rangle$ is not zero. This state corresponds to the stress tensor $T(z)$. Higher states such as $L_{-2}^2|0\rangle$ and $L_{-3}|0\rangle$ are also in the vacuum module. Therefore the Virasoro identity block includes the stress tensor family and is not merely the constant identity contribution.

</details>
