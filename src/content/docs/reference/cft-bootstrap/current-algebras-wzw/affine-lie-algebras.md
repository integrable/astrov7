## Summary

An **affine Lie algebra** is the centrally extended algebra of modes of a holomorphic current. If $J^a(z)$ are currents associated with a finite-dimensional Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$, their OPE has the form

$$
J^a(z)J^b(w)
\sim
\frac{k\kappa^{ab}}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c(w)}{z-w}.
$$

Equivalently, expanding

$$
J^a(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}J^a_n z^{-n-1},
$$

gives the mode algebra

$$
[J^a_m,J^b_n]
=i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c_{m+n}
+k m\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

The integer or real number $k$ is called the **level**. For compact unitary WZW models, $k$ is a nonnegative integer. The level is not a decoration: it fixes the central term of the current algebra, controls the Sugawara central charge, and restricts which affine highest-weight representations are allowed.

Affine Lie algebras are the algebraic engine behind WZW models, nonabelian bosonization, many rational CFTs, and the Knizhnik–Zamolodchikov equations.

For the Lie-algebra side, you should know that a finite-dimensional Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$ has generators $T^a$ obeying

$$
[T^a,T^b]=i f^{ab}{}_cT^c,
$$

and an invariant bilinear form $\kappa^{ab}$. For a compact simple algebra, $\kappa^{ab}$ can be taken proportional to the Killing form or to a trace in a chosen representation.

## Core idea

A global symmetry current in two-dimensional CFT can split into holomorphic and antiholomorphic parts. The holomorphic part has infinitely many Laurent modes:

$$
J^a(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}J^a_n z^{-n-1}.
$$

The zero modes $J^a_0$ reproduce the ordinary finite Lie algebra:

$$
[J^a_0,J^b_0]=i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c_0.
$$

The nonzero modes are new. They describe local current excitations on the circle. Their algebra is not just the loop algebra $\mathfrak g\otimes\mathbb C[t,t^{-1}]$; it has a central extension:

$$
[J^a_m,J^b_n]
=i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c_{m+n}
+k m\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

That central term is the affine analogue of the Virasoro central charge. It is invisible in the finite Lie algebra but essential in the quantum current algebra.

The slogan is

$$
\text{finite symmetry charges}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{current modes}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{affine Lie algebra}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

Let $\mathfrak g$ be a finite-dimensional Lie algebra with structure constants

$$
[T^a,T^b]=i f^{ab}{}_cT^c.
$$

Let $\kappa^{ab}$ be a symmetric invariant bilinear form. Invariance means

$$
f^{ab}{}_d\kappa^{dc}+f^{ac}{}_d\kappa^{bd}=0.
$$

We define holomorphic currents $J^a(z)$ of weight one. Their Laurent modes are

$$
J^a_n=\oint_0\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,z^nJ^a(z),
$$

so that

$$
J^a(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}J^a_nz^{-n-1}.
$$

The current OPE convention used on this page is

$$
J^a(z)J^b(w)
\sim
\frac{k\kappa^{ab}}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c(w)}{z-w}.
$$

This convention puts an $i$ in the structure-constant term. Many CFT sources absorb this $i$ by using anti-Hermitian Lie-algebra generators. When comparing formulas, translate the generator convention before comparing signs.

## From OPE to modes

The mode commutator follows from contour manipulations. Start with

$$
[J^a_m,J^b_n]
=\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}w^n
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}z^mJ^a(z)J^b(w).
$$

Insert the singular OPE:

$$
J^a(z)J^b(w)
\sim
\frac{k\kappa^{ab}}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c(w)}{z-w}.
$$

The simple pole contributes

$$
i f^{ab}{}_c\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}w^{m+n}J^c(w)
=i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c_{m+n}.
$$

The double pole contributes because

$$
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\frac{z^m}{(z-w)^2}
=m w^{m-1}.
$$

Thus

$$
k\kappa^{ab}\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}w^n m w^{m-1}
=k m\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

Combining the two contributions gives

$$
[J^a_m,J^b_n]
=i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c_{m+n}
+k m\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

This is the affine Lie algebra associated with $\mathfrak g$ at level $k$.

## Loop algebra and central extension

Before the central term, the modes obey the loop algebra

$$
[T^a_m,T^b_n]=i f^{ab}{}_cT^c_{m+n}.
$$

You can think of $T^a_n$ as

$$
T^a_n=T^a t^n,
$$

where $t$ is a coordinate on the circle. The affine algebra adds a central element $K$:

$$
[J^a_m,J^b_n]
=i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c_{m+n}+m\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}K,
$$

with

$$
[K,J^a_n]=0.
$$

In a representation of level $k$, the central element acts as

$$
K=k\mathbf 1.
$$

This is why physicists often write the central element as the number $k$ from the start.

The central extension is allowed because loop algebras have nontrivial two-cocycles. In CFT language, it is forced by the double pole in the current OPE.

## The level

The level $k$ is the coefficient of the central term. It controls several facts at once.

| Role of $k$ | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Current two-point function | $\langle J^a(z)J^b(0)\rangle=k\kappa^{ab}/z^2$. |
| Mode algebra | The central term is $k m\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}$. |
| WZW action | The Wess–Zumino term is multiplied by $k$. |
| Representation theory | At positive integer level, only finitely many integrable primaries appear. |
| Sugawara central charge | $c=k\dim\mathfrak g/(k+h^\vee)$ for compact simple $\mathfrak g$. |

For compact simple WZW models, quantum consistency requires

$$
k\in\mathbb Z_{\ge0}.
$$

For an abelian current algebra, the level is the current two-point normalization and can be encoded as a positive lattice or matrix. For noncompact or nonunitary theories, the interpretation of $k$ can be subtler.

## Highest-weight representations

A highest-weight representation of the affine algebra is generated from a state $|\lambda\rangle$ satisfying

$$
J^a_n|\lambda\rangle=0
\qquad n>0,
$$

and such that the zero modes $J^a_0$ form a highest-weight representation of the finite algebra $\mathfrak g$ with highest weight $\lambda$.

The negative modes create descendants:

$$
J^{a_1}_{-n_1}\cdots J^{a_r}_{-n_r}|\lambda\rangle,
\qquad
n_i>0.
$$

These descendants are current-algebra descendants. They are generally much larger symmetry multiplets than Virasoro descendants alone.

For compact simple $\mathfrak g$ at positive integer level, the representations that appear in the WZW model are the **integrable highest-weight representations**. They obey

$$
(\lambda,\theta)\le k,
$$

where $\theta$ is the highest root and the long roots have length squared $2$.

For $SU(2)_k$, this condition becomes

$$
2j\le k,
$$

so

$$
j=0,\frac12,1,\ldots,\frac{k}{2}.
$$

This finite list is the reason $SU(2)_k$ WZW models are rational CFTs.

## Relation to Virasoro symmetry

The affine algebra is an extension of chiral symmetry, but the theory still has a stress tensor. In WZW models, the stress tensor is built from the currents by the Sugawara construction:

$$
T(z)=\frac{1}{2(k+h^\vee)}\kappa_{ab}:J^aJ^b:(z).
$$

This gives the central charge

$$
c=\frac{k\dim\mathfrak g}{k+h^\vee}.
$$

The currents are Virasoro primaries of weight one:

$$
T(z)J^a(w)
\sim
\frac{J^a(w)}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{\partial J^a(w)}{z-w}.
$$

Thus the same theory has two compatible chiral algebras:

$$
\text{Virasoro algebra}
\subset
\text{affine current algebra plus Sugawara stress tensor}.
$$

Organizing by the affine algebra is usually much more efficient than organizing by Virasoro alone.

## Abelian example

For a single holomorphic $U(1)$ current $J(z)$, the OPE is

$$
J(z)J(w)\sim\frac{k}{(z-w)^2}.
$$

The modes obey

$$
[J_m,J_n]=k m\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

This is a Heisenberg algebra. A compact free boson has such currents, and at special radii its chiral algebra can be extended by vertex operators. This is the abelian ancestor of the nonabelian affine story.

The abelian case is simpler because there is no structure-constant term. The nonabelian case combines the current two-point singularity with the finite Lie bracket.

## Nonabelian example: su(2)

For $\mathfrak{su}(2)$, choose generators satisfying

$$
[T^a,T^b]=i\epsilon^{ab}{}_cT^c.
$$

The affine algebra is

$$
[J^a_m,J^b_n]
=i\epsilon^{ab}{}_cJ^c_{m+n}
+k m\delta^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

The $SU(2)_k$ WZW model has central charge

$$
c=\frac{3k}{k+2},
$$

and integrable primaries labeled by

$$
j=0,\frac12,1,\ldots,\frac{k}{2}.
$$

Their conformal weights are

$$
h_j=\frac{j(j+1)}{k+2}.
$$

For $k=1$, the allowed primaries are

$$
j=0,\quad \frac12,
$$

with

$$
c=1,
\qquad
h_{1/2}=\frac14.
$$

This theory is closely related to a compact boson at a special radius and to spin-chain criticality.

## Characters

Affine characters refine Virasoro characters by keeping track of representation data under the finite Lie algebra. The simplest unflavored character is still a graded trace:

$$
\chi_\lambda(\tau)=\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H_\lambda}q^{L_0-c/24}.
$$

A flavored character also inserts Cartan fugacities:

$$
\chi_\lambda(\tau,z)=\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H_\lambda}
q^{L_0-c/24}e^{2\pi i z\cdot H_0}.
$$

In rational WZW models, the finite set of integrable characters transforms among itself under modular transformations. This gives a modular $S$ matrix, and the Verlinde formula computes WZW fusion rules:

$$
N_{\lambda\mu}{}^\nu
=\sum_\rho
\frac{S_{\lambda\rho}S_{\mu\rho}S^*_{\nu\rho}}{S_{0\rho}}.
$$

The character story is one of the main reasons affine Lie algebras sit naturally inside rational CFT.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not confuse the finite Lie algebra with the affine algebra.** The finite algebra is generated by the zero modes $J^a_0$. The affine algebra includes all $J^a_n$ and a central extension.

**Do not confuse level $k$ with Virasoro central charge $c$.** They are related through Sugawara in WZW models, but they are different quantities.

**Do not ignore the bilinear-form convention.** Rescaling $\kappa^{ab}$ or the generators changes the visible value of $k$. Physical comparisons require matching trace conventions.

**Do not assume every affine representation is allowed in a compact WZW model.** Positive integer level allows only integrable highest weights.

**Do not treat the double pole as optional.** Without it, the quantum current algebra would miss the central extension and would not reproduce the correct stress tensor, characters, or fusion.

**Do not assume holomorphic and antiholomorphic sectors are always paired diagonally.** A full local WZW model must still specify left-right pairing and modular invariant data.

## Research status

Affine Lie algebras and their WZW representations are established. They are core technology in two-dimensional CFT, string theory, condensed-matter edge theories, and rational CFT.

Active work concerns extensions and applications: boundary and defect current algebra, logarithmic and nonsemisimple current algebras, categorical formulations, non-invertible symmetries, supersymmetric current algebras, and relations to three-dimensional Chern–Simons theory.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Starting from

$$
J^a(z)J^b(w)
\sim
\frac{k\kappa^{ab}}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c(w)}{z-w},
$$

show that the double pole contributes $k m\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}$ to $[J^a_m,J^b_n]$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The double-pole contribution is

$$
k\kappa^{ab}\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}w^n
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\frac{z^m}{(z-w)^2}.
$$

The inner contour gives

$$
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\frac{z^m}{(z-w)^2}=m w^{m-1}.
$$

Therefore the contribution is

$$
k m\kappa^{ab}\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}w^{m+n-1}
=k m\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2

Check that the zero modes reproduce the finite Lie algebra.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Set $m=n=0$ in the affine commutator:

$$
[J^a_0,J^b_0]
=i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c_0+k\cdot0\cdot\kappa^{ab}\delta_{0,0}.
$$

The central term vanishes, so

$$
[J^a_0,J^b_0]=i f^{ab}{}_cJ^c_0.
$$

This is the original finite Lie algebra.

</details>

### Exercise 3

For $SU(2)_k$, compute the central charge and allowed spins at $k=2$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $SU(2)_k$,

$$
c=\frac{3k}{k+2}.
$$

At $k=2$,

$$
c=\frac{6}{4}=\frac32.
$$

The integrable spins satisfy

$$
j=0,\frac12,1,\ldots,\frac{k}{2}.
$$

Thus at $k=2$ the allowed spins are

$$
j=0,\frac12,1.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4

Why is a positive integer level special for compact WZW models?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

There are two related reasons. First, in the sigma-model realization, the Wess–Zumino term is defined by extending the worldsheet to a three-manifold. Changing the extension can shift the action by a multiple of $2\pi k$, so the path-integral phase is well defined only when $k$ is quantized.

Second, in the chiral algebra, compact unitary WZW models at positive integer level have a finite set of integrable highest-weight representations. This finite representation set is what makes the theory rational.

</details>
