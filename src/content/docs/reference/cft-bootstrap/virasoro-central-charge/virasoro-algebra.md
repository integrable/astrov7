## Summary

The **Virasoro algebra** is the quantum symmetry algebra generated by the holomorphic stress tensor in a two-dimensional conformal field theory. If

$$
T(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z} L_n z^{-n-2},
\qquad
L_n=\oint_0 \frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,z^{n+1}T(z),
$$

then the modes obey

$$
[L_m,L_n]=(m-n)L_{m+n}+\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

The number $c$ is the **central charge**. Classically, local conformal transformations on the complex plane give the Witt algebra

$$
[\ell_m,\ell_n]=(m-n)\ell_{m+n}.
$$

Quantum mechanically, the stress-tensor OPE permits a central extension. This central term is not a nuisance; it is one of the main pieces of CFT data in two dimensions.

The Virasoro algebra is why two-dimensional CFT is so much more rigid than a generic higher-dimensional CFT. A single primary field has infinitely many Virasoro descendants, correlation functions obey contour Ward identities, and null descendants can impose differential equations on correlators.

## Prerequisites

You should know the complex-coordinate conventions from [Complex Coordinates](/cft-bootstrap/two-dimensional-cft/complex-coordinates/), the local transformation law of primaries from [Local Conformal Transformations](/cft-bootstrap/two-dimensional-cft/local-conformal-transformations/), the role of $T(z)$ from [Stress Tensor in 2D](/cft-bootstrap/two-dimensional-cft/stress-tensor-in-2d/), and the plane radial-quantization setup from [Radial Quantization in 2D](/cft-bootstrap/two-dimensional-cft/radial-quantization-in-2d/).

The essential contour fact is Cauchy's formula: if $A(z)B(w)$ has a singular term $(z-w)^{-k-1}C(w)$, then a contour integral of $A(z)$ around $w$ extracts derivatives of $C(w)$.

## Core idea

In two Euclidean dimensions, local conformal transformations are locally holomorphic maps

$$
z\mapsto f(z),
\qquad
\bar z\mapsto \bar f(\bar z).
$$

Infinitesimally, write

$$
z\mapsto z+\epsilon(z),
\qquad
\bar z\mapsto \bar z+\bar\epsilon(\bar z).
$$

The holomorphic part is generated by the stress tensor through contour integrals. A transformation with parameter $\epsilon(z)$ is represented by

$$
Q_\epsilon=\oint \frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,\epsilon(z)T(z).
$$

Choosing the basis

$$
\epsilon_n(z)=z^{n+1}
$$

gives the modes $L_n$. The commutator of two such charges is determined by contour deformation. Because the $TT$ OPE has a fourth-order pole, the commutator is not merely the classical Witt algebra: it includes the central extension.

The slogan is

$$
\text{local conformal symmetry in 2D}
\quad\leadsto\quad
\text{Virasoro algebra}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

This page uses the standard holomorphic CFT normalization

$$
T(z)T(w)
\sim
\frac{c/2}{(z-w)^4}
+\frac{2T(w)}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{\partial T(w)}{z-w}.
$$

For a primary field $\phi(w,\bar w)$ of weights $(h,\bar h)$,

$$
T(z)\phi(w,\bar w)
\sim
\frac{h\phi(w,\bar w)}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{\partial_w\phi(w,\bar w)}{z-w}.
$$

The antiholomorphic stress tensor has the analogous expansion

$$
\bar T(\bar z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\bar L_n \bar z^{-n-2},
$$

with central charge $\bar c$. In a parity-invariant bosonic CFT one often has $c=\bar c$, but it is important not to assume this blindly: $c-\bar c$ measures a gravitational anomaly.

We use radial quantization on the plane. Equal-radius commutators are radial-ordered commutators, and adjoints are discussed below.

## From vector fields to Witt generators

Start with holomorphic vector fields on the punctured plane. A conventional basis is

$$
\ell_n=-z^{n+1}\partial_z,
\qquad n\in\mathbb Z.
$$

A short computation gives

$$
[\ell_m,\ell_n]
=
\ell_m\ell_n-\ell_n\ell_m
=(m-n)\ell_{m+n}.
$$

This is the **Witt algebra**. It is the classical algebra of local holomorphic conformal transformations.

The three generators

$$
\ell_{-1}=-\partial_z,
\qquad
\ell_0=-z\partial_z,
\qquad
\ell_1=-z^2\partial_z
$$

generate translations, dilatations plus rotations, and special conformal transformations in the holomorphic sector. They span the finite-dimensional global subalgebra $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb C)$ in the holomorphic coordinate.

The modes $L_n$ are the quantum generators corresponding to these vector fields. Depending on sign conventions, one may put the minus sign into $\ell_n$ or into the definition of the infinitesimal transformation. The physical convention fixed here is the contour definition of $L_n$.

## Laurent modes of the stress tensor

Because $T(z)$ has holomorphic weight $2$, its Laurent expansion around the origin is

$$
T(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}L_n z^{-n-2}.
$$

The inverse formula is

$$
L_n=\oint_0\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,z^{n+1}T(z).
$$

This is not merely a formal expansion. In radial quantization, $L_n$ is an operator acting on states on circles centered at the origin. Moving the contour radially past operator insertions implements conformal transformations of those insertions.

For a primary inserted at the origin,

$$
|\phi\rangle=\phi(0)|0\rangle,
$$

the OPE $T(z)\phi(0)$ implies

$$
L_0|\phi\rangle=h|\phi\rangle,
\qquad
L_n|\phi\rangle=0\quad n>0.
$$

The negative modes create holomorphic descendants:

$$
L_{-n}|\phi\rangle,
\qquad n>0.
$$

Thus, in 2D CFT, a primary field is not just accompanied by ordinary derivative descendants. It generates a whole Virasoro module.

## Deriving the commutator

The commutator $[L_m,L_n]$ is determined by the singular part of the $TT$ OPE. Begin with

$$
[L_m,L_n]
=
\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}\,w^{n+1}
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,z^{m+1}T(z)T(w).
$$

Only the singular terms in $T(z)T(w)$ contribute. Insert

$$
T(z)T(w)
\sim
\frac{c/2}{(z-w)^4}
+\frac{2T(w)}{(z-w)^2}
+\frac{\partial T(w)}{z-w}.
$$

The second-order pole gives

$$
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,\frac{z^{m+1}}{(z-w)^2}
=
(m+1)w^m.
$$

The first-order pole gives

$$
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,\frac{z^{m+1}}{z-w}=w^{m+1}.
$$

The stress-tensor terms therefore contribute

$$
\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}\,w^{n+1}\left(2(m+1)w^mT(w)+w^{m+1}\partial T(w)\right).
$$

Integrating the derivative term by parts,

$$
\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}\,w^{m+n+2}\partial T(w)
=-(m+n+2)L_{m+n}.
$$

The total noncentral contribution is

$$
\left(2m+2-m-n-2\right)L_{m+n}
=(m-n)L_{m+n}.
$$

The fourth-order pole gives

$$
\frac{c}{2}\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}\,w^{n+1}
\frac{1}{3!}\partial_w^3 w^{m+1}.
$$

Since

$$
\partial_w^3 w^{m+1}=(m+1)m(m-1)w^{m-2},
$$

the residue is nonzero only for $m+n=0$, and equals

$$
\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

Thus

$$
[L_m,L_n]=(m-n)L_{m+n}+\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

This is the Virasoro algebra.

## Why the central term vanishes for the global subalgebra

For $m,n\in\{-1,0,1\}$, the factor

$$
m(m^2-1)
$$

vanishes. Therefore the central extension does not affect

$$
L_{-1},\quad L_0,\quad L_1.
$$

These generate the global holomorphic conformal group. This is why global conformal kinematics can often be developed without mentioning the central charge, while local 2D conformal symmetry cannot.

In particular,

$$
[L_1,L_{-1}]=2L_0,
\qquad
[L_0,L_{\pm1}]=\mp L_{\pm1}.
$$

The antiholomorphic generators $\bar L_{-1},\bar L_0,\bar L_1$ give another copy. Together they form the global conformal algebra on the plane.

## Action on primary fields

The OPE $T(z)\phi(w,\bar w)$ gives the commutator of modes with a primary field:

$$
[L_n,\phi(w,\bar w)]
=
\left(w^{n+1}\partial_w+h(n+1)w^n\right)\phi(w,\bar w).
$$

For $n=-1,0,1$, this reproduces translation, scaling plus rotation, and special conformal transformations. For all $n\in\mathbb Z$, it defines how local holomorphic transformations act on a primary.

At the origin, this simplifies. The state

$$
|\phi\rangle=\phi(0)|0\rangle
$$

satisfies

$$
L_0|\phi\rangle=h|\phi\rangle,
\qquad
L_n|\phi\rangle=0\quad n>0.
$$

This is the highest-weight condition. A Virasoro primary is a highest-weight state for the full Virasoro algebra, not merely for the global subalgebra.

## Descendants and levels

A descendant is obtained by applying negative modes:

$$
L_{-n_1}L_{-n_2}\cdots L_{-n_k}|h\rangle,
\qquad n_i>0.
$$

Its level is

$$
N=n_1+n_2+\cdots+n_k.
$$

Using the commutator with $L_0$,

$$
[L_0,L_{-n}]=nL_{-n},
$$

we find

$$
L_0\left(L_{-n_1}\cdots L_{-n_k}|h\rangle\right)
=(h+N)L_{-n_1}\cdots L_{-n_k}|h\rangle.
$$

Before null states are removed, the number of descendants at level $N$ is the number $p(N)$ of integer partitions of $N$.

For example,

| Level | Basis before null relations |
|---:|---|
| 0 | $|h\rangle$ |
| 1 | $L_{-1}|h\rangle$ |
| 2 | $L_{-2}|h\rangle$, $L_{-1}^2|h\rangle$ |
| 3 | $L_{-3}|h\rangle$, $L_{-2}L_{-1}|h\rangle$, $L_{-1}^3|h\rangle$ |

The full local operator is usually labeled by both holomorphic and antiholomorphic modules:

$$
(h,\bar h),
\qquad
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h.
$$

## Radial quantization and adjoints

In unitary radial quantization on the plane, the adjoint of a Virasoro mode is

$$
L_n^\dagger=L_{-n},
\qquad
\bar L_n^\dagger=\bar L_{-n}.
$$

This condition encodes reflection positivity. It is what turns the Virasoro algebra into a Hilbert-space constraint rather than a formal algebra.

For a highest-weight state $|h\rangle$ normalized by $\langle h|h\rangle=1$, the level-one descendant has norm

$$
\|L_{-1}|h\rangle\|^2
=\langle h|L_1L_{-1}|h\rangle
=2h.
$$

Therefore unitarity requires

$$
h\ge 0.
$$

At higher levels, positivity gives stronger constraints involving $c$ and $h$. These are the entry point to Verma modules, null states, the Kac determinant, and minimal models.

## The vacuum representation

The vacuum state $|0\rangle$ is invariant under global conformal transformations:

$$
L_{-1}|0\rangle=L_0|0\rangle=L_1|0\rangle=0.
$$

It also satisfies

$$
L_n|0\rangle=0\quad n\ge -1,
$$

because $T(z)|0\rangle$ should be regular enough at the origin for the vacuum insertion. The first nontrivial descendant of the vacuum is

$$
L_{-2}|0\rangle,
$$

which corresponds to the stress tensor itself:

$$
T(0)|0\rangle=L_{-2}|0\rangle.
$$

This is why the stress tensor belongs to the vacuum module.

## A useful contour identity

Let $\mathcal O_i(z_i,\bar z_i)$ be local operators, and let $\epsilon(z)$ be holomorphic away from insertions. The Ward identity can be written as

$$
\left\langle
\oint_C\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,\epsilon(z)T(z)
\prod_i\mathcal O_i(z_i,\bar z_i)
\right\rangle
=
\sum_{i\in C}
\left\langle
\delta_\epsilon\mathcal O_i(z_i,\bar z_i)
\prod_{j\ne i}\mathcal O_j(z_j,\bar z_j)
\right\rangle.
$$

For a primary field,

$$
\delta_\epsilon\phi(z_i,\bar z_i)
=\left(\epsilon(z_i)\partial_{z_i}+h_i\partial_{z_i}\epsilon(z_i)\right)\phi(z_i,\bar z_i).
$$

Choosing $\epsilon(z)=z^{n+1}$ gives the action of $L_n$. This is the contour form of local conformal symmetry.

## Common checks

A good Virasoro calculation should pass these checks.

| Check | What it catches |
|---|---|
| Global subalgebra | The central term must vanish for $L_{-1},L_0,L_1$. |
| Primary OPE | $T(z)\phi(w)$ must reproduce the correct $h$ and derivative terms. |
| Vacuum conditions | $L_n|0\rangle=0$ for $n\ge -1$ on the plane. |
| Hermitian conjugation | In a unitary theory, $L_n^\dagger=L_{-n}$. |
| Stress-tensor state | $T(0)|0\rangle=L_{-2}|0\rangle$. |
| Central term | The coefficient in $TT$ must give $c m(m^2-1)/12$. |

When a sign or factor is wrong, one of these checks usually catches it quickly. A small mercy from the algebra gods, who otherwise enjoy watching factors of $2\pi i$ breed in the margins.

## Common pitfalls

**Confusing the Witt algebra with the Virasoro algebra.** The Witt algebra is the centerless algebra of holomorphic vector fields. The Virasoro algebra is its central extension. In a quantum CFT, the stress-tensor modes obey the Virasoro algebra.

**Thinking that every local holomorphic map is globally allowed.** A function $f(z)$ can be locally conformal without being a globally well-defined transformation of the Riemann sphere. The modes $L_n$ with $|n|>1$ generate local transformations, not global Möbius transformations.

**Forgetting the antiholomorphic copy.** A local operator in a full 2D CFT usually carries both weights $(h,\bar h)$. The holomorphic Virasoro algebra alone is a chiral half of the story.

**Calling every primary a Virasoro primary.** A field can be primary under the global conformal group without being primary under the full Virasoro algebra. In this chapter, “primary” means Virasoro primary unless stated otherwise.

**Ignoring null states.** The free descendant count $p(N)$ is the Verma-module count before quotienting by null descendants. Minimal models rely precisely on null states and the resulting quotient modules.

**Treating the central charge as a removable constant.** The central term commutes with all generators, but it is not physically optional. It controls stress-tensor normalization, finite-size energies, anomalies, and modular asymptotics.

## Research status

The Virasoro algebra itself is settled mathematical physics. Its representation theory, characters, null states, and modular properties are standard tools in two-dimensional CFT.

Active research often starts after the basic algebra is in place: vertex operator algebras, logarithmic modules, nonsemisimple tensor categories, irrational CFTs, modular bootstrap, boundary CFT, holographic Virasoro blocks, and chiral algebras associated to higher-dimensional supersymmetric theories all use Virasoro symmetry as part of a larger structure.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Derive the Witt algebra

With

$$
\ell_n=-z^{n+1}\partial_z,
$$

show that

$$
[\ell_m,\ell_n]=(m-n)\ell_{m+n}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Act on a test function $f(z)$:

$$
\ell_m\ell_n f
=z^{m+1}\partial_z\left(z^{n+1}\partial_z f\right)
=(n+1)z^{m+n+1}\partial_z f+z^{m+n+2}\partial_z^2 f.
$$

Interchanging $m$ and $n$ gives

$$
\ell_n\ell_m f
=(m+1)z^{m+n+1}\partial_z f+z^{m+n+2}\partial_z^2 f.
$$

Subtracting,

$$
[\ell_m,\ell_n]f=(n-m)z^{m+n+1}\partial_z f.
$$

Since

$$
\ell_{m+n}=-z^{m+n+1}\partial_z,
$$

we obtain

$$
[\ell_m,\ell_n]=(m-n)\ell_{m+n}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Check the central term

Use the fourth-order pole in the $TT$ OPE to show that the central contribution to $[L_m,L_n]$ is

$$
\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The fourth-order pole contributes

$$
\frac{c}{2}\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}\,w^{n+1}
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,\frac{z^{m+1}}{(z-w)^4}.
$$

Cauchy's formula gives

$$
\oint_w\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,\frac{z^{m+1}}{(z-w)^4}
=\frac{1}{3!}\partial_w^3 w^{m+1}
=\frac{(m+1)m(m-1)}{6}w^{m-2}.
$$

Thus the remaining residue is

$$
\frac{c}{2}\frac{m(m^2-1)}{6}
\oint_0\frac{dw}{2\pi i}\,w^{m+n-1}
=
\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Norm of the first descendant

Let $|h\rangle$ be a highest-weight state with $L_0|h\rangle=h|h\rangle$, $L_n|h\rangle=0$ for $n>0$, and $\langle h|h\rangle=1$. Show that

$$
\|L_{-1}|h\rangle\|^2=2h.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Using $L_{-1}^\dagger=L_1$,

$$
\|L_{-1}|h\rangle\|^2
=\langle h|L_1L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Because $L_1|h\rangle=0$, we may insert the commutator:

$$
\langle h|L_1L_{-1}|h\rangle
=\langle h|[L_1,L_{-1}]|h\rangle.
$$

The central term vanishes for $m=1,n=-1$, and

$$
[L_1,L_{-1}]=2L_0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\|L_{-1}|h\rangle\|^2
=2\langle h|L_0|h\rangle=2h.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Stress tensor as a vacuum descendant

Show from the Laurent expansion that

$$
T(0)|0\rangle=L_{-2}|0\rangle
$$

provided $L_n|0\rangle=0$ for $n\ge -1$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The Laurent expansion gives

$$
T(z)|0\rangle=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}z^{-n-2}L_n|0\rangle.
$$

The vacuum condition removes all terms with $n\ge -1$, which would have negative or zero powers singular at the origin. The term finite at $z=0$ is obtained from $-n-2=0$, namely $n=-2$:

$$
T(z)|0\rangle=L_{-2}|0\rangle+O(z).
$$

Taking $z\to0$ gives the result.

</details>
