## Summary

Virasoro Ward identities are the practical rules that turn local conformal symmetry into equations for correlation functions. In two dimensions the holomorphic stress tensor $T(z)$ is not just another operator: its contour integrals generate local holomorphic coordinate transformations.

For primary fields $\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)$ with weights $(h_i,\bar h_i)$, the basic holomorphic Ward identity on the plane is

$$
\left\langle T(z)\prod_{i=1}^n \phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)\right\rangle
=\sum_{i=1}^n\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z-z_i)^2}+\frac{1}{z-z_i}\partial_{z_i}
\right)
\left\langle \prod_{i=1}^n \phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)\right\rangle .
$$

The antiholomorphic copy is obtained by replacing $T,z,h_i,\partial_{z_i}$ with $\bar T,\bar z,\bar h_i,\partial_{\bar z_i}$.

The integrated form is even more important. For a holomorphic test function $\epsilon(z)$, define

$$
Q_\epsilon[C]=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C dz\,\epsilon(z)T(z).
$$

Deforming the contour $C$ through the insertions gives

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C dz\,\epsilon(z)
\left\langle T(z)\prod_i \phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)\right\rangle
=
\sum_i
\left(\epsilon(z_i)\partial_{z_i}+h_i\partial\epsilon(z_i)\right)
\left\langle\prod_i \phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)\right\rangle .
$$

This page derives these identities, explains the role of the central term when other stress tensors are inserted, and shows how global conformal invariance, Virasoro symmetry, descendant insertions, and null-vector differential equations all come from the same contour-deformation mechanism.

## Core idea

In ordinary symmetry language, a Ward identity follows from inserting a conserved current and moving the integration surface. In two-dimensional CFT, the holomorphic stress tensor $T(z)$ is the current for local conformal transformations, and the integration surface is a contour.

The singular part of the OPE

$$
T(z)\phi(w,\bar w)
\sim
\frac{h\phi(w,\bar w)}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{\partial_w\phi(w,\bar w)}{z-w}
$$

contains the local transformation law of a primary field. The double pole knows the conformal weight. The simple pole knows translation of the insertion point.

Therefore the contour integral of $\epsilon(z)T(z)$ around $w$ gives

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_w dz\,\epsilon(z)T(z)\phi(w,\bar w)
=
\left(\epsilon(w)\partial_w+h\partial\epsilon(w)\right)\phi(w,\bar w).
$$

The Ward identity is this formula inserted into an arbitrary correlator.

This is the little machine behind an absurd amount of two-dimensional CFT. It turns symmetry into differential operators.

## Setup and conventions

We use the CFT-normalized holomorphic stress tensor with

$$
T(z)T(w)
\sim
\frac{c/2}{(z-w)^4}
+
\frac{2T(w)}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{\partial T(w)}{z-w},
$$

and Virasoro modes

$$
L_n=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_0 dz\,z^{n+1}T(z).
$$

A primary field of weights $(h,\bar h)$ has the holomorphic OPE

$$
T(z)\phi(w,\bar w)
\sim
\frac{h\phi(w,\bar w)}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{\partial_w\phi(w,\bar w)}{z-w}.
$$

This convention implies the infinitesimal mode action

$$
[L_n,\phi(w,\bar w)]
=
\left(w^{n+1}\partial_w+(n+1)h w^n\right)\phi(w,\bar w).
$$

For $n=-1,0,1$ this is the global $SL(2,\mathbb C)$ action on the holomorphic coordinate. For other $n$, the vector fields are local conformal transformations, not globally defined maps of the sphere.

## Deriving the one-stress-tensor identity

Let

$$
G_n=\left\langle \prod_{i=1}^n\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)\right\rangle.
$$

The OPE of $T(z)$ with $\phi_i$ implies that the correlator

$$
F(z)=\left\langle T(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)\right\rangle
$$

has the singular behavior

$$
F(z)
\sim
\frac{h_iG_n}{(z-z_i)^2}
+
\frac{\partial_{z_i}G_n}{z-z_i}
\qquad z\to z_i.
$$

On the sphere, $T(z)$ has weight two and the correlator has no extra singularity except possibly at infinity. If the insertion at infinity is the vacuum, the pole data at the finite points determine $F(z)$:

$$
F(z)
=\sum_i\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z-z_i)^2}
+
\frac{1}{z-z_i}\partial_{z_i}
\right)G_n.
$$

The derivation is almost disappointingly short. The power comes from the fact that the singularity data are exact.

## Integrated form and contour deformation

Multiply the local identity by $\epsilon(z)$ and integrate over a contour enclosing all insertions:

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C dz\,\epsilon(z)
\left\langle T(z)\prod_i\phi_i\right\rangle
=
\sum_i\left(
\epsilon_i\partial_i+h_i\partial_i\epsilon_i
\right)G_n,
$$

where $\epsilon_i=\epsilon(z_i)$ and $\partial_i\epsilon_i=\partial_z\epsilon(z)|_{z=z_i}$.

Equivalently,

$$
\left\langle Q_\epsilon[C]\prod_i\phi_i\right\rangle
=
\sum_i\left\langle
\phi_1\cdots(\delta_\epsilon\phi_i)\cdots\phi_n
\right\rangle,
$$

with

$$
\delta_\epsilon\phi_i
=
(\epsilon_i\partial_i+h_i\partial_i\epsilon_i)\phi_i.
$$

This is exactly the Noether-current Ward identity, but written in complex coordinates and upgraded to infinitely many holomorphic vector fields.

## Global Ward identities

The globally regular holomorphic vector fields on the sphere are

$$
\epsilon(z)=1,
\qquad
\epsilon(z)=z,
\qquad
\epsilon(z)=z^2.
$$

If the corresponding contour is pushed to infinity, one obtains

$$
\sum_i\partial_iG_n=0,
$$

$$
\sum_i(z_i\partial_i+h_i)G_n=0,
$$

and

$$
\sum_i(z_i^2\partial_i+2h_i z_i)G_n=0.
$$

These are the holomorphic translation, dilation/rotation, and special-conformal Ward identities. Together with the antiholomorphic identities, they fix the position dependence of two-point and three-point functions and leave four-point functions depending on cross-ratios.

For example, a chiral two-point function satisfies

$$
\langle \phi_1(z_1)\phi_2(z_2)\rangle
=\frac{C_{12}}{z_{12}^{h_1+h_2}}
$$

from translation and dilation invariance, while special conformal invariance forces $h_1=h_2$ when $C_{12}\ne 0$. In an orthonormal primary basis this becomes

$$
\langle \phi_i(z_1)\phi_j(z_2)\rangle
=\frac{\delta_{ij}}{z_{12}^{2h_i}}.
$$

## Multiple stress tensors

When more stress tensors are inserted, the same logic applies, but now $T(z)$ has singularities when it approaches another $T(w)$. The $TT$ OPE gives

$$
T(z)T(w)
\sim
\frac{c/2}{(z-w)^4}
+
\frac{2T(w)}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{\partial T(w)}{z-w}.
$$

Thus the contour integral around $w$ produces

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_w dz\,\epsilon(z)T(z)T(w)
=
\epsilon(w)\partial T(w)+2\partial\epsilon(w)T(w)+\frac{c}{12}\partial^3\epsilon(w).
$$

The first two terms are what a weight-two field would do. The last term is the Virasoro anomaly. It is the same central term that appears in

$$
[L_m,L_n]=(m-n)L_{m+n}+\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

This is also why the stress tensor is quasiprimary under the global conformal group but not primary under the full Virasoro algebra when $c\ne0$.

## Descendant insertions

Descendant fields are created by Virasoro modes acting on primaries. In operator language,

$$
L_{-n}\phi(0)
=
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_0 dz\,z^{-n+1}T(z)\phi(0),
\qquad n>0.
$$

Inside a correlator, the contour defining $L_{-n}\phi(0)$ may be deformed away from the origin. It then wraps the other insertions and possibly infinity. Therefore descendant correlators can be written as differential operators acting on primary correlators.

For instance,

$$
\langle (L_{-1}\phi)(z)\mathcal X\rangle
=\partial_z\langle \phi(z)\mathcal X\rangle.
$$

A less trivial example is $L_{-2}$. For insertions $\phi_i(z_i)$ away from $z$, contour deformation gives a differential operator of the schematic form

$$
\langle (L_{-2}\phi)(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)\rangle
=
\sum_i\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z_i-z)^2}-\frac{1}{z_i-z}\partial_i
\right)
\langle \phi(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)\rangle,
$$

with the overall sign depending on the orientation convention for the deformed contour. The key point survives all conventions: Virasoro descendants are not independent dynamical data.

## Null states and BPZ equations

Suppose the Verma module of a primary $\phi$ contains a null state. A level-two example has the form

$$
\left(L_{-2}-\alpha L_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle=0.
$$

Then, in any physical correlator,

$$
\langle (L_{-2}\phi)(z)\mathcal X\rangle
=\alpha\,\partial_z^2\langle \phi(z)\mathcal X\rangle.
$$

But the Ward identity expresses the left side by contour deformation as differential operators acting on the other insertion points. Therefore the correlator obeys a differential equation.

This is the BPZ mechanism. Null vectors are representation-theoretic facts; Ward identities translate them into differential equations for correlators. Minimal models are solvable largely because enough of these differential equations exist.

## Virasoro blocks

A four-point function may be decomposed into Virasoro conformal blocks. The Ward identities are what make the decomposition precise: once the exchanged primary and the external primaries are fixed, all descendant contributions are fixed by Virasoro symmetry.

Schematically,

$$
\langle \phi_1\phi_2\phi_3\phi_4\rangle
=\sum_{\mathcal O}
C_{12\mathcal O}C_{34\mathcal O}\,
\mathcal F_{\mathcal O}^{\mathrm{Vir}}(z)\,
\bar{\mathcal F}_{\mathcal O}^{\mathrm{Vir}}(\bar z).
$$

The primary OPE coefficients are dynamical data. The descendant coefficients inside $\mathcal F^{\mathrm{Vir}}$ are fixed by Ward identities, Gram matrices, and the representation theory of the Virasoro algebra.

This distinction matters later when comparing global and Virasoro blocks. A Virasoro block resums infinitely many global descendants associated with repeated stress-tensor exchange.

## Common pitfalls

**Forgetting that Ward identities are distributional.** The formulas above are for separated points. When insertions collide, contact terms and regularization choices can matter.

**Treating all holomorphic vector fields as global symmetries.** On the sphere only $1,z,z^2$ are globally regular. Other local vector fields are useful in contour arguments, but they can have poles or singularities at infinity.

**Calling the stress tensor primary.** The $TT$ OPE central term means $T$ is not a Virasoro primary for $c\ne0$. It is quasiprimary under the global conformal subgroup.

**Dropping the antiholomorphic sector.** A local nonchiral CFT has both Virasoro algebras. A chiral algebra by itself is not automatically a full local CFT.

**Confusing a Ward identity with an equation of motion.** Ward identities are symmetry constraints. They do not require a Lagrangian and they do not say the theory is free.

## Exercises

### Exercise: Derive the primary Ward identity

Starting from

$$
T(z)\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)
\sim
\frac{h_i\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)}{(z-z_i)^2}
+
\frac{\partial_i\phi_i(z_i,\bar z_i)}{z-z_i},
$$

show that the one-stress-tensor Ward identity follows.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Insert $T(z)$ in the correlator. Near $z=z_i$, the OPE gives the singular part

$$
\left\langle T(z)\prod_j\phi_j\right\rangle
\sim
\frac{h_i}{(z-z_i)^2}G_n+
\frac{1}{z-z_i}\partial_iG_n.
$$

The sum of these principal parts over all $i$ has exactly the required singularities. On the sphere, the difference between the true correlator and this sum is holomorphic everywhere and has no allowed pole at infinity, hence vanishes.

</details>

### Exercise: Recover the global Ward identities

Use $\epsilon(z)=1,z,z^2$ in the integrated Ward identity to derive the holomorphic global Ward identities.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $\epsilon=1$,

$$
0=\sum_i\partial_iG_n.
$$

For $\epsilon=z$,

$$
0=\sum_i(z_i\partial_i+h_i)G_n.
$$

For $\epsilon=z^2$,

$$
0=\sum_i(z_i^2\partial_i+2h_i z_i)G_n.
$$

The left side is zero because these vector fields generate globally well-defined conformal transformations of the sphere, so the contour can be pushed to infinity without residue.

</details>

### Exercise: The anomalous transformation of the stress tensor

Use the $TT$ OPE to show that

$$
\delta_\epsilon T(w)
=
\epsilon(w)\partial T(w)+2\partial\epsilon(w)T(w)+\frac{c}{12}\partial^3\epsilon(w).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
\delta_\epsilon T(w)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_w dz\,\epsilon(z)T(z)T(w).
$$

The simple pole contributes $\epsilon\partial T$. The double pole contributes $2\partial\epsilon\,T$. The fourth-order pole contributes

$$
\frac{c}{2}\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_w dz\,\frac{\epsilon(z)}{(z-w)^4}
=\frac{c}{2}\frac{1}{3!}\partial^3\epsilon(w)
=\frac{c}{12}\partial^3\epsilon(w).
$$

</details>
