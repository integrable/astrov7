## Summary

A **null state** in a Virasoro representation is a descendant state that is also highest-weight. In the chiral Verma module generated from a primary state $|h\rangle$, a state $|\chi\rangle$ at positive level is singular when

$$
L_n|\chi\rangle=0\quad n>0,
\qquad
L_0|\chi\rangle=(h+N)|\chi\rangle,
$$

where $N$ is the level of $|\chi\rangle$. In the standard contravariant inner product, a singular vector is orthogonal to the whole Verma module. In a unitary theory this means it has zero norm and should be divided out.

Null states are where representation theory becomes physics. If a descendant field is null, then insertions of that descendant vanish in physical correlators. For low-level null states this gives ordinary differential equations for correlation functions. These are the Belavin–Polyakov–Zamolodchikov, or BPZ, equations that make minimal models exactly solvable.

The basic picture is

$$
\text{Verma module}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{singular vector}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{null submodule}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{irreducible quotient}.
$$

Do not read “null” as “unimportant.” Null states are unphysical in the quotient, but their vanishing is a powerful constraint on the remaining physical correlators.

## Core idea

A generic Verma module is too large. It is freely generated by acting with all negative Virasoro modes on a highest-weight state:

$$
V(c,h)=\operatorname{span}\left\{
L_{-n_1}L_{-n_2}\cdots L_{-n_k}|h\rangle
\;:\;
 n_1\ge n_2\ge\cdots\ge n_k\ge 1
\right\}.
$$

At level $N=n_1+\cdots+n_k$, the number of formal descendant states is the partition number $p(N)$. Most values of $(c,h)$ give an irreducible module: no nonzero descendant is also highest-weight.

Special values of $(c,h)$ are different. A linear combination of descendants may become a new highest-weight state inside the module. Such a state generates a smaller Verma module embedded inside the original one. In a physical irreducible representation, the embedded null module is quotiented out.

The punchline is short but potent:

$$
\text{null descendant} = \text{representation-theoretic redundancy with observable consequences}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

Let $|h\rangle$ be a chiral Virasoro highest-weight state:

$$
L_0|h\rangle=h|h\rangle,
\qquad
L_n|h\rangle=0\quad n>0.
$$

A descendant has the form

$$
L_{-\lambda}|h\rangle
\equiv
L_{-\lambda_1}\cdots L_{-\lambda_k}|h\rangle,
\qquad
\lambda_1\ge\cdots\ge\lambda_k\ge 1.
$$

The level is $|\lambda|=\lambda_1+\cdots+\lambda_k$, and

$$
L_0L_{-\lambda}|h\rangle=(h+|\lambda|)L_{-\lambda}|h\rangle.
$$

The standard inner product is determined by

$$
\langle h|h\rangle=1,
\qquad
L_n^\dagger=L_{-n}.
$$

This is sometimes called the Shapovalov, Gram, or contravariant form. It is the form whose determinant is studied on the [Kac Determinant Preview](/cft-bootstrap/virasoro-central-charge/kac-determinant-preview/).

## Singular vectors and null states

A descendant $|\chi_N\rangle$ at level $N>0$ is a **singular vector** if

$$
L_n|\chi_N\rangle=0\quad n>0.
$$

It is then itself a highest-weight state, with weight $h+N$. Therefore the states

$$
L_{-\mu}|\chi_N\rangle
$$

span a submodule of $V(c,h)$. This submodule is stable under all Virasoro generators. Negative modes obviously stay inside the submodule. Positive modes can be commuted through the negative modes until they either hit $|\chi_N\rangle$, where they vanish, or produce lower descendants of $|\chi_N\rangle$.

Why is the word “null” justified? Take any descendant $|\psi\rangle=L_{-\lambda}|h\rangle$. Then

$$
\langle \psi|\chi_N\rangle
=
\langle h|L_{\lambda_k}\cdots L_{\lambda_1}|\chi_N\rangle.
$$

If $|\psi\rangle$ has the same level as $|\chi_N\rangle$, the positive modes annihilate $|\chi_N\rangle$ after commuting. If the levels differ, the inner product vanishes because the two states have different $L_0$ eigenvalues. Thus

$$
\langle \psi|\chi_N\rangle=0
\qquad
\text{for all } |\psi\rangle\in V(c,h).
$$

In particular,

$$
\langle \chi_N|\chi_N\rangle=0.
$$

All descendants of $|\chi_N\rangle$ are also orthogonal to the full module. The embedded submodule is therefore a null submodule.

:::note[Terminology]
Many physics texts use “singular vector,” “null vector,” and “null state” almost interchangeably. A precise convention is: a singular vector is a descendant highest-weight vector; with the radial inner product it is null; the corresponding local field is a null field.
:::

## Level-one example: the identity descendant

At level one, the only descendant is

$$
L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Its norm is

$$
\langle h|L_1L_{-1}|h\rangle
=
\langle h|[L_1,L_{-1}]|h\rangle
=2h.
$$

Thus a level-one null state appears when

$$
h=0.
$$

For the identity operator $\mathbf 1$, this is exactly what should happen:

$$
L_{-1}|0\rangle
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\partial \mathbf 1=0.
$$

The identity representation is not the full Verma module built on $h=0$. The state $L_{-1}|0\rangle$ and all of its descendants are quotiented out. This is why the vacuum module is smaller than a generic Verma module.

There is a useful lesson here. Null states are not an exotic feature of minimal models. The identity module of any sensible 2D CFT already contains a basic null descendant.

## Level-two example: the first BPZ null vector

At level two, a general descendant is

$$
|\chi\rangle=
\left(L_{-2}+aL_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle.
$$

The condition $L_1|\chi\rangle=0$ gives

$$
3+a(4h+2)=0,
$$

so

$$
a=-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}.
$$

The condition $L_2|\chi\rangle=0$ then gives

$$
16h^2+2(c-5)h+c=0.
$$

Therefore a level-two singular vector exists when

$$
|\chi_{2}\rangle=
\left(L_{-2}-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}L_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle
$$

and

$$
c=\frac{2h(5-8h)}{2h+1}.
$$

Equivalently, for a fixed $c$, the allowed $h$ values are the two Kac weights usually called $h_{1,2}$ and $h_{2,1}$. These two possibilities produce second-order BPZ equations for correlators containing the corresponding degenerate primary field.

The state above is the first place where the algebra stops being merely a bookkeeping device. A single linear relation among descendants turns into a differential equation for correlation functions.

## Null fields and decoupling in correlators

By the state–operator correspondence, a singular state corresponds to a local field. For example,

$$
L_{-1}|h\rangle
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\partial\phi_h(z),
$$

and

$$
\left(L_{-2}-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}L_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\left(L_{-2}\phi_h\right)(z)-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}\partial^2\phi_h(z).
$$

In an irreducible representation, a null field is identified with zero. Therefore every physical correlator satisfies

$$
\left\langle
\chi(z)\,\mathcal O_1(z_1)\cdots \mathcal O_n(z_n)
\right\rangle=0.
$$

This equation is not a new equation of motion imposed on the microscopic fields. It is the statement that the Hilbert space representation contains a redundant null direction.

For a level-two null vector, the stress-tensor Ward identity converts $L_{-2}\phi_h$ into differential operators acting on the other insertions. With the local-mode convention used here,

$$
\left\langle (L_{-2}\phi_h)(z)\prod_i \phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle
=
\sum_i\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z-z_i)^2}
+
\frac{1}{z-z_i}\frac{\partial}{\partial z_i}
\right)
\left\langle\phi_h(z)\prod_i \phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle.
$$

Thus the level-two null relation gives

$$
\left[
\sum_i\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z-z_i)^2}
+
\frac{1}{z-z_i}\frac{\partial}{\partial z_i}
\right)
-
\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial z^2}
\right]
\left\langle\phi_h(z)\prod_i \phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle=0.
$$

This is the prototype of a BPZ decoupling equation. After using global conformal symmetry to fix three insertion points, a four-point function with one level-two degenerate field obeys a second-order ordinary differential equation. In minimal models, these equations constrain the allowed fusion channels and turn the bootstrap into an exact algebraic problem.

## Quotienting the null submodule

Suppose $V(c,h)$ contains a singular vector $|\chi_N\rangle$. The reducible Verma module contains the submodule

$$
V_\chi=U(\mathrm{Vir}_{<0})|\chi_N\rangle,
$$

where $U(\mathrm{Vir}_{<0})$ denotes the universal enveloping algebra generated by negative modes. The irreducible module is the quotient

$$
M(c,h)=V(c,h)/V_\chi
$$

if this is the only null submodule. In minimal models, there are often several singular vectors and nested submodules. Then one quotients by the maximal proper submodule.

This quotient changes the character. A generic Verma module has character

$$
\chi_{c,h}^{\mathrm{Verma}}(q)
=
\frac{q^{h-c/24}}{\prod_{n=1}^{\infty}(1-q^n)}.
$$

If there is a null submodule generated at level $N$, a first rough correction is to subtract the descendant tower beginning at $h+N$:

$$
\chi_{c,h}^{\mathrm{quotient}}(q)
\sim
\chi_{c,h}^{\mathrm{Verma}}(q)-\chi_{c,h+N}^{\mathrm{Verma}}(q).
$$

The symbol $\sim$ is a warning: when null submodules contain further null submodules, inclusion–exclusion continues. The famous minimal-model characters arise from this nested-submodule structure.

## Degenerate representations

A primary field is called **degenerate** when its Virasoro representation contains null states. Degenerate fields are the workhorses of exactly solvable 2D CFT.

For a degenerate field, the chiral conformal family has fewer independent descendants than a generic family. This has three major consequences:

| Consequence | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Differential equations | Null-state decoupling turns correlators into solutions of finite-order differential equations. |
| Fusion restrictions | Only some intermediate representations are compatible with the null equations. |
| Minimal spectra | In minimal models, finitely many degenerate representations close under fusion. |

A useful slogan is

$$
\text{degeneracy of representations}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{differential equations for correlators}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{fusion rules}.
$$

This is one of the deepest mechanisms in 2D CFT. The local algebra knows so much that it can force global correlation functions to solve special-function equations.

## What null states are not

Null states are not the same thing as descendants. Every null state in this chapter is a descendant, but most descendants are not null.

Null states are also not the same thing as gauge redundancy, although the quotienting language looks similar. Gauge redundancy is a redundancy in the description of a theory. A Virasoro null state is a zero-norm direction in a representation of the physical chiral symmetry algebra. In string theory these ideas meet, but in ordinary 2D CFT they should not be blurred.

Finally, a null relation is not always a statement that a local expression vanishes before renormalization or before choosing a Hilbert-space sector. The clean statement is representation-theoretic:

$$
\text{the irreducible module identifies all states in the null submodule with zero}.
$$

## Common pitfalls

**Confusing singular with merely low norm.** A singular vector is annihilated by all positive $L_n$. A small norm after numerical truncation is not enough.

**Forgetting descendants of null states.** If $|\chi\rangle$ is null, then $L_{-n}|\chi\rangle$ and all other descendants of $|\chi\rangle$ must also be removed.

**Using the Verma character after quotienting.** A degenerate irreducible module has fewer states than a generic Verma module. This matters for modular invariance and partition functions.

**Assuming null means unitary.** Nonunitary theories also have singular vectors. Null-state decoupling and positivity are different conditions.

**Treating the BPZ equation as optional.** If a correlator contains a degenerate primary and one works in the irreducible quotient, the decoupling equation is mandatory.

## Research status

The structure of null states in ordinary Virasoro Verma modules is classical and well understood. It is one of the standard pillars of two-dimensional CFT.

The subtleties begin when the representation category is not semisimple. Logarithmic CFTs contain indecomposable modules, Jordan blocks for $L_0$, and null-like substructures that do not behave like the simple unitary quotient picture. Irrational theories such as Liouville theory also use Virasoro representation theory, but their spectra and degenerate insertions play a different role from minimal models.

For this volume, the safe default is: use null states exactly as described here for unitary minimal models, standard rational CFT examples, and BPZ equations; add explicit warnings when moving to logarithmic, nonunitary, or extended-chiral-algebra settings.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Level-one null state

Show that $L_{-1}|h\rangle$ has norm $2h$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Using $L_n^\dagger=L_{-n}$,

$$
\|L_{-1}|h\rangle\|^2
=\langle h|L_1L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Since $L_1|h\rangle=0$,

$$
\langle h|L_1L_{-1}|h\rangle
=\langle h|[L_1,L_{-1}]|h\rangle.
$$

The Virasoro commutator gives $[L_1,L_{-1}]=2L_0$, so

$$
\|L_{-1}|h\rangle\|^2=2h\langle h|h\rangle.
$$

With $\langle h|h\rangle=1$, the norm is $2h$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Level-two singular vector

Let

$$
|\chi\rangle=(L_{-2}+aL_{-1}^2)|h\rangle.
$$

Derive the conditions on $a$, $h$, and $c$ for $|\chi\rangle$ to be singular.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The condition $L_1|\chi\rangle=0$ gives

$$
L_1L_{-2}|h\rangle=3L_{-1}|h\rangle,
$$

and

$$
L_1L_{-1}^2|h\rangle=(4h+2)L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Therefore

$$
3+a(4h+2)=0,
$$

so

$$
a=-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}.
$$

Next,

$$
L_2L_{-2}|h\rangle=(4h+c/2)|h\rangle,
$$

and

$$
L_2L_{-1}^2|h\rangle=6h|h\rangle.
$$

Thus

$$
4h+\frac c2+6ah=0.
$$

Substituting the value of $a$ gives

$$
16h^2+2(c-5)h+c=0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
c=\frac{2h(5-8h)}{2h+1}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Orthogonality of a singular vector

Let $|\chi_N\rangle$ be a singular vector at level $N$. Show that it is orthogonal to every state in the Verma module.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A basis state has the form $|\psi\rangle=L_{-\lambda}|h\rangle$. If $|\psi\rangle$ has level different from $N$, then $|\psi\rangle$ and $|\chi_N\rangle$ have different $L_0$ eigenvalues, so their inner product vanishes.

If $|\psi\rangle$ has level $N$, then

$$
\langle\psi|\chi_N\rangle
=\langle h|L_{\lambda_k}\cdots L_{\lambda_1}|\chi_N\rangle.
$$

All $\lambda_i$ are positive. Commute the positive modes to the right. Any positive mode that reaches $|\chi_N\rangle$ annihilates it. Commutators either produce positive modes or reduce the number of modes, and the same argument repeats. Hence the inner product vanishes.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Why null states imply fusion restrictions

Explain in words why a correlator containing a level-two degenerate field has fewer possible intermediate channels than a generic four-point function.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The level-two null vector gives a second-order differential equation for the correlator. A second-order equation has a two-dimensional local solution space near a regular singular point. In an OPE limit, independent local solutions correspond to allowed leading powers, hence to allowed intermediate weights. A generic primary has infinitely many possible intermediate primaries in its OPE. The null equation cuts this down to a finite set compatible with the differential equation.

This is the mechanism behind BPZ fusion rules.

</details>
