Virasoro Symmetry and Central Charge is the chapter in the CFT and Bootstrap volume where local holomorphic conformal symmetry becomes an algebra of quantum operators. The previous chapter introduced complex coordinates, local conformal maps, the two-dimensional stress tensor, radial quantization, free examples, compact bosons, and logarithmic previews. This chapter turns those ingredients into the Virasoro machine.

The central object is the holomorphic stress tensor and its Laurent modes,

$$
T(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\frac{L_n}{z^{n+2}}.
$$

Classically, the modes generate local conformal transformations. Quantum mechanically, their algebra acquires a central term:

$$
[L_m,L_n]
=
(m-n)L_{m+n}
+
\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

The number $c$ is the **central charge**. It controls the $TT$ OPE, the cylinder vacuum energy, the conformal anomaly, the density of states, and much of the structure of two-dimensional CFT. If two-dimensional CFT is a cathedral, the Virasoro algebra is the scaffolding nobody should remove.

## Landmarks

The classical holomorphic vector fields on the punctured plane are

$$
\ell_n=-z^{n+1}\partial_z,
\qquad n\in\mathbb Z,
$$

with Witt algebra

$$
[\ell_m,\ell_n]=(m-n)\ell_{m+n}.
$$

The quantum generators are stress-tensor modes,

$$
L_n=\oint_0\frac{dz}{2\pi i}\,z^{n+1}T(z),
$$

and their algebra is the Virasoro algebra,

$$
[L_m,L_n]
=
(m-n)L_{m+n}
+
\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

The stress-tensor OPE is the local form of the same statement:

$$
T(z)T(w)
\sim
\frac{c/2}{(z-w)^4}
+
\frac{2T(w)}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{\partial T(w)}{z-w}.
$$

A primary field of holomorphic weight $h$ is characterized by

$$
T(z)\phi(w)
\sim
\frac{h\phi(w)}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{\partial\phi(w)}{z-w}.
$$

In radial quantization, the corresponding highest-weight state obeys

$$
L_0|h\rangle=h|h\rangle,
\qquad
L_n|h\rangle=0\quad n>0.
$$

Descendants are obtained by acting with lowering modes:

$$
L_{-n_1}L_{-n_2}\cdots L_{-n_k}|h\rangle,
\qquad n_i>0.
$$

On the cylinder, the central charge shifts the Hamiltonian. For a diagonal theory with $c=\bar c$,

$$
H_{\rm cyl}
=
L_0+\bar L_0-\frac{c}{12},
$$

when the spatial circle has circumference $2\pi$. Equivalently, each chiral sector contributes a vacuum shift $-c/24$.

The central charge also appears in the Weyl anomaly. On a curved two-dimensional Euclidean background,

$$
\langle T^a{}_a\rangle
\propto -cR,
$$

with the exact numerical coefficient depending on the normalization of the metric variation and stress tensor. The dedicated anomaly page fixes the coefficient in the conventions of this volume.

## Common confusions

**Witt algebra versus Virasoro algebra.** The Witt algebra is the classical algebra of vector fields. The Virasoro algebra is its central extension. In quantum CFT, the central extension is not optional bookkeeping.

**Central charge versus number of fields.** In free examples, $c$ often counts effective degrees of freedom: $c=1$ for a free compact boson and $c=1/2$ for a free Majorana fermion. In interacting or nonunitary theories, $c$ is a structural number, not a literal field count.

**Global primary versus Virasoro primary.** A Virasoro primary is annihilated by all $L_{n>0}$. A global primary only needs the global conditions associated with $L_1$ and, in the antiholomorphic sector, $\bar L_1$. Virasoro primarity is stronger.

**Null state versus zero operator.** A null descendant can be zero in the physical quotient of a unitary or minimal model, but one must distinguish the vector in the Verma module from its equivalence class after quotienting.

**Holomorphic sector versus full theory.** A chiral algebra controls one sector. A complete local CFT must combine holomorphic and antiholomorphic sectors so that correlation functions are single-valued and modularly consistent.

**Anomaly versus inconsistency.** The conformal anomaly is not a failure of local conformal methods on the plane. It is the controlled response of the quantum theory to Weyl transformations of the background metric.

## Boundaries

This chapter develops the Virasoro algebra, its central charge, and its basic representation theory. It does not replace the [Two-Dimensional CFT](/cft-bootstrap/two-dimensional-cft/) chapter, which sets up complex coordinates, local conformal transformations, the cylinder–plane map, free models, compact bosons, and orbifolds.

It also does not fully classify rational CFTs. Minimal models, modular invariant partition functions, characters, fusion rules, and rational CFT structure belong mainly to [Minimal Models and Rational CFT](/cft-bootstrap/minimal-models-rational-cft/).

Affine current algebras and WZW models have their own chapter because the Kac–Moody algebra adds internal symmetry currents on top of Virasoro symmetry. Modular bootstrap has its own chapter because it emphasizes torus consistency rather than local operator algebra.

Logarithmic CFTs require nonsemisimple Virasoro representation theory. The [Logarithmic CFT Preview](/cft-bootstrap/two-dimensional-cft/logarithmic-cft-preview/) page explains the first warning signs, but this chapter mostly follows the semisimple highest-weight route first.
