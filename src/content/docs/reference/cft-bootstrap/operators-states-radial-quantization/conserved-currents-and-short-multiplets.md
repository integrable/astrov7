## Summary

A conserved current is a local operator whose divergence vanishes. In a CFT this differential equation is also a representation-theoretic statement: the divergence is a descendant, and conservation says that this descendant is null.

For an ordinary global symmetry current,

$$
\partial^\mu J^a_\mu=0,
\qquad
\Delta_J=d-1.
$$

For a symmetric-traceless spin-$\ell$ current with $\ell\ge1$,

$$
\partial^{\mu_1}J_{\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_\ell}=0,
\qquad
\Delta_J=\ell+d-2.
$$

The dimension is not an extra assumption. It is the saturation value of the spin-$\ell$ unitarity bound. Above the bound the conformal multiplet is long; at the bound the divergence descendant has zero norm and the multiplet shortens.

This page explains the shortening mechanism, how ordinary global-symmetry charges arise from currents, why conserved higher-spin currents are special, and how these facts prepare the stress-tensor and current-correlator pages.

The page uses Euclidean CFT conventions. Lorentzian currents are obtained by analytic continuation, with the same representation-theoretic shortening condition.

## Core idea

A CFT local operator is not just a field with indices. It is a state in radial quantization. A primary creates a lowest-weight state, and its descendants are generated by $P_\mu$:

$$
|\mathcal O\rangle,
\qquad
P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle,
\qquad
P_\mu P_\nu|\mathcal O\rangle,
\qquad
\ldots
$$

For a spin-$\ell$ symmetric-traceless primary $J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}$, one particular level-one descendant is the divergence,

$$
P^{\mu_1}|J_{\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_\ell}\rangle
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\partial^{\mu_1}J_{\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_\ell}(x).
$$

In a long multiplet this descendant is a real state with positive norm. At the unitarity bound its norm becomes zero. In a unitary theory, zero-norm descendants decouple from all correlators and are set to zero in the physical representation. The operator equation

$$
\partial^{\mu_1}J_{\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_\ell}=0
$$

is therefore the local-operator version of multiplet shortening.

That is the first mental model to keep: **conservation is shortening**.

## Setup and conventions

We work in flat Euclidean $d$-dimensional space with metric $\delta_{\mu\nu}$. A symmetric-traceless spin-$\ell$ primary is written

$$
J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}(x),
\qquad
J_{(\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell)}=J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell},
\qquad
\delta^{\mu_1\mu_2}J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0.
$$

Its scaling dimension is $\Delta$. In radial quantization, the corresponding state satisfies

$$
D|J\rangle=\Delta |J\rangle,
\qquad
K_\mu|J\rangle=0,
$$

and descendants are built by applying $P_\mu$. The adjoint relation

$$
P_\mu^\dagger=K_\mu
$$

turns descendant norms into algebraic consequences of the conformal commutators.

For symmetric-traceless spin $\ell\ge1$, the unitarity bound is

$$
\Delta\ge \ell+d-2.
$$

The divergence descendant has norm proportional to

$$
\Delta-(\ell+d-2),
$$

up to a positive representation-dependent normalization. Therefore positivity requires the bound, and saturation gives a null divergence descendant.

:::note[Scope of this page]
Here “short multiplet” means a shortened representation of the ordinary conformal algebra. It does not mean a supersymmetric BPS multiplet, although supersymmetric shortening is built from the same idea: some descendant states are null because a bound is saturated.
:::

## Spin-one currents and global symmetries

An ordinary continuous global symmetry in a local QFT is generated by a conserved spin-one current

$$
\partial^\mu J^a_\mu=0,
$$

where $a$ labels the Lie algebra of the symmetry group. In a CFT, a conserved spin-one current is a primary of dimension

$$
\Delta_J=d-1.
$$

This value can be seen in two ways. First, it is the spin-one unitarity-bound saturation value. Second, the charge

$$
Q^a_\Sigma=\int_\Sigma dS_\mu\,J^{a\mu}
$$

should be dimensionless. Since $dS_\mu$ has length dimension $d-1$, the current must have scaling dimension $d-1$.

Conservation means $Q^a_\Sigma$ depends only on which operators the surface $\Sigma$ surrounds. If $\Sigma$ is moved without crossing an insertion, the charge is unchanged. If it crosses a charged local operator, the Ward identity records the symmetry action.

With $S_{d-1}$ the volume of the unit $(d-1)$-sphere, a convenient singular term in the current OPE is

$$
J^a_\mu(x)\mathcal O_i(0)
\sim
\frac{x_\mu}{S_{d-1}|x|^d}(t^a)_i{}^j\mathcal O_j(0)+\cdots .
$$

Integrating this term over a small sphere around the origin gives

$$
Q^a\mathcal O_i=(t^a)_i{}^j\mathcal O_j.
$$

This is the local CFT version of the statement that currents generate symmetries.

## Current two-point normalization

Conformal symmetry fixes the two-point function of conserved spin-one currents up to one positive coefficient. For a simple flavor symmetry with generators normalized in some chosen convention,

$$
\langle J^a_\mu(x)J^b_\nu(0)\rangle
=
\frac{C_J\delta^{ab}}{(x^2)^{d-1}}I_{\mu\nu}(x),
$$

where

$$
I_{\mu\nu}(x)=\delta_{\mu\nu}-2\frac{x_\mu x_\nu}{x^2}.
$$

The coefficient $C_J$ is part of the CFT data. Its numerical value depends on the normalization of the symmetry generators, but ratios and convention-fixed comparisons are physical. In a unitary CFT, $C_J>0$ for a nonzero current.

In bootstrap applications, $C_J$ is often constrained by studying correlators of charged operators or of the current itself. Ward identities fix some OPE coefficients involving $J^a_\mu$, while dynamics determines the rest.

## General spin currents

The same shortening logic applies to symmetric-traceless currents of higher spin:

$$
J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell},
\qquad
\partial^{\mu_1}J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0,
\qquad
\Delta=\ell+d-2.
$$

The cases $\ell=1$ and $\ell=2$ are universal enough to deserve special attention:

| Spin | Operator | Dimension | Meaning |
|---:|---|---:|---|
| $1$ | $J^a_\mu$ | $d-1$ | ordinary global symmetry current |
| $2$ | $T_{\mu\nu}$ | $d$ | stress tensor, generator of spacetime symmetries |

For $\ell>2$, a conserved current is usually a sign of exceptional structure. Free scalar and free fermion CFTs have towers of higher-spin conserved currents. Interacting unitary CFTs in $d>2$ generally do not. More precise higher-spin theorems require assumptions about unitarity, locality, the spectrum, and dimension, so the safe lesson at this stage is:

> Higher-spin conservation is not generic CFT data. It is a powerful diagnostic of extra symmetry.

Approximate higher-spin currents are also important. In weakly broken higher-spin systems, large-$N$ vector models, and lightcone bootstrap limits, the divergence of a would-be current is small or controlled rather than zero. Those refinements belong to later pages.

## Shortening conditions as equations of motion

Shortening is not limited to currents. The scalar unitarity bound gives another familiar example. A scalar primary has

$$
\Delta\ge\frac{d-2}{2},
$$

except for the identity. At saturation the level-two descendant is null:

$$
P^2|\phi\rangle=0
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\partial^2\phi=0.
$$

That is the free scalar equation of motion. Thus a free field is also a short conformal multiplet.

The common pattern is:

| Bound saturated | Null descendant | Operator equation |
|---|---|---|
| scalar $\Delta=(d-2)/2$ | $P^2|\phi\rangle$ | $\partial^2\phi=0$ |
| spin $1$, $\Delta=d-1$ | $P^\mu|J_\mu\rangle$ | $\partial^\mu J_\mu=0$ |
| spin $2$, $\Delta=d$ | $P^\mu|T_{\mu\nu}\rangle$ | $\partial^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0$ |
| spin $\ell$, $\Delta=\ell+d-2$ | $P^{\mu_1}|J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}\rangle$ | $\partial^{\mu_1}J_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0$ |

This table is a good diagnostic tool. When you see a conservation law in CFT, ask which unitarity bound has been saturated and which descendant has disappeared.

## Two-dimensional caveat

In two dimensions, it is often better to use holomorphic coordinates. A conserved spin-one current can split into chiral pieces,

$$
J_z(z),
\qquad
J_{\bar z}(\bar z),
$$

with weights $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$ in chiral cases. The stress tensor splits into

$$
T(z),
\qquad
\bar T(\bar z),
$$

and generates the Virasoro algebra rather than merely the finite-dimensional global conformal algebra.

The shortening idea remains useful, but the representation theory is richer: descendants can be global conformal descendants, Virasoro descendants, Kac–Moody descendants, or combinations. The general lesson “conservation means a null descendant or chiral simplification” survives, but the precise multiplet language changes.

## Common pitfalls

**A conserved current is not just any vector operator.** A vector primary with $\Delta>d-1$ is a perfectly valid spin-one operator, but it is not a conserved current and does not generate an ordinary global symmetry.

**The charge fixes the singular OPE.** The coefficient of the leading current–operator OPE is not arbitrary once the current and symmetry-generator normalization are fixed. It must integrate to the charge action.

**Short does not mean small.** A short multiplet can still contain infinitely many non-null descendants. Shortening means that some descendants are set to zero, not that the representation has only finitely many states.

**Conservation is convention-independent; $C_J$ is not.** Whether $\partial^\mu J_\mu=0$ is invariant. The numerical value of $C_J$ changes when the current or Lie-algebra generators are rescaled.

**Higher-spin currents are not harmless decorations.** In an interacting $d>2$ unitary CFT, an exactly conserved spin-$\ell>2$ current is a dramatic constraint. Treat it as a structural signal, not just another operator in a table.

## Research status

The representation-theoretic relation between conserved currents, unitarity-bound saturation, and null divergence descendants is standard. So are the Ward identities for ordinary global symmetry currents and the stress tensor.

Active and advanced directions include higher-spin symmetry theorems, weakly broken higher-spin symmetry, current correlators with spin, conformal collider bounds, current bootstrap systems, defect currents, supersymmetric shortening, and higher-form symmetries. These are not changes to the basic shortening mechanism; they are elaborations of what extra structure can do with it.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Use dimensional analysis of the charge

$$
Q=\int_\Sigma dS_\mu J^\mu
$$

to show that a conserved spin-one current in a CFT has $\Delta_J=d-1$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The charge must be dimensionless because it acts as a symmetry generator. The surface element $dS_\mu$ on a sphere surrounding an insertion has length dimension $d-1$, or mass dimension $-(d-1)$. If $J_\mu$ has scaling dimension $\Delta_J$, then the charge has dimension

$$
\Delta_J-(d-1).
$$

Setting this to zero gives

$$
\Delta_J=d-1.
$$

This agrees with the spin-one unitarity-bound saturation value.

</details>

### Exercise 2

Check the normalization of the current OPE

$$
J^a_\mu(x)\mathcal O_i(0)
\sim
\frac{x_\mu}{S_{d-1}|x|^d}(t^a)_i{}^j\mathcal O_j(0).
$$

Integrate over a sphere of radius $r$ centered at the origin and show that it gives the generator $t^a$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

On the sphere, $x_\mu=rn_\mu$ and $dS_\mu=n_\mu r^{d-1}d\Omega$. Therefore

$$
\int_{S_r^{d-1}}dS_\mu\frac{x_\mu}{S_{d-1}|x|^d}
=
\int d\Omega\frac{n_\mu r^{d-1}rn_\mu}{S_{d-1}r^d}
=
\frac{1}{S_{d-1}}\int d\Omega
=1.
$$

Thus the integral gives

$$
Q^a\mathcal O_i=(t^a)_i{}^j\mathcal O_j,
$$

as required. Orientation and factors of $i$ depend on Euclidean versus Lorentzian charge conventions.

</details>

### Exercise 3

A symmetric-traceless spin-$\ell$ primary in a unitary $d\ge3$ CFT has $\Delta=\ell+d-2$. What local operator equation should you expect?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The value

$$
\Delta=\ell+d-2
$$

saturates the spin-$\ell$ unitarity bound. At this value the level-one divergence descendant is null:

$$
P^{\mu_1}|J_{\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_\ell}\rangle=0.
$$

In local-operator language this is

$$
\partial^{\mu_1}J_{\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_\ell}=0.
$$

So the primary is a conserved spin-$\ell$ current.

</details>
