## Summary

Primary and descendant operators are the local-operator analogue of lowest-weight states and raising-operator descendants in ordinary representation theory. In radial quantization, a local operator $\mathcal O(0)$ creates a state

$$
|\mathcal O\rangle=\mathcal O(0)|0\rangle.
$$

A primary operator is a local operator whose state is annihilated by the special conformal generators:

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0.
$$

Descendants are obtained by acting with translations:

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

In position space these are derivatives of the primary, together with spin-index projections and possible improvements. The commutator $[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu$ implies that a level-$n$ descendant has scaling dimension $\Delta+n$ if the primary has scaling dimension $\Delta$.

This distinction is not cosmetic. The CFT data are normally listed using primary operators: their scaling dimensions, spins, global-symmetry representations, and OPE coefficients. Descendants are real operators, but their correlation functions and OPE coefficients are determined kinematically from the primary data.

:::note[The slogan]
A primary is an independent local degree of conformal representation theory. A descendant is what conformal symmetry forces to come along with it.
:::

## Core idea

The conformal algebra contains the grading operator $D$ and two vector operators with opposite grading:

$$
[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu,
\qquad
[D,K_\mu]=-K_\mu.
$$

Thus $P_\mu$ raises scaling dimension by one, while $K_\mu$ lowers scaling dimension by one. If the spectrum of $D$ is bounded below, it is natural to start from states that cannot be lowered any further. These are primary states.

A primary state is therefore the conformal analogue of a lowest-energy state in a harmonic oscillator. The descendants are the tower generated by acting with raising operators:

$$
|\mathcal O\rangle,
\qquad
P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle,
\qquad
P_\mu P_\nu|\mathcal O\rangle,
\qquad
\ldots.
$$

This tower is a conformal multiplet. The primary labels the multiplet. The descendants fill it out.

## Setup and conventions

We use the radial-quantization convention

$$
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta|\mathcal O\rangle,
\qquad
[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu,
\qquad
[D,K_\mu]=-K_\mu,
$$

and

$$
[K_\mu,P_\nu]=2\delta_{\mu\nu}D-2M_{\mu\nu}
$$

in Euclidean signature. Lorentzian formulas replace $\delta_{\mu\nu}$ by $\eta_{\mu\nu}$, with the usual care about Hermitian-generator factors of $i$.

A local operator may carry spacetime spin and internal global-symmetry indices. We write such an operator schematically as $\mathcal O_a(x)$, where $a$ packages all representation indices. At the origin, the rotation generators act by spin matrices:

$$
M_{\mu\nu}|\mathcal O_a\rangle
=(\Sigma_{\mu\nu})_a{}^b|\mathcal O_b\rangle.
$$

A primary is usually chosen to have definite scaling dimension and an irreducible representation of the rotation group $SO(d)$ in Euclidean signature. In Lorentzian signature, one often labels local operators by representations of the spatial rotation group after radial quantization or by Lorentz representations before Wick rotation.

## Definition at the origin

A local operator $\mathcal O_a(0)$ is a primary operator if the state it creates satisfies

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O_a\rangle=0,
$$

and if it has definite $D$ and rotation quantum numbers:

$$
D|\mathcal O_a\rangle=\Delta|\mathcal O_a\rangle,
$$

$$
M_{\mu\nu}|\mathcal O_a\rangle
=(\Sigma_{\mu\nu})_a{}^b|\mathcal O_b\rangle.
$$

The number $\Delta$ is the scaling dimension. The representation carried by $a$ is the spin representation. For a scalar primary, $\Sigma_{\mu\nu}=0$.

The descendants of $\mathcal O$ are the states

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O_a\rangle,
\qquad n=1,2,3,\ldots.
$$

The integer $n$ is called the level. Because the translation generators commute,

$$
[P_\mu,P_\nu]=0,
$$

the derivative indices are symmetric before decomposing into irreducible spin representations.

## Position-space meaning

At the origin, acting with $P_\mu$ corresponds to differentiating the operator:

$$
P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
\quad\leftrightarrow\quad
\partial_\mu\mathcal O(0)|0\rangle,
$$

up to the sign convention used for active charges. The invariant statement is that translations generate changes of the insertion point, so derivatives of a primary are descendants.

For example, a scalar primary $\phi$ has descendants

$$
\partial_\mu\phi,
\qquad
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\phi,
\qquad
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\partial_\rho\phi,
\qquad
\ldots.
$$

At level two, the symmetric tensor $\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\phi$ decomposes into a traceless spin-two part and a scalar trace:

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\phi
=
\left(\partial_\mu\partial_\nu-\frac{1}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2\right)\phi
+
\frac{1}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2\phi.
$$

Both terms are descendants of $\phi$. They have different spin, but they belong to the same conformal multiplet.

## Finite transformation law for primaries

The primary condition at the origin is equivalent to a simple finite transformation law. Let $x'=f(x)$ be a conformal map of flat Euclidean space, so that

$$
ds'^2=\Omega(x)^2 ds^2.
$$

A scalar primary of dimension $\Delta$ transforms as

$$
\mathcal O'(x')=\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}\mathcal O(x).
$$

For a spinning primary,

$$
\mathcal O'_a(x')
=
\Omega(x)^{-\Delta}R_a{}^b(x)\mathcal O_b(x),
$$

where $R_a{}^b(x)$ is the local rotation induced by the Jacobian after removing the scale factor. This is why primary operators are the natural building blocks for covariant correlation functions.

Under a dilatation $x'=\lambda x$, one has $\Omega=\lambda$, so

$$
\mathcal O'(x')=\lambda^{-\Delta}\mathcal O(x).
$$

Under inversion $x'=x/x^2$, one has $\Omega=1/x^2$, so a scalar primary picks up the factor $(x^2)^\Delta$. The spin rotation for inversion is the reflection matrix

$$
I_\mu{}^\nu(x)=\delta_\mu{}^\nu-2\frac{x_\mu x^\nu}{x^2}.
$$

These transformation laws are stronger than mere scale covariance. They encode how the operator behaves under the full conformal group.

## Descendants are usually not primaries

A derivative of a primary is generally not primary. The algebra shows this immediately. For a scalar primary,

$$
K_\nu P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=[K_\nu,P_\mu]|\mathcal O\rangle
=2\delta_{\mu\nu}\Delta|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Unless $\Delta=0$ or the state has zero norm in a degenerate way, $P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle$ is not annihilated by $K_\nu$. It is a descendant, not a new primary.

At higher levels, special linear combinations of derivatives can sometimes be primary. More often, one builds new primaries by subtracting descendant contamination from composite operators. This is familiar in perturbative theories: an operator like $\phi\partial^2\phi$ may mix with descendants of $\phi^2$, and the primary basis requires diagonalizing the action of $D$ and removing total derivatives.

The important lesson is:

$$
\text{derivative} \Rightarrow \text{descendant},
\qquad
\text{composite} \not\Rightarrow \text{primary automatically}.
$$

## Examples

### Identity operator

The identity operator is a scalar primary with

$$
\Delta_{\mathbf 1}=0.
$$

It creates the vacuum:

$$
|\mathbf 1\rangle=|0\rangle.
$$

Its descendants vanish because translations annihilate the vacuum:

$$
P_\mu|0\rangle=0.
$$

So the identity multiplet is extremely short.

### Free scalar field

In a free scalar CFT in $d>2$, the elementary scalar field is a primary with

$$
\Delta_\phi=\frac{d-2}{2}.
$$

Its first derivatives are descendants:

$$
\partial_\mu\phi.
$$

The equation of motion

$$
\partial^2\phi=0
$$

says that a level-two descendant is null. This is a shortening condition. The free scalar is not a generic scalar primary; it sits exactly at the scalar unitarity bound.

### Conserved current

A conserved global-symmetry current is a spin-one primary with

$$
\Delta_J=d-1,
$$

and conservation equation

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu=0.
$$

The divergence is a level-one descendant. Conservation says that this descendant vanishes, so the current multiplet is short.

### Stress tensor

The stress tensor is a spin-two primary in $d>2$, with

$$
\Delta_T=d,
$$

and conservation equation

$$
\partial^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

The stress tensor is universal: every local CFT with spacetime translations has one, up to improvement and normalization subtleties. Its conservation shortens its multiplet.

In two dimensions, the stress tensor has additional Virasoro significance and transforms with a Schwarzian term under general holomorphic maps. The global-primary language remains useful, but the full two-dimensional story is richer.

## Primary data and descendant data

A CFT spectrum is usually reported by listing primary operators:

$$
\{\mathcal O_i,\Delta_i,\rho_i\},
$$

where $\rho_i$ is a spin and global-symmetry representation. The OPE coefficients are also quoted for primaries:

$$
\lambda_{ijk}.
$$

Descendant coefficients are not additional independent numbers. They are fixed by conformal symmetry. Schematically, the OPE of two primaries takes the form

$$
\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)
\sim
\sum_k \lambda_{ijk}\,C_{ij}{}^k(x,\partial)\mathcal O_k(0),
$$

where $C_{ij}{}^k(x,\partial)$ is a differential operator that generates the descendants of $\mathcal O_k$ with coefficients fixed by conformal invariance.

This is why conformal blocks exist. A conformal block is the total contribution of one primary and all descendants in its multiplet to a four-point function.

## Two-dimensional terminology

Two-dimensional CFT has two layers of terminology.

A global primary is primary with respect to the finite global conformal algebra. In complex coordinates, the holomorphic part is generated by

$$
L_{-1},\quad L_0,\quad L_1,
$$

and similarly for the antiholomorphic generators. A global primary is annihilated by $L_1$ and $\bar L_1$ at the origin.

A Virasoro primary is stronger. It is annihilated by all positive Virasoro modes:

$$
L_n|h\rangle=0,
\qquad
\bar L_n|h,\bar h\rangle=0,
\qquad n>0.
$$

Every Virasoro primary is a global primary, but not every global primary is an independent Virasoro primary. Virasoro descendants include states obtained by $L_{-n}$ and $\bar L_{-n}$, not merely ordinary derivatives from $L_{-1}$ and $\bar L_{-1}$.

This chapter develops the global conformal structure first because it is the common language of CFT in any dimension. The Virasoro refinement belongs to [Virasoro Symmetry and Central Charge](/cft-bootstrap/virasoro-central-charge/).

## Practical checklist

When you meet an operator in a CFT, ask the following questions.

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Does it have definite $\Delta$? | If not, diagonalize mixing under dilatations first. |
| What spin representation does it carry? | Correlator tensor structures and unitarity bounds depend on spin. |
| Is it annihilated by $K_\mu$ at the origin? | This decides whether it is primary or descendant-contaminated. |
| Is it a total derivative? | Total derivatives are descendants. |
| Does it obey a conservation equation or equation of motion? | Such equations create null descendants and shorten the multiplet. |
| Is the theory two-dimensional? | Then global-primary and Virasoro-primary terminology must be separated. |

This checklist is especially useful in perturbative examples, where a naive composite-operator basis often contains both primaries and descendants.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not call every local operator a primary.** Local operators are organized into conformal multiplets. Only the lowest states are primaries.

**Do not call descendants unphysical.** Descendants appear in correlation functions and OPEs. They are just not independent CFT data.

**Do not confuse total derivatives with new operators in the spectrum.** A total derivative of a primary is a descendant. It may be useful in a Lagrangian basis, but it is not a separate primary.

**Do not forget equations of motion at a fixed point.** In a free scalar CFT, $\partial^2\phi$ vanishes as an operator. In an interacting fixed point, classical equations of motion are replaced by operator relations appropriate to the fixed point.

**Do not transfer two-dimensional Virasoro language blindly to higher dimensions.** Higher-dimensional CFT has a finite global conformal algebra. Two-dimensional CFT often has an infinite-dimensional local conformal algebra.

**Do not identify primary with fundamental.** Non-Lagrangian CFTs have primaries without fundamental fields. Conversely, Lagrangian fields can mix, improve, or fail to be primary in an interacting fixed-point basis.

## Research status

The primary-descendant distinction is a settled part of CFT representation theory. It is essential in two-dimensional CFT, higher-dimensional bootstrap, AdS/CFT, critical phenomena, and supersymmetric CFT.

The active work is not about whether primaries and descendants exist. It is about using this structure efficiently: constructing spinning primary bases, handling multiplet recombination along RG flows, organizing superconformal multiplets, quotienting null states in nonunitary or logarithmic theories, and generating conformal blocks for high-precision bootstrap computations.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Descendant dimensions

Let $|\mathcal O\rangle$ be a primary state with

$$
D|\mathcal O\rangle=\Delta|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Use $[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu$ to show that

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle
$$

has scaling dimension $\Delta+n$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For one $P_\mu$,

$$
D P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=P_\mu D|\mathcal O\rangle+[D,P_\mu]|\mathcal O\rangle
=(\Delta+1)P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Repeating the same argument gives one extra unit for each $P$:

$$
D P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle
=(\Delta+n)P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

This assumes no linear relation sets the descendant to zero.

</details>

### Exercise 2: A derivative is not usually primary

Let $|\mathcal O\rangle$ be a scalar primary. Use

$$
[K_\nu,P_\mu]=2\delta_{\mu\nu}D-2M_{\nu\mu}
$$

to show that $P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle$ is not usually primary.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A state is primary if it is annihilated by all $K_\nu$. Since $K_\nu|\mathcal O\rangle=0$,

$$
K_\nu P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=[K_\nu,P_\mu]|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

For a scalar primary,

$$
M_{\nu\mu}|\mathcal O\rangle=0,
$$

so

$$
K_\nu P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
=2\delta_{\mu\nu}\Delta|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

This is nonzero for a nonzero-norm scalar primary with $\Delta\ne0$. Therefore $P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle$ is generally a descendant, not a primary.

</details>

### Exercise 3: The identity multiplet

Explain why the identity operator has no nonzero descendants.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The identity creates the vacuum:

$$
|\mathbf 1\rangle=|0\rangle.
$$

The vacuum is translation invariant, so

$$
P_\mu|0\rangle=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
P_\mu|\mathbf 1\rangle=0,
$$

and all higher descendants also vanish. The identity multiplet contains only the identity state.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Level-two descendants of a scalar

For a scalar primary $\phi$, decompose $\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\phi$ into a traceless symmetric tensor and a scalar trace.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The trace is

$$
\delta^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\phi=\partial^2\phi.
$$

The traceless part is obtained by subtracting $1/d$ times the trace:

$$
\left(\partial_\mu\partial_\nu-\frac{1}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2\right)\phi.
$$

Thus

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\phi
=
\left(\partial_\mu\partial_\nu-\frac{1}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2\right)\phi
+
\frac{1}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2\phi.
$$

Both terms are level-two descendants of $\phi$, but they carry different spin.

</details>
