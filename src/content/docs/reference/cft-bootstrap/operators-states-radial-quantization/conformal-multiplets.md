## Summary

A conformal multiplet is the representation of the conformal algebra generated by one primary operator and all of its descendants. If $|\mathcal O\rangle$ is a primary state with scaling dimension $\Delta$ and spin representation $\rho$, then its multiplet is

$$
\mathcal V_{\Delta,\rho}
=
\operatorname{span}\left\{
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O_a\rangle
\,:\,
 n=0,1,2,\ldots
\right\}.
$$

The primary is the lowest-dimension state in the multiplet. Acting with $P_\mu$ raises the dimension by one. Acting with $K_\mu$ lowers descendants back toward the primary. The rotation generators $M_{\mu\nu}$ organize each level into spin representations.

Multiplets are the natural units of CFT. The spectrum lists primaries, not every derivative separately. The OPE sums over primary multiplets, not isolated operators. A conformal block is the contribution of one conformal multiplet to a four-point function.

## Core idea

The conformal algebra naturally splits into lowering, grading, rotation, and raising pieces:

$$
K_\mu
\quad
D,M_{\mu\nu}
\quad
P_\mu.
$$

A primary state is killed by all $K_\mu$:

$$
K_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle=0.
$$

The rest of its multiplet is generated by $P_\mu$:

$$
|\mathcal O\rangle
\longrightarrow
P_\mu|\mathcal O\rangle
\longrightarrow
P_\mu P_\nu|\mathcal O\rangle
\longrightarrow
\cdots.
$$

This is the same structural idea as a highest-weight or lowest-weight representation. In the radial-quantization convention used here, the primary is lowest in dimension, because descendants have dimensions $\Delta+n$.

The word “multiplet” is doing real work. The descendants are not independent CFT data, but they are not optional. Once a primary is present, conformal symmetry forces the entire tower to be present unless null relations shorten it.

## Setup and conventions

Let $|\mathcal O_a\rangle$ be a primary state with

$$
D|\mathcal O_a\rangle=\Delta|\mathcal O_a\rangle,
\qquad
K_\mu|\mathcal O_a\rangle=0,
$$

and with spin representation $\rho$ under rotations:

$$
M_{\mu\nu}|\mathcal O_a\rangle
=(\Sigma_{\mu\nu})_a{}^b|\mathcal O_b\rangle.
$$

The level-$n$ descendants are

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O_a\rangle.
$$

Since

$$
[D,P_\mu]=P_\mu,
$$

the level-$n$ descendants have dimension

$$
\Delta+n.
$$

Because

$$
[P_\mu,P_\nu]=0,
$$

the derivative indices are symmetric before decomposing into irreducible rotation representations.

## Levels and spin decomposition

The multiplet is graded by level:

| Level | Typical state | Dimension |
|---:|---|---:|
| 0 | $|\mathcal O_a\rangle$ | $\Delta$ |
| 1 | $P_\mu|\mathcal O_a\rangle$ | $\Delta+1$ |
| 2 | $P_\mu P_\nu|\mathcal O_a\rangle$ | $\Delta+2$ |
| $n$ | $P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\mathcal O_a\rangle$ | $\Delta+n$ |

For a scalar primary, the spin content is especially transparent. At level $n$, the descendants transform like symmetric rank-$n$ tensors of $SO(d)$, before quotienting null states. These decompose into traceless tensors plus traces.

At level zero:

$$
\phi.
$$

At level one:

$$
\partial_\mu\phi,
$$

a vector.

At level two:

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\phi
=
\left(\partial_\mu\partial_\nu-\frac{1}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2\right)\phi
+
\frac{1}{d}\delta_{\mu\nu}\partial^2\phi.
$$

So level two contains a traceless symmetric spin-two descendant and a scalar descendant.

The same idea works for spinning primaries, but now one tensors the spin representation $\rho$ with symmetric products of the vector representation and decomposes into irreducible representations.

## Generic multiplets

A generic multiplet has no null descendants. In that case, every formal descendant generated by $P_\mu$ is an independent state, after ordinary tensor decompositions.

For a scalar primary in $d$ dimensions, the number of level-$n$ derivative descendants before equations of motion or null relations is

$$
\binom{n+d-1}{n}.
$$

This is the number of symmetric rank-$n$ tensors built from $d$ commuting translation generators. Ignoring spin refinements, the scalar multiplet has the generating function

$$
\chi_{\Delta}^{\mathrm{scalar}}(q)
=
\frac{q^\Delta}{(1-q)^d}.
$$

The factor $q^\Delta$ records the primary dimension, and the denominator records arbitrary powers of $d$ translation generators. This is not yet the full character with angular fugacities, but it is a useful first count.

For a primary with spin representation $\rho$, the rough counting becomes

$$
\chi_{\Delta,\rho}(q)
\sim
\dim(\rho)\frac{q^\Delta}{(1-q)^d},
$$

again before resolving the detailed spin decomposition.

:::note[Why the counting is useful]
The simple factor $(1-q)^{-d}$ is the representation-theory shadow of ordinary derivatives. It is also why conformal-block calculations can be systematic: descendants are generated in a controlled graded tower.
:::

## Null descendants and quotienting

A null descendant is a descendant state with zero norm. In a unitary theory, null states decouple from all physical correlators and should be quotiented out of the multiplet.

The simplest symbolic picture is

$$
\mathcal V_{\Delta,\rho}^{\mathrm{physical}}
=
\frac{\text{formal descendants of the primary}}{\text{null descendants and their descendants}}.
$$

Null descendants are not a small technicality. They encode equations of motion, conservation laws, gauge redundancies after passing to gauge-invariant operators, and shortening conditions.

### Identity multiplet

The identity primary has

$$
\Delta=0,
\qquad
|\mathbf 1\rangle=|0\rangle.
$$

Since the vacuum is translation invariant,

$$
P_\mu|0\rangle=0.
$$

Thus all descendants vanish. The identity multiplet contains only the identity.

### Free scalar multiplet

In the free scalar CFT in $d>2$,

$$
\Delta_\phi=\frac{d-2}{2}.
$$

The equation of motion

$$
\partial^2\phi=0
$$

sets the level-two scalar descendant to zero:

$$
P^2|\phi\rangle=0.
$$

The physical multiplet is shorter than a generic scalar multiplet.

### Conserved current multiplet

A conserved current is a spin-one primary with

$$
\Delta_J=d-1.
$$

Conservation says

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu=0.
$$

In state language, this is a level-one null descendant:

$$
P^\mu|J_\mu\rangle=0.
$$

### Stress-tensor multiplet

The stress tensor is a spin-two primary with

$$
\Delta_T=d.
$$

Conservation says

$$
\partial^\mu T_{\mu\nu}=0,
$$

or in state language,

$$
P^\mu|T_{\mu\nu}\rangle=0.
$$

This shortening is universal in local CFTs with a conserved stress tensor.

## Multiplets and unitarity bounds

Unitarity bounds arise by requiring nonnegative norms for all descendants. In radial quantization,

$$
P_\mu^\dagger=K_\mu.
$$

Therefore descendant norms reduce to matrix elements of commutators involving $K_\mu$ and $P_\mu$. For a scalar primary,

$$
\langle P_\nu\mathcal O|P_\mu\mathcal O\rangle
=
2\delta_{\mu\nu}\Delta\langle\mathcal O|\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

This first-level check gives $\Delta\ge0$. Stronger scalar constraints come from higher levels and give the familiar nonidentity bound

$$
\Delta\ge\frac{d-2}{2}
$$

for unitary CFTs in $d>2$. Saturating this bound produces the free-scalar shortening $P^2|\phi\rangle=0$.

For symmetric traceless spin $\ell\ge1$, the unitary bound is

$$
\Delta\ge \ell+d-2.
$$

Saturating it gives a conserved-current type shortening:

$$
\partial^{\mu_1}\mathcal O_{\mu_1\cdots\mu_\ell}=0.
$$

The full derivation belongs to [Unitarity Bounds](/cft-bootstrap/operators-states-radial-quantization/unitarity-bounds/), but the multiplet logic is already clear: positivity restricts which multiplets can appear in a unitary CFT.

## Multiplets and the quadratic Casimir

All states in a conformal multiplet have the same eigenvalue of the conformal Casimir. For a symmetric traceless primary of spin $\ell$, the quadratic Casimir eigenvalue is

$$
C_2(\Delta,\ell)
=
\Delta(\Delta-d)+\ell(\ell+d-2).
$$

Descendants have different dimensions and spins at each level, but they belong to the same irreducible representation of the full conformal algebra, so the conformal Casimir eigenvalue is fixed by the primary.

This is the representation-theoretic reason conformal blocks satisfy Casimir differential equations. A block is the contribution of a definite exchanged multiplet. The Casimir equation says: this contribution belongs to the representation labeled by $(\Delta,\ell)$.

## Multiplets in the OPE

The OPE of two primaries is organized by exchanged primary multiplets:

$$
\mathcal O_i(x)\mathcal O_j(0)
\sim
\sum_{\mathcal O_k\ \mathrm{primary}}
\lambda_{ijk}\,C_{ij}{}^k(x,\partial)\mathcal O_k(0).
$$

The sum is over primaries. The differential operator $C_{ij}{}^k(x,\partial)$ generates the descendants of $\mathcal O_k$ with coefficients fixed by conformal symmetry.

This is one of the major simplifications of CFT. In a generic QFT, an OPE can contain many operators with independent Wilson coefficients. In a CFT, once the primary coefficient $\lambda_{ijk}$ is known, the entire descendant tower is fixed.

For a four-point function, this leads to the conformal-block decomposition

$$
\langle\mathcal O_1\mathcal O_2\mathcal O_3\mathcal O_4\rangle
=
\sum_{\mathcal O_k\ \mathrm{primary}}
\lambda_{12k}\lambda_{34k}\,G_{\Delta_k,\rho_k}(u,v),
$$

where $u$ and $v$ are cross-ratios, and $G_{\Delta_k,\rho_k}$ is the conformal block associated with the full multiplet of $\mathcal O_k$.

## Multiplet recombination

Multiplet shortening can disappear when a theory is deformed away from a free or special point. This phenomenon is called multiplet recombination.

A classic schematic example is the scalar equation of motion. In a free theory,

$$
\partial^2\phi=0,
$$

so $\partial^2\phi$ is a null descendant. In an interacting fixed point, an equation of motion may instead relate it to another operator:

$$
\partial^2\phi \sim \mathcal O_{\mathrm{interaction}}.
$$

Then the would-be short multiplet combines with another multiplet into a longer one. This idea appears in the Wilson–Fisher fixed point, supersymmetric shortening, and analytic bootstrap treatments near free limits.

The important caution is that multiplet recombination is a statement about operator relations at the fixed point, not about classical equations copied blindly from a microscopic Lagrangian.

## Two-dimensional refinement

In two-dimensional CFT, global conformal multiplets are not the end of the story. The local conformal algebra is enlarged to the Virasoro algebra, with generators $L_n$ and $\bar L_n$.

A Virasoro primary obeys

$$
L_n|h,\bar h\rangle=0,
\qquad
\bar L_n|h,\bar h\rangle=0,
\qquad n>0.
$$

Its Virasoro descendants are generated by

$$
L_{-n},\quad \bar L_{-n},
\qquad n>0.
$$

The global descendants generated by $L_{-1}$ and $\bar L_{-1}$ are only a small subset of the Virasoro descendants. Thus a single Virasoro conformal block usually resums much more structure than a global conformal block.

The global multiplet language remains essential because it applies in all dimensions and because higher-dimensional bootstrap uses global conformal blocks. The Virasoro enhancement is developed separately in the two-dimensional chapters.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not list descendants as independent CFT data.** The independent data are primary dimensions, representation labels, and primary OPE coefficients. Descendant data are fixed by symmetry.

**Do not assume a formal multiplet is physical before quotienting null states.** Equations of motion and conservation laws remove descendants.

**Do not confuse spin at a level with the spin of the primary.** A scalar primary has spinning descendants. A spin-$\ell$ primary has descendants in many spin representations.

**Do not think shortening is rare decoration.** The identity, conserved currents, stress tensors, free fields, and supersymmetric protected operators are all examples where shortening is central.

**Do not confuse global and Virasoro multiplets.** In two dimensions, Virasoro descendants greatly enlarge the multiplet relative to global descendants.

**Do not identify a conformal block with a single primary.** A block is the primary plus all descendants, organized by conformal symmetry.

## Research status

Conformal multiplets are a standard and settled part of CFT representation theory. The global multiplet structure is the basis of higher-dimensional conformal blocks and the numerical bootstrap.

Active research uses and refines this structure. Examples include spinning conformal blocks, mixed-correlator bootstrap systems, multiplet recombination near perturbative fixed points, superconformal shortening, defect multiplets, logarithmic multiplets in nonunitary theories, and high-performance algorithms for descendant recursion.

The main conceptual caveat is that representation theory is kinematic. It tells us how a possible CFT must organize operators. Crossing symmetry, unitarity, locality, and dynamics decide which multiplets and OPE coefficients actually coexist in a full theory.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Counting scalar descendants

For a scalar primary in $d$ dimensions, show that the number of independent formal level-$n$ descendants before null relations is

$$
\binom{n+d-1}{n}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A level-$n$ descendant is obtained by acting with $n$ commuting translation generators:

$$
P_{\mu_1}\cdots P_{\mu_n}|\phi\rangle.
$$

Because $[P_\mu,P_\nu]=0$, the indices are symmetric. Counting symmetric rank-$n$ tensors in $d$ dimensions is the same as counting monomials of degree $n$ in $d$ variables. By the stars-and-bars formula, this number is

$$
\binom{n+d-1}{n}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Scalar character

Use the counting from Exercise 1 to show that

$$
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\binom{n+d-1}{n}q^{\Delta+n}
=
\frac{q^\Delta}{(1-q)^d}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The binomial expansion gives

$$
\frac{1}{(1-q)^d}
=
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\binom{n+d-1}{n}q^n.
$$

Multiplying by $q^\Delta$ gives

$$
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\binom{n+d-1}{n}q^{\Delta+n}
=
\frac{q^\Delta}{(1-q)^d}.
$$

This counts formal scalar-primary descendants by dimension.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Shortening from a conserved current

Let $J_\mu$ be a spin-one primary. Explain why

$$
\partial^\mu J_\mu=0
$$

is a shortening condition.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The divergence $\partial^\mu J_\mu$ is obtained by applying one derivative to $J_\mu$. In state language, it corresponds to the level-one descendant

$$
P^\mu|J_\mu\rangle.
$$

If the current is conserved, this descendant vanishes:

$$
P^\mu|J_\mu\rangle=0.
$$

Therefore the current multiplet has fewer physical descendants than a generic spin-one multiplet. That is precisely a shortening condition.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Why a conformal block is a multiplet contribution

Suppose a primary $\mathcal O_k$ appears in the OPE $\mathcal O_1\times\mathcal O_2$. Explain why its descendants must also contribute to the same four-point conformal block.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The OPE in a CFT is organized by conformal multiplets:

$$
\mathcal O_1(x)\mathcal O_2(0)
\sim
\sum_k \lambda_{12k}C_{12}{}^k(x,\partial)\mathcal O_k(0).
$$

The differential operator $C_{12}{}^k(x,\partial)$ generates descendants of $\mathcal O_k$. Their coefficients are fixed by conformal symmetry once $\lambda_{12k}$ is known.

Therefore an exchanged primary cannot appear alone. Its whole descendant tower appears with it, and the combined contribution is the conformal block.

</details>
