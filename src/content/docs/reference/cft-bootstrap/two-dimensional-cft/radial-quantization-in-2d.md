## Summary

Radial quantization treats the radius on the complex plane as Euclidean time. Write

$$
z=e^{\tau+i\sigma},
\qquad
\bar z=e^{\tau-i\sigma},
\qquad
\sigma\sim\sigma+2\pi.
$$

Then circles $|z|=e^\tau$ become equal-time slices on the cylinder $\mathbb R_\tau\times S^1_\sigma$. Radial ordering on the plane becomes time ordering on the cylinder.

The two-dimensional stress tensor gives the mode operators

$$
L_n=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint dz\,z^{n+1}T(z),
\qquad
\bar L_n=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint d\bar z\,\bar z^{n+1}\bar T(\bar z).
$$

The generators of dilatations and rotations on the plane are

$$
D=L_0+\bar L_0,
\qquad
J=L_0-\bar L_0.
$$

For a primary operator $\phi$ of weights $(h,\bar h)$,

$$
D|\phi\rangle=(h+\bar h)|\phi\rangle,
\qquad
J|\phi\rangle=(h-\bar h)|\phi\rangle.
$$

Thus scaling dimension and spin are

$$
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h.
$$

This page explains how the plane, cylinder, state-operator correspondence, Virasoro modes, radial ordering, and cylinder vacuum energy fit together.

## Core idea

The core idea is that the punctured complex plane is a cylinder in disguise:

$$
\mathbb C^\times
\cong
\mathbb R_\tau\times S^1_\sigma,
\qquad
z=e^{\tau+i\sigma}.
$$

Local operators inserted near the origin prepare incoming states. Operators inserted near infinity define outgoing states. Contours of constant $|z|$ are Hilbert-space slices. The OPE converges because radial quantization lets us insert a complete set of states between concentric circles.

## Setup and conventions

We work on the Euclidean plane with complex coordinates

$$
z=re^{i\sigma},
\qquad
r=|z|,
\qquad
\tau=\log r.
$$

Then

$$
z=e^w,
\qquad
w=\tau+i\sigma.
$$

The flat plane metric becomes

$$
ds^2=dz\,d\bar z
=e^{2\tau}(d\tau^2+d\sigma^2).
$$

Since a CFT is insensitive to local Weyl rescalings on flat space up to anomalies and contact terms, we remove the factor $e^{2\tau}$ and work on the cylinder metric

$$
ds^2_{\mathrm{cyl}}=d\tau^2+d\sigma^2,
\qquad
\sigma\sim\sigma+2\pi.
$$

A primary field transforms from the plane to the cylinder as

$$
\phi_{\mathrm{cyl}}(w,\bar w)
=
\left(\frac{dz}{dw}\right)^h
\left(\frac{d\bar z}{d\bar w}\right)^{\bar h}
\phi_{\mathrm{pl}}(z,\bar z)
=
z^h\bar z^{\bar h}\phi_{\mathrm{pl}}(z,\bar z).
$$

The stress tensor transforms anomalously:

$$
T_{\mathrm{cyl}}(w)
=z^2T_{\mathrm{pl}}(z)-\frac{c}{24},
$$

because $\{e^w,w\}=-1/2$. Similarly,

$$
\bar T_{\mathrm{cyl}}(\bar w)
=\bar z^2\bar T_{\mathrm{pl}}(\bar z)-\frac{\bar c}{24}.
$$

These formulas fix the cylinder Casimir shift.

## Radial ordering

In ordinary Euclidean time quantization, time ordering places later times to the left. In radial quantization, the time variable is $\tau=\log |z|$. Therefore radial ordering means

$$
\mathcal R\{A(z_1)B(z_2)\}
=
\begin{cases}
A(z_1)B(z_2), & |z_1|>|z_2|,\\
B(z_2)A(z_1), & |z_2|>|z_1|.
\end{cases}
$$

On the cylinder this is just time ordering in $\tau$.

The operator product expansion is naturally an expansion valid in a radial domain. For instance, if $|z|>|w|$, the product $A(z)B(w)$ can be interpreted as an operator acting on the Hilbert space on any circle whose radius lies between $|z|$ and $|w|$. Inserting a complete set of states on that circle gives the descendant expansion.

This is why OPE convergence in a Euclidean CFT is not a formal slogan. It is a Hilbert-space statement about evolution between concentric circles.

## States from operators

The vacuum state is prepared by the path integral over the unit disk with no insertions. A local operator inserted at the origin prepares a state:

$$
|\phi\rangle
=
\lim_{z,\bar z\to0}\phi(z,\bar z)|0\rangle.
$$

For a primary field of weights $(h,\bar h)$,

$$
L_0|\phi\rangle=h|\phi\rangle,
\qquad
\bar L_0|\phi\rangle=\bar h|\phi\rangle,
$$

and

$$
L_n|\phi\rangle=0,
\qquad
\bar L_n|\phi\rangle=0,
\qquad
n>0.
$$

Descendant states are obtained by applying lowering modes:

$$
L_{-n_1}\cdots L_{-n_k}
\bar L_{-m_1}\cdots\bar L_{-m_r}|\phi\rangle,
\qquad
n_i,m_j>0.
$$

Their weights are shifted by the total level:

$$
L_0L_{-n}|\phi\rangle=(h+n)L_{-n}|\phi\rangle,
\qquad
\bar L_0\bar L_{-m}|\phi\rangle=(\bar h+m)\bar L_{-m}|\phi\rangle.
$$

This is the two-dimensional form of the conformal multiplet tower.

## Dilatations and rotations

The plane coordinate transformation

$$
z\mapsto \lambda z
$$

is radial time translation on the cylinder:

$$
\tau\mapsto \tau+\log\lambda.
$$

The generator is

$$
D=L_0+\bar L_0.
$$

A rotation

$$
z\mapsto e^{i\alpha}z
$$

is spatial translation on the cylinder:

$$
\sigma\mapsto \sigma+\alpha,
$$

with generator

$$
J=L_0-\bar L_0.
$$

Therefore the scaling dimension and spin of a state are

$$
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h.
$$

For a local bosonic operator on the plane, mutual locality often imposes integrality conditions on spins and monodromies. In fermionic CFTs, spin structures allow half-integer spins. In logarithmic and nonsemisimple CFTs, $L_0$ may not be diagonalizable; then the simple eigenvalue language must be refined.

## Cylinder Hamiltonian and vacuum energy

The stress-tensor transformation under $z=e^w$ gives a shift in cylinder energy. In a theory with left and right central charges $c$ and $\bar c$, the cylinder Hamiltonian and momentum are

$$
H_{\mathrm{cyl}}
=
L_0+\bar L_0-\frac{c+\bar c}{24},
$$

and

$$
P_{\mathrm{cyl}}
=
L_0-\bar L_0-\frac{c-\bar c}{24}.
$$

If $c=\bar c$, then

$$
H_{\mathrm{cyl}}
=L_0+\bar L_0-\frac{c}{12}.
$$

The plane vacuum has

$$
L_0|0\rangle=\bar L_0|0\rangle=0,
$$

so its cylinder energy is

$$
E_0=-\frac{c+\bar c}{24}.
$$

For a parity-invariant unitary CFT with $c=\bar c$, this is $E_0=-c/12$ on a cylinder of circumference $2\pi$. For a general circumference $L$, energies scale as $2\pi/L$.

:::note[Cylinder conventions]
Some books use $z=e^{-iw}$ with Lorentzian cylinder coordinate $w=t+\sigma$, while others use $z=e^{\tau+i\sigma}$ in Euclidean signature. The formulas differ by signs and factors of $i$, but the invariant content is the same: the cylinder vacuum energy is fixed by the Schwarzian derivative.
:::

## Inner products and radial conjugation

The bra corresponding to a ket prepared at the origin is obtained by radial reflection. On the plane, radial conjugation maps

$$
z\mapsto \frac{1}{\bar z},
\qquad
\bar z\mapsto\frac{1}{z}.
$$

For a Hermitian primary field of weights $(h,\bar h)$, a common convention is

$$
\phi(z,\bar z)^\dagger
=
\bar z^{-2h}z^{-2\bar h}
\phi\left(\frac{1}{\bar z},\frac{1}{z}\right),
$$

with appropriate matrix conjugation if the field carries internal indices. This formula ensures that the two-point function becomes the Hilbert-space inner product.

The bra state is

$$
\langle\phi|
=
\lim_{z,\bar z\to\infty}
z^{2h}\bar z^{2\bar h}\langle0|\phi(z,\bar z),
$$

again for a Hermitian scalar-like primary in this convention. The powers are not decorative. They remove the leading falloff of the primary two-point function.

With this adjoint, the Virasoro modes obey in a unitary CFT

$$
L_n^\dagger=L_{-n},
\qquad
\bar L_n^\dagger=\bar L_{-n}.
$$

This relation is the 2D version of reflection positivity. It is what turns Virasoro descendant norm calculations into unitarity constraints.

## Mode expansion of fields

A holomorphic field $W(z)$ of weight $h$ has a radial mode expansion

$$
W(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\frac{W_n}{z^{n+h}},
$$

with

$$
W_n=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint dz\,z^{n+h-1}W(z).
$$

For the stress tensor $h=2$, so

$$
T(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\frac{L_n}{z^{n+2}}.
$$

For a holomorphic current $J(z)$ of weight one,

$$
J(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\frac{J_n}{z^{n+1}}.
$$

Mode expansions are not just Fourier series. They encode how local operator insertions act on states crossing a circle. Commutation relations are obtained by contour manipulations and OPE singularities.

## Lorentzian continuation

The Euclidean cylinder coordinate is

$$
w=\tau+i\sigma.
$$

To pass to Lorentzian time, set

$$
\tau=it.
$$

Then holomorphic and antiholomorphic dependence becomes left- and right-moving dependence on light-cone cylinder coordinates. Schematically,

$$
w=i(t+\sigma),
\qquad
\bar w=i(t-\sigma),
$$

up to convention choices. The Hamiltonian and momentum are still controlled by

$$
H\sim L_0+\bar L_0-\text{Casimir shift},
\qquad
P\sim L_0-\bar L_0-\text{chiral shift}.
$$

In real-time applications, ordering, branch cuts, and $i\epsilon$ prescriptions must be specified. Euclidean radial ordering is the clean starting point, but Lorentzian correlators require analytic continuation.

## Common pitfalls

1. **Forgetting the Weyl factor.** The plane metric becomes $e^{2\tau}$ times the cylinder metric. CFT lets us remove it, but the stress tensor remembers the map through the Schwarzian shift.

2. **Missing the cylinder Casimir energy.** The cylinder Hamiltonian is not simply $L_0+\bar L_0$. It includes $-(c+\bar c)/24$ for circumference $2\pi$.

3. **Confusing radial ordering with ordinary angular ordering.** Radial ordering orders by $|z|$, not by the polar angle $\sigma$.

4. **Assuming every state is created by one simple field.** In a well-behaved local CFT, the state-operator correspondence is powerful, but descendants, null states, twisted-sector states, and defect Hilbert spaces need careful interpretation.

5. **Treating $z$ and $\bar z$ as always independent.** In Euclidean complexified calculations they are often independent variables. For physical real Euclidean configurations, $\bar z$ is the complex conjugate of $z$.

6. **Forgetting spin structures.** Fermions on the cylinder can have Neveu–Schwarz or Ramond boundary conditions. Their mode numbers and vacuum energies differ.

## Research status

The plane-to-cylinder map, Virasoro mode expansion, and state-operator correspondence are standard tools in two-dimensional CFT. They are used in minimal models, WZW models, compact bosons, string worldsheet theory, finite-size scaling, modular bootstrap, entanglement calculations, and boundary CFT.

Active refinements appear in logarithmic CFT, nonunitary theories, fermionic and spin CFTs, defect Hilbert spaces, interfaces, noncompact target-space theories, and rigorous vertex-operator-algebra formulations.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Plane metric to cylinder metric

Use $z=e^{\tau+i\sigma}$ to show that

$$
dz\,d\bar z=e^{2\tau}(d\tau^2+d\sigma^2).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate

$$
z=e^{\tau+i\sigma},
\qquad
\bar z=e^{\tau-i\sigma}.
$$

Then

$$
dz=z(d\tau+i d\sigma),
\qquad
d\bar z=\bar z(d\tau-i d\sigma).
$$

Multiplying gives

$$
dz\,d\bar z=z\bar z\,(d\tau+i d\sigma)(d\tau-i d\sigma)
=e^{2\tau}(d\tau^2+d\sigma^2).
$$

The factor $e^{2\tau}$ is the Weyl factor relating the plane to the cylinder.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Cylinder stress-tensor shift

Given

$$
T_{\mathrm{new}}(w)
=\left(\frac{dz}{dw}\right)^2T_{\mathrm{old}}(z)
+\frac{c}{12}\{z,w\},
$$

show that $z=e^w$ gives

$$
T_{\mathrm{cyl}}(w)=z^2T_{\mathrm{pl}}(z)-\frac{c}{24}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $z=e^w$,

$$
z'=z,
\qquad
z''=z,
\qquad
z'''=z.
$$

Therefore

$$
\{z,w\}
=
\frac{z'''}{z'}-\frac{3}{2}\left(\frac{z''}{z'}\right)^2
=1-\frac{3}{2}
=-\frac{1}{2}.
$$

Also $dz/dw=z$. Hence

$$
T_{\mathrm{cyl}}(w)
=z^2T_{\mathrm{pl}}(z)+\frac{c}{12}\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)
=z^2T_{\mathrm{pl}}(z)-\frac{c}{24}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Dimension and spin from zero modes

A primary state obeys

$$
L_0|h,\bar h\rangle=h|h,\bar h\rangle,
\qquad
\bar L_0|h,\bar h\rangle=\bar h|h,\bar h\rangle.
$$

Show that $D=L_0+\bar L_0$ and $J=L_0-\bar L_0$ have eigenvalues $\Delta=h+\bar h$ and $s=h-\bar h$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Act directly:

$$
D|h,\bar h\rangle
=(L_0+\bar L_0)|h,\bar h\rangle
=(h+\bar h)|h,\bar h\rangle.
$$

Similarly,

$$
J|h,\bar h\rangle
=(L_0-\bar L_0)|h,\bar h\rangle
=(h-\bar h)|h,\bar h\rangle.
$$

Thus the scaling dimension is $\Delta=h+\bar h$ and the spin is $s=h-\bar h$.

</details>
