## Summary

A two-dimensional CFT placed on a torus should not depend on how we choose a basis of cycles for that torus. This geometric statement becomes **modular invariance** of the torus partition function.

The torus is described by a modular parameter

$$
\tau\in\mathbb H,
$$

but the same complex torus is obtained after

$$
\tau\mapsto\frac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d},
\qquad
\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\ c&d\end{pmatrix}\in SL(2,\mathbb Z),
$$

with the overall sign identified. The modular group is generated by

$$
S:\tau\mapsto-\frac1\tau,
\qquad
T:\tau\mapsto\tau+1.
$$

A local CFT torus partition function must satisfy

$$
Z(\tau,\bar\tau)=Z\left(\frac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d},\frac{a\bar\tau+b}{c\bar\tau+d}\right).
$$

In rational CFT, this condition becomes a finite matrix problem involving characters. It strongly constrains which left- and right-moving representations can be paired into a consistent local theory.

$$
\chi_i(\tau)=\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal V_i}q^{L_0-c/24},
\qquad
q=e^{2\pi i\tau},
$$

and with a full Hilbert space of the form

$$
\mathcal H=\bigoplus_{i,j}M_{ij}\mathcal V_i\otimes\overline{\mathcal V}_j.
$$

## Core idea

The torus can be built as

$$
\mathbb C/(\mathbb Z+\tau\mathbb Z).
$$

The two cycles generated by $1$ and $\tau$ are not uniquely chosen. A different integer basis of the same lattice gives the same torus. Physics cannot depend on this bookkeeping choice.

In Hamiltonian language, the torus partition function is a thermal trace on a spatial circle:

$$
Z(\tau,\bar\tau)=\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H}
q^{L_0-c/24}\bar q^{\bar L_0-\bar c/24}.
$$

In Euclidean path-integral language, it is the CFT path integral on the torus. Modular invariance says these two descriptions are consistent under large diffeomorphisms of the torus.

The slogan is

$$
\text{same torus, different cycle basis}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{same partition function}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

The modular group is

$$
PSL(2,\mathbb Z)=SL(2,\mathbb Z)/\{\pm I\}.
$$

It acts on $\tau$ by

$$
\tau\mapsto\frac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d},
\qquad
ad-bc=1.
$$

It is generated by

$$
S:\tau\mapsto-\frac1\tau,
\qquad
T:\tau\mapsto\tau+1,
$$

with relations

$$
S^2=(ST)^3=1
$$

in $PSL(2,\mathbb Z)$.

For a rational CFT, characters transform as

$$
\chi_i(-1/\tau)=\sum_j S_{ij}\chi_j(\tau),
$$

and

$$
\chi_i(\tau+1)=\sum_j T_{ij}\chi_j(\tau).
$$

In an irreducible-character basis, $T$ is diagonal:

$$
T_{ij}=e^{2\pi i(h_i-c/24)}\delta_{ij}.
$$

## Partition functions from characters

A rational-CFT torus partition function can be written as

$$
Z(\tau,\bar\tau)=\sum_{i,j}M_{ij}\chi_i(\tau)\overline{\chi_j(\tau)},
$$

where $M_{ij}$ is a matrix of nonnegative integers. The entry $M_{ij}$ is the multiplicity of

$$
\mathcal V_i\otimes\overline{\mathcal V}_j
$$

in the full Hilbert space.

The vacuum should occur once in a connected local CFT:

$$
M_{00}=1.
$$

Modular invariance becomes the pair of matrix equations

$$
S M S^\dagger=M,
\qquad
T M T^\dagger=M.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
SM=MS,
\qquad
TM=MT,
$$

when $S$ and $T$ are unitary. Thus a classification of modular-invariant torus partition functions becomes a finite algebra problem.

## The diagonal invariant

The simplest modular invariant is

$$
Z_{\rm diag}=\sum_i |\chi_i|^2.
$$

This corresponds to

$$
M_{ij}=\delta_{ij}.
$$

It is modular invariant because character transformations are unitary:

$$
\sum_i S_{ij}S^*_{ik}=\delta_{jk}.
$$

The diagonal invariant is often the first local theory associated with a rational chiral algebra, but it is not the only possible local theory.

For the Ising CFT,

$$
Z_{\rm Ising}=|\chi_0|^2+|\chi_{1/2}|^2+|\chi_{1/16}|^2.
$$

For the tricritical Ising CFT,

$$
Z_{\rm TCI}=\sum_{h\in\{0,1/10,3/5,3/2,3/80,7/16\}}|\chi_h|^2.
$$

These are diagonal Virasoro minimal-model invariants.

## The T constraint

The $T$ constraint is often the quickest first check. Since

$$
\chi_i(\tau+1)=e^{2\pi i(h_i-c/24)}\chi_i(\tau),
$$

a term

$$
\chi_i\overline{\chi_j}
$$

is invariant under $T$ only if

$$
e^{2\pi i(h_i-h_j)}=1.
$$

Thus

$$
h_i-h_j\in\mathbb Z
$$

whenever $M_{ij}\ne0$.

This condition has a simple physical meaning. A local operator built from left representation $i$ and right representation $j$ has spin

$$
J=h_i-h_j.
$$

For an ordinary bosonic local CFT, local operators must have integer spin. The $T$ constraint is the torus version of this locality condition.

Spin CFTs and fermionic theories require a refined discussion with spin structures. This page is about ordinary bosonic modular invariance unless stated otherwise.

## The S constraint

The $S$ transformation exchanges the two cycles of the torus. In thermal language, it relates a high-temperature trace to a low-temperature trace. This is why it controls asymptotic density of states and Cardy growth.

For rational CFT, $S$ invariance says

$$
S M S^\dagger=M.
$$

Unlike the $T$ constraint, this is not merely an integer-spin test. It mixes characters. It is strong enough to rule out many possible spectra that satisfy the $T$ condition.

In diagonal theories, $S$ invariance follows from unitarity of the $S$ matrix. In non-diagonal theories, $M$ must be chosen so that the left-right pairing commutes with $S$.

The same $S$ matrix also determines fusion through the Verlinde formula:

$$
N_{ij}{}^k
=\sum_\ell\frac{S_{i\ell}S_{j\ell}S^*_{k\ell}}{S_{0\ell}}.
$$

This is the remarkable link between modular invariance and OPE-channel algebra.

## Example: Ising model

The Ising $S$ matrix in the basis $(\chi_0,\chi_{1/2},\chi_{1/16})$ is

$$
S=\frac12
\begin{pmatrix}
1&1&\sqrt2\\
1&1&-\sqrt2\\
\sqrt2&-\sqrt2&0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The diagonal partition function

$$
Z=|\chi_0|^2+|\chi_{1/2}|^2+|\chi_{1/16}|^2
$$

is invariant because $S$ is unitary and $T$ is diagonal.

The same $S$ matrix gives quantum dimensions:

$$
d_i=\frac{S_{i0}}{S_{00}},
$$

so

$$
d_{\mathbf 1}=1,
\qquad
d_\epsilon=1,
\qquad
d_\sigma=\sqrt2.
$$

It also reproduces the Ising fusion rules through the Verlinde formula. Modular transformations, fusion, and operator algebra are not separate miracles; they are parts of one rational-CFT consistency structure.

## Non-diagonal invariants

A non-diagonal modular invariant has some $M_{ij}\ne0$ with $i\ne j$. Such invariants are not exotic footnotes; they are essential in rational CFT.

They can arise from:

| Mechanism | Idea |
|---|---|
| Simple-current extensions | Extend the chiral algebra by integer-spin simple currents. |
| Automorphism invariants | Pair left and right representations using a fusion-rule automorphism. |
| Extended-algebra diagonals | A theory non-diagonal in one character basis becomes diagonal after enlarging the chiral algebra. |
| Exceptional invariants | Special modular invariants not generated by the simplest extension patterns. |

For example, the three-state Potts model is related to the $c=4/5$ minimal model but is not just the diagonal Virasoro invariant. Its natural rational structure involves an extended chiral algebra.

The moral is that a Kac table alone does not specify the full local CFT. One must also specify the modular invariant.

## Compact boson example

The compact boson illustrates why modular invariance is also a consistency condition beyond minimal models. For a boson compactified on a circle of radius $R$, the momentum and winding charges are integers:

$$
n,w\in\mathbb Z.
$$

In the convention used in the compact-boson page,

$$
p_L=\frac{n}{R}+\frac{wR}{2},
\qquad
p_R=\frac{n}{R}-\frac{wR}{2}.
$$

The torus partition function contains the lattice sum

$$
Z_R(\tau,
\bar\tau)=\frac{1}{|\eta(\tau)|^2}
\sum_{n,w\in\mathbb Z}
q^{p_L^2/2}\bar q^{p_R^2/2}.
$$

Modular invariance relies on the full momentum-winding lattice, not just momentum modes. The $S$ transformation effectively exchanges momentum and winding data. This is one reason T-duality is visible on the torus:

$$
R\longleftrightarrow \frac{2}{R}.
$$

The compact boson is rational only at special radii with respect to an extended chiral algebra, but modular invariance of the full compact-boson partition function is more general.

## Cardy growth preview

The $S$ transformation relates the high-temperature and low-temperature regimes of the torus. This gives the Cardy formula for the asymptotic density of states in a unitary compact CFT with discrete spectrum.

If the vacuum dominates the low-temperature channel, then at large dimension the density of states grows as

$$
\rho(\Delta)\sim
\exp\left(2\pi\sqrt{\frac{c_{\rm eff}\Delta}{6}}\right),
$$

with the appropriate effective central charge depending on the lowest weight in the spectrum.

For a unitary theory with vacuum lowest weight, $c_{\rm eff}=c$. This is a preview of thermal and modular-bootstrap methods. The central point here is that modular invariance turns the existence of a vacuum into a universal high-energy statement.

## Modular invariance versus crossing

Crossing symmetry is consistency of local operator products on the sphere. Modular invariance is consistency of the torus path integral under large diffeomorphisms. They are different constraints, but in a full CFT they are deeply related.

| Constraint | Geometry | What it tests |
|---|---|---|
| Crossing | Sphere four-point functions | Associativity of the OPE. |
| Modular invariance | Torus partition function | Consistent finite-temperature spectrum and left-right pairing. |
| Verlinde relation | Torus plus punctures | Fusion from modular $S$ data. |

In rational CFT, Moore–Seiberg consistency ties these structures together through fusing and braiding matrices. For a first pass, it is enough to remember that modular invariance is not a replacement for crossing. A theory should satisfy both.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not confuse modular invariance with conformal invariance.** Conformal invariance is local symmetry. Modular invariance is invariance under large diffeomorphisms of the torus.

**Do not forget left-right pairing.** Chiral characters alone do not define a full local CFT. The matrix $M_{ij}$ matters.

**Do not assume the diagonal invariant is always the physical model of interest.** Non-diagonal invariants are common and important.

**Do not apply bosonic modular-invariance rules blindly to fermionic spin CFTs.** Fermionic theories require spin structures and a refined modular covariance statement.

**Do not treat $T$ invariance as the only condition.** Integer spin is necessary for bosonic local operators, but $S$ invariance imposes additional constraints.

**Do not say modular invariance fixes every OPE coefficient.** It constrains the spectrum and fusion data strongly, but local correlators still require OPE coefficients and crossing consistency.

## Research status

Modular invariance of bosonic two-dimensional CFT torus partition functions is established. The rational-CFT formulation using finite character vectors, modular matrices, fusion rules, and modular-invariant partition functions is standard.

Active research develops modular constraints in broader settings: fermionic and spin CFTs, logarithmic CFT, nonsemisimple categories, supersymmetric indices, higher-genus constraints, moonshine, holomorphic CFT classification, modular bootstrap bounds, and connections to topological phases.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Show that the diagonal partition function

$$
Z=\sum_i |\chi_i|^2
$$

is invariant under $S$ if the character $S$ matrix is unitary.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under $S$,

$$
\chi_i\mapsto\sum_j S_{ij}\chi_j.
$$

Then

$$
Z\mapsto
\sum_i\sum_{j,k}S_{ij}S^*_{ik}\chi_j\overline{\chi_k}.
$$

Using unitarity,

$$
\sum_i S_{ij}S^*_{ik}=\delta_{jk},
$$

so

$$
Z\mapsto\sum_j |\chi_j|^2=Z.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2

Explain why $T$ invariance requires $h_i-h_j\in\mathbb Z$ whenever $M_{ij}\ne0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The term $\chi_i\overline{\chi_j}$ transforms under $T$ by the phase

$$
e^{2\pi i(h_i-c/24)}e^{-2\pi i(h_j-c/24)}=e^{2\pi i(h_i-h_j)}.
$$

For the term to be invariant, this phase must be one. Hence

$$
h_i-h_j\in\mathbb Z.
$$

This is the integer-spin condition for bosonic local operators.

</details>

### Exercise 3

For the Ising diagonal invariant, list the three local scalar primaries.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The diagonal invariant pairs each chiral representation with the same antiholomorphic representation:

$$
(0,0),
\qquad
\left(\frac{1}{16},\frac{1}{16}\right),
\qquad
\left(\frac12,\frac12\right).
$$

These correspond to

$$
\mathbf 1,
\qquad
\sigma,
\qquad
\epsilon.
$$

Their dimensions are $0$, $1/8$, and $1$.

</details>

### Exercise 4

Why is a compact-boson partition function with only momentum modes not modular invariant?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The modular $S$ transformation exchanges the two cycles of the torus. In the compact boson, this transformation mixes momentum and winding data. A sum over momentum modes alone is not closed under this exchange.

Including both integers $n$ and $w$ gives a momentum-winding lattice sum. That full lattice sum can be modular invariant, while the momentum-only sum generally cannot.

</details>
