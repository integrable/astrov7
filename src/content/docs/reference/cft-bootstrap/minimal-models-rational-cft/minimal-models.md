## Summary

A Virasoro minimal model is a two-dimensional CFT whose chiral spectrum contains only finitely many irreducible Virasoro highest-weight representations. The standard family is labeled by coprime integers $2\leq p<p'$ and is denoted

$$
\mathcal M(p,p').
$$

Its central charge is

$$
c=1-\frac{6(p-p')^2}{pp'},
$$

and its primary weights are

$$
h_{r,s}=\frac{(p'r-ps)^2-(p'-p)^2}{4pp'},
\qquad
1\leq r\leq p-1,
\qquad
1\leq s\leq p'-1,
$$

with the field identification

$$
(r,s)\sim(p-r,p'-s).
$$

Minimal models are the first exact examples where the slogan “CFT data define the theory” can be made concrete. Their spectrum is finite at the primary level, their fusion rules close, their null states impose differential equations, and their characters assemble into modular-invariant torus partition functions.

:::note[Convention for labels]
Some sources write $\mathcal M(p',p)$ or interchange the roles of $p$ and $p'$. This page uses $2\leq p<p'$ and the formula above. The physics is unchanged if one translates labels consistently.
:::

## Core idea

The Virasoro algebra has many highest-weight representations. A generic Verma module $V_{c,h}$ is huge: it is freely generated by negative modes $L_{-n}$ acting on a highest-weight state $|h\rangle$, up to descendants related by the algebra.

Minimal models arise when representation theory becomes unusually degenerate. For special central charges and weights, Verma modules contain null states. These null states generate submodules that must be quotiented out in a physical irreducible representation. For the minimal values

$$
c=1-\frac{6(p-p')^2}{pp'},
$$

the allowed irreducible representations that close under fusion form a finite list. The Kac table is this finite list before the final field identification.

The result is an interacting CFT that is nevertheless exactly controllable. The model has infinitely many descendant states, but only finitely many primary families.

## Setup and conventions

We work in Euclidean complex coordinates $z,\bar z$. The holomorphic stress tensor has OPE

$$
T(z)T(w)
\sim
\frac{c/2}{(z-w)^4}
+
\frac{2T(w)}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{\partial T(w)}{z-w},
$$

and Virasoro modes obey

$$
[L_m,L_n]
=(m-n)L_{m+n}
+\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

A highest-weight state satisfies

$$
L_0|h\rangle=h|h\rangle,
\qquad
L_n|h\rangle=0\quad n>0.
$$

A full local CFT pairs holomorphic and antiholomorphic representations. This page first describes the chiral Virasoro data. The full local theory also requires a choice of left–right pairing, usually encoded by a modular-invariant torus partition function.

## The Kac data

The minimal-model central charge can be parameterized by coprime positive integers $p,p'$ with $2\leq p<p'$:

$$
c_{p,p'}=1-\frac{6(p-p')^2}{pp'}.
$$

The Kac weights are

$$
h_{r,s}^{(p,p')}
=
\frac{(p'r-ps)^2-(p'-p)^2}{4pp'},
$$

where

$$
1\leq r\leq p-1,
\qquad
1\leq s\leq p'-1.
$$

They obey

$$
h_{r,s}=h_{p-r,p'-s},
$$

so the Kac labels are identified as

$$
(r,s)\sim(p-r,p'-s).
$$

Therefore the number of distinct Virasoro primaries is

$$
N_{\text{primaries}}
=\frac{(p-1)(p'-1)}{2}.
$$

The table is not just bookkeeping. It encodes which null states appear, which fusion channels are allowed, and which differential equations are available for correlators.

## Why only finitely many primaries survive

The Kac determinant tells us where a Verma module develops null states. At minimal-model central charge, the degenerate weights $h_{r,s}$ organize into a doubly periodic pattern. The physical minimal model keeps only a finite rectangle of these weights and identifies opposite entries.

The rough mechanism is this:

$$
\text{Verma module}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{null submodules}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{irreducible quotient}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{finite fusion closure}.
$$

The quotient is essential. If a null descendant $|\chi\rangle$ has zero norm and is orthogonal to all physical states, then a unitary theory should remove it. In nonunitary minimal models, null states are still quotiented in the construction of irreducible modules, even though not every norm is positive.

This is the algebraic reason minimal models are solvable: null-state decoupling gives differential equations for correlators, and fusion closure says the OPE never leaves a finite set of primary families.

## The unitary minimal series

The unitary Virasoro minimal models are

$$
\mathcal M(m,m+1),
\qquad
m=3,4,5,\ldots.
$$

Their central charges are

$$
c_m=1-\frac{6}{m(m+1)}.
$$

The first few are:

| Model | Central charge | Number of Virasoro primaries | Common interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| $\mathcal M(3,4)$ | $1/2$ | $3$ | Ising CFT |
| $\mathcal M(4,5)$ | $7/10$ | $6$ | tricritical Ising CFT |
| $\mathcal M(5,6)$ | $4/5$ | $10$ | three-state Potts related, with modular-invariant caveats |
| $\mathcal M(6,7)$ | $6/7$ | $15$ | next unitary model |

The central charges approach $1$ from below:

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty}c_m=1.
$$

That limit should not be confused with a single compact-boson theory. The $c=1$ line has continuously varying radii and different rational points; the minimal series accumulates at $c=1$ but does not become one fixed theory without further specification.

## The Ising model as the smallest unitary example

For $\mathcal M(3,4)$,

$$
c=1-\frac{6}{3\cdot 4}=\frac12.
$$

The labels before identification are

$$
(r,s)\in\{1,2\}\times\{1,2,3\}.
$$

After $(r,s)\sim(3-r,4-s)$ there are three primaries:

| Label | Weight | Standard name |
|---|---:|---|
| $(1,1)$ | $0$ | identity $\mathbf 1$ |
| $(1,2)$ | $1/16$ | spin field $\sigma$ |
| $(2,1)$ | $1/2$ | energy field $\epsilon$ |

The fusion rules are

$$
\sigma\times\sigma=\mathbf 1+\epsilon,
\qquad
\sigma\times\epsilon=\sigma,
\qquad
\epsilon\times\epsilon=\mathbf 1.
$$

These three lines are a compact example of the bootstrap dream: a tiny amount of data determines a nontrivial critical theory.

## Fusion closure

For the diagonal Virasoro minimal models, the chiral fusion rules can be written in Kac labels as

$$
(r,s)\times(r',s')
=
\sum_{r''}\sum_{s''}(r'',s''),
$$

where $r''$ runs by steps of $2$ through

$$
|r-r'|+1,
\ |r-r'|+3,
\ldots,
\min(r+r'-1,2p-r-r'-1),
$$

and $s''$ runs by steps of $2$ through

$$
|s-s'|+1,
\ |s-s'|+3,
\ldots,
\min(s+s'-1,2p'-s-s'-1).
$$

The formula is easiest to remember as “truncated $SU(2)$-type fusion in each Kac-table direction.” The truncation is what keeps the OPE inside the finite table.

Fusion rules determine which OPE coefficients are allowed to be nonzero. They do not by themselves determine every numerical OPE coefficient. Crossing, normalization, null equations, and locality complete the data.

## Characters and full local theories

The character of a chiral representation is the graded trace

$$
\chi_i(\tau)=\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H_i} q^{L_0-c/24},
\qquad
q=e^{2\pi i\tau}.
$$

Characters package the infinite tower of descendants above each primary. A full diagonal minimal model has a torus partition function of the schematic form

$$
Z(\tau,\bar\tau)=\sum_i \chi_i(\tau)\overline{\chi_i(\tau)},
$$

though other modular invariants can exist. The difference between chiral data and full local CFT data is not pedantry. It is the difference between knowing the representations of one Virasoro algebra and knowing a local two-dimensional theory on closed surfaces.

## Common pitfalls

Do not count both $(r,s)$ and $(p-r,p'-s)$ as distinct primaries. They are the same Virasoro primary in $\mathcal M(p,p')$.

Do not confuse a null descendant with an absent descendant. The null vector exists in the Verma module, but the irreducible physical module quotients by the null submodule.

Do not call every degenerate Virasoro representation a minimal model. Minimal models require a finite, fusion-closed, consistently paired set of representations.

Do not infer that all nonunitary minimal models are useless. The Yang–Lee model is nonunitary and physically important as the CFT of an edge singularity.

Do not identify the $\mathcal M(5,6)$ A-series diagonal theory too casually with the lattice three-state Potts model. The Potts model uses related Virasoro data but a different modular invariant and extended symmetry structure.

## Research status

The Virasoro minimal models themselves are established textbook material. Their spectra, fusion rules, characters, modular invariants, and null-vector equations are classic exact CFT data.

Active directions begin when one changes the surrounding problem: boundary conditions, defects, non-diagonal modular invariants, logarithmic limits, nonunitary models, categorical formulations, generalized symmetries, and numerical-bootstrap diagnostics of exactly solvable points. Minimal models remain useful not because the basic list is mysterious, but because it is a controlled arena where modern questions can be asked without losing the algebraic ground underfoot.

## Exercises

### Count the primaries

Show that the number of distinct Virasoro primaries in $\mathcal M(p,p')$ is $(p-1)(p'-1)/2$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The rectangle of Kac labels has

$$
(p-1)(p'-1)
$$

entries. The identification

$$
(r,s)\sim(p-r,p'-s)
$$

pairs every entry with a distinct opposite entry. There is no fixed point because a fixed point would require $r=p/2$ and $s=p'/2$, which would make both $p$ and $p'$ even; this is impossible for coprime $p,p'$. Therefore the number of distinct primaries is

$$
\frac{(p-1)(p'-1)}{2}.
$$

</details>

### Compute the Ising weights

Use the formula for $h_{r,s}$ in $\mathcal M(3,4)$ to compute the weights of $(1,1)$, $(1,2)$, and $(2,1)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Here $p=3$, $p'=4$, and

$$
h_{r,s}=\frac{(4r-3s)^2-1}{48}.
$$

For $(1,1)$,

$$
h_{1,1}=\frac{(4-3)^2-1}{48}=0.
$$

For $(1,2)$,

$$
h_{1,2}=\frac{(4-6)^2-1}{48}=\frac{3}{48}=\frac{1}{16}.
$$

For $(2,1)$,

$$
h_{2,1}=\frac{(8-3)^2-1}{48}=\frac{24}{48}=\frac12.
$$

These are the identity, spin, and energy weights of the Ising CFT.

</details>

### Check the unitary central charges

Show that the unitary minimal series has $c_m=1-6/(m(m+1))$ and that $c_m\to1$ as $m\to\infty$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The unitary series is $\mathcal M(m,m+1)$. Substituting $p=m$ and $p'=m+1$ gives

$$
c=1-\frac{6(m-(m+1))^2}{m(m+1)}
=1-\frac{6}{m(m+1)}.
$$

Since $m(m+1)\to\infty$, the correction term goes to zero and

$$
\lim_{m\to\infty}c_m=1.
$$

</details>

### Recover the Ising fusion rule

Use the minimal-model fusion formula to show that $\sigma\times\sigma=\mathbf 1+\epsilon$ in the Ising model, where $\sigma=(1,2)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In $\mathcal M(3,4)$, take $(r,s)=(r',s')=(1,2)$. The $r''$ range is

$$
|1-1|+1=1
\quad\text{to}\quad
\min(1+1-1,2\cdot3-1-1-1)=1,
$$

so $r''=1$. The $s''$ range is

$$
|2-2|+1=1
\quad\text{to}\quad
\min(2+2-1,2\cdot4-2-2-1)=3,
$$

with step size $2$, so $s''=1,3$. Thus

$$
(1,2)\times(1,2)=(1,1)+(1,3).
$$

Using the field identification, $(1,3)\sim(2,1)$. Therefore

$$
\sigma\times\sigma=\mathbf 1+\epsilon.
$$

</details>
