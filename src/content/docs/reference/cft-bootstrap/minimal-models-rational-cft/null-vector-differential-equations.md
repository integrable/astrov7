## Summary

A **null-vector differential equation** is a differential equation for a two-dimensional CFT correlator obtained from a null state in a Virasoro representation. If a primary field $\phi_{r,s}$ has a null descendant, then that descendant must decouple from physical correlators. The decoupling condition becomes a differential equation in the insertion points.

The simplest and most important case is a level-two null state. For a primary $\phi_h(z)$ with weight $h$ satisfying the level-two degeneracy condition, the null vector is

$$
\left(L_{-2}-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}L_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle.
$$

Its decoupling gives the BPZ equation

$$
\left[
\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}\partial_z^2
-
\sum_i\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z_i-z)^2}
-
\frac{1}{z_i-z}\partial_{z_i}
\right)
\right]
\left\langle \phi_h(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle=0.
$$

This is one of the great miracles of two-dimensional CFT: representation theory turns correlators into solvable differential equations. Minimal models exploit this mechanism systematically.

T(z)\phi_i(w)\sim

## Core idea

A null state is a descendant state that has zero norm and is orthogonal to the whole module. In a unitary theory, it must be set to zero. In minimal models and many nonunitary theories, the physical irreducible module is obtained by quotienting out the null submodule.

In operator language, if

$$
|\chi\rangle=\mathcal N(L_{-1},L_{-2},\ldots)|h\rangle
$$

is null, then the corresponding descendant field

$$
\chi(z)=\mathcal N(L_{-1},L_{-2},\ldots)\phi_h(z)
$$

decouples from all physical correlators:

$$
\left\langle \chi(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle=0.
$$

Virasoro Ward identities rewrite insertions of $L_{-n}\phi_h(z)$ as differential operators acting on the correlator. The null-state condition therefore becomes a differential equation.

The logic is

$$
\text{null descendant}
\longrightarrow
\text{decoupling equation}
\longrightarrow
\text{BPZ differential equation}
\longrightarrow
\text{finite-dimensional conformal-block problem}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

We use the Virasoro algebra

$$
[L_m,L_n]=(m-n)L_{m+n}+\frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

A primary field $\phi_h(z)$ creates a highest-weight state $|h\rangle$ with

$$
L_0|h\rangle=h|h\rangle,
\qquad
L_n|h\rangle=0\quad n>0.
$$

Descendants are generated by negative modes:

$$
L_{-n_1}L_{-n_2}\cdots L_{-n_k}|h\rangle,
\qquad
n_i>0.
$$

The level is

$$
N=n_1+n_2+\cdots+n_k.
$$

The Kac label $(r,s)$ indicates a degenerate highest-weight representation with a null state at level

$$
N=rs.
$$

For the minimal model $\mathcal M(p,p')$,

$$
h_{r,s}=\frac{(p'r-ps)^2-(p'-p)^2}{4pp'}.
$$

## Ward identity for descendants

Let

$$
G(z;z_i)=\left\langle \phi_h(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle.
$$

Because $L_{-1}$ generates translations, its insertion is simple:

$$
\left\langle (L_{-1}\phi_h)(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle
=\partial_z G(z;z_i).
$$

The $L_{-2}$ insertion is obtained by writing it as a contour integral of the stress tensor around $z$ and deforming the contour around the other insertions. With the conventions above,

$$
\left\langle (L_{-2}\phi_h)(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle
=
\sum_i\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z_i-z)^2}
-
\frac{1}{z_i-z}\partial_{z_i}
\right)G(z;z_i).
$$

More generally, for $n\ge2$,

$$
\left\langle (L_{-n}\phi_h)(z)\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)\right\rangle
=
\sum_i\left(
\frac{(n-1)h_i}{(z_i-z)^n}
-
\frac{1}{(z_i-z)^{n-1}}\partial_{z_i}
\right)G(z;z_i).
$$

The signs depend on the convention for the contour deformation and the ordering of $z_i-z$. This page uses the convention displayed above consistently.

## Level-two null vector

At level two, a general descendant is a linear combination of

$$
L_{-2}|h\rangle,
\qquad
L_{-1}^2|h\rangle.
$$

Look for a null vector of the form

$$
|\chi\rangle=\left(L_{-2}+aL_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle.
$$

Requiring $L_1|\chi\rangle=0$ gives

$$
a=-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}.
$$

Requiring $L_2|\chi\rangle=0$ then gives the relation

$$
c=\frac{2h(5-8h)}{2h+1}.
$$

Thus the level-two null vector can be written as

$$
|\chi\rangle
=\left(L_{-2}-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}L_{-1}^2\right)|h\rangle.
$$

In a minimal model, this occurs for the Kac labels $(2,1)$ and $(1,2)$.

## The BPZ equation

Set the null descendant to zero inside correlators:

$$
\left\langle
\left(L_{-2}-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}L_{-1}^2\right)\phi_h(z)
\prod_i\phi_i(z_i)
\right\rangle=0.
$$

Using the descendant Ward identities gives

$$
\left[
\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}\partial_z^2
-
\sum_i\left(
\frac{h_i}{(z_i-z)^2}
-
\frac{1}{z_i-z}\partial_{z_i}
\right)
\right]G(z;z_i)=0.
$$

This is the level-two BPZ equation. It is a second-order differential equation because the null vector sits at level two.

A level-$rs$ null vector gives an order-$rs$ differential equation, though its explicit form becomes more complicated.

## Four-point reduction

For a four-point function with one level-two degenerate field, conformal symmetry reduces the problem to an ordinary differential equation in the cross-ratio. Put the fields at

$$
\infty,\quad 1,\quad z,\quad 0.
$$

A typical chiral correlator has the form

$$
\left\langle
\phi_4(\infty)\phi_3(1)\phi_h(z)\phi_1(0)
\right\rangle
=\text{known prefactor}\times \mathcal F(z).
$$

The BPZ equation becomes a second-order ordinary differential equation for $\mathcal F(z)$. In level-two cases, it is often a hypergeometric equation. The two independent local solutions are the two conformal blocks allowed by fusion with the degenerate field.

This is the mechanism behind many exact minimal-model correlators. The finite number of conformal blocks is not guessed; it is forced by the null vector.

## Fusion from differential equations

A degenerate field has restricted fusion. In the Coulomb-gas or BPZ parametrization, fusing a level-two degenerate field with a primary shifts its Kac label in only a few allowed ways. Schematically,

$$
\phi_{2,1}\times\phi_{r,s}
\sim
\phi_{r-1,s}+\phi_{r+1,s},
$$

and

$$
\phi_{1,2}\times\phi_{r,s}
\sim
\phi_{r,s-1}+\phi_{r,s+1},
$$

with terms outside the Kac table removed or identified in a minimal model.

This rule can be read from the BPZ equation: a second-order equation has two local solutions near an OPE limit, and the exponents of those solutions correspond to the allowed intermediate weights.

For higher degenerate fields $\phi_{r,s}$, the fusion truncation is larger but still finite. This is one route to the minimal-model fusion rules.

## Example: the Ising spin field

In the Ising model,

$$
c=\frac12,
\qquad
h_\sigma=\frac{1}{16}.
$$

The spin field $\sigma$ is level-two degenerate. The level-two coefficient is

$$
\frac{3}{2(2h_\sigma+1)}
=\frac{3}{2(1/8+1)}
=\frac{3}{9/4}
=\frac43.
$$

Thus the null vector is

$$
\left(L_{-2}-\frac43 L_{-1}^2\right)|\sigma\rangle.
$$

The four-spin correlator therefore satisfies a second-order equation. Its two chiral solutions correspond to the two channels in

$$
\sigma\times\sigma=\mathbf 1+\epsilon.
$$

This is why the exact four-spin function can be written using two Virasoro conformal blocks rather than an arbitrary infinite sum of primaries.

## Example: the Ising energy field

The Ising energy field has

$$
h_\epsilon=\frac12.
$$

It is also level-two degenerate, with

$$
\frac{3}{2(2h_\epsilon+1)}=\frac{3}{2(2)}=\frac34.
$$

The null vector is

$$
\left(L_{-2}-\frac34L_{-1}^2\right)|\epsilon\rangle.
$$

The fusion rule

$$
\epsilon\times\epsilon=\mathbf 1
$$

is even more restrictive than a generic level-two situation. The diagonal local Ising theory also has spin-flip symmetry, which removes correlators with an odd number of spin fields.

## Higher-level equations

A degenerate field $\phi_{r,s}$ has a null vector at level $rs$. The corresponding decoupling equation is an order-$rs$ differential equation in a correlator containing $\phi_{r,s}$.

For example:

| Degenerate field | Null level | Equation order |
|---:|---:|---:|
| $\phi_{1,2}$ | $2$ | second order |
| $\phi_{2,1}$ | $2$ | second order |
| $\phi_{1,3}$ | $3$ | third order |
| $\phi_{3,1}$ | $3$ | third order |
| $\phi_{2,2}$ | $4$ | fourth order |

The explicit null vector at high level is a polynomial in the Virasoro lowering operators:

$$
|\chi_{r,s}\rangle
=\mathcal N_{r,s}(L_{-1},L_{-2},\ldots,L_{-rs})|h_{r,s}\rangle.
$$

Applying the Ward identities translates each mode into a differential operator. This is straightforward in principle but rapidly becomes algebraically heavy.

## What the equations determine

A BPZ equation determines the local conformal blocks associated with a degenerate insertion. It does not automatically determine the full physical correlator. A full correlator must also satisfy:

| Constraint | Role |
|---|---|
| Single-valuedness | The full nonchiral correlator must be local on the plane. |
| Crossing symmetry | Different OPE-channel decompositions must agree. |
| Fusion rules | Only allowed intermediate representations appear. |
| OPE coefficients | Relative weights of blocks are dynamical data. |
| Modular invariance | Torus partition functions must be consistent. |

In minimal models, the combination of BPZ equations, crossing, fusion, and modular invariance is strong enough to solve many correlators exactly.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not confuse a null vector with a zero operator before quotienting.** In a Verma module, the null vector exists as a descendant. In the irreducible physical module, the null submodule is quotiented out, so the corresponding field decouples.

**Do not forget that BPZ equations are chiral.** A full local correlator combines holomorphic and antiholomorphic solutions in a way compatible with locality and crossing.

**Do not think every correlator in a minimal model is a second-order equation.** The order depends on the null level of the degenerate insertion. Level two is just the simplest and most used case.

**Do not confuse the number of differential-equation solutions with the full spectrum.** A correlator with one degenerate insertion has finitely many local channels in that fusion, but the theory’s full spectrum is determined by the whole Kac table and modular invariant.

**Do not drop prefactors too early.** The reduced function $\mathcal F(z)$ satisfies a simpler equation only after the correct conformal prefactor is removed.

**Do not expect the same positivity as in unitary theories.** Nonunitary minimal models still have BPZ equations, but their inner products and OPE coefficients need not obey unitary positivity.

## Research status

The BPZ construction and its use in minimal models are established. It is one of the foundational exact methods of two-dimensional CFT.

Current research uses related null-vector and degenerate-field methods in Liouville theory, logarithmic CFT, supersymmetric CFT, boundary CFT, random geometry, Schramm–Loewner evolution, and analytic bootstrap calculations. In these broader settings, null vectors may coexist with noncompact spectra, nonsemisimple modules, or nontrivial boundary conditions.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Derive the coefficient in the level-two null vector. Start from

$$
|\chi\rangle=(L_{-2}+aL_{-1}^2)|h\rangle
$$

and impose $L_1|\chi\rangle=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Using the Virasoro algebra,

$$
[L_1,L_{-2}]=3L_{-1},
\qquad
[L_1,L_{-1}]=2L_0.
$$

Acting on the highest-weight state gives

$$
L_1L_{-2}|h\rangle=3L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Also,

$$
L_1L_{-1}^2|h\rangle
=2(2h+1)L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Thus

$$
L_1|\chi\rangle
=\left[3+2a(2h+1)\right]L_{-1}|h\rangle.
$$

Setting this to zero gives

$$
a=-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2

Using the same ansatz, impose $L_2|\chi\rangle=0$ and show that

$$
c=\frac{2h(5-8h)}{2h+1}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

We use

$$
[L_2,L_{-2}]=4L_0+\frac{c}{2},
\qquad
[L_2,L_{-1}]=3L_1.
$$

Therefore

$$
L_2L_{-2}|h\rangle=\left(4h+\frac{c}{2}\right)|h\rangle,
$$

and

$$
L_2L_{-1}^2|h\rangle=6h|h\rangle.
$$

With

$$
a=-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)},
$$

the condition $L_2|\chi\rangle=0$ becomes

$$
4h+\frac{c}{2}-\frac{18h}{2(2h+1)}=0.
$$

Solving for $c$ gives

$$
\frac{c}{2}=\frac{h(5-8h)}{2h+1},
$$

so

$$
c=\frac{2h(5-8h)}{2h+1}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3

For the Ising spin field, $h=1/16$. Compute the coefficient multiplying $L_{-1}^2$ in the level-two null vector.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The coefficient is

$$
-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}.
$$

For $h=1/16$,

$$
2h+1=\frac18+1=\frac98.
$$

Therefore

$$
-\frac{3}{2(2h+1)}
=-\frac{3}{2\cdot 9/8}
=-\frac{3}{9/4}
=-\frac43.
$$

The null vector is

$$
\left(L_{-2}-\frac43L_{-1}^2\right)|\sigma\rangle.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4

Explain why a level-two null vector gives a second-order equation for a correlator.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The level-two null vector is a linear combination of $L_{-2}|h\rangle$ and $L_{-1}^2|h\rangle$. Inside a correlator, $L_{-1}$ becomes a derivative with respect to the insertion point, so $L_{-1}^2$ becomes a second derivative.

The $L_{-2}$ insertion is rewritten using the stress-tensor Ward identity as a first-order differential operator acting on the other insertion points, plus rational functions of positions. Equating the null descendant to zero therefore produces a differential equation whose highest derivative is $\partial_z^2$.

Thus the equation is second order.

</details>
