## Summary

A **connected correlator** is the part of a time-ordered Green function that does not factor into lower-point correlators. In source language, connected correlators are generated by $W[J]$ rather than $Z[J]$:

$$
Z[J]=e^{iW[J]},
\qquad
G^{\mathrm c}_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\left.
\frac{1}{i^{n-1}}
\frac{\delta^n W[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots \delta J(x_n)}
\right|_{J=0}.
$$

Scattering theory uses this connected part because the physically quoted invariant amplitude describes one nontrivial reaction, not vacuum bubbles, spectators that pass through unchanged, or two independent experiments happening side by side. For scalar external particles, LSZ reduction applied to the connected stripped Green function gives

$$
 i\mathcal M_{n,\mathrm c}
=
\left[
\prod_{a=1}^n
Z_a^{-1/2}
\lim_{p_a^2\to m_a^2}
\frac{p_a^2-m_a^2}{i}
\right]
\widetilde G^{\mathrm c}_{n,\mathrm{stripped}}(p_1,\ldots,p_n).
$$

This page explains why the connected part is the right object for scattering, how disconnected pieces show up as identity or product terms in the S-matrix, and how cluster decomposition is encoded diagrammatically.

## Core idea

The full time-ordered correlator knows about everything compatible with the field insertions: vacuum fluctuations, disconnected contractions, multiple independent connected components, and the genuinely connected part. LSZ does not magically decide which part is the scattering process. We choose the connected part before extracting the amplitude.

The useful hierarchy is:

| Object | What it contains | Scattering role |
|---|---|---|
| full correlator | all Wick-contraction and diagrammatic components | too large for one reaction |
| normalized correlator | vacuum bubbles removed | still includes disconnected external components |
| connected correlator | all external insertions tied into one component | input for connected LSZ amplitude |
| connected amputated correlator | external propagators removed | off-shell scattering kernel |
| connected amplitude | amputated and on shell | object used in rates and cross sections |

## Setup and conventions

We use qft.org conventions: mostly-minus metric, plane waves $e^{-ip\cdot x}$, and covariantly normalized one-particle states. The S-matrix convention is

$$
\langle f|S|i\rangle
=
\langle f|i\rangle+
\langle f|iT|i\rangle,
$$

with the connected invariant amplitude defined by

$$
\langle f|iT|i\rangle_{\mathrm c}
=
i(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_f-P_i)\mathcal M_{fi}.
$$

For scalar fields, the full time-ordered $n$-point function is

$$
G_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\langle0|T\{\phi(x_1)\cdots\phi(x_n)\}|0\rangle,
$$

and its connected part is denoted $G_n^{\mathrm c}$. Momentum-space connected correlators are written with the total delta function stripped:

$$
\int \prod_{a=1}^n d^4x_a\,
 e^{i\sum_a p_a\cdot x_a}
G_n^{\mathrm c}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}\!\left(\sum_a p_a\right)
\widetilde G^{\mathrm c}_{n,\mathrm{stripped}}(p_1,\ldots,p_n).
$$

All momenta in this formula are incoming. Physical outgoing momenta are represented by incoming momenta with the opposite sign.

## Connected correlators as cumulants

Connected correlators are QFT cumulants. The generating functional $Z[J]$ produces full correlators, while $W[J]$ produces connected correlators:

$$
Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}.
$$

The first few relations show the meaning. If the one-point function is allowed to be nonzero,

$$
G_1(x)=G_1^{\mathrm c}(x),
$$

and

$$
G_2(x,y)
=
G_2^{\mathrm c}(x,y)+G_1^{\mathrm c}(x)G_1^{\mathrm c}(y).
$$

For a vacuum with $\langle\phi\rangle=0$, this reduces to $G_2=G_2^{\mathrm c}$. The first genuinely interesting example is the four-point function. In a theory with $\langle\phi\rangle=0$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_4(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
&=G_4^{\mathrm c}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)\\
&\quad+G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_1,x_2)G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_3,x_4)\\
&\quad+G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_1,x_3)G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_2,x_4)\\
&\quad+G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_1,x_4)G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_2,x_3).
\end{aligned}
$$

In a free scalar theory, Wick's theorem gives only the three pairings on the last three lines, so

$$
G_{4,\mathrm{free}}^{\mathrm c}=0.
$$

That is not an accident. Free particles do not scatter. The disconnected four-point function describes particles propagating independently, not a nontrivial $2\to2$ reaction.

## Vacuum bubbles and normalized correlators

Perturbation theory often first produces expressions of the form

$$
\frac{\langle0|T\{\phi(x_1)\cdots\phi(x_n)S\}|0\rangle}
{\langle0|S|0\rangle}.
$$

The denominator removes vacuum bubbles: diagrams with no connection to any external insertion. This cancellation is the diagrammatic meaning of choosing $Z[0]=1$. Without it, every ordinary correlator would be multiplied by a field-independent vacuum persistence factor.

Vacuum bubbles are disconnected, but not every disconnected piece is a vacuum bubble. A diagram may split into two components, each with external insertions. Such terms survive in the full normalized correlator; they are removed only when we pass to the connected correlator.

This distinction matters because the two operations answer different questions:

| Operation | Removes | Leaves |
|---|---|---|
| normalize by $Z[0]$ | components with no external insertions | full external correlator |
| take connected part | products of lower external correlators | one connected external component |

## Scattering and disconnected S-matrix terms

The S-matrix itself contains disconnected terms. The most basic one is the identity operator: particles can fail to scatter. For distinguishable scalar particles, a schematic $2\to2$ matrix element has the form

$$
\langle p_3,p_4|S|p_1,p_2\rangle
=
\langle p_3,p_4|p_1,p_2\rangle
+i(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(p_1+p_2-p_3-p_4)\mathcal M_{2\to2}.
$$

The first term contains delta functions such as

$$
(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p_1}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p_3-\mathbf p_1)
(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p_2}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p_4-\mathbf p_2),
$$

with additional permutations for identical particles. Those delta functions say that the outgoing particles are the incoming particles relabeled. They are not part of the nontrivial scattering amplitude $\mathcal M_{2\to2}$.

For more particles, disconnected S-matrix terms can also describe two independent reactions occurring in widely separated regions. Such a term carries more delta functions than a single connected process. A connected amplitude has exactly one overall conservation law,

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_f-P_i),
$$

while a product of two independent connected amplitudes has two separate momentum-conservation delta functions, one for each component.

## Cluster decomposition

The physical principle behind connected amplitudes is **cluster decomposition**: distant experiments should decorrelate. If two sets of operator insertions are separated by a large spacelike distance, the full correlator approaches a product of correlators for the two sets, while the connected correlator tends to zero.

Schematically,

$$
G_{m+n}(x_1,\ldots,x_m,y_1+a,\ldots,y_n+a)
\xrightarrow{|a|\to\infty}
G_m(x_1,\ldots,x_m)G_n(y_1,\ldots,y_n),
$$

so the connected part obeys

$$
G^{\mathrm c}_{m+n}(x_1,\ldots,x_m,y_1+a,\ldots,y_n+a)
\xrightarrow{|a|\to\infty}0,
$$

under the usual assumptions of a unique vacuum and a suitable mass gap or sufficiently controlled long-distance behavior. In momentum space, the same principle is reflected in the delta-function structure: connected amplitudes carry only the conservation law for the whole experiment, not separate conservation laws for distant subexperiments.

This is why connectedness is not a cosmetic diagram label. It encodes the locality of independent experiments.

## Diagrammatic criterion

For perturbative diagrams, the rule is simple:

A diagram contributes to $G_n^{\mathrm c}$ if every external insertion belongs to one connected component after vacuum bubbles have been removed.

A few useful consequences follow immediately.

| Diagram type | Contribution |
|---|---|
| vacuum bubble times external graph | cancels from normalized correlators |
| two disconnected external components | contributes to full $G_n$, not $G_n^{\mathrm c}$ |
| one connected component containing all external insertions | contributes to $G_n^{\mathrm c}$ |
| connected component after external-leg amputation | contributes to the connected amputated kernel |

LSZ reduction does not reconnect a disconnected diagram. If the Green function factorizes into two external components, multiplying by inverse external propagators and taking on-shell limits leaves a product of two lower connected amplitudes, not one larger amplitude.

## Example: φ⁴ scattering begins in the connected four-point function

Consider real scalar $\phi^4$ theory,

$$
\mathcal L
=
\frac12\partial_\mu\phi\partial^\mu\phi
-
\frac12m^2\phi^2
-
\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4.
$$

At zeroth order in $\lambda$, the four-point function is just the sum of free pairings:

$$
G_4^{(0)}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
=
D_F(x_1-x_2)D_F(x_3-x_4)+\text{two more pairings}.
$$

This is disconnected, so

$$
G_4^{(0),\mathrm c}=0.
$$

At first order in $\lambda$, the connected contact contribution is

$$
G_4^{(1),\mathrm c}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
=
(-i\lambda)\int d^4z\,
\prod_{a=1}^4D_F(x_a-z).
$$

Fourier transforming gives four external propagators multiplying the local vertex factor. After amputation and LSZ at tree level, the connected amplitude is

$$
i\mathcal M_{2\to2}^{\mathrm{tree}}=-i\lambda.
$$

The free pairings are still present in the full correlator and in the identity part of the S-matrix. They are simply not the connected scattering amplitude.

## Common pitfalls

**Calling every disconnected term unphysical.** Vacuum bubbles cancel from normalized correlators, but disconnected external components can represent identity propagation, spectators, or products of independent reactions. They are not the connected amplitude for one reaction, but they are part of the full S-matrix bookkeeping.

**Applying LSZ to the full correlator without separating pieces.** LSZ applied to the full correlator produces identity terms and products as well as the connected amplitude. The usual invariant amplitude $\mathcal M$ is extracted from the connected, amputated, on-shell part.

**Confusing connected with one-particle irreducible.** A connected diagram may fall apart when one internal line is cut. One-particle irreducible diagrams are a smaller class used to build connected correlators, not the same thing as connected scattering amplitudes.

**Ignoring extra delta functions.** A connected amplitude has one overall momentum-conservation delta function. Disconnected products carry extra delta functions, and those extra deltas are often the fastest diagnostic that a term is not one connected reaction.

**Forgetting long-range subtleties.** In theories with massless particles, confinement, or infrared divergences, naive cluster and S-matrix statements may require inclusive observables, dressed states, or different asymptotic variables.

## Research status

The material on this page is textbook-standard for ordinary relativistic QFTs with a stable vacuum and well-defined asymptotic particle states. The main subtleties are not in the definition of connected correlators, but in the hypotheses behind a particle S-matrix. Confining theories, massless gauge theories, finite-temperature systems, finite-volume spectra, and curved spacetimes require refined observables or modified asymptotic constructions.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Free four-point connected function

For a real free scalar field with $\langle\phi\rangle=0$, use Wick's theorem to show that $G_4^{\mathrm c}=0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Wick's theorem gives

$$
G_4(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
=
D_{12}D_{34}+D_{13}D_{24}+D_{14}D_{23},
$$

where $D_{ab}=D_F(x_a-x_b)$. Since $G_2^{\mathrm c}=D_F$ and $G_1=0$, the cumulant decomposition is

$$
G_4
=
G_4^{\mathrm c}+G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_1,x_2)G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_3,x_4)+\text{two more pairings}.
$$

Comparing the two expressions gives

$$
G_4^{\mathrm c}=0.
$$

This is the correlator version of the statement that free particles do not scatter.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Identity term in two-particle scattering

For distinguishable scalar particles with covariant normalization, write the identity contribution to $\langle p_3,p_4|S|p_1,p_2\rangle$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The identity part is the two-particle inner product. For distinguishable particles with $p_3$ matched to $p_1$ and $p_4$ matched to $p_2$,

$$
\langle p_3,p_4|p_1,p_2\rangle
=
(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p_1}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p_3-\mathbf p_1)
(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p_2}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p_4-\mathbf p_2).
$$

For identical particles one must also include the exchanged pairing. These terms are disconnected identity terms, not the nontrivial connected amplitude.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Delta functions for independent reactions

Suppose a disconnected S-matrix term is a product of two independent connected reactions, $A$ and $B$. Show that it carries two conservation delta functions rather than one.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Each connected reaction has its own total incoming and outgoing momentum. Thus the product contains

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_{A,f}-P_{A,i})
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_{B,f}-P_{B,i}).
$$

Multiplying the two delta functions implies total conservation for the combined process, but it is stronger: it enforces momentum conservation separately in each disconnected component. A single connected amplitude has only

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_f-P_i).
$$

The extra delta function is the momentum-space fingerprint of disconnectedness.

</details>

### Exercise 4: φ⁴ contact amplitude

Starting from

$$
G_4^{(1),\mathrm c}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
=
(-i\lambda)\int d^4z\,
\prod_{a=1}^4D_F(x_a-z),
$$

show that tree-level LSZ reduction gives $i\mathcal M=-i\lambda$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Fourier transformation gives the stripped connected Green function

$$
\widetilde G_{4,\mathrm{stripped}}^{(1),\mathrm c}
=
(-i\lambda)\prod_{a=1}^4\frac{i}{p_a^2-m^2+i\epsilon}.
$$

At tree level $Z=1$. LSZ multiplies by

$$
\prod_{a=1}^4\frac{p_a^2-m^2}{i}
$$

and then takes $p_a^2\to m^2$. The four external propagators cancel, leaving

$$
i\mathcal M=-i\lambda.
$$

</details>
