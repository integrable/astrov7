## Summary

An **anomalous dimension** is the part of an operator’s scaling behavior that is generated by quantum renormalization rather than by engineering dimensional analysis. If a local operator $\mathcal O$ has classical mass dimension $\Delta_{\rm cl}$, then near a fixed point its scaling dimension has the schematic form

$$
\Delta
=
\Delta_{\rm cl}
+
\gamma.
$$

Away from a fixed point, $\gamma$ is not usually a single number. It is a function of running couplings and, for operators with the same quantum numbers, a matrix.

The operator-renormalization relation is

$$
\mathcal O_i^0
=
Z_{ij}(\mu)\,[\mathcal O_j]_R(\mu).
$$

With this convention, define the anomalous-dimension matrix by

$$
\gamma
=
-\,Z^{-1}\mu\frac{dZ}{d\mu}.
$$

Then the renormalized operator basis obeys

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_i]_R
=
\gamma_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R.
$$

This sign convention is common in EFT and operator-renormalization discussions, but not universal. The relation between $Z$ and $\gamma$ is the thing to check when comparing sources.

This is a preview because the full conceptual home of anomalous dimensions is renormalization, RG, and EFT. The perturbative-scattering reason to introduce them here is concrete: form factors and current matrix elements of composite operators contain logarithms, and anomalous dimensions tell those logarithms how to organize.

## Core idea

Renormalization replaces divergent bare quantities with finite renormalized quantities plus a scale $\mu$. The scale is not a new physical knob by itself. It is a bookkeeping device introduced by the renormalization scheme. Physical quantities cannot depend on arbitrary choices of $\mu$.

The price of introducing $\mu$ is that renormalized fields, couplings, and composite operators depend on it. Couplings run according to beta functions. Operators run according to anomalous dimensions.

The logic is:

```txt
UV singularity near an insertion
  → local operator counterterm
  → renormalization matrix Z
  → anomalous-dimension matrix γ
  → RG evolution of matrix elements and Wilson coefficients.
```

This is why anomalous dimensions appear everywhere once local operators enter the story. They govern scale dependence of form factors, current matrix elements, moments of parton distributions, weak effective Hamiltonians, conformal operators, and short-distance expansion coefficients.

A good slogan is:

```txt
An anomalous dimension is a logarithmic UV divergence turned into a differential equation.
```

## Setup and conventions

Let $\mathcal O_i^0$ denote bare local operators and $[\mathcal O_i]_R$ finite renormalized operators. We use

$$
\mathcal O_i^0
=
Z_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R.
$$

The matrix $Z_{ij}$ can mix operators with the same conserved quantum numbers and compatible dimensions. In a scheme such as minimal subtraction, $Z_{ij}$ contains poles in $\epsilon=(4-d)/2$ and depends on the renormalized couplings and the scale $\mu$.

The bare operators do not depend on $\mu$:

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}\mathcal O_i^0=0.
$$

Differentiating the renormalization relation gives

$$
0
=
\left(\mu\frac{dZ_{ij}}{d\mu}\right)[\mathcal O_j]_R
+
Z_{ij}\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_j]_R.
$$

Multiplying by $Z^{-1}$ gives

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_i]_R
=
\gamma_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R,
\qquad
\gamma=-Z^{-1}\mu\frac{dZ}{d\mu}.
$$

That is the convention used on this page. If a source defines $\mathcal O_R=Z\mathcal O_0$ instead, or defines $\gamma$ with the opposite sign, the displayed RG equation changes accordingly.

## Engineering dimension versus anomalous dimension

The engineering dimension is fixed by dimensional analysis. For example, in four spacetime dimensions,

$$
[\phi]=1,
\qquad
[\overline\psi\psi]=3,
\qquad
[F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}]=4.
$$

These dimensions count powers of mass in the Lagrangian and correlation functions before quantum scaling corrections are included.

An anomalous dimension is different. It is not a correction because someone forgot dimensional analysis. It is the statement that the renormalized operator has scale dependence induced by short-distance quantum fluctuations.

At a fixed point, where all beta functions vanish, the distinction becomes especially clean. A scaling operator $\mathcal O_a$ satisfies

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_a]_R
=
\gamma_a[\mathcal O_a]_R,
$$

and its full scaling dimension is

$$
\Delta_a
=
\Delta_{a,{\rm cl}}
+
\gamma_a.
$$

Away from a fixed point, the quantity $\gamma_a(g(\mu))$ is better thought of as an RG function along a flow rather than a permanent number attached to the operator.

## Operator mixing

Most interesting operator bases are not one-dimensional. Operators can mix if symmetries allow it. A typical basis might contain

$$
\mathcal O_1,\mathcal O_2,\ldots,\mathcal O_N,
$$

with the same Lorentz representation, internal charges, gauge quantum numbers, and compatible dimension. Renormalization then gives a matrix

$$
\mathcal O_i^0
=
Z_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R,
$$

and the anomalous dimension is a matrix

$$
\gamma_{ij}
=
-\left(Z^{-1}\mu\frac{dZ}{d\mu}\right)_{ij}.
$$

The physical message is that scale evolution can rotate one operator into another. If an EFT Hamiltonian contains

$$
\mathcal H_{\rm eff}
=
\sum_i C_i(\mu)[\mathcal O_i]_R(\mu),
$$

then the Wilson coefficients must run oppositely so that $\mathcal H_{\rm eff}$ is $\mu$ independent. With the convention above,

$$
\mu\frac{dC_i}{d\mu}
=
- C_j\gamma_{ji},
$$

for row-vector coefficient notation. Column-vector notation transposes this equation. This is the source of many sign and transpose annoyances in EFT papers. The physics is simply that the product $C_i[\mathcal O_i]_R$ is independent of the arbitrary renormalization scale.

## One-loop extraction in minimal subtraction

In minimal subtraction, anomalous dimensions can be read from pole terms. For a single multiplicatively renormalized operator, suppose

$$
\mathcal O^0=Z_{\mathcal O}\mathcal O_R,
\qquad
Z_{\mathcal O}
=
1+\frac{a\,g^2}{\epsilon}+O(g^4),
$$

where $g$ is a dimensionless renormalized coupling in $d=4-2\epsilon$. In dimensional regularization, the $d$-dimensional beta function starts as

$$
\mu\frac{dg}{d\mu}
=
-\epsilon g+O(g^3).
$$

Using

$$
\gamma_{\mathcal O}
=
-Z_{\mathcal O}^{-1}\mu\frac{dZ_{\mathcal O}}{d\mu}
=
-Z_{\mathcal O}^{-1}\beta(g)\frac{\partial Z_{\mathcal O}}{\partial g},
$$

we find at this order

$$
\gamma_{\mathcal O}
=
2a\,g^2+O(g^4).
$$

The exact coefficient depends on the normalization of $g$, the sign convention for $Z_{\mathcal O}$, and the definition of $\epsilon$. The structural point is robust: the coefficient of the UV pole determines the anomalous dimension.

For a matrix $Z_{ij}$, the same idea applies with matrix multiplication and, beyond leading order, with care about noncommuting matrices and scheme conventions.

## Form factors and logarithms

Consider a renormalized form factor of a local operator,

$$
F_i(Q,\mu,g(\mu))
=
\langle f|[\mathcal O_i]_R(\mu)|i\rangle,
$$

where $Q$ is a physical momentum scale. The matrix element can contain logarithms such as

$$
\log\frac{Q^2}{\mu^2}.
$$

The arbitrary $\mu$ dependence of those logarithms is canceled by the explicit operator running and by the running of couplings. Schematically,

$$
\left[
\mu\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu}
+\beta(g)\frac{\partial}{\partial g}
-\gamma
\right]F
=
0,
$$

where the sign and index placement depend on whether $F$ is treated as a vector in operator space or as a matrix element of a single operator.

This equation is more useful than it may first look. If the perturbative calculation produces large logarithms, choosing $\mu\sim Q$ reduces them. If the form factor involves several separated scales, RG evolution can resum towers of logarithms. This is the same logic behind RG-improved amplitudes, factorization formulas, and EFT running.

## Fields also have anomalous dimensions

Elementary fields can be renormalized too:

$$
\phi_0=Z_\phi^{1/2}\phi_R.
$$

The field anomalous dimension is often defined by

$$
\gamma_\phi
=
\frac12\mu\frac{d\log Z_\phi}{d\mu},
$$

or with the opposite sign depending on whether the bare-to-renormalized relation is written as $\phi_0=Z_\phi^{1/2}\phi_R$ or $\phi_R=Z_\phi^{-1/2}\phi_0$. This is one reason field anomalous dimensions and operator anomalous dimensions can look sign-shifted across references.

For scattering amplitudes, external field renormalization is already accounted for by LSZ residues or by renormalized external states. Composite-operator anomalous dimensions are additional: they belong to the local insertion itself.

Do not confuse these two statements:

```txt
field renormalization:
  changes the normalization of elementary-field correlation functions;

operator renormalization:
  changes the normalization and mixing of local composite insertions.
```

Both are real. They answer different questions.

## Conserved currents and protected operators

Not every operator acquires an anomalous dimension. Conserved currents are often protected. For an exactly conserved vector current associated with an unbroken global symmetry, the charge normalization is fixed. In many schemes, the current needs no multiplicative renormalization:

$$
\gamma_J=0.
$$

Similarly, the total stress tensor is protected by translation symmetry:

$$
\gamma_T=0
$$

for the total conserved $T^{\mu\nu}$, although pieces of the stress tensor or improved/composite representatives may have scheme-dependent renormalization.

Protected does not mean “has no loop corrections in matrix elements.” A current form factor can receive loop corrections as a function of $q^2$. Protection means the operator’s normalization and anomalous dimension are constrained by a Ward identity. A form factor can be nontrivial while the current itself has zero anomalous dimension.

This distinction is crucial in perturbative calculations:

| Object | Can have loop corrections? | Can have anomalous dimension? |
|---|---:|---:|
| conserved total charge current | yes | often no |
| total stress tensor | yes | no, as the translation generator |
| scalar density $\overline\psi\psi$ | yes | yes |
| EFT four-fermion operator | yes | yes, often with mixing |
| Wilson coefficient multiplying an operator | yes | runs oppositely to the operator |

## Fixed points and scaling operators

At a conformal or scale-invariant fixed point, anomalous dimensions become part of the operator spectrum. The RG equation can be diagonalized into scaling operators,

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_a]_R
=
\gamma_a[\mathcal O_a]_R.
$$

Then a two-point function of scalar scaling operators has the power-law form

$$
\langle \mathcal O_a(x)\mathcal O_a(0)\rangle
\propto
\frac{1}{(x^2)^{\Delta_a}},
\qquad
\Delta_a=\Delta_{a,{\rm cl}}+\gamma_a.
$$

This is why anomalous dimensions are central in critical phenomena and CFT. In perturbative QFT, one often computes them near a weakly coupled fixed point, such as the Wilson–Fisher fixed point in $4-\epsilon$ dimensions or a Banks–Zaks fixed point in gauge theory.

The same matrix $\gamma$ that appears as a renormalization bookkeeping object in perturbation theory becomes spectral data at a fixed point.

## Common pitfalls

**Calling every logarithm an anomalous dimension.** A logarithm in a calculation may come from UV renormalization, IR physics, threshold behavior, or kinematics. An anomalous dimension is tied to renormalization-scale dependence of a field or operator.

**Ignoring operator mixing.** If several operators share the same quantum numbers, a single anomalous dimension is usually the wrong object. The correct object is a matrix.

**Mixing sign conventions.** Some authors define $\mathcal O_R=Z\mathcal O_0$; others define $\mathcal O_0=Z\mathcal O_R$. Some put a minus sign into $\gamma$. Always check the RG equation, not just the symbol.

**Thinking protected currents have trivial form factors.** Ward identities can protect current normalization and anomalous dimension while leaving nontrivial momentum-dependent form factors.

**Forgetting Wilson coefficients.** In EFT, operator anomalous dimensions are only half the story. Wilson coefficients run oppositely so that physical amplitudes are independent of $\mu$.

**Confusing engineering dimension with scaling dimension.** Engineering dimension is dimensional bookkeeping. Scaling dimension at a fixed point includes anomalous contributions.

**Overinterpreting anomalous dimensions away from fixed points.** Away from a fixed point, anomalous dimensions are RG functions along a flow. They are not usually permanent, scheme-independent numbers.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Derive the operator RG equation

Starting from

$$
\mathcal O_i^0=Z_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R,
$$

and assuming $\mu\,d\mathcal O_i^0/d\mu=0$, derive

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_i]_R
=
\gamma_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R,
\qquad
\gamma=-Z^{-1}\mu\frac{dZ}{d\mu}.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate the bare-renormalized relation:

$$
0
=
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}\left(Z_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R\right)
=
\left(\mu\frac{dZ_{ij}}{d\mu}\right)[\mathcal O_j]_R
+
Z_{ij}\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_j]_R.
$$

Multiplying by $Z^{-1}$ gives

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_i]_R
=
-\left(Z^{-1}\mu\frac{dZ}{d\mu}\right)_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R.
$$

Therefore

$$
\gamma=-Z^{-1}\mu\frac{dZ}{d\mu}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Multiplicative running

Suppose a single operator obeys

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}\mathcal O_R(\mu)
=
\gamma\,\mathcal O_R(\mu),
$$

with constant $\gamma$. Solve for $\mathcal O_R(\mu)$ in terms of $\mathcal O_R(\mu_0)$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Write $d/d\log\mu=\mu\,d/d\mu$. The equation is

$$
\frac{d}{d\log\mu}\mathcal O_R(\mu)
=
\gamma\,\mathcal O_R(\mu).
$$

Integrating from $\mu_0$ to $\mu$ gives

$$
\mathcal O_R(\mu)
=
\left(\frac{\mu}{\mu_0}\right)^\gamma
\mathcal O_R(\mu_0).
$$

If $\gamma$ depends on the coupling, and the coupling runs, this simple power is replaced by an RG-evolution exponential along the flow.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Wilson coefficients run oppositely

Let

$$
\mathcal H_{\rm eff}=C_i(\mu)[\mathcal O_i]_R(\mu).
$$

Using

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}[\mathcal O_i]_R
=
\gamma_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R,
$$

derive the coefficient RG equation required by $\mu\,d\mathcal H_{\rm eff}/d\mu=0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate:

$$
0
=
\mu\frac{d\mathcal H_{\rm eff}}{d\mu}
=
\left(\mu\frac{dC_i}{d\mu}\right)[\mathcal O_i]_R
+
C_i\gamma_{ij}[\mathcal O_j]_R.
$$

Relabel the dummy index in the first term so both terms multiply $[\mathcal O_j]_R$:

$$
0
=
\left(\mu\frac{dC_j}{d\mu}
+
C_i\gamma_{ij}\right)[\mathcal O_j]_R.
$$

Therefore

$$
\mu\frac{dC_j}{d\mu}
=
- C_i\gamma_{ij}.
$$

Equivalently, if $C$ is written as a row vector,

$$
\mu\frac{dC}{d\mu}=-C\gamma.
$$

Column-vector conventions transpose this equation.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Conserved current protection

Explain why an exactly conserved current associated with a fixed charge normalization cannot have an arbitrary multiplicative anomalous dimension.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The charge is the spatial integral of the time component of the current:

$$
Q=\int d^3\mathbf x\,J^0(t,\mathbf x).
$$

If the current were multiplicatively rescaled by arbitrary RG running, the charge normalization would also run unless another compensating convention were introduced. But the charge generates an exact symmetry and has fixed eigenvalues on states. Ward identities enforce this normalization.

Therefore the conserved current is protected in schemes that preserve the symmetry. Its matrix elements can have nontrivial momentum dependence, but the operator normalization and anomalous dimension are constrained by the Ward identity.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Pole coefficient and anomalous dimension

Let

$$
Z_{\mathcal O}=1+\frac{a g^2}{\epsilon}+O(g^4),
\qquad
\mu\frac{dg}{d\mu}=-\epsilon g+O(g^3).
$$

Using the convention $\gamma_{\mathcal O}=-Z_{\mathcal O}^{-1}\mu\,dZ_{\mathcal O}/d\mu$, show that $\gamma_{\mathcal O}=2ag^2+O(g^4)$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

At leading order,

$$
\mu\frac{dZ_{\mathcal O}}{d\mu}
=
\left(\mu\frac{dg}{d\mu}\right)
\frac{\partial}{\partial g}
\left(1+\frac{a g^2}{\epsilon}\right)
=
(-\epsilon g)\frac{2ag}{\epsilon}
=
-2ag^2.
$$

Since $Z_{\mathcal O}^{-1}=1+O(g^2)$,

$$
\gamma_{\mathcal O}
=
-Z_{\mathcal O}^{-1}\mu\frac{dZ_{\mathcal O}}{d\mu}
=
2ag^2+O(g^4).
$$

Higher orders require the higher beta function and higher pole structure.

</details>
