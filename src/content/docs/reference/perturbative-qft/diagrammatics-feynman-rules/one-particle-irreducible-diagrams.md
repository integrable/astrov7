## Summary

A connected diagram is **one-particle irreducible**, or **1PI**, if it remains connected after cutting any one internal line. If cutting some internal line separates the graph into two pieces, the graph is **one-particle reducible**, or **1PR**.

The distinction matters because 1PI diagrams are the irreducible building blocks of perturbation theory. Connected diagrams can be assembled by joining 1PI pieces with propagators, while the 1PI pieces themselves are generated by the effective action

$$
\Gamma[\varphi]=W[J]-\int d^4x\,J(x)\varphi(x),
\qquad
\frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)}=-J(x).
$$

The functional derivatives of $\Gamma$ are called **proper vertices** or **1PI vertex functions**. The two-point proper vertex is the inverse-propagator kernel, up to the conventional Lorentzian factors of $i$; its loop corrections are self-energy insertions. Higher proper vertices are the amputated interaction kernels used to build connected amplitudes.

This page explains the graph-theoretic definition, the effective-action definition, and the practical use of 1PI diagrams in scalar perturbation theory. The goal is not to replace the later effective-action and renormalization pages, but to make the diagrammatic object precise enough that self-energies, vertex corrections, and counterterm diagrams have a clean home.

## Core idea

A connected diagram can still contain a fragile internal bridge. If one internal propagator is the only path between two subgraphs, then the diagram is reducible: it is really two smaller interaction kernels connected by a propagator.

A 1PI diagram has no such internal bridge. It is an irreducible kernel. That is why 1PI diagrams are also called **proper** diagrams: they are the pieces that cannot be explained as two lower-order processes joined by propagation of an intermediate field.

| Object | Diagrammatic content | Generated by |
|---|---|---|
| full correlators | connected and disconnected diagrams | $Z[J]$ |
| connected correlators | connected diagrams | $W[J]$ |
| 1PI vertices | connected diagrams that cannot be split by cutting one internal line, with external propagators amputated | $\Gamma[\varphi]$ |
| connected amplitudes | trees made from full propagators and 1PI vertices | $\Gamma$ plus propagators |

The slogan is:

$$
\text{connected diagrams}
=\text{trees built from 1PI vertices and propagators}.
$$

This is one of the most useful reorganizations in perturbative QFT. It turns a huge set of connected diagrams into a smaller set of irreducible kernels plus a combinatorial rule for joining them.

## Setup and conventions

We work in the qft.org mostly-minus convention. For a real scalar theory, the running example is

$$
\mathcal L
=\frac12\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi
-\frac12m^2\phi^2
-\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4.
$$

The free scalar propagator is

$$
D_0(p)=\frac{i}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon}.
$$

We use normalized source functionals, so $Z[0]=1$, and define

$$
Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}.
$$

The source-dependent classical field is

$$
\varphi(x)=\frac{\delta W[J]}{\delta J(x)}.
$$

The effective action is the Legendre transform

$$
\Gamma[\varphi]
=W[J]-\int d^4x\,J(x)\varphi(x),
\qquad
\frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)}=-J(x).
$$

Its functional Taylor expansion defines the 1PI vertex functions:

$$
\Gamma[\varphi]
=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n!}
\int d^4x_1\cdots d^4x_n\,
\Gamma^{(n)}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
\varphi(x_1)\cdots\varphi(x_n).
$$

Different authors place factors of $i$ differently in the definition of momentum-space proper vertices. This page keeps the conceptual statement invariant: derivatives of $\Gamma$ generate amputated 1PI kernels. When a sign or factor of $i$ matters, we state the convention explicitly.

## Graph-theoretic definition

A diagram is **1PI** if it satisfies two conditions:

1. it is connected;
2. cutting any one internal line leaves it connected.

A diagram is **1PR** if it is connected but fails the second condition.

The word "particle" in one-particle irreducible is historical. The internal line being cut is a propagator line, not necessarily an observed particle. In a scalar diagram, the test is easy to visualize. In a gauge theory, spinor theory, or theory with several fields, the same graph-theoretic test applies, but the line also carries indices, field species, and possibly gauge-dependent structure.

For external legs, use the following rule: do not test irreducibility by cutting external lines. External legs are labels for insertions or asymptotic states. The 1PI test concerns internal propagation that mediates between two subgraphs.

### Examples

In scalar $\phi^3$ theory, the tree-level four-point exchange diagram is connected but 1PR. Its single internal line is a bridge between two cubic vertices. Cutting that line leaves two separate three-point pieces.

In scalar $\phi^4$ theory, the one-loop four-point bubble has two quartic vertices connected by two internal lines. Cutting either internal line leaves the other line connecting the two vertices, so the graph remains connected. The bubble is 1PI.

The one-loop tadpole correction to the scalar two-point function is also 1PI. Cutting the tadpole loop line does not separate the diagram into two components; it leaves a single vertex with the external attachments still connected through that vertex. This example is a nice antidote to the bad rule "all loops are obviously 1PI." Loops often are 1PI, but the real test is whether any single internal line is a bridge.

## Effective action and proper vertices

The effective action packages all 1PI diagrams. More precisely, $\Gamma[\varphi]$ is the generating functional for 1PI vertex functions.

At vanishing background field, the first few derivatives are:

$$
\Gamma^{(1)}(x)
=\left.\frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)}\right|_{\varphi=0},
$$

$$
\Gamma^{(2)}(x,y)
=\left.\frac{\delta^2\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)\delta\varphi(y)}\right|_{\varphi=0},
$$

and

$$
\Gamma^{(n)}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=\left.\frac{\delta^n\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x_1)\cdots\delta\varphi(x_n)}\right|_{\varphi=0}.
$$

If the vacuum is chosen so that $\langle\phi\rangle=0$, then $\Gamma^{(1)}=0$. The two-point kernel $\Gamma^{(2)}$ is the proper inverse-propagator kernel. The higher $\Gamma^{(n)}$ are proper interaction vertices.

The Legendre transform explains why $\Gamma$ produces 1PI diagrams rather than merely connected diagrams. Since $W[J]$ generates connected diagrams, differentiating $W$ with respect to $J$ keeps external source legs. Passing to $\Gamma[\varphi]$ trades the source $J$ for the classical field $\varphi$ and removes diagrams that can be separated by cutting a single connected propagator. The remaining kernels are precisely the 1PI pieces.

A useful exact chain-rule identity is

$$
\int d^4z\,
\frac{\delta^2\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)\delta\varphi(z)}
\frac{\delta^2W}{\delta J(z)\delta J(y)}
=-\delta^{(4)}(x-y).
$$

This is the functional statement that the second derivative of the effective action is the inverse connected two-point kernel, up to the Lorentzian sign convention coming from $\Gamma=W-\int J\varphi$.

:::tip[Fast dictionary]
For most perturbative calculations, "proper vertex" means "amputated 1PI vertex." A raw correlator diagram may have external propagator tails; the proper vertex generated by $\Gamma$ does not.
:::

## Self-energy and propagator resummation

The two-point 1PI diagrams are called **self-energy insertions**. They correct the propagation of a field.

Let the sum of amputated 1PI two-point insertions be written as

$$
-i\Sigma(p^2).
$$

With the free scalar propagator

$$
D_0(p)=\frac{i}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon},
$$

the connected two-point function is the geometric series

$$
\begin{aligned}
D(p)
&=D_0(p)+D_0(p)\big[-i\Sigma(p^2)\big]D_0(p)\\
&\quad+D_0(p)\big[-i\Sigma(p^2)\big]D_0(p)
\big[-i\Sigma(p^2)\big]D_0(p)+\cdots.
\end{aligned}
$$

Summing the series gives

$$
D(p)=\frac{i}{p^2-m^2-\Sigma(p^2)+i\epsilon}.
$$

This formula is a convention check as much as a physics formula. If an author defines the 1PI insertion as $i\Pi(p^2)$ instead of $-i\Sigma(p^2)$, the denominator is written with $+\Pi$ instead. The invariant statement is that the full inverse propagator equals the free inverse propagator minus the proper self-energy, once the sign convention for the insertion has been fixed.

The pole of the full propagator defines the physical mass in an on-shell scheme:

$$
p^2=m_{\mathrm{phys}}^2
\quad\text{where}\quad
p^2-m^2-\Sigma(p^2)=0.
$$

Near the pole, the residue defines the field-strength factor. Those issues belong to renormalized perturbation theory, but the diagrammatic origin is already visible here: only 1PI self-energy insertions appear in the denominator.

## Higher proper vertices

For $n\ge3$, $\Gamma^{(n)}$ is an amputated 1PI $n$-point interaction kernel. In scalar $\phi^4$ theory, the tree-level four-point proper vertex is the quartic vertex. In momentum-space Feynman-rule language, its factor is

$$
-i\lambda.
$$

At one loop, the four-point proper vertex receives bubble corrections in the $s$-, $t$-, and $u$-channels. Each bubble is 1PI. The full connected four-point function then attaches external propagators to this proper vertex and also includes reducible diagrams if the theory has lower-point proper vertices.

This distinction is especially clean in a scalar theory with cubic interactions. The tree-level four-point exchange diagram is connected, but it is not a four-point proper vertex. It is made from two three-point proper vertices connected by a propagator. Schematically,

$$
G_{4,\mathrm{c}}
=\text{external propagators}
\times
\left(
\Gamma^{(4)}
+
\Gamma^{(3)}D\Gamma^{(3)}
+\cdots
\right).
$$

The ellipsis represents other channels and higher-order insertions. The important point is structural: $\Gamma^{(4)}$ is not the whole connected four-point function. It is the irreducible kernel from which connected four-point functions are assembled.

## Connected diagrams as trees of 1PI blocks

A connected diagram is either 1PI or it contains at least one internal bridge. Cutting a bridge separates the graph into two smaller connected pieces. Repeating this cutting process decomposes the diagram into 1PI components connected by internal propagator lines.

This decomposition is the graph-theoretic content behind the effective action. Instead of summing all connected diagrams directly, one may:

1. compute the 1PI proper vertices;
2. use the full propagator as the line connecting them;
3. assemble connected Green functions as trees of these proper vertices.

This is why the effective action is sometimes called the quantum action. Its vertices are not merely the bare classical vertices; they include loop corrections. But once those proper vertices are known, connected correlators follow from tree diagrams built with the quantum-corrected ingredients.

The phrase "tree diagrams built from $\Gamma$" can be misleading on first encounter. These trees are not necessarily tree-level in the original coupling expansion. A single vertex of the tree may already contain many loops because it is a 1PI quantum vertex.

## Checks

A useful first check is the free theory. The free connected two-point function is nonzero, but all connected higher-point functions are products of two-point functions and therefore have no connected part beyond $n=2$. The effective action is quadratic:

$$
\Gamma_0[\varphi]
=\int d^4x\,
\left(
\frac12\partial_\mu\varphi\,\partial^\mu\varphi
-\frac12m^2\varphi^2
\right),
$$

so the only nonzero proper vertex is $\Gamma_0^{(2)}$. That matches the absence of interactions.

A second check is the scalar $\phi^4$ tree-level four-point function. The contact diagram is connected and 1PI, so it contributes directly to $\Gamma^{(4)}$. There is no tree-level 1PR exchange in pure $\phi^4$ theory at order $\lambda$, because there is no cubic vertex.

A third check is the scalar $\phi^3$ tree-level four-point function. The exchange diagram is connected but 1PR, so it belongs to $G_{4,\mathrm{c}}$ but not to $\Gamma^{(4)}$. It is built from two $\Gamma^{(3)}$ vertices connected by a propagator.

## Common pitfalls

**Confusing connected with 1PI.** Every 1PI diagram is connected, but not every connected diagram is 1PI. A connected exchange graph with a single internal bridge is the standard counterexample.

**Cutting external legs.** The 1PI test cuts internal lines only. External legs are operator insertions or external particle labels; cutting them would make almost every graph look reducible for the wrong reason.

**Thinking all loop diagrams are 1PI.** Many simple loop diagrams are 1PI, but the loop is not the definition. A diagram with loops can still contain a bridge line that makes it one-particle reducible.

**Forgetting amputation.** Proper vertices generated by $\Gamma$ are amputated kernels. A correlator diagram with external propagator tails may have the same internal topology, but it is not yet the proper vertex used in the effective action.

**Treating individual 1PI vertices as observables.** In gauge theories and in off-shell calculations, 1PI Green functions can depend on gauge choice and scheme. Physical observables are obtained after forming properly defined matrix elements or gauge-invariant correlators.

## Research status

This page is textbook-standard perturbative QFT. The graph-theoretic definition of 1PI diagrams and the effective-action generation of proper vertices are settled within ordinary perturbation theory.

The subtleties begin when extra structure enters: massless fields can create infrared singularities, gauge-fixed 1PI functions may be gauge dependent, composite operators require their own renormalization, and nonperturbative definitions of $\Gamma$ can be delicate. These issues do not change the basic definition, but they do affect what a given 1PI function means physically.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Classify four diagrams

Classify each diagram as disconnected, connected but 1PR, or 1PI:

1. two disjoint scalar propagators connecting four external insertions in a free theory;
2. a scalar $\phi^3$ tree exchange diagram for four external points;
3. a scalar $\phi^4$ one-loop bubble correction to the four-point function;
4. a scalar $\phi^4$ one-loop tadpole correction to the two-point function.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

1. The two disjoint propagators are disconnected.
2. The $\phi^3$ exchange diagram is connected but 1PR: cutting the internal exchange line separates the two cubic vertices.
3. The $\phi^4$ bubble is 1PI: the two quartic vertices are connected by two internal lines, and cutting either one leaves the other line connecting the graph.
4. The tadpole correction is 1PI: cutting the tadpole loop line does not split the diagram into two components.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Dyson resummation from a 1PI insertion

Let

$$
D_0(p)=\frac{i}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon},
$$

and let the amputated 1PI two-point insertion be $-i\Sigma(p^2)$. Sum

$$
D_0+D_0(-i\Sigma)D_0+D_0(-i\Sigma)D_0(-i\Sigma)D_0+\cdots.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Factor out $D_0$:

$$
D=D_0\left[1+(-i\Sigma)D_0+(-i\Sigma D_0)^2+\cdots\right].
$$

This is a geometric series:

$$
D=\frac{D_0}{1+i\Sigma D_0}.
$$

Since

$$
i\Sigma D_0=i\Sigma\frac{i}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon}
=-\frac{\Sigma}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon},
$$

we get

$$
D(p)=\frac{i}{p^2-m^2-\Sigma(p^2)+i\epsilon}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Legendre-transform inverse relation

Starting from

$$
\varphi(x)=\frac{\delta W[J]}{\delta J(x)},
\qquad
\frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)}=-J(x),
$$

show that

$$
\int d^4z\,
\frac{\delta^2\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)\delta\varphi(z)}
\frac{\delta^2W}{\delta J(z)\delta J(y)}
=-\delta^{(4)}(x-y).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate

$$
\frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)}=-J(x)
$$

with respect to $J(y)$. The left-hand side requires the chain rule:

$$
\frac{\delta}{\delta J(y)}
\frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)}
=\int d^4z\,
\frac{\delta^2\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)\delta\varphi(z)}
\frac{\delta\varphi(z)}{\delta J(y)}.
$$

Using $\varphi(z)=\delta W/\delta J(z)$ gives

$$
\frac{\delta\varphi(z)}{\delta J(y)}
=\frac{\delta^2W}{\delta J(z)\delta J(y)}.
$$

The derivative of the right-hand side is $-\delta^{(4)}(x-y)$, giving the desired identity.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Why the exchange graph is not a proper four-point vertex

In scalar $\phi^3$ theory, consider the four-point tree diagram made from two cubic vertices connected by one internal propagator. Explain why it contributes to the connected four-point function but not to $\Gamma^{(4)}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The diagram is connected, so it contributes to $G_{4,\mathrm{c}}$. But it is one-particle reducible: cutting the single internal propagator separates it into two three-point pieces. Therefore it is not part of the proper four-point vertex $\Gamma^{(4)}$. Instead, it is generated by joining two proper three-point vertices with a propagator.

</details>
