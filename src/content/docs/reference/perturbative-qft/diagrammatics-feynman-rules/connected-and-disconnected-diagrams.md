## Summary

A Feynman diagram is **connected** if every vertex and external insertion in the graph can be reached from every other one by following lines. It is **disconnected** if it splits into more than one component.

This distinction is not cosmetic. The full four-point function of a scalar field contains disconnected products such as

$$
G_2(x_1,x_2)G_2(x_3,x_4),
$$

while the connected four-point function $G_{4,c}$ isolates the part that cannot be factored into lower-point correlators. When the one-point function vanishes,

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_4(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
&=
G_{4,c}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(x_1,x_2)G_{2,c}(x_3,x_4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(x_1,x_3)G_{2,c}(x_2,x_4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(x_1,x_4)G_{2,c}(x_2,x_3).
\end{aligned}
$$

The source-functional statement is even cleaner. If

$$
Z[J]=e^{iW[J]},
$$

then $Z[J]$ generates full correlators and $W[J]$ generates connected correlators. Vacuum bubbles are diagrams with no external insertions; they cancel from normalized correlators because of the denominator $Z_{\mathrm{un}}[0]$.

For scattering, the physically interesting amplitude is usually the connected, amputated part of the Green function. Disconnected pieces either describe no scattering, independent sub-processes, or vacuum factors. This page explains the distinction and gives practical tests for what should be kept.

## Core idea

Full correlators answer: what is the expectation value of a product of operators?

Connected correlators answer: what part of that expectation value is not already explained by lower-point expectation values?

The difference is the same difference that appears in probability theory between moments and cumulants. Moments include products of independent fluctuations. Cumulants remove those products. In QFT language:

| Object | Generated by | Diagrammatic content |
|---|---|---|
| full correlators | $Z[J]$ | all diagrams, including disconnected products |
| connected correlators | $W[J]=-i\log Z[J]$ | connected diagrams only |
| vacuum bubbles | $Z_{\mathrm{un}}[0]$ | diagrams with no external insertions |
| amputated connected functions | LSZ input | connected diagrams with external propagators removed |

The connected part is usually where dynamics lives. In a free theory, all connected correlators above two points vanish, but full higher-point functions are still nonzero because Wick theorem produces products of two-point functions. In an interacting theory, connected higher-point functions encode genuine interactions.

## Setup and conventions

Let

$$
G_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\langle\Omega|T\phi(x_1)\cdots\phi(x_n)|\Omega\rangle
$$

be a normalized time-ordered $n$-point function. We couple the field to a source $J$ and use

$$
G_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\left.\frac{1}{i^n}
\frac{\delta^n Z[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}\right|_{J=0},
\qquad
Z[0]=1.
$$

The connected generating functional is defined by

$$
Z[J]=e^{iW[J]},
\qquad
W[J]=-i\log Z[J].
$$

Connected correlators are generated by

$$
G_{n,c}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\left.\frac{1}{i^{n-1}}
\frac{\delta^n W[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}\right|_{J=0}.
$$

This convention is chosen so that $G_{2,c}=D_F$ in the free scalar theory.

For simplicity, many formulas below assume

$$
G_1(x)=\langle\phi(x)\rangle=0.
$$

This is true in many symmetric scalar examples, such as unbroken $\phi\mapsto-\phi$ theory. When $G_1\neq0$, the same logic holds, but the moment–cumulant formulas also contain one-point pieces.

## Graph-theoretic connectedness

A diagram is connected if it has a single connected component after external insertions are included as endpoints. Equivalently, every part of the diagram can be reached from every other part by moving along propagator lines through vertices.

Examples:

| Diagram type | Connected? | What it contributes to |
|---|---:|---|
| one quartic vertex attached to four external points | yes | $G_{4,c}$ and $G_4$ |
| two separate propagators pairing four external points | no | $G_4$, not $G_{4,c}$ |
| a vacuum bubble times a connected two-point graph | disconnected from external insertions | cancels in normalized $G_2$ |
| two independent scattering subgraphs | disconnected | disconnected S-matrix contribution |

A disconnected diagram is not automatically a mistake. Full correlators contain disconnected diagrams. The mistake is using a disconnected full-correlator contribution when the intended object is a connected correlator or a scattering amplitude.

## Full versus connected correlators

The relation between full and connected correlators is the QFT version of the relation between moments and cumulants.

For one and two points,

$$
G_1(x_1)=G_{1,c}(x_1),
$$

and

$$
G_2(x_1,x_2)
=
G_{2,c}(x_1,x_2)+G_{1,c}(x_1)G_{1,c}(x_2).
$$

If $G_1=0$, this reduces to

$$
G_2=G_{2,c}.
$$

For three points,

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_3(x_1,x_2,x_3)
&=G_{3,c}(x_1,x_2,x_3)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(x_1,x_2)G_{1,c}(x_3)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(x_1,x_3)G_{1,c}(x_2)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(x_2,x_3)G_{1,c}(x_1)\\
&\quad+G_{1,c}(x_1)G_{1,c}(x_2)G_{1,c}(x_3).
\end{aligned}
$$

If $G_1=0$, this becomes simply

$$
G_3=G_{3,c}.
$$

For four points with $G_1=0$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_4(1,2,3,4)
&=G_{4,c}(1,2,3,4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(1,2)G_{2,c}(3,4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(1,3)G_{2,c}(2,4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(1,4)G_{2,c}(2,3),
\end{aligned}
$$

where $G_4(1,2,3,4)$ abbreviates $G_4(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)$.

In a free scalar theory, $G_{4,c}=0$, but $G_4$ is not zero. Wick theorem gives exactly the three products of two-point functions above.

## Why the logarithm keeps connected diagrams

The functional identity

$$
Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}
$$

says that full diagrams exponentiate connected diagrams. This is the linked-cluster theorem in source-functional form.

The reason is combinatorial. Suppose $C[J]$ is the sum of connected components. A disconnected diagram is a collection of connected components. If one component appears $n_1$ times, another $n_2$ times, and so on, the exponential

$$
\exp C[J]
$$

produces precisely the factors

$$
\frac{1}{n_1!n_2!\cdots}
$$

needed for indistinguishable repetitions of the same connected component. Thus the exponential of connected pieces is the sum over arbitrary disconnected collections of connected pieces.

The logarithm reverses this operation:

$$
iW[J]=\log Z[J].
$$

So $W[J]$ contains connected diagrams and only connected diagrams.

A quick check occurs in the free theory. The normalized Gaussian source functional is

$$
Z_0[J]
=
\exp\left[-\frac12\int J D_F J\right].
$$

Therefore

$$
iW_0[J]
=-\frac12\int J D_F J,
\qquad
W_0[J]
=\frac{i}{2}\int J D_F J.
$$

Since $W_0[J]$ is quadratic in $J$, it generates only the connected two-point function. All higher full free correlators come from expanding the exponential $Z_0=e^{iW_0}$, so they are products of two-point connected pieces.

## Vacuum bubbles

A vacuum bubble is a diagram with no external insertions. In an unnormalized path integral or operator expression, vacuum bubbles multiply diagrams that do have external insertions.

For an unnormalized generating functional,

$$
Z_{\mathrm{un}}[J]
=
\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\exp\left\{iS[\phi]+i\int J\phi\right\},
$$

the normalized functional is

$$
Z[J]=\frac{Z_{\mathrm{un}}[J]}{Z_{\mathrm{un}}[0]},
\qquad Z[0]=1.
$$

The denominator is exactly the vacuum-to-vacuum factor. Diagrammatically, it removes all components disconnected from every source insertion.

For an operator correlator, the same statement appears as

$$
\langle T\mathcal O\rangle
=
\frac{\langle0|T\{\mathcal O S\}|0\rangle_0}{\langle0|S|0\rangle_0}.
$$

The numerator contains diagrams attached to $\mathcal O$ multiplied by vacuum bubbles. The denominator contains the same vacuum bubbles. The ratio cancels them.

:::note[Vacuum bubbles are not meaningless]
Vacuum bubbles cancel from normalized ordinary correlators in flat-space QFT without gravity. They can still contribute to the vacuum energy, the effective action at zero source, finite-temperature free energies, Casimir energies, and gravity-coupled problems. Canceling from one observable is not the same as not existing.
:::

## Disconnected diagrams with external insertions

Do not confuse vacuum bubbles with all disconnected diagrams.

A vacuum bubble has no external insertions. A disconnected external diagram has multiple components, each attached to at least one external insertion. The latter does not cancel against $Z_{\mathrm{un}}[0]$ because it is not a vacuum factor.

For example, the free scalar four-point function contains

$$
D_F(x_1-x_2)D_F(x_3-x_4).
$$

This diagram is disconnected, but both components have external insertions. It remains in the full four-point function. It is removed only when we pass to $G_{4,c}$.

The hierarchy is:

```txt
normalize by Z_un[0]
  removes vacuum bubbles;

take log Z[J]
  keeps only connected external diagrams;

amputate external propagators
  prepares connected Green functions for LSZ;

take on-shell limits and residues
  gives scattering amplitudes.
```

Each step removes a different kind of extra structure.

## Connected diagrams and scattering

The S-matrix includes disconnected physics. For example, the identity operator in

$$
S=1+iT
$$

says that particles can pass through without scattering. Multi-particle matrix elements may also contain disconnected products, such as one pair scattering while another particle is a spectator.

For a genuine $2\to2$ connected scattering process, qft.org conventions define

$$
\langle f|iT|i\rangle
=
i(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_f-P_i)\mathcal M_{fi}.
$$

The amplitude $\mathcal M_{fi}$ is extracted from the connected, amputated part of the relevant Green function, with external momenta placed on shell and external residues or wavefunctions included according to LSZ.

This is why perturbative scattering pages usually draw connected diagrams only. Disconnected diagrams either reproduce the identity part of $S$, describe independent sub-processes, or cancel as vacuum factors.

## Cluster decomposition

Connected correlators also encode locality at long distance. Roughly, if two sets of operators are separated by a large spacelike distance, then in a theory with a unique vacuum and a mass gap one expects

$$
\langle\mathcal O_A\mathcal O_B\rangle
\longrightarrow
\langle\mathcal O_A\rangle\langle\mathcal O_B\rangle,
$$

so the connected part decays:

$$
\langle\mathcal O_A\mathcal O_B\rangle_c
\longrightarrow 0.
$$

This property is called cluster decomposition. It says that sufficiently distant experiments decorrelate. In momentum-space scattering theory, the corresponding idea is that connected amplitudes carry only one overall momentum-conservation delta function. Extra delta functions indicate disconnected components.

There are caveats. Massless particles can produce power-law rather than exponential decay. Gauge theories require care with gauge-invariant operators and charged sectors. Spontaneous symmetry breaking, finite volume, degenerate vacua, curved spacetime, and thermal states all require more precise statements. The simple slogan is still useful: connected correlators measure non-factorizing correlations.

## Momentum-space test for connectedness

Momentum-space expressions provide a practical diagnostic.

For a connected $n$-point function, translation invariance gives exactly one overall delta function:

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}\!\left(p_1+\cdots+p_n\right).
$$

A disconnected product has one delta function per connected component. For example,

$$
\widetilde G_2(p_1,p_2)\widetilde G_2(p_3,p_4)
$$

comes with

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(p_1+p_2)
\,(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(p_3+p_4),
$$

not just one overall delta function. The extra delta function is the momentum-space fingerprint of a disconnected component.

This is one reason connected amplitudes are so natural in scattering. A connected scattering process conserves total momentum, but does not separately conserve the momentum of arbitrary subsets of external particles.

## Worked example: free four-point function

For a free real scalar field with $\langle\phi\rangle=0$, Wick theorem gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_4^{(0)}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
&=D_F(x_1-x_2)D_F(x_3-x_4)\\
&\quad+D_F(x_1-x_3)D_F(x_2-x_4)\\
&\quad+D_F(x_1-x_4)D_F(x_2-x_3).
\end{aligned}
$$

Each term is a product of two disconnected two-point components. Therefore

$$
G_{4,c}^{(0)}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)=0.
$$

This is not because the full four-point function vanishes. It does not. It is because a free Gaussian theory has no genuine connected four-point correlation.

## Worked example: first connected four-point term in φ⁴ theory

In scalar $\phi^4$ theory,

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{int}}=-\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4,
$$

the first connected contribution to the four-point function is

$$
G_{4,c}^{(1)}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
=
(-i\lambda)\int d^4z\,
\prod_{a=1}^4 D_F(x_a-z).
$$

This graph is connected: all four external insertions communicate through the same interaction point $z$. The full four-point function through this order is

$$
G_4
=
G_4^{(0)}+G_{4,c}^{(1)}+O(\lambda^2),
$$

where $G_4^{(0)}$ is the disconnected free Wick part. The connected part through this order is

$$
G_{4,c}=G_{4,c}^{(1)}+O(\lambda^2).
$$

Thus interactions first show up in $G_{4,c}$ at order $\lambda$.

## Common pitfalls

**Calling every disconnected diagram a vacuum bubble.** A vacuum bubble has no external insertions. A disconnected product of two two-point functions has external insertions and remains in the full four-point function.

**Forgetting normalization.** Vacuum bubbles cancel only after dividing by $Z_{\mathrm{un}}[0]$ or $\langle0|S|0\rangle_0$.

**Thinking connected means one-particle-irreducible.** A connected diagram may fall apart when one internal line is cut. Connected is weaker than one-particle-irreducible.

**Using full correlators as amplitudes.** Scattering amplitudes come from connected, amputated, on-shell objects, not full correlators with disconnected Wick products and external propagators included.

**Ignoring one-point functions.** If $\langle\phi\rangle\neq0$, moment–cumulant formulas include one-point terms. Shift to fluctuations around the correct vacuum when appropriate.

**Assuming cluster decomposition without hypotheses.** Massless fields, gauge theories, degenerate vacua, topological sectors, finite volume, and thermal states can all complicate naive clustering statements.

## Research status

**Established:** The full/connected decomposition, logarithmic generation of connected diagrams, and vacuum-bubble cancellation in normalized correlators are standard perturbative QFT.

**Convention-dependent:** Factors of $i$ in $W[J]$ depend on Lorentzian versus Euclidean conventions and on the source normalization. The connected/full distinction does not.

**Subtle:** Cluster decomposition requires assumptions about the vacuum, spectrum, sectors, and observables. Gauge theories and massless theories require particular care.

**Active:** Nonperturbative definitions of connected correlators, real-time finite-density systems, gauge-invariant charged sectors, curved-spacetime vacuum structure, and factorization in quantum gravity remain active research areas.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Free four-point connected part

Use Wick theorem to show that the connected four-point function of a free scalar field with $\langle\phi\rangle=0$ vanishes.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Wick theorem gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_4^{(0)}(1,2,3,4)
&=D_F(1,2)D_F(3,4)\\
&\quad+D_F(1,3)D_F(2,4)\\
&\quad+D_F(1,4)D_F(2,3).
\end{aligned}
$$

With $G_1=0$, the full/connected relation is

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_4(1,2,3,4)
&=G_{4,c}(1,2,3,4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(1,2)G_{2,c}(3,4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(1,3)G_{2,c}(2,4)\\
&\quad+G_{2,c}(1,4)G_{2,c}(2,3).
\end{aligned}
$$

In the free theory $G_{2,c}=D_F$. Comparing with Wick theorem shows

$$
G_{4,c}^{(0)}=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Logarithm and the four-point cumulant

Assume $G_1=0$. Starting from $Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}$, show that

$$
G_{4,c}(1,2,3,4)
=
G_4(1,2,3,4)
-G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4)
-G_2(1,3)G_2(2,4)
-G_2(1,4)G_2(2,3).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Because $Z=e^{iW}$, derivatives of $Z$ generate all partitions of derivatives acting on factors of $iW$. At four derivatives and with $G_1=0$, only two types of partitions survive:

1. all four derivatives act on one $W$, giving the connected four-point function;
2. the derivatives split into two pairs, giving products of connected two-point functions.

Using the convention

$$
G_n=\left.\frac{1}{i^n}\frac{\delta^n Z}{\delta J_1\cdots\delta J_n}\right|_{J=0},
\qquad
G_{n,c}=\left.\frac{1}{i^{n-1}}\frac{\delta^n W}{\delta J_1\cdots\delta J_n}\right|_{J=0},
$$

the four-point relation becomes

$$
G_4
=G_{4,c}+G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4)+G_2(1,3)G_2(2,4)+G_2(1,4)G_2(2,3).
$$

Solving for $G_{4,c}$ gives the desired formula.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Vacuum-bubble cancellation

Suppose an unnormalized two-point numerator factorizes diagrammatically as

$$
N(x,y)=A(x,y)B,
$$

where $A(x,y)$ is the sum of diagrams attached to the external insertions and $B$ is the vacuum-bubble factor. If the denominator is $Z_{\mathrm{un}}[0]=B$, show that normalized correlators contain no vacuum-bubble factor.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The normalized correlator is

$$
G_2(x,y)=\frac{N(x,y)}{Z_{\mathrm{un}}[0]}.
$$

Substituting the factorized form gives

$$
G_2(x,y)=\frac{A(x,y)B}{B}=A(x,y).
$$

Thus the vacuum-bubble factor cancels. This cancellation removes components with no external insertions; it does not remove disconnected components that do attach to external insertions.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Momentum-space connectedness test

A four-point momentum-space expression contains

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(p_1+p_2)
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(p_3+p_4)
F(p_1,p_3).
$$

Does this have the delta-function structure of a connected four-point function?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

No. A connected four-point function should have one overall delta function,

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(p_1+p_2+p_3+p_4),
$$

with the remaining dependence contained in a reduced correlator. The expression in the problem has two separate delta functions, which separately conserve momentum in the $(1,2)$ and $(3,4)$ subsets. That is the momentum-space signature of two disconnected components.

</details>

### Exercise 5: First connected term in φ⁴ theory

In scalar $\phi^4$ theory, explain why the order-$\lambda$ contact diagram contributes to $G_{4,c}$ but not to $G_{2,c}$ as a four-point interaction.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The order-$\lambda$ contact diagram has one quartic vertex with four scalar fields at the same interaction point. If each of the four fields contracts with one of the four external insertions, the result is

$$
(-i\lambda)\int d^4z\,D_F(x_1-z)D_F(x_2-z)D_F(x_3-z)D_F(x_4-z),
$$

which is connected and has four external insertions. Therefore it contributes to $G_{4,c}$.

For a two-point function at order $\lambda$, the same quartic vertex can attach two fields to the external points and contract the remaining two fields with each other. That gives a tadpole correction to $G_{2,c}$, not a four-point interaction. The number and assignment of external insertions determine which correlator the diagram contributes to.

</details>
