## Summary

A **renormalized amplitude** is the finite scattering amplitude obtained after regularizing loop diagrams, adding counterterm diagrams, amputating external propagators, putting external particles on shell, and applying the correct LSZ residue factors.

With qft.org's $S$-matrix convention,

$$
\langle f|iT|i\rangle
=
i(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_f-P_i)\mathcal M_{fi},
$$

so the object inserted into cross sections and decay rates is $\mathcal M$, not $i\mathcal M$.

For stable scalar particles, a useful schematic formula is

$$
\mathcal M_{\rm ren}
=
\left(\prod_{a\in {\rm external}} Z_{{\rm pole},a}^{1/2}\right)
\mathcal A_{\rm amp}^{\rm ren}\bigg|_{p_a^2=M_a^2},
$$

where $\mathcal A_{\rm amp}^{\rm ren}$ is the finite connected Green function amputated by the full renormalized two-point functions, and $Z_{{\rm pole},a}$ is the residue of the exact propagator for the external particle. In an on-shell scheme for stable particles, the convention is often chosen so that these external residues are one. In a minimal-subtraction scheme, the residue factors must generally be kept or converted into physical normalization separately.

The important moral is boring and powerful: no single diagram is usually the answer. The finite amplitude is a **renormalized sum** of ordinary diagrams and counterterm diagrams, followed by the appropriate external-particle normalization.

## Core idea

Renormalization makes scattering amplitudes finite without erasing the real momentum dependence generated by quantum fluctuations.

The workflow is:

| Step | What happens | What is removed or kept |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | regulate loop integrals | divergent expressions become controlled functions of a regulator |
| 2 | add local counterterms | ultraviolet poles and chosen finite local pieces are canceled |
| 3 | sum diagrams at fixed order | the result is finite but scheme-dependent as a function of renormalized parameters |
| 4 | amputate external propagators | external propagation is separated from the interaction kernel |
| 5 | take on-shell limits | external momenta become physical particle momenta |
| 6 | apply LSZ residues | external states get the correct normalization |
| 7 | form observables | phase space, flux, spin sums, identical-particle factors, and infrared inclusiveness are added |

The counterterms are local because ultraviolet divergences arise from short-distance regions. They can cancel polynomial or local divergent pieces. They cannot cancel nonlocal physical dependence such as

$$
\ln\frac{-s-i\epsilon}{\mu^2},
\qquad
\sqrt{1-\frac{4m^2}{s}},
\qquad
\operatorname{Im}\mathcal M(s).
$$

Those terms encode thresholds, branch cuts, running, and unitarity. Renormalization removes fake infinities, not the physics.

## Setup and conventions

We use the qft.org mostly-minus convention and write amplitudes by

$$
\langle f|iT|i\rangle
=
i(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_f-P_i)\mathcal M_{fi}.
$$

The scalar example on this page is real $\phi^4$ theory,

$$
\mathcal L
=
\frac12\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi
-
\frac12m^2\phi^2
-
\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4
+
\mathcal L_{\rm ct},
$$

with counterterm Lagrangian

$$
\mathcal L_{\rm ct}
=
\frac12\delta Z\,\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi
-
\frac12\delta m^2\phi^2
-
\frac{\delta\lambda}{4!}\phi^4
-\delta\Omega.
$$

Here $\delta\Omega$ is the vacuum-energy counterterm. It affects vacuum diagrams and the effective action, but it does not enter ordinary connected scattering amplitudes in flat spacetime without gravity.

For a scalar propagator written as

$$
G_R(p)=
\frac{i}{p^2-m^2-\Sigma_R(p^2)+i\epsilon},
$$

a stable physical pole at $p^2=M^2$ has residue

$$
G_R(p)
\sim
\frac{iZ_{\rm pole}}{p^2-M^2+i\epsilon}
+\text{regular terms}.
$$

For scalar fields with this self-energy convention,

$$
Z_{\rm pole}^{-1}
=
1-\Sigma_R'(M^2).
$$

The LSZ residue factors in amplitudes are built from these pole residues, not from the name of the field variable alone.

:::note[Stable particles only]
This page states LSZ formulas for stable asymptotic particles. Unstable particles do not appear as external in/out states in the same way. Resonances are inferred from internal propagator poles, line shapes, and inclusive final states, not from asymptotic one-particle states.
:::

## Amputated correlators and physical amplitudes

Let $\widetilde G_{n,c}$ be a connected momentum-space Green function of renormalized fields. Near the one-particle poles of its external legs, it can be written schematically as

$$
\widetilde G_{n,c}(p_1,\ldots,p_n)
=
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}\!\left(\sum_i p_i\right)
\left[\prod_{i=1}^n G_R(p_i)\right]
 i\mathcal A_{n,{\rm amp}}(p_1,\ldots,p_n),
$$

where all momenta are taken incoming. The amputated function $\mathcal A_{n,{\rm amp}}$ is not yet automatically the physical scattering amplitude. To obtain a physical amplitude, continue the appropriate legs from incoming to outgoing by crossing, put the external momenta on their mass shells, and include the LSZ residues.

For stable scalar particles,

$$
\mathcal M_{n}
=
\left(\prod_{i=1}^{n}Z_{{\rm pole},i}^{1/2}\right)
\mathcal A_{n,{\rm amp}}\bigg|_{p_i^2=M_i^2}
$$

up to the standard crossing assignments for initial and final particles.

This formula explains why self-energy corrections on external legs are treated with care. If one computes connected diagrams with full external propagators and then applies LSZ, external self-energy insertions contribute through pole shifts and residues. If one computes amputated diagrams with renormalized external legs, the same physics is encoded in the external LSZ factors and renormalization conditions. Counting both ways at once double counts.

## Diagrammatic expansion at fixed order

At a given order in perturbation theory, a renormalized amplitude has the schematic form

$$
\mathcal M_{\rm ren}^{(N)}
=
\sum_{\Gamma\le N}\mathcal M_{\Gamma}^{\rm reg}
+
\sum_{{\rm ct}\le N}\mathcal M_{\rm ct}^{\rm reg}
+\mathcal M_{\rm LSZ}^{(N)}.
$$

The first sum contains ordinary loop diagrams. The second sum contains diagrams with counterterm vertices. The last term represents the perturbative expansion of the external residue factors when they are not already set to one by the chosen renormalization conditions.

Counterterms count by perturbative order. For example, a one-loop amplitude may include one insertion of a one-loop counterterm. A two-loop amplitude may include two-loop ordinary diagrams, one-loop diagrams with one-loop counterterms, tree diagrams with two-loop counterterms, and products from external residue factors.

This is not bookkeeping for bookkeeping's sake. It is what makes amplitudes finite and reproducible.

## One-loop scalar four-point example

In $\phi^4$ theory, the tree-level four-point amplitude is

$$
\mathcal M_{\rm tree}=-\lambda.
$$

At one loop, the four-point vertex receives the $s$, $t$, and $u$ channel fish diagrams. With the bubble-integral convention used in the loop-expansion pages,

$$
I(P^2)
=
\mu^{2\epsilon}
\int\frac{d^d\ell}{(2\pi)^d}
\frac{i}{\ell^2-m^2+i\epsilon}
\frac{i}{(\ell+P)^2-m^2+i\epsilon},
$$

and

$$
I(P^2)=-iB(P^2;m^2,m^2),
$$

where

$$
B(P^2;m^2,m^2)
=
\frac{1}{16\pi^2}
\left[
\frac{1}{\overline\epsilon}
-
\int_0^1 dx\,
\ln\frac{m^2-x(1-x)P^2-i\epsilon}{\mu^2}
\right]
+O(\epsilon).
$$

The regulated one-loop vertex contribution is

$$
\mathcal V_4^{(1)}(s,t,u)
=
\frac{(-i\lambda)^2}{2}
\left[I(s)+I(t)+I(u)\right],
$$

and the coupling counterterm vertex is

$$
\mathcal V_{4,{\rm ct}}=-i\delta\lambda.
$$

The ultraviolet pole in the loop part is

$$
\mathcal V_{4,{\rm div}}^{(1)}
=
 i\frac{3\lambda^2}{32\pi^2}\frac{1}{\overline\epsilon}.
$$

The $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ counterterm

$$
\delta\lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}
=
\frac{3\lambda^2}{32\pi^2}\frac{1}{\overline\epsilon}
+O(\lambda^3)
$$

cancels this pole. The finite one-loop amplitude can then be written as

$$
\mathcal M_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}^{(1)}(s,t,u)
=
-\lambda
+
\frac{\lambda^2}{2}
\left[
B_{\rm fin}(s)+B_{\rm fin}(t)+B_{\rm fin}(u)
\right]
+O(\lambda^3),
$$

with

$$
B_{\rm fin}(P^2)
=
-\frac{1}{16\pi^2}
\int_0^1dx\,
\ln\frac{m^2-x(1-x)P^2-i\epsilon}{\mu^2}.
$$

This formula is not merely finite. It also has analytic structure. If $P^2$ crosses the two-particle threshold $4m^2$, the logarithm develops an imaginary part. That imaginary part is the perturbative shadow of unitarity: intermediate particles can go on shell.

:::tip[What was subtracted?]
Only the local ultraviolet pole was subtracted. The finite logarithm was not subtracted in $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$. A momentum-subtraction scheme could remove a finite value at one chosen kinematic point, but it still could not remove the full nonlocal dependence on $s,t,u$.
:::

## Scheme dependence and physical independence

The expression

$$
\mathcal M_{\rm ren}(s,t,u;\lambda,m,\mu)
$$

depends on the renormalization scheme because the symbols $\lambda$, $m$, and even the field normalization are scheme-defined quantities. A different scheme uses different coordinates on the same physical theory.

For example, suppose two schemes are related by

$$
\lambda'=
\lambda+c\lambda^2+O(\lambda^3).
$$

If the tree amplitude is $-\lambda$, then expressing it in terms of $\lambda'$ gives

$$
-\lambda
=
-\lambda'+c\lambda'^2+O(\lambda'^3).
$$

That finite shift must be accompanied by a corresponding finite change in the one-loop remainder. The split between “tree parameter” and “loop correction” changes, but the physical amplitude expressed in terms of measured inputs does not.

This is why a quoted amplitude should always say what scheme defines its parameters. A formula involving $\lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}(\mu)$ is not the same type of statement as a formula involving a pole mass and an on-shell coupling.

## Renormalized amplitudes and scale dependence

Minimal-subtraction amplitudes contain explicit logarithms of the renormalization scale:

$$
\ln\frac{m^2-x(1-x)s-i\epsilon}{\mu^2}.
$$

This does not mean physical predictions depend on an arbitrary scale. The renormalized coupling and mass also depend on $\mu$. At all orders, the explicit $\mu$ dependence of the loop functions cancels the implicit $\mu$ dependence of the running parameters.

At finite perturbative order, the cancellation is only as good as the order to which the calculation has been performed. Choosing $\mu$ near the characteristic momentum transfer reduces large logarithms and improves the behavior of the truncated expansion. This is the practical reason renormalization-scale variation is used as a rough diagnostic of missing higher orders.

The systematic version of this statement is the [Callan–Symanzik Preview](/perturbative-qft/renormalized-perturbation-theory/callan-symanzik-preview/).

## Infrared and massless caveats

Renormalization handles ultraviolet divergences. It does not automatically make every scattering amplitude physically measurable.

In massless theories, amplitudes can contain soft and collinear infrared divergences. These divergences are not removed by ultraviolet counterterms. They cancel only after defining sufficiently inclusive infrared-safe observables, or after factorizing the amplitude into hard, soft, and collinear pieces.

The distinction is:

| Issue | Typical origin | What fixes it |
|---|---|---|
| ultraviolet divergence | large loop momentum, short distances | local counterterms and renormalization |
| soft divergence | unresolved low-energy massless radiation | inclusive sums or soft factorization |
| collinear divergence | unresolved radiation parallel to a massless external particle | inclusive definitions, jet functions, PDFs, or fragmentation functions |
| unstable-particle pole | resonance rather than asymptotic state | complex-pole schemes, line shapes, inclusive final states |

A UV-renormalized amplitude can still be infrared divergent. Do not call such an amplitude an observable without specifying the infrared-safe quantity.

## Gauge-theory caveats

In gauge theories, individual diagrams and off-shell Green functions generally depend on the gauge-fixing choice. A physical amplitude between allowed external states is gauge independent after all diagrams, counterterms, ghosts, and external-state projections are included.

Three practical rules keep the calculation honest:

1. Use a regulator and counterterm scheme that preserves the relevant gauge identities, or track the symmetry-restoring counterterms explicitly.
2. Include all diagrams at the same perturbative order, including ghost diagrams and counterterm diagrams.
3. Check Ward or Slavnov–Taylor identities where possible.

Gauge independence is a property of the completed physical quantity, not of a random subset of diagrams.

## Common pitfalls

**Reporting $i\mathcal M$ as $\mathcal M$.** Feynman diagrams usually produce $i\mathcal M$. With qft.org conventions, a scalar quartic vertex $-i\lambda$ gives $\mathcal M=-\lambda$.

**Forgetting counterterm diagrams.** A loop diagram by itself is usually regulator-dependent. The renormalized amplitude includes counterterm insertions fixed by the chosen scheme.

**Double counting external self-energies.** External self-energy effects can be included through explicit external-leg diagrams before LSZ or through pole residues after amputation. Do not include the same correction twice.

**Confusing finite with physical.** A UV-finite amplitude may still be scheme-dependent, gauge-dependent if off shell, or infrared divergent if massless.

**Trying to subtract nonlocal logs with local counterterms.** Counterterms are local. They can cancel UV poles and chosen local finite pieces, but not the full momentum dependence of a loop amplitude.

**Treating scheme dependence as ambiguity in nature.** Scheme dependence is coordinate dependence in parameter space. Physical predictions require specifying input observables and computing output observables consistently.

## Research status

- **Established:** The order-by-order construction of UV-renormalized amplitudes from regulated diagrams, counterterms, and LSZ reduction is textbook-standard.
- **Convention-dependent:** Signs, factors of $i$, residue conventions, and the exact split between explicit external-leg diagrams and LSZ factors depend on amplitude and field-normalization conventions.
- **Scheme-dependent:** Renormalized parameters such as $\lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}(\mu)$ and $m_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}(\mu)$ are not directly measured numbers until matched to observables.
- **Active:** Precision collider calculations, EFT matching, unstable-particle schemes, multi-loop amplitudes, and infrared factorization require refined versions of the simple scalar story.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Pole cancellation in the scalar four-point amplitude

Use

$$
\mathcal V_{4,{\rm div}}^{(1)}
=
 i\frac{3\lambda^2}{32\pi^2}\frac{1}{\overline\epsilon}
$$

and

$$
\mathcal V_{4,{\rm ct}}=-i\delta\lambda
$$

to determine $\delta\lambda$ in $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ at one loop.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The divergent part of the total one-loop vertex plus counterterm is

$$
\mathcal V_{4,{\rm div}}^{(1)}+\mathcal V_{4,{\rm ct}}
=
 i\frac{3\lambda^2}{32\pi^2}\frac{1}{\overline\epsilon}
-i\delta\lambda.
$$

Cancellation requires this expression to vanish, so

$$
\delta\lambda
=
\frac{3\lambda^2}{32\pi^2}\frac{1}{\overline\epsilon}.
$$

Thus

$$
\delta\lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}
=
\frac{3\lambda^2}{32\pi^2}\frac{1}{\overline\epsilon}
+O(\lambda^3).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: From pole residue to LSZ factor

Suppose a renormalized scalar propagator has the pole form

$$
G_R(p)\sim \frac{iZ_{\rm pole}}{p^2-M^2+i\epsilon}.
$$

If the four-point function is amputated by the full renormalized two-point functions to give $\mathcal A_{4,{\rm amp}}$, what LSZ residue factor relates it to the physical four-scalar amplitude?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Each external scalar leg contributes a factor $Z_{\rm pole}^{1/2}$. For four identical external scalar particles, the total external residue factor is

$$
\left(Z_{\rm pole}^{1/2}\right)^4=Z_{\rm pole}^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\mathcal M_4
=
Z_{\rm pole}^2\,
\mathcal A_{4,{\rm amp}}\big|_{p_i^2=M^2}.
$$

In an on-shell renormalization convention with unit pole residue, this factor is one.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why a local counterterm cannot remove a threshold cut

The finite bubble function contains

$$
\int_0^1dx\,
\ln\left[m^2-x(1-x)s-i\epsilon\right].
$$

Explain why a local $\phi^4$ counterterm cannot remove the full $s$ dependence of this expression.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A local $\phi^4$ counterterm contributes a momentum-independent four-point vertex,

$$
-i\delta\lambda.
$$

More general local higher-derivative counterterms contribute polynomials in the external momenta. The bubble logarithm is not a polynomial in $s$. It has branch-point behavior when

$$
s=4m^2,
$$

where the two internal particles can go on shell. A finite local counterterm can change the value of the amplitude at a chosen subtraction point or add polynomial terms, but it cannot remove a nonlocal logarithm or its branch cut.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Scheme change at one loop

Suppose two coupling schemes are related by

$$
\lambda'=\lambda+c\lambda^2+O(\lambda^3).
$$

If an amplitude in the first scheme is

$$
\mathcal M=-\lambda+\lambda^2F(s,t,u)+O(\lambda^3),
$$

write it in terms of $\lambda'$ through order $\lambda'^2$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Invert the relation perturbatively:

$$
\lambda=\lambda'-c\lambda'^2+O(\lambda'^3).
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal M
&=-(\lambda'-c\lambda'^2)
+(\lambda'^2+O(\lambda'^3))F(s,t,u)
+O(\lambda'^3)\\
&=-\lambda'+\lambda'^2\left[F(s,t,u)+c\right]+O(\lambda'^3).
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the finite one-loop remainder shifts by

$$
F\mapsto F+c.
$$

The split between the tree parameter and finite loop term is scheme-dependent.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Amplitude versus cross section

For unpolarized $2\to2$ scattering, explain why a finite $\mathcal M$ is not yet a cross section.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The amplitude is the dynamical part of the scattering probability. A differential cross section also requires kinematic and statistical ingredients. In the center-of-mass frame for two-body final states, a typical formula is

$$
\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}
=
\frac{1}{64\pi^2s}
\frac{|\mathbf p_f|}{|\mathbf p_i|}
\overline{|\mathcal M|^2},
$$

possibly multiplied by identical-final-state factors when integrated over phase space. The bar denotes spin or polarization averages and sums when present. Thus $\mathcal M$ must be squared, summed or averaged over quantum numbers, and combined with flux and phase-space factors before it becomes a cross section.

</details>
