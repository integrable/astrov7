## Summary

The vacuum in Wightman QFT is not merely the informal phrase "the state with no particles." It is a distinguished unit vector $\Omega\in\mathcal H$ satisfying two structural requirements. First, it is invariant under spacetime symmetries,

$$
U(a,\Lambda)\Omega=\Omega,
$$

at least for the connected Poincaré group in the standard formulation. Second, it is **cyclic** for the fields: finite linear combinations of vectors of the form

$$
\phi_{i_1}(f_1)\cdots \phi_{i_n}(f_n)\Omega
$$

are dense in the Hilbert space. More compactly, if $\mathfrak P(\phi)$ denotes the polynomial algebra generated by smeared fields, then

$$
\overline{\mathfrak P(\phi)\Omega}=\mathcal H.
$$

This page explains why the vacuum and cyclicity axioms are paired. The invariant vacuum supplies the reference state from which vacuum expectation values are computed. Cyclicity says that the field-generated states are not just a convenient subspace; after completion, they are the whole Hilbert space of the theory in this representation.

## Prerequisites

You should know the Wightman data from [Wightman Axioms](/rigorous-qft/wightman-axiomatic-qft/wightman-axioms/), the common-domain setup from [Hilbert Space and Fields](/rigorous-qft/wightman-axiomatic-qft/hilbert-space-and-fields/), the covariance law from [Poincaré Covariance](/rigorous-qft/wightman-axiomatic-qft/poincare-covariance/), and the positive-energy condition from [Spectral Condition](/rigorous-qft/wightman-axiomatic-qft/spectral-condition/).

The word **cyclic** comes from operator algebra and functional analysis: a vector is cyclic for a set of operators if applying those operators to the vector and taking finite linear combinations gives a dense subspace.

## Core idea

A Wightman theory contains a Hilbert space, fields, a Poincaré representation, and a vacuum. Without cyclicity, these data could contain irrelevant extra Hilbert-space directions that no product of fields ever reaches from the vacuum. Such directions would not change the vacuum expectation values,

$$
\langle\Omega,\phi_{i_1}(f_1)\cdots\phi_{i_n}(f_n)\Omega\rangle,
$$

but they would make the Hilbert space artificially larger. Cyclicity removes this redundancy.

The axiom is also what makes vacuum correlation functions powerful. If every state is approximable by field polynomials acting on $\Omega$, then the inner products of those polynomial states are determined by the Wightman functions. This is the seed of the Wightman reconstruction theorem: a suitable collection of vacuum distributions can be used to rebuild $\mathcal H$, $\Omega$, translations, Lorentz transformations, and smeared fields.

A useful slogan is:

$$
\text{cyclicity}
=
\text{the vacuum is a generating vector for the field algebra}.
$$

This slogan should not be read as an experimental protocol. Density is a Hilbert-space statement, not a claim that an apparatus in a small laboratory can prepare arbitrary global states with bounded resources.

## Setup and conventions

Let $\mathcal H$ be the positive Hilbert space of a Wightman theory. Let $U(a,\Lambda)$ be the strongly continuous unitary representation of the proper orthochronous Poincaré group, with translations

$$
U(a,I)=e^{ia\cdot P}.
$$

Let $\mathcal D$ be the common invariant dense domain for the smeared fields $\phi_i(f)$. The vacuum vector is a unit vector

$$
\Omega\in\mathcal D,
\qquad
\|\Omega\|=1.
$$

The standard vacuum invariance axiom is

$$
U(a,\Lambda)\Omega=\Omega.
$$

Equivalently, for translations,

$$
P_\mu\Omega=0,
$$

in the spectral-theoretic sense. Thus the vacuum has zero total energy-momentum. In a positive-energy representation, this makes it the bottom of the translation spectrum.

The polynomial field domain generated from the vacuum is

$$
\mathcal D_0
=
\operatorname{span}
\left\{
\phi_{i_1}(f_1)\cdots\phi_{i_n}(f_n)\Omega:
 n\ge0
\right\}.
$$

The case $n=0$ contributes the vector $\Omega$ itself. The cyclicity axiom is

$$
\overline{\mathcal D_0}=\mathcal H.
$$

In many treatments one chooses the Wightman domain itself to be $\mathcal D_0$, or at least a closely related invariant dense domain. The important point is not the name of the domain but the density of states created from the vacuum by smeared fields.

## Vacuum invariance

Vacuum invariance says that the reference state does not choose an origin, a preferred inertial frame, or a nonzero four-momentum. For any field product,

$$
W_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\langle\Omega,
\phi_1(x_1)\cdots\phi_n(x_n)
\Omega\rangle,
$$

translation invariance of $\Omega$ implies

$$
W_n(x_1+a,\ldots,x_n+a)=W_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n).
$$

For scalar fields, Lorentz invariance of the vacuum and covariance of the fields give

$$
W_n(\Lambda x_1,\ldots,\Lambda x_n)
=
W_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
$$

for proper orthochronous Lorentz transformations. For fields with spin or internal indices, the correlator transforms with the corresponding finite-dimensional matrices on the field labels.

The vacuum is usually also assumed unique among translation-invariant vectors, at least up to a complex phase. Some presentations include uniqueness as part of the axioms; others separate it because models with several sectors or broken symmetries require selecting a representation. When uniqueness holds, it is closely tied to clustering properties of correlations. When uniqueness fails, a single Wightman representation may still be built after choosing one vacuum sector.

:::note[One representation at a time]
A Wightman theory is usually a theory in a fixed Hilbert-space representation. In systems with multiple phases, theta vacua, charged sectors, or spontaneous symmetry breaking, the phrase "the vacuum" means the cyclic vector of the chosen representation unless the page says otherwise.
:::

## Cyclicity as a density statement

The cyclicity axiom is a density statement:

$$
\forall \Psi\in\mathcal H,
\quad
\forall \epsilon>0,
\quad
\exists A\in\mathfrak P(\phi)
\quad
\text{such that}\quad
\|\Psi-A\Omega\|<\epsilon.
$$

Here $A$ is a finite linear combination of finite products of smeared fields. No infinite product is required in the algebraic expression; the Hilbert-space limit enters only through closure.

This distinction matters. Cyclicity does **not** say every vector is exactly equal to $A\Omega$ for a finite field polynomial $A$. It says finite field-polynomial states are dense. The same pattern appears in Fock space: finite-particle vectors are dense in bosonic or fermionic Fock space, even though a general vector can be an infinite superposition of particle-number sectors.

A quick redundancy argument explains why cyclicity is natural. Let

$$
\mathcal K=\overline{\mathcal D_0}.
$$

If $\mathcal K\ne\mathcal H$, then $\mathcal K^\perp$ is a nonzero closed subspace. Vectors in $\mathcal K^\perp$ are orthogonal to every state obtained by applying fields to $\Omega$:

$$
\langle\Psi,
\phi_{i_1}(f_1)\cdots\phi_{i_n}(f_n)\Omega\rangle=0,
\qquad
\Psi\in\mathcal K^\perp.
$$

Such vectors are invisible to all vacuum-created states. If the purpose of the theory is to describe the Hilbert space generated by the fields from the vacuum, then one should replace $\mathcal H$ by $\mathcal K$. The cyclicity axiom says this replacement has already been made.

## Why cyclicity makes correlators determine the theory

Let

$$
A=\sum_\alpha c_\alpha
\phi_{\alpha_1}(f_{\alpha_1})\cdots
\phi_{\alpha_{m_\alpha}}(f_{\alpha_{m_\alpha}})
$$

and similarly for $B$. The inner product of two polynomial states is

$$
\langle A\Omega,B\Omega\rangle
=
\langle\Omega,A^*B\Omega\rangle.
$$

The right-hand side is a finite linear combination of Wightman functions smeared against test functions. Therefore the full family of vacuum expectation values determines the inner products on $\mathcal D_0$.

Cyclicity then extends this information to all of $\mathcal H$ by completion. This is the basic Hilbert-space mechanism behind reconstruction:

$$
\{W_n\}_{n\ge0}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{inner products on polynomial states}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\mathcal H.
$$

The positivity condition on Wightman functions is exactly the condition that the inner product produced in this way is nonnegative:

$$
\langle A\Omega,A\Omega\rangle\ge0.
$$

After quotienting null vectors and completing, one obtains a Hilbert space. The vacuum becomes the class of the constant polynomial, and fields act by left multiplication followed by closure/domain control.

This is why the vacuum is not just one state among many in the axiomatic framework. It is the vector from which the theory is generated and against which its correlation functions are measured.

## Cyclicity versus uniqueness

Cyclicity and uniqueness of the vacuum are logically different.

| Property | Statement | What it controls |
|---|---|---|
| Vacuum invariance | $U(a,\Lambda)\Omega=\Omega$ | The vacuum has no spacetime position, frame, or momentum. |
| Cyclicity | $\overline{\mathfrak P(\phi)\Omega}=\mathcal H$ | The fields generate the representation from the vacuum. |
| Uniqueness | The invariant subspace is $\mathbb C\Omega$ | There is only one vacuum vector in this representation. |
| Clustering | Widely separated vacuum correlations factorize | Distant experiments become independent in the vacuum. |

A cyclic vacuum need not be the only invariant vector unless uniqueness is separately assumed or derived. Conversely, a unique invariant vector would not by itself imply that all states are generated by the chosen fields. For example, one could artificially append another Hilbert-space sector on which the fields never act; the vacuum might still be unique in the original sector, but the enlarged theory would not be cyclic.

In ordinary relativistic QFT usage, one usually wants both: a cyclic vacuum representation and, for a pure vacuum phase, a unique translation-invariant vacuum.

## Relation to the Reeh–Schlieder theorem

Global cyclicity is an axiom. The Reeh–Schlieder theorem is a much stronger consequence under Wightman-type hypotheses. Roughly, it says that for any nonempty open region $O$, the vectors produced by field polynomials with test functions supported in $O$ are already dense:

$$
\overline{\mathfrak P(O)\Omega}=\mathcal H.
$$

This is surprising because $O$ can be a small bounded spacetime region. The theorem uses positive energy and analyticity in an essential way; it is not a naive consequence of ordinary quantum mechanics.

The theorem does **not** mean that a laboratory in $O$ can physically create any desired state with finite probability and bounded energy cost. The operators involved may be unbounded, the approximations may be extremely ill-conditioned, and the theorem is about dense subspaces, not operational controllability. It also does not violate microcausality: local observable algebras associated with spacelike separated regions still commute.

For this page, the important lesson is modest. Global cyclicity ensures that all states are generated somewhere by the fields. Reeh–Schlieder says that, under the Wightman hypotheses, even local regions have unexpectedly rich vacuum-generated state spaces.

## Free scalar example

For the free real scalar field, the Hilbert space is bosonic Fock space over the one-particle Hilbert space. The field splits into creation and annihilation parts,

$$
\phi(f)=a^*(f_+)+a(f_+),
$$

where $f_+$ denotes the positive-energy one-particle wavefunction extracted from the test function. Since

$$
a(f_+)\Omega=0,
$$

one has

$$
\phi(f)\Omega=a^*(f_+)\Omega.
$$

As $f$ varies over test functions, the wavefunctions $f_+$ form a dense subspace of the one-particle Hilbert space. Products of fields applied to $\Omega$ produce finite-particle vectors, plus lower-particle terms from commutators. Finite-particle vectors are dense in Fock space, so the free scalar vacuum is cyclic.

This example is useful but potentially misleading. In an interacting Wightman theory, cyclicity is not proved by decomposing fields into creation and annihilation operators. It is imposed or reconstructed as a structural property of the representation.

## Common pitfalls

**Mistaking cyclic for periodic.** A cyclic vector is not a vector that returns to itself after time evolution. It is a vector whose orbit under an operator algebra is dense.

**Forgetting the closure.** The axiom is $\overline{\mathfrak P(\phi)\Omega}=\mathcal H$, not $\mathfrak P(\phi)\Omega=\mathcal H$.

**Confusing global cyclicity with Reeh–Schlieder.** Global cyclicity is part of the Wightman setup. Reeh–Schlieder is a theorem about local algebras under additional analytic input from positive energy.

**Thinking cyclicity proves a particle interpretation.** Cyclicity says states are generated by fields from the vacuum. It does not say the theory has stable one-particle states, asymptotic completeness, or a Fock-space basis.

**Ignoring superselection sectors.** Charged sectors may not be created from the vacuum by neutral local observables. A Wightman field system can include charged fields, but the observable algebra alone may have a cyclic vacuum only for the vacuum sector.

**Treating gauge-fixed vacua as physical without qualification.** Indefinite metric, ghosts, and gauge redundancy can obscure the positive physical Hilbert space. A Wightman vacuum is a vector in a positive Hilbert space, not just a formal perturbative state.

## Research status

Within Wightman theory, vacuum cyclicity is a standard structural axiom. The broad theorem-level picture is settled: cyclicity is needed to avoid invisible Hilbert-space summands and to make vacuum expectation values determine the representation after positivity and completion.

The difficult questions are model-dependent. For many physically important four-dimensional interacting theories, especially non-Abelian gauge theories outside perturbation theory, constructing the physical Hilbert space, proving the existence and uniqueness of the vacuum, and establishing the right cyclicity properties remain part of the larger constructive problem.

In algebraic QFT, the vacuum is usually treated as a state on a net of local algebras, and cyclicity properties become representation-theoretic statements. In gauge theories and theories with generalized symmetries, the distinction between local observables, charged fields, line operators, and sector-generating operators becomes central.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: The generated domain is invariant under fields

Suppose $\mathcal D_0$ is the span of vectors $\phi_{i_1}(f_1)\cdots\phi_{i_n}(f_n)\Omega$. Show formally that $\phi_j(g)\mathcal D_0\subset\mathcal D_0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

It is enough to check a spanning vector. Acting with $\phi_j(g)$ gives

$$
\phi_j(g)\phi_{i_1}(f_1)\cdots\phi_{i_n}(f_n)\Omega,
$$

which is again a finite product of smeared fields applied to $\Omega$. Therefore it lies in the span defining $\mathcal D_0$.

This is only a formal domain argument unless the field system has been defined so that all products act on a common invariant domain. The Wightman setup includes precisely such domain control.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Invisible orthogonal summands

Let $\mathcal K=\overline{\mathcal D_0}$. If $\Psi\in\mathcal K^\perp$, show that

$$
\langle\Psi,A\Omega\rangle=0
$$

for every polynomial $A$ in smeared fields. Explain why such vectors are invisible to vacuum-created states.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

By definition, $A\Omega\in\mathcal D_0\subset\mathcal K$. If $\Psi\in\mathcal K^\perp$, then $\Psi$ is orthogonal to every vector in $\mathcal K$, hence to $A\Omega$:

$$
\langle\Psi,A\Omega\rangle=0.
$$

All matrix elements from the vacuum to polynomial field states miss $\Psi$. If such vectors were present, the Hilbert space would contain directions not generated from the vacuum by the fields. Cyclicity rules this out by requiring $\mathcal K=\mathcal H$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why cyclicity is enough for uniqueness in reconstruction

Assume two Wightman systems have the same vacuum expectation values and both have cyclic vacua. Define a map on polynomial states by

$$
A\Omega\mapsto A\Omega'.
$$

Why is this map expected to extend to a unitary equivalence after quotienting possible null vectors?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The inner product of two polynomial states is determined by vacuum expectation values:

$$
\langle A\Omega,B\Omega\rangle
=
\langle\Omega,A^*B\Omega\rangle.
$$

If the two systems have the same vacuum expectation values, then the proposed map preserves all inner products on polynomial states. Null vectors are mapped to null vectors. After quotienting by null vectors and completing, the map extends by continuity to an isometry between the completed Hilbert spaces.

Cyclicity makes the polynomial states dense in both Hilbert spaces, so the isometry has dense range and is therefore unitary.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Free scalar cyclicity

For the free scalar field, explain why products of smeared fields applied to the Fock vacuum generate a dense subspace of Fock space.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The one-particle vectors have the form $a^*(h)\Omega$, where $h$ lies in the one-particle Hilbert space. For a test function $f$,

$$
\phi(f)\Omega=a^*(f_+)\Omega,
$$

and the set of $f_+$ obtained from test functions is dense in the one-particle space. Products of fields applied to $\Omega$ produce symmetrized products of creation operators, together with lower-particle terms from commutation relations. Hence finite linear combinations of such vectors contain a dense set of finite-particle vectors. Since finite-particle vectors are dense in Fock space, the vacuum is cyclic.

</details>
