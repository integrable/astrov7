## Summary

The **classical master equation** is the defining consistency condition of the BV formalism. If $(F,G)$ is the BV antibracket and $S_{\mathrm{BV}}$ is the BV master action, the equation is

$$
\frac12(S_{\mathrm{BV}},S_{\mathrm{BV}})=0.
$$

The factor $1/2$ is conventional. The content is that the Hamiltonian vector field generated by $S_{\mathrm{BV}}$ is nilpotent:

$$
s_{\mathrm{BV}}F=(S_{\mathrm{BV}},F),
\qquad
s_{\mathrm{BV}}^2=0.
$$

This one equation packages several familiar consistency requirements: the original action is gauge invariant, the Euler–Lagrange equations satisfy Noether identities, the gauge transformations close in the appropriate on-shell or off-shell sense, and higher reducibility relations are coherently encoded.

The equation is **classical** because it is written before regularization of functional integration. Quantum perturbation theory replaces it, when possible, by a renormalized quantum master equation. Failure to solve the quantum equation is the cohomological form of an anomaly.

## Core idea

The classical master equation says that the BV action is compatible with the odd symplectic geometry of field–antifield space. Equivalently, the BV action defines a cohomological vector field.

$$
S_{\mathrm{BV}}
\quad\leadsto\quad
s_{\mathrm{BV}}=(S_{\mathrm{BV}},-)
\quad\leadsto\quad
s_{\mathrm{BV}}^2=0.
$$

The point is not that one equation magically replaces all calculations. The point is that the gauge-theoretic consistency conditions are organized by one graded Hamiltonian equation.

## Setup and conventions

Let $\Phi^A$ denote all fields, ghosts, and possible nonminimal variables, and let $\Phi_A^*$ denote the corresponding antifields. We use the ghost-number convention

$$
\operatorname{gh}(\Phi_A^*)=-1-\operatorname{gh}(\Phi^A).
$$

The BV antibracket has ghost number $+1$:

$$
\operatorname{gh}((F,G))
  =\operatorname{gh}(F)+\operatorname{gh}(G)+1.
$$

The BV action has ghost number zero and even parity:

$$
\operatorname{gh}(S_{\mathrm{BV}})=0,
\qquad
\epsilon(S_{\mathrm{BV}})=0.
$$

Therefore $(S_{\mathrm{BV}},S_{\mathrm{BV}})$ has ghost number $+1$ and odd parity. Setting it to zero is a meaningful homogeneous equation.

For local field theory, all formulas should be read as formulas for local functionals, usually modulo boundary terms. Boundary contributions are not harmless in theories with boundaries, corners, defects, or asymptotic regions; those require BV-BFV refinements and are beyond this page.

## Statement of the equation

A classical BV theory consists, locally and formally, of the following data.

| Data | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $\mathcal F_{\mathrm{BV}}$ | graded field–antifield space |
| $(F,G)$ | odd Poisson bracket of ghost number $+1$ |
| $S_{\mathrm{BV}}$ | even ghost-number-zero master action |
| $\frac12(S_{\mathrm{BV}},S_{\mathrm{BV}})=0$ | classical master equation |

The classical BV differential is

$$
s_{\mathrm{BV}}=(S_{\mathrm{BV}},-).
$$

Using the graded Jacobi identity, one obtains

$$
s_{\mathrm{BV}}^2F
=(S_{\mathrm{BV}},(S_{\mathrm{BV}},F))
=\frac12((S_{\mathrm{BV}},S_{\mathrm{BV}}),F).
$$

Thus the classical master equation implies

$$
s_{\mathrm{BV}}^2=0.
$$

Conversely, if the antibracket is nondegenerate and constants are ignored in the usual way, nilpotence of the Hamiltonian vector field is equivalent to the master equation up to a constant obstruction of the appropriate degree, which is absent in the standard local setting.

## Expansion by antifield number

The master action is often expanded by antifield number:

$$
S_{\mathrm{BV}}
=S_0+S_1+S_2+\cdots .
$$

Here $S_0$ is the original classical action. The term $S_1$ records infinitesimal gauge transformations. In a simple irreducible theory,

$$
S_1=\int_M \phi_i^*R^i_\alpha(\phi)c^\alpha.
$$

The term $S_2$ records the gauge algebra. For a closed algebra with structure functions $f^\gamma_{\alpha\beta}$, one has a term of the form

$$
S_2
=-\frac12\int_M c^*_\gamma
  f^\gamma_{\alpha\beta}(\phi)
  c^\alpha c^\beta
+\cdots .
$$

The dots may include terms quadratic in the original antifields if the algebra closes only modulo equations of motion. Higher terms encode higher coherence data.

Plugging $S_0+S_1+S_2+\cdots$ into the master equation sorts the gauge theory into layers.

## Layer 1: gauge invariance and Noether identities

The first nontrivial condition is

$$
(S_0,S_1)=0.
$$

Since $S_0$ depends only on the original fields, this condition says schematically

$$
\int_M
\frac{\delta S_0}{\delta\phi^i}
R^i_\alpha(\phi)c^\alpha
=0.
$$

Because the ghosts $c^\alpha$ are arbitrary, this is the Noether identity

$$
R^{\dagger i}_\alpha(\phi)
\frac{\delta S_0}{\delta\phi^i}=0,
$$

after integrating by parts. Thus the master equation knows that the action is gauge invariant and that the Euler–Lagrange equations are not independent.

This is the same content as the gauge-redundancy page, but expressed in the antifield language.

## Layer 2: closure of the gauge algebra

The next layer involves

$$
(S_1,S_1)+2(S_0,S_2)=0.
$$

This equation says that the commutator of two gauge transformations is again a gauge transformation, possibly only modulo the equations of motion. In components, one often encounters a relation of the schematic form

$$
R^j_\beta\partial_j R^i_\alpha
-
R^j_\alpha\partial_j R^i_\beta
=
R^i_\gamma f^\gamma_{\alpha\beta}
+
E_j(S_0)M^{ji}_{\alpha\beta}.
$$

The first term on the right is ordinary closure. The second term is proportional to the equations of motion. If it is present, the algebra is called **open**. BV handles this naturally because the missing closure is supplied by antifield-dependent terms.

This is one of the main reasons BV is superior to a naive BRST rule. A BRST operator written only on fields and ghosts may fail to square to zero off shell. The full BV differential still squares to zero on the enlarged space.

## Layer 3: Jacobi and higher coherence

At the next layers, the master equation imposes Jacobi-type identities and their higher analogues. For a gauge algebra with constant structure constants, this reduces to the ordinary Jacobi identity

$$
f^\delta_{\alpha[\beta}f^\epsilon_{\gamma]\delta}=0.
$$

For field-dependent or open algebras, the Jacobi identity holds only after adding further terms involving antifields and possibly ghosts for ghosts. The BV action packages these higher identities in a single equation.

A useful modern interpretation is that a classical BV theory encodes an $L_\infty$-type structure on fields, ghosts, and equations of motion. This language is not needed for basic Yang–Mills, but it becomes natural in deformation theory, topological theories, and derived geometry.

## Closed irreducible example

For a closed irreducible gauge algebra, the minimal master action has the schematic form

$$
S_{\mathrm{BV}}
=
S_0[\phi]
+
\int_M \phi_i^*R^i_\alpha(\phi)c^\alpha
-
\frac12\int_M c^*_\gamma
f^\gamma_{\alpha\beta}(\phi)c^\alpha c^\beta
+\cdots .
$$

If the $f^\gamma_{\alpha\beta}$ are constant and the algebra closes off shell, the dots may vanish. Then the classical master equation reduces to three familiar facts.

| Part of the CME | Familiar content |
|---|---|
| $(S_0,S_1)=0$ | $S_0$ is gauge invariant. |
| $(S_1,S_1)+2(S_0,S_2)=0$ | Gauge transformations close. |
| Higher ghost terms | Jacobi identity and representation compatibility. |

For field-dependent or open algebras, the same table remains morally correct, but the equations include extra terms and higher identities.

## Maxwell example

For Maxwell theory,

$$
S_0[A]
=-\frac14\int_M d^dx\,F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu},
$$

with gauge transformation

$$
sA_\mu=\partial_\mu c,
\qquad
sc=0.
$$

The minimal BV action is

$$
S_{\mathrm{BV}}
=S_0[A]
+
\int_M d^dx\,A^{*\mu}\partial_\mu c.
$$

The only nontrivial part of the master equation is

$$
(S_0,\int A^*\partial c)=0.
$$

This is

$$
\int_M d^dx\,
\frac{\delta S_0}{\delta A_\mu}
\partial_\mu c=0.
$$

After integration by parts, it becomes

$$
-\int_M d^dx\,
c\,\partial_\mu
\frac{\delta S_0}{\delta A_\mu}=0.
$$

The identity holds because

$$
\frac{\delta S_0}{\delta A_\mu}
=\partial_\nu F^{\nu\mu},
$$

and

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu F^{\nu\mu}=0
$$

by antisymmetry of $F^{\nu\mu}$. Thus the Maxwell master equation is the cohomological form of the elementary identity behind gauge invariance.

## Yang–Mills example

For Yang–Mills theory, the minimal BV action can be written

$$
S_{\mathrm{BV}}
=
S_{\mathrm{YM}}
+
\int_M d^dx\,\operatorname{tr}
\left(
A^{*\mu}D_\mu c
-
\frac12 c^*[c,c]
\right).
$$

The master equation implies the BRST rules

$$
sA_\mu=D_\mu c,
\qquad
sc=-\frac12[c,c],
$$

and their nilpotence. The calculation uses the Bianchi identity, the gauge covariance of the Euler–Lagrange equations, and the Jacobi identity in the Lie algebra. The benefit of BV is not that this example is impossible without it; the benefit is that the same equation continues to work when the gauge algebra is more complicated.

## Consistent deformations

The classical master equation is also a deformation equation. Suppose one has a free or simpler BV theory with action $S$ and wants to deform it:

$$
S_g=S+gS_1+g^2S_2+\cdots .
$$

The deformed action must satisfy

$$
(S_g,S_g)=0.
$$

Expanding in $g$ gives first

$$
(S,S)=0,
$$

then

$$
(S,S_1)=0,
$$

and then

$$
(S,S_2)=-\frac12(S_1,S_1).
$$

Thus the first-order deformation $S_1$ must be a cocycle for the original BV differential

$$
s=(S,-).
$$

Two first-order deformations differing by an exact term are related by a field redefinition or canonical transformation. Obstructions to extending a first-order deformation to higher order live in the next cohomology group.

This is the rigorous form of a powerful physics statement: consistent interactions of gauge fields are strongly constrained by cohomology.

## Relation to the quantum master equation

The classical master equation is not the final word in quantum field theory. Formally, the quantum BV equation has the shape

$$
\frac12(S,S)-i\hbar\Delta S=0,
$$

or, in Euclidean conventions and after changing factors of $i$,

$$
\frac12(S,S)+\hbar\Delta S=0.
$$

Here $\Delta$ is a second-order BV operator that represents the divergence of the Hamiltonian vector field with respect to a measure. In infinite-dimensional QFT, $\Delta S$ is singular and must be replaced by a renormalized construction.

The failure to solve the quantum master equation after renormalization is an anomaly. Cohomologically, an anomaly is a degree-one obstruction class. This is why anomaly calculations are naturally phrased in BRST/BV language.

## This is the most important part of this page

The classical master equation is the statement that the classical gauge theory has been encoded as a nilpotent Hamiltonian vector field on the BV space. Its first layer is gauge invariance, but its full content is larger: it also contains Noether identities, closure, Jacobi identities, reducibility, and open-algebra corrections.

When you see

$$
\frac12(S_{\mathrm{BV}},S_{\mathrm{BV}})=0,
$$

read it as

$$
\text{equations of motion}
+\text{gauge redundancy}
+\text{higher identities}
=\text{one coherent differential}.
$$

This is why the equation is the natural starting point for gauge fixing, consistent deformations, local BRST cohomology, and the later quantum master equation.

## Common pitfalls

**The master equation is one more equation of motion.** It is not an equation for physical fields. It is a consistency equation for the master action as a functional on field–antifield space.

**The term $(S,S)$ is automatically zero because the bracket is antisymmetric.** The BV antibracket is odd. For an even functional $S$, the bracket $(S,S)$ need not vanish automatically.

**The CME only says the original action is gauge invariant.** Gauge invariance is only the first layer. The same equation also encodes closure, Jacobi identities, reducibility, and open-algebra data.

**The quantum master equation is obtained by just adding $\Delta S$.** In finite dimensions this slogan is useful. In QFT the BV Laplacian is ultraviolet-singular and must be interpreted through regularization and renormalization.

**Gauge fixing should be imposed before writing the CME.** The master equation is normally written before gauge fixing. Gauge fixing is a later choice of Lagrangian submanifold.

## Research status

The classical master equation is a standard and settled part of the local algebraic theory of gauge systems. It is the natural language for perturbative gauge theory, deformation theory, topological field theories, and modern derived formulations of classical field theory.

The active questions concern global and analytic refinements. On manifolds with boundary, the correct compatibility condition is not merely BV but BV-BFV, where boundary phase spaces and boundary charges must be included. In infinite-dimensional QFT, the formal odd symplectic geometry must be combined with renormalization. In nonperturbative gauge theory, the master equation is an organizing principle but not a complete existence theorem.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: nilpotence from the master equation

Assume the antibracket satisfies the graded Jacobi identity and define $sF=(S,F)$, where $S$ is even. Show that $(S,S)=0$ implies $s^2F=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Using the graded Jacobi identity with $S,S,F$, one obtains

$$
(S,(S,F))
=\frac12((S,S),F).
$$

Therefore

$$
s^2F=(S,(S,F))
=\frac12((S,S),F).
$$

If $(S,S)=0$, then $s^2F=0$ for every functional $F$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: the Maxwell master equation

For

$$
S_{\mathrm{BV}}
=-\frac14\int F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}
+
\int A^{*\mu}\partial_\mu c,
$$

show that the only nontrivial part of $(S_{\mathrm{BV}},S_{\mathrm{BV}})=0$ reduces to

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu F^{\nu\mu}=0.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The antifield term has no $c^*$ term because $sc=0$ for the Abelian gauge algebra. The nontrivial bracket is between the classical action and the antifield term:

$$
\left(S_0,\int A^{*\mu}\partial_\mu c\right)
=
\int
\frac{\delta S_0}{\delta A_\mu}
\partial_\mu c.
$$

The Euler–Lagrange expression is

$$
\frac{\delta S_0}{\delta A_\mu}
=\partial_\nu F^{\nu\mu}.
$$

After integrating by parts,

$$
\int \partial_\nu F^{\nu\mu}\partial_\mu c
=
-\int c\,\partial_\mu\partial_\nu F^{\nu\mu}.
$$

Since $F^{\nu\mu}=-F^{\mu\nu}$ while $\partial_\mu\partial_\nu$ is symmetric, the contraction vanishes:

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu F^{\nu\mu}=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: first-order deformations

Let $S_g=S+gS_1+O(g^2)$ and suppose $(S_g,S_g)=0$. Show that $S_1$ is closed under the original BV differential $s=(S,-)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Expand the master equation:

$$
0=(S_g,S_g)
=(S,S)+2g(S,S_1)+O(g^2).
$$

The undeformed theory satisfies $(S,S)=0$. Therefore the coefficient of $g$ must vanish:

$$
(S,S_1)=0.
$$

Since $s=(S,-)$, this is

$$
sS_1=0.
$$

Thus first-order consistent deformations are BV cohomology classes of ghost number zero, modulo exact terms corresponding to field redefinitions or canonical transformations.

</details>
