## Summary

The BRST complex is a cohomological replacement for the infinitesimal gauge quotient. Instead of literally dividing a field space $\Phi$ by a gauge group $\mathcal G$, one enlarges the algebra of functions on fields by adding ghost variables and equips it with an odd differential

$$
s^2=0,
\qquad
\operatorname{gh}(s)=+1.
$$

For a simple Yang–Mills connection $A$ and ghost $c$, the classical BRST differential is

$$
sA_\mu=D_\mu c,
\qquad
sc=-\frac12[c,c].
$$

Gauge-invariant classical observables are represented by degree-zero BRST cohomology classes:

$$
\text{observables}
\approx H^0(s).
$$

This page defines the BRST complex, explains why ghosts appear, gives the Yang–Mills example, introduces gauge-fixing doublets, and states the main limitations that motivate the BV formalism.

## Core idea

The BRST complex replaces the sentence “work on the quotient by gauge transformations” with a differential graded algebra. Gauge parameters become odd ghost fields. Gauge invariance becomes BRST closedness. Pure gauge changes become BRST exact terms.

The basic dictionary is:

| Quotient language | BRST language |
|---|---|
| gauge parameter $\epsilon$ | ghost $c$ with ghost number $+1$ |
| infinitesimal gauge transformation | BRST variation $s\phi$ |
| gauge-invariant function | $sO=0$ at ghost number $0$ |
| physically trivial representative change | $O\sim O+sK$ |
| gauge algebra closure | nilpotence $s^2=0$ |
| anomaly candidate | ghost-number-one cohomology class |

The cohomology is not a decorative reformulation. It is what lets gauge theory survive covariant perturbation theory, renormalization, and comparison between gauges.

## Setup and conventions

We use ghost number $\operatorname{gh}$ and Grassmann parity $|\cdot|$. The BRST differential $s$ is odd and raises ghost number by one:

$$
|s|=1,
\qquad
\operatorname{gh}(s)=+1.
$$

It acts as a graded derivation:

$$
s(XY)=(sX)Y+(-1)^{|X|}X(sY).
$$

A minimal BRST complex for a closed irreducible gauge theory contains:

| Variable | Meaning | Ghost number | Parity |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| $\phi^i$ | original fields | $0$ | original parity |
| $c^\alpha$ | ghosts replacing gauge parameters | $+1$ | opposite to parameter parity |
| derivatives of fields and ghosts | local jet variables | inherited | inherited |

For gauge fixing, one often adds a nonminimal pair:

| Variable | Meaning | Ghost number | Parity |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| $\bar c_\alpha$ | antighost | $-1$ | odd for bosonic gauge symmetry |
| $b_\alpha$ | Nakanishi–Lautrup field | $0$ | even for bosonic gauge symmetry |

The sign conventions below use a left action of a Lie algebra on fields. Other sources may differ by signs in $sA$ or $s\psi$; nilpotence and cohomology are the invariant content.

## Definition

A BRST complex is a differential graded algebra

$$
(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{BRST}},s)
$$

where $\mathcal F_{\mathrm{BRST}}$ is generated by fields, ghosts, and their derivatives, and $s$ is an odd derivation satisfying

$$
s^2=0.
$$

The BRST cohomology at ghost number $k$ is

$$
H^k(s)=
\frac{\ker(s:\mathcal F^k\to\mathcal F^{k+1})}
{\operatorname{im}(s:\mathcal F^{k-1}\to\mathcal F^k)}.
$$

At the classical level, $H^0(s)$ is the first cohomological model for gauge-invariant observables. In local field theory one often needs the local cohomology modulo total derivatives,

$$
H^k(s\mid d),
$$

because actions, currents, counterterms, and anomalies are integrated local forms and are defined up to total derivatives.

## Yang–Mills example

Let $A_\mu=A_\mu^aT_a$ be a connection and let $c=c^aT_a$ be an odd Lie-algebra-valued ghost. The BRST differential is

$$
sA_\mu=D_\mu c
=\partial_\mu c+[A_\mu,c],
\qquad
sc=-\frac12[c,c].
$$

The field strength transforms covariantly:

$$
sF_{\mu\nu}=[F_{\mu\nu},c].
$$

If $\psi$ is a matter field in a representation where an infinitesimal gauge transformation acts as $\delta_\epsilon\psi=-\epsilon\psi$, then

$$
s\psi=-c\psi.
$$

The classical Yang–Mills action

$$
S_{\mathrm{YM}}
=-\frac{1}{4g^2}\int d^dx\,\operatorname{tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu})
$$

is BRST closed because the trace of a commutator vanishes:

$$
sS_{\mathrm{YM}}=0.
$$

Gauge-invariant polynomials such as $\operatorname{tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu})$ are BRST closed. They represent degree-zero cohomology classes unless they are also BRST exact.

## Why nilpotence encodes the gauge algebra

For ordinary Yang–Mills theory, nilpotence follows from the closure of the Lie algebra and the Jacobi identity. Schematically,

$$
s^2A_\mu
= D_\mu(sc)+[sA_\mu,c]
= -\frac12D_\mu[c,c]+[D_\mu c,c]=0,
$$

with graded signs understood. Similarly,

$$
s^2c
=-\frac12s[c,c]=0
$$

by the graded Jacobi identity.

This is the first deep point: the ghost transformation is not arbitrary. The formula $sc=-\frac12[c,c]$ is precisely what makes the gauge algebra into a differential.

For an Abelian gauge theory, the bracket vanishes, so

$$
sA_\mu=\partial_\mu c,
\qquad
sc=0.
$$

The Abelian BRST complex is therefore much simpler, but it still encodes the quotient by $A\sim A+d\epsilon$.

## Gauge fixing and BRST doublets

To implement a covariant gauge in perturbation theory, one adds antighosts and Nakanishi–Lautrup fields with

$$
s\bar c=b,
\qquad
sb=0.
$$

The pair $(\bar c,b)$ is a BRST doublet. It should not change the physical cohomology. It is introduced to choose a convenient representative of the gauge quotient.

A gauge-fixing fermion $\Psi$ has ghost number $-1$. The gauge-fixed action has the schematic form

$$
S_{\mathrm{gf}}=S_0+s\Psi.
$$

For a Lorenz-type gauge in Yang–Mills theory, one may choose

$$
\Psi=\int d^dx\,\operatorname{tr}
\left(\bar c\left(\partial^\mu A_\mu+\frac{\xi}{2} b\right)\right).
$$

Then $s\Psi$ produces the gauge-fixing term, the $b$-field term, and the ghost kinetic interaction. After eliminating $b$, one obtains the familiar quadratic gauge-fixing term proportional to $(\partial^\mu A_\mu)^2$ and a Faddeev–Popov ghost term.

The important statement is not the exact sign of every term. It is that changing the gauge-fixing fermion changes the representative, while BRST cohomology is intended to keep gauge-invariant quantities unchanged.

:::note[BRST doublets]
A pair $u,v$ with $su=v$ and $sv=0$ is cohomologically trivial under standard hypotheses. This is why antighosts and $b$-fields can be used for gauge fixing without adding physical observables.
:::

## Physical observables as cohomology

At the first classical level, an observable is BRST closed at ghost number zero:

$$
sO=0,
\qquad
\operatorname{gh}(O)=0.
$$

Two observables differing by an exact term are identified:

$$
O\sim O+sK.
$$

Thus the candidate physical observables are classes in $H^0(s)$. In local field theory, the sharper statement often uses local forms and total derivatives:

$$
s\omega+d\eta=0,
\qquad
\omega\sim \omega+s\alpha+d\beta.
$$

This is the origin of $H^0(s\mid d)$ for local observables, counterterms, and action deformations. Ghost number one cohomology controls candidate anomalies. Negative ghost numbers become meaningful in the antifield formalism, where conservation laws and Noether identities are also represented cohomologically.

## What the BRST complex does not yet solve

The minimal BRST complex is excellent for closed irreducible gauge algebras. It is not the final language for every gauge theory.

A gauge theory may be:

| Situation | Why ordinary BRST is not enough |
|---|---|
| open algebra | gauge transformations close only on shell |
| reducible symmetry | there are relations among gauge transformations |
| field-dependent structure functions | the “structure constants” depend on fields |
| boundary-sensitive theory | gauge transformations may carry boundary charges |
| anomalous quantum theory | the quantum measure or renormalization may fail to preserve $s$ |

The BV formalism was designed to handle these issues systematically. It adds antifields, an odd symplectic structure, and a master equation. The BRST differential then becomes part of a larger cohomological resolution.

## This is the most important part of this page

The BRST complex is the algebraic shadow of the gauge quotient. The ghost is not a mysterious extra particle; it is the gauge parameter turned into an odd coordinate. The nilpotent differential $s$ is not an optional symmetry imposed after quantization; it is the cohomological encoding of gauge redundancy.

For a simple gauge theory, the logic is:

$$
\text{gauge orbits}
\longrightarrow
\text{ghosts}
\longrightarrow
s^2=0
\longrightarrow
H^0(s)
\longrightarrow
\text{physical observables}.
$$

Gauge fixing adds variables, but only in cohomologically trivial pairs. Anomalies and BV obstructions are meaningful because they are failures of this cohomological structure at the next level.

## Common pitfalls

**Saying BRST is just a symmetry.** BRST behaves like a symmetry of the gauge-fixed action, but its deeper role is to encode gauge redundancy cohomologically.

**Forgetting ghost number.** Cohomology without grading loses the meaning of observables, gauge parameters, counterterms, and anomalies. Ghost number zero, one, and negative ghost numbers play different roles.

**Treating $sO=0$ as the whole story for local actions.** Local Lagrangians and currents are defined up to total derivatives. The relevant object is often $H^k(s\mid d)$ rather than plain $H^k(s)$.

**Believing gauge fixing changes physical cohomology.** Properly introduced antighosts and $b$-fields form BRST doublets and should not add physical classes.

**Using the simple Yang–Mills formulas for every gauge theory.** Gravity, higher-form gauge fields, reducible theories, and theories with open algebras require BV refinements.

## Research status

The BRST complex for ordinary Yang–Mills theory is standard, and its cohomology is a central tool in perturbative gauge theory, algebraic renormalization, and anomaly classification. The local BRST cohomology of many standard gauge systems is well understood.

The simple BRST complex is not a universal nonperturbative definition of gauge theory. It must be refined for open or reducible gauge symmetries, for boundary-sensitive systems, and for quantum anomalies. These refinements are precisely why the BV formalism and the classical and quantum master equations are the next pages in this chapter.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Abelian nilpotence

For Abelian gauge theory, let $sA_\mu=\partial_\mu c$ and $sc=0$. Show that $s^2A_\mu=0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Apply $s$ once more:

$$
s^2A_\mu=s(\partial_\mu c)=\partial_\mu(sc)=0.
$$

The Abelian bracket vanishes, so no ghost self-interaction is needed.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Covariant transformation of the field strength

Using $sA_\mu=D_\mu c$, show that $sF_{\mu\nu}=[F_{\mu\nu},c]$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The curvature varies under a connection variation as

$$
\delta F_{\mu\nu}=D_\mu\delta A_\nu-D_\nu\delta A_\mu.
$$

With $\delta A_\mu=sA_\mu=D_\mu c$,

$$
sF_{\mu\nu}=[D_\mu,D_\nu]c=[F_{\mu\nu},c].
$$

This is the infinitesimal statement that curvature transforms covariantly under gauge transformations.

</details>

### Exercise 3: BRST invariance of Yang–Mills action

Use $sF_{\mu\nu}=[F_{\mu\nu},c]$ to show that $s\int\operatorname{tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu})=0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
s\operatorname{tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu})
=2\operatorname{tr}([F_{\mu\nu},c]F^{\mu\nu}).
$$

By cyclicity of the trace, $\operatorname{tr}([F_{\mu\nu},c]F^{\mu\nu})=0$. Therefore the integrated action is BRST closed, up to the usual boundary assumptions.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Doublet cohomology intuition

Let $su=v$ and $sv=0$. Why should expressions depending only on the doublet variables not create new physical cohomology classes?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The pair is contractible: $v$ is exact because $v=su$, and $u$ is paired with $v$ rather than being closed by itself. Under standard polynomial or local assumptions one constructs a homotopy that removes dependence on $u$ and $v$ from cohomology representatives. This is the algebraic reason antighosts and $b$-fields do not add physical observables.

</details>
