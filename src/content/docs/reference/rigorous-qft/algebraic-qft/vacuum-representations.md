## Summary

A **vacuum representation** is a Hilbert-space realization of an algebraic QFT in which the theory has a Poincaré-invariant positive-energy vacuum vector. Starting from an abstract quasi-local algebra $\mathcal A$, a state is a positive normalized linear functional

$$
\omega:\mathcal A\to\mathbb C,
\qquad
\omega(\mathbf 1)=1,
\qquad
\omega(A^*A)\ge 0.
$$

A vacuum state on Minkowski spacetime is, roughly, a state invariant under spacetime symmetries and whose GNS representation carries translations with spectrum in the closed forward light cone. The GNS construction produces

$$
(\mathcal H_0,\pi_0,\Omega_0),
\qquad
\omega_0(A)=\langle\Omega_0,\pi_0(A)\Omega_0\rangle.
$$

The local $C^*$-algebras then become concrete operator algebras on $\mathcal H_0$, and one often passes to local von Neumann algebras

$$
\mathcal M_0(\mathcal O)=\pi_0(\mathcal A(\mathcal O))''.
$$

The key point is that the algebraic theory does not start with one privileged Hilbert space. The Hilbert space appears after choosing a state. The vacuum representation is the special representation selected by the empty, translation-invariant, positive-energy state when such a state exists.

## Core idea

In ordinary quantum mechanics, one usually starts with a Hilbert space and then studies states as vectors or density matrices on that Hilbert space. Algebraic QFT reverses this order. It starts with an algebra of observables and treats a state as a rule for assigning expectation values.

This reversal is not just formal elegance. In QFT, different physical situations can require inequivalent Hilbert-space representations of the same observable algebra. The vacuum, thermal equilibrium states, charged sectors, and infinite-volume phases need not all live naturally in one common Hilbert space.

The vacuum representation is therefore a **chosen realization** of the abstract algebra, not the algebraic theory itself. It is the realization appropriate to the Minkowski vacuum sector.

## Setup and conventions

Let

$$
\mathcal O\longmapsto\mathcal A(\mathcal O)
$$

be a Haag–Kastler net on Minkowski spacetime, and let $\mathcal A$ be the quasi-local $C^*$-algebra generated by the local algebras. Suppose the Poincaré group acts by automorphisms

$$
\alpha_g:\mathcal A\to\mathcal A,
\qquad
\alpha_g(\mathcal A(\mathcal O))=\mathcal A(g\mathcal O).
$$

A state is a positive normalized linear functional $\omega$ on $\mathcal A$. Its expectation value on an observable $A$ is written $\omega(A)$.

The GNS construction associates to every state $\omega$ a Hilbert space $\mathcal H_\omega$, a representation $\pi_\omega:\mathcal A\to B(\mathcal H_\omega)$, and a cyclic vector $\Omega_\omega$ such that

$$
\omega(A)=
\langle\Omega_\omega,\pi_\omega(A)\Omega_\omega\rangle.
$$

Cyclic means

$$
\overline{\pi_\omega(\mathcal A)\Omega_\omega}=\mathcal H_\omega.
$$

The GNS construction is unique up to unitary equivalence for the chosen state.

## Vacuum states

A vacuum state is usually required to satisfy a package of conditions adapted to Minkowski spacetime. Presentations differ slightly, but the standard physical content is the following.

First, the state is invariant under spacetime symmetries, or at least under translations:

$$
\omega_0(\alpha_g(A))=\omega_0(A)
\qquad
(A\in\mathcal A).
$$

Second, in the GNS representation, the symmetry action is implemented by a strongly continuous unitary representation $U_0(g)$ satisfying

$$
U_0(g)\,\pi_0(A)\,U_0(g)^{-1}
=
\pi_0(\alpha_g(A)),
$$

and

$$
U_0(g)\Omega_0=\Omega_0.
$$

Third, the translation subgroup has self-adjoint generators $P^\mu$ with joint spectrum contained in the closed forward light cone:

$$
\operatorname{sp}(P)
\subset
\overline V_+.
$$

This is the algebraic counterpart of the Wightman spectral condition. It says that the vacuum representation is a positive-energy representation.

Some treatments also include uniqueness of the invariant vacuum vector, irreducibility of the quasi-local representation, or cyclicity of the vacuum for the global algebra. These are important but should not be silently confused with the minimal positivity and invariance requirements.

## The GNS construction in this setting

For a state $\omega_0$, define the null left ideal

$$
\mathcal N_{\omega_0}
=
\{A\in\mathcal A:\omega_0(A^*A)=0\}.
$$

The dense GNS pre-Hilbert space is the quotient

$$
\mathcal A/\mathcal N_{\omega_0},
$$

with inner product

$$
\langle[A],[B]\rangle
=
\omega_0(A^*B).
$$

The representation is left multiplication:

$$
\pi_0(C)[A]=[CA].
$$

The vector $\Omega_0=[\mathbf 1]$ represents the state because

$$
\langle\Omega_0,\pi_0(A)\Omega_0\rangle
=
\omega_0(A).
$$

For an invariant state, the symmetry automorphisms act on equivalence classes by

$$
U_0(g)[A]=[\alpha_g(A)],
$$

provided the required continuity and domain conditions are satisfied. This gives the unitary implementation of covariance in the vacuum representation.

This construction is one reason algebraic QFT is conceptually clean: the Hilbert space is not guessed. It is reconstructed from expectation values.

## Local von Neumann algebras

The abstract local algebras $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ are often $C^*$-algebras. In a representation they become concrete operator algebras

$$
\pi_0(\mathcal A(\mathcal O))\subset B(\mathcal H_0).
$$

For many structural theorems one passes to the weak closure, equivalently the double commutant,

$$
\mathcal M_0(\mathcal O)
=
\pi_0(\mathcal A(\mathcal O))''.
$$

The resulting net

$$
\mathcal O\longmapsto\mathcal M_0(\mathcal O)
$$

is a net of von Neumann algebras in the vacuum representation. Modular theory, the Reeh–Schlieder theorem, Haag duality, the split property, and type III phenomena are naturally formulated at this level.

The double commutant is not an arbitrary enlargement. It records the operational closure appropriate to a chosen Hilbert-space representation: limits of bounded observables in the weak operator topology are included.

## Reeh–Schlieder as the first surprise

Under standard relativistic assumptions, the vacuum is cyclic for local algebras associated with suitable open regions:

$$
\overline{\mathcal M_0(\mathcal O)\Omega_0}
=
\mathcal H_0.
$$

In many settings it is also separating for local algebras when the causal complement is nonempty: if

$$
A\Omega_0=0,
\qquad A\in\mathcal M_0(\mathcal O),
$$

then $A=0$.

This is the Reeh–Schlieder phenomenon. It does **not** mean that a laboratory in $\mathcal O$ can physically create an arbitrary distant particle with finite resources. It says that the set of vectors obtained by applying bounded local operators to the vacuum is dense in Hilbert space. The approximation may require operators with enormous norm and has no simple nonrelativistic localization interpretation.

The theorem is nevertheless one of the clearest warnings against importing finite-dimensional tensor-factor intuition into continuum QFT. The vacuum is profoundly entangled across spacetime regions, and local von Neumann algebras behave very differently from matrix algebras assigned to lattice sites.

## Vacuum sector versus other sectors

A vacuum representation describes the vacuum sector. Other states can give inequivalent representations.

A thermal equilibrium state may be invariant under time translations and satisfy a KMS condition rather than being represented by a vacuum vector. Charged sectors may be represented by states that are not obtainable by acting with strictly local observables on the vacuum. Infinite systems can have distinct phases whose representations are not unitarily equivalent.

This is one of the main reasons the algebraic approach is useful. The same quasi-local algebra can organize multiple physically meaningful representations. Instead of pretending that every state is a vector in one universal Hilbert space, algebraic QFT treats representations as part of the physical analysis.

## Checks

To check that a proposed state is a vacuum state, proceed in layers.

First, verify positivity and normalization:

$$
\omega_0(A^*A)\ge 0,
\qquad
\omega_0(\mathbf 1)=1.
$$

Second, verify invariance under the appropriate spacetime automorphisms:

$$
\omega_0\circ\alpha_g=\omega_0.
$$

Third, construct or identify the GNS representation and check that the symmetry is unitarily implemented. Strong continuity matters because it gives self-adjoint generators for translations.

Fourth, check the spectrum condition:

$$
\operatorname{sp}(P)
\subset
\overline V_+.
$$

Finally, state clearly which extra conditions are being assumed: uniqueness of the vacuum, irreducibility, local normality, Haag duality, additivity, or the split property.

## Common pitfalls

**Thinking the vacuum is part of the abstract algebra alone.** The algebra gives observables. A vacuum is a state on that algebra, usually selected using dynamics and spacetime symmetry.

**Treating all representations as equivalent.** In finite-dimensional quantum mechanics, irreducible representations are often essentially unique. In QFT, inequivalent representations are common and physically meaningful.

**Confusing GNS cyclicity with local cyclicity.** The GNS vector is cyclic for the represented global algebra by construction. Reeh–Schlieder local cyclicity is a much stronger theorem that uses locality, spectrum, and analyticity-type input.

**Reading Reeh–Schlieder as faster-than-light control.** Density of local excitations in Hilbert space is not the same as an operational protocol for sending signals or creating arbitrary distant particles with bounded resources.

**Assuming uniqueness of the vacuum without checking it.** Many theorems assume a unique translation-invariant vacuum, but uniqueness can fail in theories with spontaneous symmetry breaking or multiple phases.

## Research status

Vacuum representations are established parts of algebraic QFT on Minkowski spacetime. The GNS construction is standard operator-algebraic mathematics, and the positive-energy vacuum representation is the natural algebraic analogue of the Hilbert-space vacuum sector in Wightman QFT.

The active research questions concern physically preferred representations in broader settings: thermal and nonequilibrium states, charged sectors, gauge theories, curved spacetimes without Poincaré symmetry, and interacting models whose existence is itself difficult. In those settings one often replaces the unique vacuum idea by state-selection criteria appropriate to the background and problem.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: The GNS null space is invisible to the state

Let $A\in\mathcal N_{\omega}$, so $\omega(A^*A)=0$. Explain why $[A]=0$ in the GNS Hilbert space.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The GNS pre-Hilbert space is the quotient $\mathcal A/\mathcal N_\omega$. Elements of $\mathcal N_\omega$ are identified with zero by definition. The condition $\omega(A^*A)=0$ says precisely that the vector represented by $A$ has zero norm in the pre-inner product, so it must be quotiented out to obtain a genuine Hilbert space.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Invariance gives an invariant cyclic vector

Suppose $\omega_0\circ\alpha_g=\omega_0$ and define $U_0(g)[A]=[\alpha_g(A)]$ on GNS equivalence classes. Show formally that $U_0(g)\Omega_0=\Omega_0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The GNS vacuum vector is $\Omega_0=[\mathbf 1]$. Since automorphisms preserve the identity,

$$
U_0(g)\Omega_0
=U_0(g)[\mathbf 1]
=[\alpha_g(\mathbf 1)]
=[\mathbf 1]
=\Omega_0.
$$

The invariance of the state is needed to ensure that $U_0(g)$ is well-defined and isometric on the quotient.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why positive energy belongs to the representation

Why is the spectrum condition naturally stated in the GNS representation rather than directly as a property of the abstract algebra?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The spectrum condition refers to self-adjoint generators $P^\mu$ of spacetime translations. These generators arise from a strongly continuous unitary representation of translations on a Hilbert space. The abstract $C^*$-algebra may carry translation automorphisms, but self-adjoint energy–momentum operators live in a chosen Hilbert-space representation. Therefore the spectrum condition is naturally a property of the covariant representation selected by the state.

</details>
