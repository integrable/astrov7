## Summary

**Haag duality** is the statement that the algebra of observables localized in a region is exactly the commutant of the algebra localized in the causal complement. If $\mathcal O'$ denotes the spacelike complement of $\mathcal O$, locality always gives the inclusion

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)
\subset
\mathcal A(\mathcal O')'.
$$

Haag duality asks for equality:

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)
=
\mathcal A(\mathcal O')'.
$$

In a Hilbert-space representation one usually states this for von Neumann algebras,

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)
=
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')',
\qquad
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)=\mathcal A(\mathcal O)''.
$$

The meaning is simple but powerful: every bounded operator that commutes with all observables outside $\mathcal O$ is already generated by observables inside $\mathcal O$. Locality says outside observables cannot detect inside measurements by commutators. Haag duality says there are no additional invisible observables hiding in the commutant.

Haag duality is therefore a **completeness condition for local observables**. It is stronger than locality, related to modular theory, and crucial in the Doplicher–Haag–Roberts analysis of superselection sectors. Its failure is often physically informative: it may signal missing charged fields, gauge constraints, topological operators, nontrivial spacetime topology, or an overly small observable net.

The page uses two different primes. For a spacetime region, $\mathcal O'$ means the causal complement. For an algebra, $\mathcal M'$ means the operator commutant. Haag duality combines both meanings:

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)=\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'.
$$

## Core idea

In algebraic QFT, a local net assigns algebras to regions:

$$
\mathcal O\longmapsto\mathcal A(\mathcal O).
$$

The locality axiom says that if $\mathcal O_1$ and $\mathcal O_2$ are spacelike separated, then

$$
[A,B]=0,
\qquad
A\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1),
\quad
B\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2).
$$

Taking $\mathcal O_2=\mathcal O'$, locality implies

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)
\subset
\mathcal A(\mathcal O')'.
$$

This inclusion says: every observable assigned to $\mathcal O$ commutes with every observable assigned to the outside of $\mathcal O$.

But the right-hand side may be larger. It consists of **all** bounded operators commuting with the outside algebra, whether or not the original net assigned them to $\mathcal O$. Haag duality is the condition that no such extra operators exist:

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)=\mathcal A(\mathcal O')'
}
$$

or, in a representation,

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)=\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'
}.
$$

This is why Haag duality is often described as local maximality, local completeness, or causal completeness of the observable net.

## Setup and conventions

Let $M$ be Minkowski spacetime. For a region $\mathcal O\subset M$, define its causal complement by

$$
\mathcal O'
=
\{x\in M: x\text{ is spacelike separated from every }y\in\mathcal O\}.
$$

The causal completion or causal hull is

$$
\mathcal O''=(\mathcal O')'.
$$

A region is causally complete if

$$
\mathcal O=\mathcal O''
$$

up to the chosen regularity convention. Double cones and wedges are standard examples of regions for which duality questions are especially important.

In a representation $\pi$ of the quasi-local algebra, write

$$
\mathcal M_\pi(\mathcal O)
=\pi(\mathcal A(\mathcal O))''.
$$

If no representation is displayed, the vacuum representation is usually meant.

Haag duality for a class of regions $\mathfrak R$ says

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)
=
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')',
\qquad
\mathcal O\in\mathfrak R.
$$

The class of regions matters. A theory may satisfy wedge duality, double-cone duality, interval duality on the line, or duality only after passing to a dual net.

## Locality versus Haag duality

The difference between locality and Haag duality is the difference between an inclusion and an equality.

| Property | Algebraic statement | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Locality | $\mathcal M(\mathcal O)\subset\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'$ | Inside observables commute with outside observables. |
| Haag duality | $\mathcal M(\mathcal O)=\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'$ | Every operator commuting with the outside is already an inside observable. |

Thus locality is a compatibility condition. Haag duality is a completeness condition.

One can fail Haag duality while preserving locality. In that case there are operators in

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'
$$

that commute with all outside observables but are not contained in $\mathcal M(\mathcal O)$. These operators are not necessarily mistakes; they may encode genuine global, charged, or topological structure.

## The dual net

Given any local net, one can define its **dual net** by

$$
\mathcal M^d(\mathcal O)
=
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'.
$$

Locality says

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)
\subset
\mathcal M^d(\mathcal O).
$$

Haag duality is precisely the statement

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)=\mathcal M^d(\mathcal O).
$$

The dual net is often larger than the original observable net. Passing to it can restore duality, but it can also change the interpretation of sectors or add operators that were not originally regarded as observables. Therefore dualization is not a purely harmless operation; it must be interpreted in the physical context.

A useful diagnostic is:

$$
\frac{\mathcal M^d(\mathcal O)}{\mathcal M(\mathcal O)}
\quad\text{measures possible missing local completeness.}
$$

This quotient notation is schematic rather than a literal algebraic quotient in general. It means that the relative inclusion records the extra operators present in the commutant of the outside.

## Wedge duality and the Bisognano–Wichmann theorem

For a Rindler wedge $W$, wedge duality is

$$
\mathcal M(W)'=\mathcal M(W'),
$$

or equivalently

$$
\mathcal M(W)=\mathcal M(W')'.
$$

The Bisognano–Wichmann theorem is one of the most important sources of such duality statements. In a suitable Wightman theory, the modular group of the wedge algebra $\mathcal M(W)$ with respect to the vacuum is implemented by Lorentz boosts preserving $W$, and the modular conjugation is related to a spacetime reflection and CPT-type operation.

Tomita–Takesaki theory gives

$$
J_W\mathcal M(W)J_W=\mathcal M(W)'.
$$

If the modular conjugation also implements the geometric reflection taking $W$ to $W'$, then

$$
\mathcal M(W)'=\mathcal M(W').
$$

This is the conceptual bridge:

$$
\text{geometric modular action}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{duality between regions and complements.}
$$

The details require the hypotheses of the relevant theorem, but the lesson is stable: Haag duality is deeply connected to modular localization.

## Double-cone duality

For double cones, Haag duality is usually written

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)
=
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'.
$$

Double-cone duality is especially important because double cones are bounded local regions. If it holds, then the observable net is locally complete in the strongest bounded-region sense.

Double-cone duality is often more delicate than wedge duality. Wedge duality may follow from modular arguments even when double-cone duality requires additional assumptions such as additivity, split property, conformal covariance, or completeness of the observable content.

A common hierarchy is:

$$
\text{locality}
\quad<\quad
\text{wedge duality}
\quad<\quad
\text{double-cone Haag duality},
$$

where the arrows indicate increasing strength, not a universal theorem without hypotheses.

## Why DHR theory uses Haag duality

Doplicher–Haag–Roberts theory studies representations that look like the vacuum outside some bounded region. A DHR representation $\pi$ localized in $\mathcal O$ satisfies, schematically,

$$
\pi|_{\mathcal A(\mathcal O')}
\simeq
\pi_0|_{\mathcal A(\mathcal O')},
$$

where $\pi_0$ is the vacuum representation.

To turn such localized representations into localized endomorphisms of the observable algebra, one needs to know that the local observable net is complete enough. Haag duality supplies this completeness. It ensures that if a charge is invisible outside $\mathcal O$, then its localized algebraic action can be controlled inside $\mathcal O$ through commutant relations.

Without duality, the categorical structure of sectors may still exist in modified form, but the clean DHR endomorphism picture becomes more complicated.

## Gauge theory and failure of duality

Gauge theories provide important warnings. The physical observable algebra consists of gauge-invariant operators, but charged fields are not themselves gauge-invariant observables. This can lead to a mismatch between what commutes with the outside and what the observable net assigns to the inside.

There are several mechanisms by which duality can fail or need refinement:

| Mechanism | Typical meaning |
|---|---|
| Charged fields omitted from observables | The observable net may be smaller than a field net. |
| Gauss law constraints | Charge in a region is tied to flux through its boundary. |
| Topological operators | Operators may be localized in a complement-sensitive or homological way. |
| Non-simply connected regions | Wilson or 't Hooft type operators may detect topology. |
| Long-range forces | Electric charge may not be localizable in bounded regions in the DHR sense. |

This is not a defect of algebraic QFT. On the contrary, the failure pattern of Haag duality is often a precise algebraic signature of gauge structure or topological order.

## Essential duality and causal completion

Sometimes the natural statement is not Haag duality for arbitrary regions, but **essential duality** or duality for causal completions. A net may satisfy

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'
=\mathcal M(\mathcal O'')
$$

for suitable regular regions. This says that the algebra determined by the outside is the algebra of the causal completion of $\mathcal O$.

If $\mathcal O$ is already causally complete, this reduces to Haag duality. If not, the equation says that the net depends on the causal domain determined by the region, not on arbitrary thin representatives.

This is the algebraic version of a familiar relativistic idea: observables in a domain of dependence should be determined by data on any Cauchy surface for that domain, provided the theory has the appropriate time-slice behavior.

## Relation to the split property

Haag duality and the split property are different statements.

Haag duality says

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)=\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'.
$$

The split property says that for nested regions $\mathcal O_1\Subset\mathcal O_2$, there exists a type I factor

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O_1)
\subset
\mathcal N
\subset
\mathcal M(\mathcal O_2).
$$

Duality concerns **completeness relative to the causal complement**. Splitness concerns **statistical independence for separated inclusions**. Neither is merely another name for the other.

They often work together. For example, modular arguments plus split assumptions can imply strong structural results, and the split property helps make subsystem reasoning compatible with type III local algebras. Haag duality then tells us that the relevant inside algebra is exactly the commutant of the outside algebra.

## Common pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: treating locality and Haag duality as the same axiom.** Locality is the inclusion. Haag duality is equality. The difference is physically significant.

**Pitfall 2: forgetting the two meanings of prime.** In $\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'$, the first prime is causal complement and the second prime is algebra commutant.

**Pitfall 3: assuming duality must always hold.** Duality can fail in interesting theories. Failure can encode gauge constraints, topological sectors, or missing charged fields.

**Pitfall 4: using the wrong class of regions.** A statement may hold for wedges but fail for double cones, or hold for simply connected regions but require modification for multiply connected regions.

**Pitfall 5: ignoring representation dependence.** Haag duality is usually a property of the von Neumann net in a representation, often the vacuum representation. Charged, thermal, or non-vacuum representations can change duality behavior.

## Research status

**Established.** Haag duality is a central structural property in algebraic QFT. It is known in many free-field and conformal settings, follows from modular/geometric arguments in important cases, and is a standard hypothesis or conclusion in sector theory.

**Subtle.** The exact statement depends on the representation, region class, spacetime topology, and choice of observable net. Wedge duality, double-cone duality, essential duality, and duality for a dual net are not interchangeable.

**Active.** Modern work continues to analyze duality in gauge theories, generalized symmetries, thermal sectors, curved spacetimes, low-dimensional theories with braid statistics, lattice-to-continuum limits, and topological phases. In these settings, failures of Haag duality are often not errors but data.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1

Show that locality implies

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)
\subset
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

By locality, every $A\in\mathcal M(\mathcal O)$ commutes with every $B\in\mathcal M(\mathcal O')$. Therefore $A$ lies in the commutant of $\mathcal M(\mathcal O')$. Since this is true for every $A\in\mathcal M(\mathcal O)$,

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)
\subset
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'.
$$

Haag duality is the additional claim that this inclusion is equality.

</details>

### Exercise 2

What does it mean physically if

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)
\subsetneq
\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'?
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

It means that there are bounded operators that commute with all observables outside $\mathcal O$ but are not included in the algebra assigned to $\mathcal O$. This can indicate that the observable net is not locally complete for that class of regions. Depending on the theory, the extra operators may reflect missing charged fields, gauge constraints, topological operators, or representation-dependent effects. The failure is therefore diagnostic rather than automatically pathological.

</details>

### Exercise 3

Define the dual net by

$$
\mathcal M^d(\mathcal O)=\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'.
$$

Why does Haag duality mean $\mathcal M=\mathcal M^d$?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

By definition, $\mathcal M^d(\mathcal O)$ is the largest algebra allowed by commuting with the outside algebra $\mathcal M(\mathcal O')$. Locality gives

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)\subset\mathcal M^d(\mathcal O).
$$

Haag duality says this inclusion is equality for every region in the chosen class:

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)=\mathcal M(\mathcal O')'=\mathcal M^d(\mathcal O).
$$

Thus the original net and the dual net coincide.

</details>
