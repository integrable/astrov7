## Summary

**Additivity** and the **time-slice axiom** answer two different questions about how much information is contained in a local net of observables.

Additivity asks whether the algebra assigned to a region is generated by the algebras of smaller regions covering it. In its common form, if an open region $\mathcal O$ is covered by subregions $\mathcal O_i$, then

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)
=
C^*\!\left(\bigcup_i \mathcal A(\mathcal O_i)\right)
$$

or the corresponding von Neumann closure in a Hilbert-space representation. The slogan is:

$$
\text{large local algebra}=
\text{algebra generated by smaller local algebras}.
$$

The time-slice axiom asks whether observables in an arbitrarily thin neighborhood of a Cauchy surface generate the observables in its whole domain of dependence. If $\mathcal N$ contains a Cauchy surface for a globally hyperbolic region $\mathcal O$, then the inclusion should induce an isomorphism

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal N)\xrightarrow{\ \simeq\ }\mathcal A(\mathcal O).
$$

This is the algebraic form of hyperbolic dynamics. It says that, once the equations of motion are imposed, the algebra in a Cauchy neighborhood already contains the algebraic information in the causal development. It does **not** say that one finite measurement determines the future, nor that a sharp Hilbert-space tensor factor sits on a Cauchy surface.

## Prerequisites

You should know [Local Nets of Algebras](/rigorous-qft/algebraic-qft/local-nets-of-algebras/), [Haag–Kastler Axioms](/rigorous-qft/algebraic-qft/haag-kastler-axioms/), and [Isotony, Locality, and Covariance](/rigorous-qft/algebraic-qft/isotony-locality-and-covariance/). The pages [Haag Duality](/rigorous-qft/algebraic-qft/haag-duality/) and [Split Property](/rigorous-qft/algebraic-qft/split-property/) explain two other locality-strengthening conditions that should not be confused with additivity or time-slice.

For the time-slice part, it helps to know the basic idea of a Cauchy surface: every inextendible causal curve crosses it exactly once. The later page on globally hyperbolic spacetimes will give the geometric details.

## Core idea

A local net is not only a list of algebras. It is a compatibility structure. Isotony already says that smaller regions give subalgebras of larger regions:

$$
\mathcal O_1\subset \mathcal O_2
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)\subset \mathcal A(\mathcal O_2).
$$

Additivity asks for a converse generation principle. If a large region is assembled from small regions, then no new observables should appear merely because one has named the union as a larger region.

The time-slice axiom asks for a dynamical generation principle. If a region $\mathcal N$ contains enough spacetime to determine a whole domain of dependence, then the algebra assigned to $\mathcal N$ should already be the algebra assigned to that domain.

These two ideas are often adjacent in lists of Haag–Kastler-style axioms, but they are not the same:

| Condition | What it controls | Slogan |
|---|---|---|
| Isotony | Inclusion of regions. | Smaller regions have fewer observables. |
| Additivity | Generation by local covers. | The net is genuinely local. |
| Time-slice | Generation by Cauchy neighborhoods. | Dynamics is encoded algebraically. |
| Haag duality | Completeness relative to causal complements. | Inside equals commutant of outside. |
| Split property | Statistical independence with a collar. | Separated regions behave tensor-product-like. |

The time-slice axiom is especially important in curved-spacetime and locally covariant QFT, because it turns Cauchy embeddings into algebra isomorphisms. That is what allows a functorial QFT to compare observables across different choices of Cauchy neighborhood and to define relative Cauchy evolution.

## Setup and conventions

Work first on a fixed globally hyperbolic spacetime $M$. In Minkowski space this can be $\mathbb R^{1,d-1}$; in curved-spacetime applications it is a smooth oriented and time-oriented Lorentzian manifold with good causal behavior.

Let $\mathsf{Reg}(M)$ be a suitable collection of open regions. Depending on the framework, one may use double cones, causally convex relatively compact opens, or all globally hyperbolic subregions with finitely many connected components. A local net assigns

$$
\mathcal O\longmapsto \mathcal A(\mathcal O)
$$

to each region, where $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ is usually a unital $C^*$-algebra or a von Neumann algebra in a chosen representation.

For a family of subalgebras $\{\mathcal B_i\}$ of a $C^*$-algebra, write

$$
C^*\!\left(\bigcup_i\mathcal B_i\right)
$$

for the smallest $C^*$-subalgebra containing all $\mathcal B_i$. In a Hilbert-space representation, write

$$
\left(\bigcup_i\mathcal M_i\right)''
$$

for the von Neumann algebra generated by the von Neumann algebras $\mathcal M_i$.

The **domain of dependence** $D(\mathcal N)$ of a region $\mathcal N$ is the set of points whose causal curves are forced to meet $\mathcal N$ in the appropriate sense. A Cauchy neighborhood is an open set containing a Cauchy surface of the spacetime or of a globally hyperbolic subregion.

## Additivity

### Basic form

Let $\mathcal O$ be a region and let $\{\mathcal O_i\}_{i\in I}$ be an open cover of $\mathcal O$ by admissible subregions:

$$
\mathcal O=\bigcup_{i\in I}\mathcal O_i.
$$

A net is **additive** if

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)
=
C^*\!\left(\bigcup_{i\in I}\mathcal A(\mathcal O_i)\right)
$$

whenever the cover belongs to the class allowed in the axiom. In a vacuum representation, the corresponding von Neumann statement is often written

$$
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)
=
\left(\bigcup_{i\in I}\mathcal M(\mathcal O_i)\right)'',
\qquad
\mathcal M(\mathcal O)=\mathcal A(\mathcal O)''.
$$

The right-hand side is automatically contained in $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ by isotony. The content of additivity is the reverse inclusion: every observable localized in the larger region can be approximated or generated from observables localized in smaller pieces.

### Why additivity is not automatic

Isotony alone permits pathological nets. For example, one could start with a reasonable net $\mathcal A_0$ and artificially define

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)=\mathcal A_0(\mathcal O)\otimes \mathcal Z
$$

for some extra algebra $\mathcal Z$ that is inserted only at sufficiently large regions or only at a chosen scale. With enough care, such constructions can preserve inclusion while violating the idea that observables are generated locally. Additivity rules out many forms of hidden nonlocal baggage.

A physically reasonable net should not acquire a new independent observable just because the observer describes a region as one large open set instead of as a union of smaller laboratories.

### Additivity versus strong additivity

There are several related conditions.

**Ordinary additivity** says that the algebra of a region is generated by algebras of an open cover.

**Weak additivity** often refers to a Poincaré-covariant Minkowski-space condition: translates of the algebra of one fixed nonempty region generate the global algebra. A typical form is

$$
\mathcal A
=
C^*\!\left(\bigcup_{a\in\mathbb R^d}\mathcal A(\mathcal O+a)\right).
$$

This is weaker than full local additivity, but it rules out the possibility that one nonempty local algebra sees only a superselection-isolated fragment of the global theory.

**Strong additivity** is common in chiral conformal nets. Roughly, removing a point from an interval should not reduce the generated algebra:

$$
\mathcal A(I)
=
\mathcal A(I_1)\vee\mathcal A(I_2)
$$

when $I\setminus\{p\}=I_1\cup I_2$. This is much stronger and is not the generic condition meant by additivity in higher-dimensional algebraic QFT.

These variants should be kept separate. They have different hypotheses, different uses, and different failure modes.

## Additivity and causally complete regions

In relativistic QFT it is natural to compare a region with its causal completion. For a subset $\mathcal O$, one often writes

$$
\mathcal O''=(\mathcal O')'
$$

for the double causal complement, when this notation is meaningful. The region $\mathcal O''$ is the causal completion of $\mathcal O$.

One might expect

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)=\mathcal A(\mathcal O'')
$$

for every $\mathcal O$. That expectation is not merely additivity. It is a causal-dynamical requirement related to primitive causality or the time-slice property. Additivity says that a region is generated by its local cover. It does not by itself say that the algebra of a small Cauchy neighborhood equals the algebra of its causal development.

This distinction matters because a cover of $\mathcal O$ is a spatial or topological refinement, while a causal completion adds new spacetime points determined by evolution.

## The time-slice axiom

The **time-slice axiom** says that observables in an arbitrarily thin neighborhood of a Cauchy surface generate the algebra of the whole spacetime region determined by that surface.

In a fixed globally hyperbolic spacetime $M$, let $\mathcal N\subset M$ be an open neighborhood of a Cauchy surface $\Sigma$. Then the inclusion

$$
\iota:\mathcal N\hookrightarrow M
$$

should induce an isomorphism

$$
\alpha_\iota:\mathcal A(\mathcal N)\xrightarrow{\ \simeq\ }\mathcal A(M).
$$

For a subregion $\mathcal O$, a relative version says that if $\mathcal N$ contains a Cauchy surface for $\mathcal O$, then

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal N)\xrightarrow{\ \simeq\ }\mathcal A(\mathcal O).
$$

On a fixed spacetime, this is often expressed as **primitive causality**:

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal N)=\mathcal A(D(\mathcal N))
$$

for suitable open regions $\mathcal N$.

The word "primitive" here is historical. The condition is not primitive in the sense of being simpler than locality; it is a compact algebraic expression of hyperbolic propagation.

## Why the time-slice axiom is dynamical

For a linear classical field obeying a hyperbolic equation, initial data on a Cauchy surface determine a unique solution in the domain of dependence. The time-slice axiom is the quantum algebraic version of this statement.

Consider a Klein–Gordon field on a globally hyperbolic spacetime:

$$
P\phi=0,
\qquad
P=\Box_g+m^2+\xi R.
$$

Smeared fields are not indexed by solutions directly; they are indexed by test functions modulo the equation of motion. If $E$ is the causal propagator, the symplectic space of classical solutions can be described using compactly supported test functions modulo $P$-exact functions:

$$
C_0^\infty(M)/P C_0^\infty(M).
$$

The hyperbolic propagation of $P$ implies that every equivalence class has a representative supported in any chosen Cauchy neighborhood. After quantization, the corresponding CCR algebra therefore satisfies the time-slice axiom.

The proof for interacting theories is much subtler. In perturbative algebraic QFT, one works with formal power series of local functionals and uses causal perturbation theory plus the field equation to prove suitable time-slice statements in that formal setting.

## Additivity plus time-slice

Additivity and time-slice often work together.

Suppose $\mathcal N$ is a Cauchy neighborhood of $\mathcal O$, and suppose $\mathcal N$ is covered by small regions $\mathcal N_i$. Additivity gives

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal N)
=
C^*\!\left(\bigcup_i\mathcal A(\mathcal N_i)\right).
$$

The time-slice axiom then gives

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)
\simeq
\mathcal A(\mathcal N)
=
C^*\!\left(\bigcup_i\mathcal A(\mathcal N_i)\right).
$$

Thus all observables in the causal development are generated by arbitrarily local observables in the Cauchy neighborhood.

This is the rigorous version of a familiar physical idea:

$$
\text{local initial observables}+
\text{equations of motion}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{observables throughout the domain}.
$$

The advantage of the algebraic formulation is that it avoids choosing canonical coordinates, a preferred time function, or pointlike field values on a sharp surface.

## Relation to equations of motion

The time-slice axiom is closely tied to imposing equations of motion. Off-shell algebras generally do not satisfy it.

For a scalar field, the off-shell algebra contains functionals of arbitrary configurations $\phi$. Observables supported away from a Cauchy neighborhood cannot be reduced to observables in the neighborhood, because no equation of motion has identified configurations with the same Cauchy data. After quotienting by the ideal generated by the field equation, propagation relates those regions.

One can summarize the logic as

$$
\begin{array}{ccl}
\text{off-shell fields} &\Rightarrow& \text{locality may hold, time-slice usually fails},
\text{on-shell fields} &\Rightarrow& \text{time-slice can hold by hyperbolicity}.
\end{array}
$$

In gauge theory the situation is more delicate. The equations of motion have constraints, gauge redundancies, and sometimes zero modes or global charges. A correct time-slice theorem may need BRST/BV cohomology, gauge-invariant observables, boundary conditions, or a careful treatment of compactly supported gauge transformations.

## What the axiom does not say

The time-slice axiom is sometimes misread as an unrealistic determinism claim. It is not.

It does **not** say that a state on $\mathcal A(\mathcal N)$ is physically easy to prepare. It says that the algebraic inclusion induced by a Cauchy neighborhood is an isomorphism.

It does **not** say that one can measure finitely many observables on a hypersurface and reconstruct all future measurement outcomes. It is an equality of algebras, not a finite-data algorithm.

It does **not** require a sharp tensor product of Hilbert spaces across time. Local algebras in continuum QFT are infinite-dimensional and usually type III in vacuum representations.

It does **not** make time evolution an inner automorphism of every local algebra. In a stationary spacetime with a suitable Hamiltonian one may represent time translations by unitaries, but the time-slice axiom is more general and does not require global time-translation symmetry.

## Locally covariant form

In locally covariant QFT, one packages spacetimes and embeddings into a category. A morphism

$$
\psi:M\to N
$$

is called a **Cauchy morphism** if its image contains a Cauchy surface of $N$. A locally covariant theory is a functor

$$
\mathcal A:\mathsf{Loc}\to\mathsf{Alg}.
$$

The time-slice axiom says that every Cauchy morphism is sent to an isomorphism:

$$
\psi\text{ Cauchy}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\mathcal A(\psi):\mathcal A(M)\xrightarrow{\ \simeq\ }\mathcal A(N).
$$

This is one of the reasons the functorial framework is powerful. It separates geometric information from algebraic information: Cauchy embeddings are geometric equivalences for hyperbolic dynamics, and the theory must send them to algebraic equivalences.

This formulation will be the starting point of [QFT as a Functor on Spacetimes](/rigorous-qft/locally-covariant-qft/qft-as-functor-on-spacetimes/).

## Common pitfalls

**Confusing additivity with isotony.** Isotony gives inclusions from smaller to larger regions. Additivity says the larger algebra is generated by the smaller ones. Only the second is a generation condition.

**Confusing additivity with Haag duality.** Additivity is about building a region from a cover. Haag duality is about comparing a region with the commutant of its causal complement.

**Forgetting the closure.** In $C^*$-algebras one takes the $C^*$-algebra generated by the subalgebras. In von Neumann algebras one takes the von Neumann algebra generated by them. Writing a bare union is almost never correct.

**Treating strong additivity as generic.** Strong additivity in chiral conformal nets is a special and powerful property. It should not be silently imported into all dimensions and all QFTs.

**Thinking time-slice is a Hamiltonian axiom.** The time-slice axiom does not require a preferred Hamiltonian or global timelike Killing vector. It is a causal propagation condition.

**Applying time-slice off shell.** Off-shell function spaces remember arbitrary configurations. Time-slice becomes natural only after equations of motion, or their cohomological replacement, are imposed.

**Ignoring constraints and gauge symmetry.** Gauge theory can require a cohomological formulation of observables. Naively imposing time-slice on gauge potentials can miss global and constraint-related subtleties.

## Research status

**Established.** Additivity, weak additivity, primitive causality, and time-slice conditions are standard parts of the algebraic QFT toolkit. Free hyperbolic fields on globally hyperbolic spacetimes satisfy time-slice in the expected on-shell algebras. Perturbative algebraic QFT has precise time-slice theorems for important classes of formally interacting scalar theories.

**Subtle.** The correct formulation depends on the region category, topology on algebras, representation choice, and whether one works with $C^*$-algebras, von Neumann algebras, or perturbative topological algebras of formal power series.

**Active.** Gauge theories, boundary conditions, topological sectors, generalized global symmetries, nontrivial spacetime topology, and theories with constraints continue to motivate refined versions. In these cases the right statement may involve gauge-invariant subalgebras, BRST/BV cohomology, relative Cauchy evolution, or additivity failures that are physically meaningful rather than pathological.

## Exercises

1. **Additivity from a two-set cover.** Let $\mathcal O=\mathcal O_1\cup\mathcal O_2$ and suppose the net is additive for this cover. Show that any subalgebra $\mathcal B\subset\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ containing $\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)$ and $\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)$ must contain $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Additivity says

$$
\mathcal A(\mathcal O)
=
C^*\!\left(\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)
\cup
\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)\right).
$$

By definition, the $C^*$-algebra generated by the two subalgebras is the smallest $C^*$-subalgebra containing both. If $\mathcal B$ is a $C^*$-subalgebra of $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ containing both, then

$$
C^*\!\left(\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)
\cup
\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)\right)
\subset \mathcal B.
$$

Therefore $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)\subset\mathcal B$. Since $\mathcal B\subset\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ by assumption, equality follows.

</details>

2. **Why isotony is not additivity.** Construct a toy net on a line of regions by setting $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)=\mathbb C$ for small regions and $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)=M_2(\mathbb C)$ for one large region containing them. Explain why isotony can hold while additivity fails.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The inclusion $\mathbb C\mathbf 1\subset M_2(\mathbb C)$ gives isotony from every small region into the large region. But the algebra generated by all small-region algebras is still only $\mathbb C\mathbf 1$, not $M_2(\mathbb C)$. Thus the large region contains extra observables not generated by the smaller ones. This is exactly the kind of hidden nonlocal addition that additivity excludes.

</details>

3. **Time-slice in one sentence.** Let $\psi:M\to N$ be a Cauchy morphism in a locally covariant theory. Write the functorial time-slice condition and state its physical meaning.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The condition is

$$
\mathcal A(\psi):\mathcal A(M)\xrightarrow{\ \simeq\ }\mathcal A(N).
$$

It means that the algebra of observables in a spacetime region containing a Cauchy surface is algebraically equivalent to the algebra of observables in the whole spacetime determined by that Cauchy data.

</details>
