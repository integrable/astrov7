## Summary

A **local net of algebras** assigns an algebra of observables to each admissible spacetime region. In its most compact form, the assignment is

$$
\mathcal O\longmapsto \mathcal A(\mathcal O),
$$

where $\mathcal O$ is an open spacetime region and $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ is the algebra generated by measurements that can be performed inside $\mathcal O$.

This is the starting move of algebraic QFT. Instead of asking first for fields $\phi(x)$, a Lagrangian, or a path integral, algebraic QFT asks what the localized **observable content** of the theory is. The shift matters because pointlike fields are often singular, nonunique, gauge-dependent, or defined only after choosing a representation. Local observable algebras are designed to remember what can be measured without committing too early to a particular field coordinate system.

The slogan is

$$
\text{theory on spacetime}
\quad = \quad
\text{compatible family of local observable algebras}.
$$

It is also helpful to have seen the Wightman assumptions in [Wightman Axioms](/rigorous-qft/wightman-axiomatic-qft/wightman-axioms/), especially locality and covariance.

## Core idea

The physical idea is simple: a bounded laboratory region should have its own observables. A larger region should contain at least those observables, and two spacelike separated laboratories should not be able to signal by changing the order in which their measurements are performed.

Algebraic QFT turns that idea into a map from spacetime geometry to algebra. The spacetime side records containment and causal separation. The algebra side records sums, products, adjoints, limits, commutators, and states. A net is the compatibility rule tying the two sides together.

This is not merely a different notation for fields. The same observable net can have several different field coordinatizations. Conversely, a gauge-dependent field may not itself be an observable even though it helps generate or describe the local observable algebra. This is one reason algebraic QFT is useful for gauge theory, superselection sectors, thermal states, curved-spacetime QFT, and questions about entanglement where naive Hilbert-space factorization is too simple.

## Setup and conventions

For this page, spacetime is $d$-dimensional Minkowski space $M=\mathbb R^{1,d-1}$ with the mostly-minus convention fixed in [Conventions](/rigorous-qft/conventions/). The causal complement of a region $\mathcal O$ is denoted $\mathcal O'$. Thus $\mathcal O_1\subset \mathcal O_2'$ means every point of $\mathcal O_1$ is spacelike separated from every point of $\mathcal O_2$.

The index set of regions is usually chosen to be a convenient class such as bounded open double cones, causally complete open sets, or relatively compact globally hyperbolic subregions in curved spacetime. The exact choice matters for some technical theorems, but the first-pass picture is independent of it.

The algebra $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ may be treated at different levels:

| Algebraic level | Typical role | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| abstract $*$-algebra | formal algebra of local observables | useful before taking analytic completions |
| unital $C^*$-algebra | representation-independent normed observable algebra | natural for states and the quasi-local algebra |
| von Neumann algebra | algebra in a chosen Hilbert-space representation | natural for modular theory, thermal states, and local factors |

This page uses $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ generically. When a distinction matters, it says whether the algebra is a $C^*$-algebra or a von Neumann algebra.

## Definition

A **local net of observable algebras** on a spacetime $M$ consists of an assignment

$$
\mathcal O \longmapsto \mathcal A(\mathcal O)
$$

from admissible open regions $\mathcal O\subset M$ to unital $*$-algebras, $C^*$-algebras, or von Neumann algebras, together with inclusion maps whenever $\mathcal O_1\subset \mathcal O_2$. At minimum the assignment should satisfy isotony:

$$
\mathcal O_1\subset \mathcal O_2
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)\subset \mathcal A(\mathcal O_2).
$$

A net becomes a relativistic local net after adding locality and covariance conditions. In a Hilbert-space representation, locality is usually written

$$
\mathcal O_1\subset \mathcal O_2'
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
[A,B]=0
\quad
\text{for all } A\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1),\,B\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2).
$$

The inclusion and commutation relations are the operational content of the words "observable localized in $\mathcal O$".

## The quasi-local algebra

If the local algebras are $C^*$-algebras, isotony lets us combine them into a single global algebra. Formally, one first takes the algebraic union

$$
\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}
=\bigcup_{\mathcal O}\mathcal A(\mathcal O),
$$

where the union is understood through the inclusion maps. The **quasi-local algebra** is the norm closure

$$
\mathcal A
=\overline{\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}}^{\|\cdot\|}.
$$

This global algebra is not the main physical primitive; the net is. The same $\mathcal A$ without the localization map would forget which observables are local, which pairs are spacelike, and which regions generate which subalgebras.

A **state** on the quasi-local algebra is a positive normalized linear functional

$$
\omega:\mathcal A\to\mathbb C,
\qquad
\omega(A^*A)\ge 0,
\qquad
\omega(1)=1.
$$

The Hilbert-space representation is then recovered from a state by the Gelfand–Naimark–Segal construction. This point will become central in later pages: algebraic QFT does not identify the theory with a single preferred Hilbert-space representation from the beginning.

## From fields to local algebras

Suppose a Wightman field $\phi$ is available. Since $\phi(x)$ is not an operator at a point, a region should be associated with smeared fields $\phi(f)$ with $\operatorname{supp} f\subset\mathcal O$. But $\phi(f)$ is usually unbounded, and unbounded operators are not themselves convenient algebra elements. A common route is to generate bounded operators from them, for example by exponentiating suitable self-adjoint smeared fields:

$$
W(f)=e^{i\phi(f)},
\qquad
\operatorname{supp}f\subset\mathcal O.
$$

For the free scalar field, these $W(f)$ are Weyl operators. The local algebra $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ is generated by all $W(f)$ with support in $\mathcal O$, subject to the canonical commutation relations. Locality follows from the Pauli–Jordan commutator distribution: if the supports of $f$ and $g$ are spacelike separated, the symplectic pairing vanishes, and the corresponding Weyl operators commute.

This example is important because it shows the bridge between the Wightman and algebraic languages. Wightman fields can generate a local net, but the algebraic framework can also discuss nets where no preferred pointlike field coordinatization is part of the definition.

## What the net remembers

A local net remembers several kinds of information at once.

| Net structure | Physical meaning |
|---|---|
| $\mathcal O_1\subset\mathcal O_2$ implies $\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)\subset\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)$ | observables available in a small laboratory remain available in a larger one |
| $\mathcal O_1\subset\mathcal O_2'$ implies commutation | spacelike separated measurements are compatible |
| covariance $\alpha_g(\mathcal A(\mathcal O))=\mathcal A(g\mathcal O)$ | the localization assignment respects spacetime symmetry |
| additivity | large regions are generated by smaller local pieces |
| vacuum or thermal states | expectation values are functionals on the algebra |

The crucial point is that locality is not an extra statement about a Hamiltonian after quantization. It is built into the localization structure of the algebra itself.

## Checks

A proposed local net should pass several basic checks.

First, check **isotony**. If a smearing function is supported in $\mathcal O_1$ and $\mathcal O_1\subset\mathcal O_2$, then it is also supported in $\mathcal O_2$. The algebra generated in the smaller region should therefore sit inside the algebra generated in the larger region.

Second, check **spacelike commutativity**. In a field-generated example, the commutator distribution or Weyl symplectic form should vanish for spacelike separated supports.

Third, check **covariance**. If a symmetry $g$ moves the region, it should also move the corresponding algebra:

$$
\alpha_g\big(\mathcal A(\mathcal O)\big)=\mathcal A(g\mathcal O).
$$

Fourth, check **state compatibility**. A state $\omega$ should produce local expectation values $\omega(A)$ for $A\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ and correlation data $\omega(A_1\cdots A_n)$ without requiring a tensor product factorization of spacetime into independent Hilbert spaces.

## Common pitfalls

**Confusing fields with observables.** A pointlike field can be a useful coordinate system for a theory, but it need not be a bounded local observable. Gauge-dependent fields are especially dangerous here.

**Forgetting the region map.** The global algebra $\mathcal A$ alone is not the local QFT. The assignment $\mathcal O\mapsto\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ is part of the data.

**Assuming Hilbert-space factorization.** In continuum QFT, a spatial split into a region and its complement usually does not give $\mathcal H=\mathcal H_R\otimes\mathcal H_{R^c}$ in the naive quantum-mechanical way. The algebraic substitute is the inclusion of local algebras, with the split property giving an approximate tensor-factorization statement under additional hypotheses.

**Treating every property as an axiom.** Isotony and locality are basic net conditions. Haag duality, the split property, type III structure, and additivity variants are important but should not be silently folded into the minimal definition.

## Research status

The definition of a local net is established and central in algebraic QFT. Free fields, many conformal nets, perturbative algebraic models, and several low-dimensional rigorous models fit naturally into this language.

What is not automatic is the existence of a physically desired net for every formal Lagrangian. Four-dimensional interacting Yang–Mills theory, for example, is not made rigorous merely by writing a net-shaped notation. The net is a framework for stating and proving properties; it is not a shortcut around construction.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Isotony from smearing supports

Let $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ be generated by bounded functions of smeared fields $\phi(f)$ with $\operatorname{supp}f\subset\mathcal O$. Show that $\mathcal O_1\subset\mathcal O_2$ implies $\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)\subset\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Every test function supported in $\mathcal O_1$ is also supported in $\mathcal O_2$. Therefore every generator assigned to $\mathcal O_1$ is among the allowed generators for $\mathcal O_2$. The algebra generated by the smaller list of generators is contained in the algebra generated by the larger list.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Locality for Weyl operators

For a free scalar Weyl net, suppose

$$
W(f)W(g)=e^{-i\sigma(f,g)/2}W(f+g),
$$

where $\sigma(f,g)$ is the Pauli–Jordan symplectic form. Show that $W(f)$ and $W(g)$ commute whenever $\sigma(f,g)=0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The Weyl relation gives

$$
W(f)W(g)=e^{-i\sigma(f,g)/2}W(f+g),
$$

while reversing the order gives

$$
W(g)W(f)=e^{-i\sigma(g,f)/2}W(f+g)
=e^{i\sigma(f,g)/2}W(f+g).
$$

If $\sigma(f,g)=0$, the two phase factors are both $1$, so $W(f)W(g)=W(g)W(f)$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why the global algebra is not enough

Explain why two different localization maps $\mathcal O\mapsto\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ on the same quasi-local algebra can describe different local physics.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The quasi-local algebra records possible algebraic combinations of observables, but it does not by itself tell which observables are localized in which regions. Locality, covariance, causal complement relations, additivity, and entanglement questions all use the region assignment. Changing the assignment can change which observables count as spacelike separated or local, even if the ambient algebra is isomorphic.

</details>
