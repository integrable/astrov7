## Summary

The propagation of singularities theorem says that singularities of solutions to a hyperbolic equation do not move arbitrarily. They travel along the Hamiltonian flow of the principal symbol.

Let $P$ be a differential operator with principal symbol $p$ and let $u$ be a distribution. If

$$
Pu=f,
$$

then away from the singularities of $f$, the wavefront set of $u$ lies in the characteristic set

$$
\operatorname{Char}(P)=\{(x,k)\in T^*M\setminus0:p(x,k)=0\},
$$

and is invariant along the bicharacteristic flow generated by the Hamilton vector field $H_p$.

For a normally hyperbolic scalar operator

$$
P=\Box_g+m^2+\xi R,
$$

the principal symbol is

$$
p(x,k)=g^{\mu\nu}(x)k_\mu k_\nu.
$$

The characteristic set is the bundle of nonzero null covectors, and the bicharacteristics project to null geodesics. This is why the singularities of the causal propagator, the Feynman propagator, and Hadamard two-point functions are lightlike.

## Core idea

A distribution can be singular at a point in many possible cotangent directions. A differential equation restricts both the allowed directions and their motion.

The principal symbol of the operator sees the highest-derivative part of the equation. This is the part that controls high-frequency behavior. Since wavefront sets are high-frequency objects, they are governed by the principal symbol rather than by lower-order mass, curvature, or potential terms.

For hyperbolic field equations, the principal symbol creates a geometric optics picture:

$$
\text{singular wave packet}
\quad\leadsto\quad
\text{ray in }T^*M
\quad\leadsto\quad
\text{projected causal curve in }M.
$$

In QFT, this theorem is the analytic bridge between field equations and the singular geometry of propagators. The light-cone singularity is not guessed from flat space; it is forced by the characteristic flow of the wave operator.

## Setup and conventions

Let $M$ be a smooth manifold and let $P$ be a linear differential operator of order $m$. In local coordinates,

$$
P=\sum_{|\alpha|\le m}a_\alpha(x)\partial_x^\alpha.
$$

Its principal symbol is

$$
p(x,k)=\sum_{|\alpha|=m}a_\alpha(x)(ik)^\alpha,
$$

up to the usual harmless convention-dependent factors of $i$. For microlocal propagation, what matters is the homogeneous polynomial in the covector $k$ determined by the top-order part of $P$.

The characteristic set is

$$
\operatorname{Char}(P)
=
\{(x,k)\in T^*M\setminus0:p(x,k)=0\}.
$$

Where $p(x,k)\ne0$, the operator is elliptic in that cotangent direction. Elliptic regularity says that singularities of $u$ cannot hide there unless $Pu$ is also singular there.

A compact way to say this is

$$
\operatorname{WF}(u)
\subset
\operatorname{Char}(P)\cup\operatorname{WF}(Pu).
$$

Thus, if $Pu$ is smooth, then

$$
\operatorname{WF}(u)\subset\operatorname{Char}(P).
$$

This is only the first half of the story. The propagation theorem says how $\operatorname{WF}(u)$ moves inside $\operatorname{Char}(P)$.

## Hamiltonian flow on the cotangent bundle

The cotangent bundle $T^*M$ has a canonical symplectic form. In local coordinates $(x^\mu,k_\mu)$, the Hamilton vector field of a function $p$ is

$$
H_p
=
\frac{\partial p}{\partial k_\mu}\frac{\partial}{\partial x^\mu}
-\frac{\partial p}{\partial x^\mu}\frac{\partial}{\partial k_\mu}.
$$

A bicharacteristic is an integral curve

$$
\lambda\mapsto (x(\lambda),k(\lambda))
$$

satisfying

$$
\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}=\frac{\partial p}{\partial k_\mu},
\qquad
\frac{dk_\mu}{d\lambda}=-\frac{\partial p}{\partial x^\mu}.
$$

If the curve lies in $\operatorname{Char}(P)$, it is a null bicharacteristic for a wave operator. The propagation theorem says that, for operators of real principal type, $\operatorname{WF}(u)\setminus\operatorname{WF}(Pu)$ is a union of such bicharacteristic curves.

In words:

$$
\text{singularities propagate along }H_p.
$$

The theorem is local in phase space. It does not say that all possible rays are occupied; it says that once a singular covector appears, the equation propagates it along the characteristic flow unless a source, boundary, or interaction changes the problem.

## Normally hyperbolic operators

For scalar QFT on a Lorentzian spacetime, the basic operator is normally hyperbolic:

$$
P=\Box_g+m^2+\xi R.
$$

The principal symbol is

$$
p(x,k)=g^{\mu\nu}(x)k_\mu k_\nu.
$$

The characteristic set is therefore

$$
\operatorname{Char}(P)
=
\{(x,k):k\ne0,\ g^{\mu\nu}k_\mu k_\nu=0\}.
$$

These are the nonzero null covectors. The Hamilton equations become

$$
\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}
=2g^{\mu\nu}k_\nu,
$$

and

$$
\frac{dk_\mu}{d\lambda}
=-\frac{\partial g^{\alpha\beta}}{\partial x^\mu}k_\alpha k_\beta.
$$

After raising the covector to a vector, the projected curve $x(\lambda)$ is a null geodesic, up to affine reparametrization. Thus, singularities of solutions to the wave equation move along null geodesics.

This explains why the mass does not change the leading singular directions. The mass term is lower order and does not affect $p(x,k)$.

## The causal propagator

The retarded and advanced propagators solve

$$
PE_{\rm ret}=PE_{\rm adv}=\operatorname{id}
$$

on test functions, with support in the causal future or past. Their difference

$$
E=E_{\rm ret}-E_{\rm adv}
$$

is the causal propagator appearing in the commutator

$$
[\phi(f),\phi(g)]=iE(f,g)\mathbf 1.
$$

The Schwartz kernel of $E$ is singular on the null relation. Microlocally, its wavefront set is built from pairs of null-related covectors with both time orientations. The Hadamard two-point function is a positive-frequency part of this causal singularity.

The propagation theorem gives the geometric reason. A delta source at $y$ creates singular covectors at the source, and the hyperbolic equation transports them along null bicharacteristics to points $x$ on the light cone of $y$.

## Hadamard singularities from propagation

A Hadamard two-point function is a bisolution away from the diagonal:

$$
P_x\omega_2=0,
\qquad
P_y\omega_2=0.
$$

Therefore, away from the diagonal, the propagation theorem constrains its wavefront set in each variable. Singularities must lie on null bicharacteristics for $P_x$ and $P_y$.

This is the analytic backbone of Radzikowski's formula

$$
\operatorname{WF}(\omega_2)
=
\{(x,k_x;y,-k_y):(x,k_x)\sim(y,k_y),\ k_x\triangleright0\}.
$$

Propagation alone gives the null-geodesic motion. Positivity and the spectral orientation select the future-directed half. The Hadamard theorem is therefore a combination of hyperbolic PDE, positivity, and local spectral information.

## Feynman propagators and parametrices

The Feynman propagator is a distinguished inverse or parametrix with a specific boundary-value prescription. In flat space, it is the distribution with denominator

$$
\frac{i}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon}
$$

in the mostly-minus convention. In curved space, a global momentum-space denominator is unavailable, but the microlocal singularity still has a Feynman orientation.

A Feynman parametrix has wavefront set with positive-frequency propagation in one time ordering and negative-frequency propagation in the other. This is why time-ordered products can be built using a Feynman two-point function, provided the underlying state is Hadamard and the remaining diagonal singularities are handled by renormalization.

The propagation theorem controls the off-diagonal singularities. Extension of distributions controls what happens when points collide.

## Real principal type and what can fail

The clean propagation theorem applies to operators of real principal type. Roughly, this means that $p$ is real and its Hamilton vector field does not vanish on the characteristic set in the relevant conic region. Normally hyperbolic scalar operators satisfy the needed condition away from the zero section.

Several QFT situations require more care:

| Situation | Why the basic theorem is not enough |
|---|---|
| Gauge fields | The operator has constraints and gauge degeneracy. One must fix gauge, pass to complexes, or work modulo gauge. |
| Boundaries | Singularities can reflect, diffract, or generate boundary wavefront sets. |
| Non-smooth metrics | The principal symbol is less regular, so standard pseudodifferential tools may fail. |
| Interactions | Perturbative singularities involve products, pullbacks, and extensions at diagonals. |
| Horizons and asymptotic regions | Global state selection requires additional boundary or spectral data. |

The theorem remains the model: singularities are governed by the characteristic geometry. But the characteristic geometry may have extra structure.

## Common pitfalls

**Propagation of singularities means propagation of support.** No. A solution can have support inside a causal cone, but the wavefront set tracks high-frequency singular directions. Support and singularity are different notions.

**The mass shell controls the wavefront set.** For high-frequency singularities of $\Box_g+m^2$, the mass is lower order. The characteristic set is null, not massive timelike.

**All null geodesics are singular.** The theorem says singularities propagate along null bicharacteristics if they are present. It does not say every null geodesic carries a singularity in every solution.

**The theorem replaces positivity.** It does not. Propagation gives the null relation; positivity and spectral orientation are additional QFT input.

**The Feynman propagator is just another Green function.** Its wavefront orientation is part of the data. That orientation is what makes time-ordering compatible with microlocal product criteria.

## Research status

The propagation of singularities theorem is a standard theorem in microlocal analysis and is a settled analytic input to rigorous QFT on smooth globally hyperbolic spacetimes.

Its QFT use is also well established for normally hyperbolic free fields, Hadamard states, parametrices, and perturbative algebraic constructions. The active frontier is not whether singularities propagate along bicharacteristics; it is how to adapt the full microlocal package to gauge complexes, boundaries, defects, low-regularity geometry, and nonperturbative interacting theories.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Characteristic set of the Klein–Gordon operator

Let $P=\Box_g+m^2+\xi R$. Find the characteristic set of $P$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The characteristic set depends only on the principal symbol. The terms $m^2$ and $\xi R$ are zeroth order, so they do not contribute. The principal symbol is

$$
p(x,k)=g^{\mu\nu}(x)k_\mu k_\nu.
$$

Therefore

$$
\operatorname{Char}(P)
=
\{(x,k)\in T^*M\setminus0:g^{\mu\nu}k_\mu k_\nu=0\}.
$$

This is the bundle of nonzero null covectors.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Why smooth sources do not create off-characteristic singularities

Suppose $Pu=f$ and $f$ is smooth. Explain why $\operatorname{WF}(u)$ must lie in $\operatorname{Char}(P)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Microlocal elliptic regularity gives

$$
\operatorname{WF}(u)
\subset
\operatorname{Char}(P)\cup\operatorname{WF}(Pu).
$$

Since $Pu=f$ is smooth,

$$
\operatorname{WF}(Pu)=\operatorname{WF}(f)=\varnothing.
$$

Thus

$$
\operatorname{WF}(u)\subset\operatorname{Char}(P).
$$

In words, if the equation has a smooth source, any singularity in the solution must occur in a characteristic direction.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Projection of bicharacteristics

For $p(x,k)=g^{\mu\nu}k_\mu k_\nu$, show why the base-space projection of the Hamilton flow is null.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The Hamilton equation for $x$ is

$$
\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}
=\frac{\partial p}{\partial k_\mu}
=2g^{\mu\nu}k_\nu.
$$

Thus the tangent vector to the projected curve is proportional to $k^\mu=g^{\mu\nu}k_\nu$. On the characteristic set,

$$
g^{\mu\nu}k_\mu k_\nu=0.
$$

Therefore the projected tangent vector is null. The remaining Hamilton equation gives the geodesic equation up to affine reparametrization.

</details>
