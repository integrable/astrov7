## Summary

Algebraic renormalization in curved spacetime is the rigorous version of the statement that counterterms must be local. In flat spacetime, translation invariance, momentum space, and the vacuum often hide this principle. On a curved spacetime there is no global momentum and no preferred vacuum, so the principle must be stated directly: Wick products and time-ordered products must be local, covariant, causal, and compatible with scaling.

The main objects are locally covariant products such as

$$
:\!\Phi^k\!:_M(f),
\qquad
T_M\bigl(A_1(x_1)\cdots A_n(x_n)\bigr),
$$

where $A_i$ are local polynomial fields. The singularities are controlled by the Hadamard condition and microlocal analysis. Renormalization becomes the problem of extending distributions from configurations of distinct points to the coincident diagonals.

The main theorem-level message is:

$$
\text{renormalization freedom on curved spacetime}
=
\text{finite local curvature counterterms}.
$$

The ambiguities are not arbitrary functions of spacetime. Local covariance restricts them to local polynomials in the metric, curvature, masses, couplings, and their covariant derivatives with the correct dimension and tensor character.

## Core idea

In perturbative QFT, singularities arise when operator insertions collide. In Minkowski space one often diagnoses the problem in momentum space as ultraviolet divergence. In curved spacetime, momentum space is not globally available. The intrinsic statement is instead:

$$
\text{renormalization}=
\text{extension of singular distributions to diagonals}.
$$

For example, a time-ordered product is initially well-defined away from coincident points. The problem is to extend it to all of $M^n$ while preserving causality, local covariance, symmetry, scaling, and the field equation identities. Microlocal analysis gives the language for controlling which products of distributions exist and how singular an extension may be.

This is the curved-spacetime analogue of Epstein–Glaser renormalization. The difference is that all choices must be made locally and geometrically, without using translation invariance or a preferred state.

## Setup and conventions

Let $M=(\mathcal M,g)$ be a globally hyperbolic spacetime and let

$$
P_M=\Box_g+m^2+\xi R
$$

be the scalar field operator. Start with the free field algebra generated by $\Phi_M(f)$ and the causal propagator $E_M$. To discuss Wick products and time-ordered products, one enlarges the algebra to include normally ordered polynomial fields and their derivatives, defined using Hadamard two-point functions or, more invariantly, a Hadamard parametrix.

The local polynomial fields include expressions such as

$$
\Phi^2,
\qquad
\nabla_\mu\Phi\nabla_\nu\Phi,
\qquad
\Phi^4,
\qquad
T_{\mu\nu}.
$$

These symbols are not pointwise products of operator-valued distributions. They are new renormalized fields defined by a set of axioms and extension choices.

The default convention in this volume is $\hbar=c=1$ and mostly-minus metric signature. The discussion below is algebraic and does not depend on choosing a Hilbert-space representation.

## Wick products from Hadamard subtraction

The first step is defining composite fields of the free theory. For the Wick square, the schematic point-splitting formula is

$$
:\!\Phi^2\!:_M(x)
\sim
\lim_{y\to x}
\left(
\Phi_M(x)\Phi_M(y)-H_M(x,y)\mathbf 1
\right),
$$

where $H_M$ is the singular Hadamard parametrix. The rigorous object is the smeared field $:\!\Phi^2\!:_M(f)$, not the pointwise expression.

For higher Wick products one subtracts all singular pairings. Symbolically,

$$
:\!\Phi^k\!:
=\text{ordinary product}-\text{Hadamard contractions}.
$$

The use of a Hadamard parametrix rather than a preferred vacuum is essential. Normal ordering with respect to one chosen state is generally not locally covariant, because the state contains global information. Hadamard subtraction isolates the universal local singularity.

Even after imposing local covariance, Wick products are not unique. In four dimensions the Wick square has the finite renormalization freedom

$$
:\!\Phi^2\!:'
=
:\!\Phi^2\!:
+
\bigl(a_1m^2+a_2R\bigr)\mathbf 1,
$$

where $a_1,a_2$ are dimensionless constants. This is not a defect. It is the curved-spacetime version of finite renormalization.

## Time-ordered products and diagonals

Time-ordered products are more singular than Wick products because they encode products of fields at multiple spacetime points. Away from the total diagonal

$$
\Delta_n=\{(x,\ldots,x)\in M^n\},
$$

causal ordering and Wick expansion determine the product recursively. The renormalization problem is the extension to $\Delta_n$ and to all partial diagonals where some subset of points collide.

A time-ordered product is a family of maps, schematically

$$
T_M^{(n)}:
\mathcal P(M)^{\otimes n}\to\mathcal W(M),
$$

where $\mathcal P(M)$ denotes local polynomial expressions and $\mathcal W(M)$ is an enlarged Wick-polynomial algebra. More concretely, one wants distributions corresponding to

$$
T_M\bigl(A_1(x_1)\cdots A_n(x_n)\bigr)
$$

for local fields $A_i$.

The extension is constrained by several principles.

| Principle | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Local covariance | The construction commutes with admissible spacetime embeddings. |
| Causal factorization | Products factor when one set of insertions is later than another. |
| Symmetry | Time-ordered products are graded symmetric in their arguments. |
| Microlocal spectrum condition | Wavefront sets remain in the allowed causal cones. |
| Scaling | The extension has controlled behavior under metric rescaling. |
| Field equation identities | Products respect the free equation, up to controlled local terms. |

These axioms do not eliminate all freedom. They reduce it to finite local covariant counterterms.

## Local covariance forbids arbitrary functions

A common mistake is to think that, because curved spacetime has no translation invariance, renormalization constants may become arbitrary functions $c(x)$. That would make the theory depend on extra background choices not contained in the metric and couplings.

Local covariance forbids this. A finite counterterm must be built naturally from the local geometric and coupling data. For a scalar expression of mass dimension two in four dimensions, the available local covariant terms are

$$
m^2,
\qquad
R.
$$

For dimension four scalar counterterms, the possibilities include terms of the schematic form

$$
m^4,
\qquad
m^2R,
\qquad
R^2,
\qquad
R_{\mu\nu}R^{\mu\nu},
\qquad
R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma},
\qquad
\Box_g R,
$$

modulo dimension-specific identities and total divergences. Tensor counterterms must have the appropriate tensor character; for the stress tensor they arise from metric variations of local curvature action terms.

Thus curved-spacetime renormalization is not less predictive than flat-spacetime renormalization. It is more explicit about the geometric counterterms that flat space cannot reveal.

## Interacting fields as formal power series

Perturbative algebraic QFT defines interacting fields by Bogoliubov's formula using a local interaction $V$. Formally, introduce the local $S$-matrix

$$
S_M(V)=T_M\left(e^{iV}\right),
$$

understood as a formal power series. The interacting version of a local observable $F$ is obtained from the relative $S$-matrix,

$$
R_V(F)
=
\left.\frac{d}{d\lambda}\right|_{\lambda=0}
S_M(V)^{-1}\star S_M(V+\lambda F).
$$

Here $\star$ is the product in the chosen free-field algebra. This expression is not a convergent nonperturbative definition in general. It is a rigorous formal power series once the time-ordered products have been constructed.

The algebraic point is important: the construction gives interacting local fields without first choosing a vacuum Hilbert space. States can be chosen later, typically from Hadamard state spaces.

## The stress tensor and curvature ambiguities

The stress tensor illustrates the meaning of finite renormalization. Classically, for a scalar field, $T_{\mu\nu}$ is obtained by varying the action with respect to the metric. Quantum mechanically, the expression contains products such as $\nabla_\mu\Phi\nabla_\nu\Phi$ and $\Phi^2$, so it must be renormalized.

Local covariance and conservation do not determine $T_{\mu\nu}$ uniquely. In four dimensions, two acceptable definitions may differ by conserved local curvature tensors and mass-dependent metric terms, schematically

$$
T'_{\mu\nu}=T_{\mu\nu}
+a_1m^4g_{\mu\nu}
+a_2m^2G_{\mu\nu}
+a_3I_{\mu\nu}
+a_4J_{\mu\nu},
$$

where $G_{\mu\nu}$ is the Einstein tensor and $I_{\mu\nu},J_{\mu\nu}$ are conserved tensors obtained by varying local curvature actions such as

$$
\int R^2\,d\operatorname{vol}_g,
\qquad
\int R_{\alpha\beta}R^{\alpha\beta}\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
$$

The trace anomaly is not a contradiction. It is the statement that one cannot generally preserve all classically expected properties, such as tracelessness for a conformally coupled massless scalar, after imposing the quantum requirements of locality, covariance, positivity, and conservation.

## Curved spacetime versus flat spacetime

The curved-spacetime framework clarifies what renormalization really means.

| Flat-spacetime habit | Curved-spacetime lesson |
|---|---|
| Use momentum cutoffs or dimensional regularization. | Renormalization is extension of distributions to coincident points. |
| Normal order with respect to the vacuum. | Subtract the local Hadamard singularity instead. |
| Counterterms are constants. | Counterterms are local covariant curvature polynomials. |
| Translation invariance controls choices. | Local covariance and scaling control choices. |
| The stress tensor is a Noether current. | The stress tensor is a renormalized locally covariant metric-response field. |

Momentum-space methods can still be useful in special backgrounds or local computations. They are not the foundational language on arbitrary curved spacetimes.

## Common pitfalls

**Normal ordering with respect to one state is not locally covariant.** A state may encode global information, such as a thermal bath or cosmological history. A locally covariant Wick product must use local geometric subtraction, with finite local ambiguities.

**Curvature counterterms are not optional decorations.** They are forced by local covariance and dimensional analysis. A theory that is renormalizable in flat spacetime generally needs curvature couplings when placed on curved backgrounds.

**The renormalized stress tensor is not unique.** Conservation, covariance, and correct classical limit still leave finite conserved curvature terms. Physical renormalization conditions must fix them.

**The construction is perturbative.** Algebraic renormalization produces formal power series for interacting fields. It does not by itself prove nonperturbative existence of interacting QFTs in four dimensions.

**Local covariance is stronger than coordinate covariance.** Writing tensor equations in arbitrary coordinates is not enough. The construction must commute with embeddings of spacetimes.

**An anomaly is not merely a bad regulator.** Some classical identities cannot be imposed simultaneously with the quantum axioms. The trace anomaly is the central curved-spacetime example.

## Research status

The scalar-field theory of local covariant Wick products and time-ordered products on globally hyperbolic curved spacetimes is mathematically well established perturbatively. It gives a theorem-level replacement for informal curved-spacetime Feynman rules and characterizes finite renormalization freedom.

Perturbative gauge theories can also be handled, but they require BRST/BV complexes, Ward identities, ghost fields, antifields, and cohomological control of anomalies. The scalar case should be read as the clean model, not the whole story.

The nonperturbative construction of interacting four-dimensional QFTs on general curved spacetimes remains far beyond what these perturbative theorems provide. The framework is nevertheless indispensable: any such construction should reproduce its local covariance, Hadamard behavior, and renormalized composite fields in the appropriate regime.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Classify the Wick-square ambiguity

In four spacetime dimensions, the scalar field has mass dimension $1$. Use local covariance and dimensional analysis to explain why the Wick square can shift by $(a_1m^2+a_2R)\mathbf 1$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The field $\Phi$ has dimension $1$, so $:\!\Phi^2\!:$ has dimension $2$. A finite ambiguity added to it as a multiple of the identity must be a locally covariant scalar of dimension $2$ built from the background data. The mass-squared $m^2$ has dimension $2$, and the scalar curvature $R$ has dimension $2$. Constants have dimension $0$, while terms such as $R^2$ or $m^4$ have dimension $4$. Thus the allowed dimension-two scalar ambiguity is

$$
(a_1m^2+a_2R)\mathbf 1,
$$

up to the chosen list of background couplings and conventions.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Why arbitrary functions are not allowed

Suppose someone proposes

$$
:\!\Phi^2\!:'(x)=:\!\Phi^2\!:(x)+c(x)\mathbf 1
$$

with an arbitrary smooth function $c$. Why does this violate local covariance?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A locally covariant field must be constructed naturally from the spacetime and the theory's background data. An arbitrary function $c(x)$ is extra structure: two isometric neighborhoods with the same metric, mass, and curvature data could be assigned different values of $c$. That would make the field fail to commute with admissible spacetime embeddings. Replacing $c(x)$ by $a_1m^2+a_2R(x)$ is different, because $R$ is naturally determined by the metric and transforms correctly under embeddings.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Causal factorization

Let $F$ and $G$ be local observables whose supports are causally ordered, with $\operatorname{supp}F$ later than $\operatorname{supp}G$. What should causal factorization say schematically about $S(F+G)$?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Causal factorization says that the local $S$-matrix factors according to causal order:

$$
S(F+G)=S(F)\star S(G)
$$

when the support of $F$ lies later than the support of $G$ in the relevant causal sense. This condition is the algebraic replacement for time ordering. It is one of the recursive constraints that determine time-ordered products away from coincident diagonals and restrict the allowed extensions to the diagonals.

</details>
