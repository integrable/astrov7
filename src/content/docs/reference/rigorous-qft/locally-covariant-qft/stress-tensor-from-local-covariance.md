## Summary

In locally covariant QFT, the stress-energy tensor can be characterized without assuming a translation symmetry. It is the infinitesimal generator of the response of the observable algebra to compact changes of the background metric.

With the convention of [Relative Cauchy Evolution](/rigorous-qft/locally-covariant-qft/relative-cauchy-evolution/), define

$$
\delta_M[h](A)
=
\left.\frac{d}{ds}\right|_{s=0}\operatorname{rce}_M[sh](A)
$$

for a compactly supported metric perturbation $h_{\mu\nu}$. When the theory has a locally covariant stress tensor, the derivative is implemented by

$$
\delta_M[h](A)=\frac{i}{2}[T_M(h),A],
\qquad
T_M(h)=\int_M T_M^{\mu\nu}h_{\mu\nu}\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
$$

This is the rigorous curved-spacetime analogue of the physics slogan

$$
T^{\mu\nu}=\text{response of the theory to a change of metric}.
$$

The stress tensor is not merely a Noether current for translations. In a generic curved spacetime there are no translations, but compact metric variations still make sense. Local covariance turns that metric response into a precise algebraic object.

:::caution[What is and is not automatic]
The existence of relative Cauchy evolution follows from local covariance plus the time-slice axiom. The existence of a differentiable metric response and of a stress tensor implementing that response is extra structure or a theorem for a class of models. An arbitrary functor $\mathcal A:\mathsf{Loc}\to\mathsf{Alg}$ need not contain a stress tensor as an operator-valued distribution.
:::

## Core idea

In flat spacetime, one often introduces the stress tensor by Noether's theorem: invariance under translations gives a conserved current $T^{\mu\nu}$. This viewpoint is powerful, but it hides the more invariant meaning of the stress tensor.

The more geometric statement is that the stress tensor measures the variation of the theory with respect to the metric. Classically, if the matter action is $S[\phi,g]$, one can define $T^{\mu\nu}$ by the response convention

$$
\left.\frac{d}{ds}\right|_{s=0}S[\phi,g+s h]
=
\frac12\int_M T^{\mu\nu}[\phi,g]h_{\mu\nu}\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
$$

Quantum mechanically, the action may be only a perturbative device, and observables are operator-valued distributions or algebraic elements. Relative Cauchy evolution replaces the classical variation of the action by an algebraic variation of observables:

$$
g\mapsto g+s h
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
A\mapsto \operatorname{rce}_M[sh](A).
$$

The stress tensor is the local field that implements the derivative of this response.

## Setup and conventions

Let $M=(\mathcal M,g)$ be an object of $\mathsf{Loc}$ and let

$$
h\in C_c^\infty(S^2T^*\mathcal M)
$$

be an admissible compactly supported metric perturbation. We write $M[sh]=(\mathcal M,g+s h)$ for sufficiently small real $s$.

The relative Cauchy evolution is an automorphism

$$
\operatorname{rce}_M[sh]\in\operatorname{Aut}(\mathcal A(M)).
$$

Assume that for every relevant observable $A$ the derivative

$$
\delta_M[h](A)
=
\left.\frac{d}{ds}\right|_{s=0}\operatorname{rce}_M[sh](A)
$$

exists in the topology appropriate to the algebra or representation being used.

We define the smeared stress tensor by pairing a symmetric compactly supported covariant tensor $h_{\mu\nu}$ with a contravariant tensor-density field $T^{\mu\nu}$ as

$$
T_M(h)=\int_M T_M^{\mu\nu}h_{\mu\nu}\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
$$

With this convention, the response formula is

$$
\delta_M[h](A)=\frac{i}{2}[T_M(h),A].
$$

If one varies the inverse metric $g^{\mu\nu}$ instead of $g_{\mu\nu}$, or if one defines relative Cauchy evolution with the opposite ordering, signs and factors of $2$ change. The invariant content is that the stress tensor is the infinitesimal generator of metric response.

## Why translations are not enough

The Noether derivation of $T^{\mu\nu}$ uses spacetime translations. This is perfect in Minkowski space, but it cannot be the fundamental definition in a general globally hyperbolic spacetime. A generic curved spacetime has no nonzero Killing vector fields, no preferred energy, and no canonical Hamiltonian.

Nevertheless, every Lorentzian metric can be varied locally. The variation

$$
g_{\mu\nu}\longmapsto g_{\mu\nu}+s h_{\mu\nu}
$$

is meaningful whenever $h$ is smooth, symmetric, compactly supported, and small enough that global hyperbolicity is preserved. Relative Cauchy evolution then gives a canonical way to compare the theory on $g$ and $g+s h$.

Thus local covariance changes the logic:

| Flat-spacetime route | Locally covariant route |
|---|---|
| Translation symmetry. | Compact metric perturbation. |
| Noether current. | Infinitesimal relative Cauchy evolution. |
| Conserved energy-momentum. | Covariantly conserved local field. |
| Hamiltonian generates time translations. | Smeared $T^{\mu\nu}$ generates metric response. |

The flat-spacetime Noether tensor is then recovered as a special case, not used as the starting point.

## From finite response to infinitesimal generator

Relative Cauchy evolution is a finite comparison. The stress tensor appears when this comparison is differentiable at the unperturbed metric.

Because each $\operatorname{rce}_M[sh]$ is an automorphism, the derivative $\delta_M[h]$ is a derivation:

$$
\delta_M[h](AB)
=
\delta_M[h](A)B+A\delta_M[h](B).
$$

If the derivation is inner, it is implemented by a commutator with some smeared operator. The stress-tensor statement is that the implementing object can be chosen locally and covariantly as $T_M(h)$:

$$
\delta_M[h]=\frac{i}{2}\operatorname{ad}_{T_M(h)}.
$$

Here

$$
\operatorname{ad}_{T_M(h)}(A)=[T_M(h),A].
$$

In many models $T_M(h)$ is not a bounded algebra element. It is an operator-valued distribution, an element of an extended Wick-polynomial algebra, or an unbounded operator on a common invariant domain. This is why the formula should be read in the same careful sense as any formula involving local quantum fields.

## Conservation from covariance

The stress tensor obtained this way is covariantly conserved because pure changes of coordinates cannot change the physics.

Let $X$ be a compactly supported smooth vector field. The metric perturbation induced by the infinitesimal diffeomorphism generated by $X$ is

$$
h_{\mu\nu}=(\mathcal L_X g)_{\mu\nu}
=\nabla_\mu X_\nu+\nabla_\nu X_\mu.
$$

Local covariance says that this perturbation is a change of description, not a physical change of the background. Therefore the corresponding infinitesimal relative Cauchy evolution is trivial:

$$
\delta_M[\mathcal L_X g](A)=0
$$

for all observables $A$, modulo the usual qualifications about domains, representations, and central terms.

Using the stress-tensor formula gives

$$
0=\frac{i}{2}\left[\int_M T^{\mu\nu}
(\nabla_\mu X_\nu+\nabla_\nu X_\mu)
\,d\operatorname{vol}_g,\ A\right].
$$

If $T^{\mu\nu}$ is symmetric, the smeared expression is

$$
\int_M T^{\mu\nu}(\mathcal L_X g)_{\mu\nu}\,d\operatorname{vol}_g
=2\int_M T^{\mu\nu}\nabla_\mu X_\nu\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
$$

Integrating by parts and using compact support of $X$ gives

$$
2\int_M T^{\mu\nu}\nabla_\mu X_\nu\,d\operatorname{vol}_g
=-2\int_M (\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu})X_\nu\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
$$

Since $X$ is arbitrary, one obtains the distributional conservation law

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

This is a good example of the philosophical shift in locally covariant QFT. Conservation is not derived from a global translation symmetry. It follows from functorial covariance under local changes of coordinates.

## Naturality of the stress tensor

A locally covariant field is a natural transformation from a test-function functor to the observable functor. For the stress tensor this means, schematically, that if

$$
\psi:M\to N
$$

is an admissible spacetime embedding, then

$$
\mathcal A(\psi)\bigl(T_M(h)\bigr)
=T_N(\psi_*h),
$$

where $\psi_*h$ denotes the pushed-forward compactly supported test tensor on $N$.

This equation says that the stress tensor is not a separate object chosen independently on each spacetime. It is one locally covariant rule assigning a tensor-valued quantum field to every spacetime.

This naturality property is a strong constraint. It excludes, for example, defining $T_{\mu\nu}$ by subtracting a nonlocal quantity depending on a preferred vacuum state, unless that state itself is chosen by a covariant rule. Generic globally hyperbolic spacetimes have no such preferred state.

## Renormalization ambiguities

The stress tensor is a composite field. Even in free scalar QFT, defining it requires renormalization. Local covariance does not remove the ambiguity; it organizes it.

The allowed changes must be local, covariant, conserved, and compatible with the field equation and scaling requirements. In four-dimensional scalar field theory, a typical ambiguity has the schematic form

$$
T_{\mu\nu}\longmapsto T_{\mu\nu}
+a_1m^4g_{\mu\nu}+a_2m^2G_{\mu\nu}
+a_3{}^{(1)}H_{\mu\nu}+a_4{}^{(2)}H_{\mu\nu},
$$

where $G_{\mu\nu}$ is the Einstein tensor and ${}^{(1)}H_{\mu\nu}$, ${}^{(2)}H_{\mu\nu}$ are conserved local curvature tensors obtained by varying curvature-squared counterterms such as

$$
\int_M R^2\,d\operatorname{vol}_g,
\qquad
\int_M R_{\alpha\beta}R^{\alpha\beta}\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
$$

The constants $a_i$ are renormalization choices. They are not determined by local covariance alone. Physical input, renormalization conditions, or comparison with a more fundamental theory is needed to fix them.

This is the curved-spacetime version of a familiar fact: composite operators are not defined by simply multiplying fields at the same point. Local covariance tells us which counterterms are admissible.

## Trace anomaly

For a classically conformal theory, one might expect

$$
T^\mu{}_\mu=0.
$$

After renormalization on curved spacetimes, this may fail even when conservation and local covariance are preserved. The result is the trace anomaly:

$$
\langle T^\mu{}_\mu\rangle
\neq 0
$$

in general curved backgrounds.

The trace anomaly is not a failure of the relative-Cauchy-evolution idea. It is one of its lessons. Local covariance, conservation, positivity requirements, and scaling cannot always be imposed together with the classical trace condition after renormalization. The stress tensor remembers the short-distance singularity structure of the quantum field.

## Example: scalar field with curvature coupling

For a real scalar field with classical equation

$$
(\Box_g+m^2+\xi R_g)\phi=0,
$$

the classical stress tensor depends on the curvature-coupling parameter $\xi$. The term $\xi R_g\phi^2$ in the action contributes terms involving $G_{\mu\nu}\phi^2$ and second derivatives of $\phi^2$.

In the quantum theory, $\phi^2$ and $T_{\mu\nu}$ require Wick-ordering or a more invariant Hadamard subtraction. Local covariance restricts the subtraction to be built locally and covariantly from the metric, curvature, mass, coupling constants, and the causal structure. The relative Cauchy evolution of the scalar field algebra then has an infinitesimal generator matching the renormalized stress tensor, up to the permitted curvature counterterms.

This example explains why the stress tensor belongs naturally with Hadamard states and microlocal analysis. The singularity structure of the two-point function controls the subtraction needed to define nonlinear observables.

## What the formula does not say

The formula

$$
\delta_M[h](A)=\frac{i}{2}[T_M(h),A]
$$

is compact, but it hides several technical choices.

First, it may hold only on a dense common domain in a Hilbert-space representation. Second, $T_M(h)$ may belong to an enlarged algebra of Wick polynomials rather than to the original bounded algebra of local observables. Third, in gauge theory the physical stress tensor must be compatible with constraints, ghosts, BRST/BV cohomology, or edge modes. Fourth, in topological theories the metric response may be trivial or cohomologically exact.

The right lesson is not that every QFT automatically has a simple stress-tensor operator. The right lesson is that when a stress tensor exists in the locally covariant setting, relative Cauchy evolution gives its intrinsic characterization.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not define the curved-spacetime stress tensor by flat-space translations.** Generic curved spacetimes have no translation group. Metric response is the more general definition.

**Do not treat $T_{\mu\nu}(x)$ as an ordinary operator.** The stress tensor is an operator-valued distribution or a local covariant field. It must be smeared, and products involving it require renormalization.

**Do not ignore curvature counterterms.** Local covariance narrows the ambiguity to local curvature expressions; it does not eliminate all finite renormalization choices.

**Do not confuse conservation with tracelessness.** Covariant conservation can hold even when the trace has an anomaly.

**Do not assume unitary implementation.** A relative Cauchy evolution automorphism may not be implemented by a unitary in every state representation.

**Do not forget sign conventions.** Varying $g_{\mu\nu}$ versus $g^{\mu\nu}$ changes signs. Reversing the convention for relative Cauchy evolution also changes signs.

## Research status

The relationship between relative Cauchy evolution and the stress tensor is established in the original locally covariant framework and in important free-field examples. Hollands–Wald style local covariance gives a systematic account of local Wick polynomials, time-ordered products, renormalization ambiguities, and conservation of the interacting stress tensor in perturbative curved-spacetime QFT.

For interacting nonperturbative QFT in four dimensions, the same conceptual characterization remains a guiding principle, but the existence of the full theory and of the required composite fields is part of the broader constructive problem. For gauge theories, a correct stress tensor must be compatible with the physical observable algebra, constraints, and possible boundary or asymptotic data.

In perturbative quantum gravity, the metric is no longer merely an external background. The relative-Cauchy-evolution viewpoint still helps formulate background dependence and background independence, but the target theory must account for diffeomorphism gauge symmetry and the perturbative nature of the construction.

## Exercises

1. **Derivation property.** Suppose $\delta_M[h]$ is the derivative of $\operatorname{rce}_M[sh]$ at $s=0$. Prove that it is a derivation.

   <details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

   Since $\operatorname{rce}_M[sh]$ is an algebra automorphism,

   $$
   \operatorname{rce}_M[sh](AB)
   =\operatorname{rce}_M[sh](A)\operatorname{rce}_M[sh](B).
   $$

   Differentiate at $s=0$ and use $\operatorname{rce}_M[0]=\operatorname{id}$:

   $$
   \delta_M[h](AB)=\delta_M[h](A)B+A\delta_M[h](B).
   $$

   </details>

2. **Conservation from pure gauge perturbations.** Let $h_{\mu\nu}=\nabla_\mu X_\nu+\nabla_\nu X_\mu$ with $X$ compactly supported. Assuming $T^{\mu\nu}=T^{\nu\mu}$ and $T(\mathcal L_Xg)=0$, derive $\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0$ distributionally.

   <details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

   The smearing is

   $$
   T(\mathcal L_Xg)
   =\int_M T^{\mu\nu}(\nabla_\mu X_\nu+\nabla_\nu X_\mu)
   \,d\operatorname{vol}_g
   =2\int_M T^{\mu\nu}\nabla_\mu X_\nu\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
   $$

   Compact support removes the boundary term, so

   $$
   2\int_M T^{\mu\nu}\nabla_\mu X_\nu\,d\operatorname{vol}_g
   =-2\int_M(\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu})X_\nu\,d\operatorname{vol}_g.
   $$

   If this vanishes for all compactly supported $X$, then $\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0$ as a distribution.

   </details>

3. **Why a preferred vacuum is not needed.** Explain why the formula defining $\delta_M[h]$ uses no state.

   <details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

   Relative Cauchy evolution is an automorphism of the abstract algebra $\mathcal A(M)$. It is built from the functorial algebra maps induced by spacetime embeddings and their time-slice inverses. None of these steps requires choosing a state or a Hilbert-space representation. A state can be used later to evaluate expectation values such as $\omega(T(h))$, but the algebraic response exists before that choice.

   </details>

4. **Renormalization ambiguity.** Why is it reasonable that the stress tensor can be shifted by conserved local curvature tensors times the identity?

   <details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

   The stress tensor is obtained by varying the metric. If the effective action is shifted by a local curvature counterterm, such as $\int R^2 d\operatorname{vol}_g$, its metric variation shifts the stress tensor by a local curvature tensor. Diffeomorphism invariance of the counterterm implies that this tensor is covariantly conserved. Because the counterterm is a c-number functional of the background metric, the resulting shift is proportional to the identity in the quantum algebra. This changes expectation values but not commutators with ordinary observables.

   </details>
