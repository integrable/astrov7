## Summary

Quantum field theory in curved spacetime treats matter quantum mechanically while the spacetime metric is a fixed classical background. The rigorous version is not obtained by choosing a vacuum and building one Fock space. On a generic curved spacetime there may be no time-translation symmetry, no preferred positive-frequency splitting, and no canonical particle interpretation.

The modern rigorous framework is algebraic and local. For each globally hyperbolic spacetime $M=(\mathcal M,g)$, one constructs an algebra of observables $\mathcal A(M)$. States are positive linear functionals on that algebra, and physically reasonable states are usually required to satisfy a Hadamard or microlocal spectrum condition. Local covariance organizes the assignment $M\mapsto\mathcal A(M)$ into a functor.

For the real scalar field, the basic data are a normally hyperbolic operator

$$
P_M=\Box_g+m^2+\xi R,
$$

advanced and retarded Green operators, and the causal propagator

$$
E_M=E_M^{\mathrm{ret}}-E_M^{\mathrm{adv}}.
$$

The field algebra is generated by smeared fields $\Phi_M(f)$ subject to

$$
\Phi_M(P_Mf)=0,
\qquad
[\Phi_M(f),\Phi_M(g)]=iE_M(f,g)\mathbf 1.
$$

This page explains how the rigorous curved-spacetime picture fits together: geometry, hyperbolic PDE, algebras, states, Hadamard singularities, local covariant fields, renormalized composite observables, and the limits of the framework.

## Core idea

Curved-spacetime QFT is what remains of QFT when Minkowski symmetry is removed but locality and hyperbolic propagation are kept.

In Minkowski spacetime, many constructions are tied together by the Poincaré group. The vacuum is selected by translation invariance and the spectral condition. Particles are irreducible representations. Energy positivity controls the analytic structure of correlation functions. The stress tensor is related to translation symmetry.

In a general globally hyperbolic spacetime, these tools are not available in the same form. The rigorous theory replaces them as follows.

| Minkowski-space structure | Curved-spacetime replacement |
|---|---|
| One preferred vacuum | A class of admissible states, usually Hadamard states. |
| Positive-frequency splitting | Microlocal spectrum condition. |
| Poincaré covariance | Local covariance under admissible spacetime embeddings. |
| Time translations | Time-slice axiom and relative Cauchy evolution. |
| Particle interpretation | Local observables and state-dependent detector responses. |
| Normal ordering relative to the vacuum | Locally covariant Wick products with curvature ambiguities. |

The result is more modest than a full quantum theory of gravity and more precise than the formal phrase "put QFT on a curved background." It is a mathematically controlled theory of quantum fields on classical globally hyperbolic Lorentzian manifolds.

## Setup and conventions

We work with smooth, oriented, time-oriented, globally hyperbolic Lorentzian spacetimes. The metric signature convention is the mostly-minus convention of this volume. For scalar fields this leads to the formal equation

$$
(\Box_g+m^2+\xi R)\phi=0,
$$

where $R$ is the scalar curvature and $\xi$ is the curvature-coupling parameter. The special values $\xi=0$ and, in four dimensions, $\xi=1/6$ are respectively minimal and conformal coupling for the massless scalar field.

The key geometric hypothesis is global hyperbolicity. It gives well-posed Cauchy problems and advanced/retarded Green operators for normally hyperbolic equations. It also rules out the most severe causal pathologies, such as closed timelike curves, from the standard framework.

The metric is fixed. This means that the theory includes quantum matter propagating on $(\mathcal M,g)$, but not a quantum superposition of metrics. Semiclassical gravity, where $g$ is sourced by an expectation value such as $\langle T_{\mu\nu}\rangle$, is a further approximation scheme. Quantum gravity is a different problem.

## Linear scalar field as the model construction

The real scalar field illustrates the rigorous construction without hiding the main issues.

Let $P_M=\Box_g+m^2+\xi R$ and let

$$
E_M^{\mathrm{ret}},E_M^{\mathrm{adv}}:C_c^\infty(M)\to C^\infty(M)
$$

be the retarded and advanced Green operators. They satisfy

$$
P_ME_M^{\mathrm{ret/adv}}f=f,
\qquad
E_M^{\mathrm{ret/adv}}P_Mf=f,
$$

with the support properties

$$
\operatorname{supp}E_M^{\mathrm{ret}}f
\subset J_M^+(\operatorname{supp}f),
\qquad
\operatorname{supp}E_M^{\mathrm{adv}}f
\subset J_M^-(\operatorname{supp}f).
$$

The causal propagator

$$
E_M=E_M^{\mathrm{ret}}-E_M^{\mathrm{adv}}
$$

maps compactly supported sources to smooth solutions of the homogeneous equation. It determines the symplectic form on the classical solution space and the commutator in the quantum theory.

The algebraic quantum field is generated by $\Phi_M(f)$ with relations

$$
\Phi_M(af+bg)=a\Phi_M(f)+b\Phi_M(g),
$$

$$
\Phi_M(P_Mf)=0,
$$

$$
\Phi_M(f)^*=\Phi_M(\overline f),
$$

and

$$
[\Phi_M(f),\Phi_M(g)]
=iE_M(f,g)\mathbf 1.
$$

This is the same conceptual pattern as the free scalar field in Minkowski spacetime, but the construction is now local and geometric rather than dependent on Fourier modes or a preferred vacuum.

## States and the loss of a preferred vacuum

A state on $\mathcal A(M)$ is a positive normalized linear functional

$$
\omega:\mathcal A(M)\to\mathbb C.
$$

In flat spacetime, the vacuum is singled out by Poincaré invariance and the spectral condition. In a generic curved spacetime, those criteria do not exist. Even in spacetimes with symmetries, there can be several natural but inequivalent state choices depending on boundary conditions, horizons, asymptotics, and physical preparation.

The rigorous response is not to invent a universal vacuum. It is to specify a physically admissible class of states. For free fields, the central class is the Hadamard states. A quasifree state is determined by its two-point distribution $\omega_2(x,y)$, and the Hadamard condition says that its short-distance singularity has a universal local form. In microlocal language, the condition is expressed by the wavefront set

$$
\operatorname{WF}(\omega_2)=
\left\{
(x,k_x;y,-k_y)
\ \middle|\
(x,k_x)\sim(y,k_y),\ k_x\triangleright 0
\right\}.
$$

Here $(x,k_x)\sim(y,k_y)$ means that $x$ and $y$ are joined by a null geodesic and the covectors are cotangent and parallel transported along it; $k_x\triangleright0$ denotes future-directed positive-frequency covectors.

This condition is the curved-spacetime analogue of positive energy. It is local, covariant, and strong enough to make Wick products and stress tensors definable after renormalization.

## Composite fields and renormalization

Products such as $\Phi_M(x)^2$ or $T_{\mu\nu}(x)$ are not defined by multiplying point fields. They require subtraction of universal singularities. In curved spacetime, the subtraction must be local and covariant, so it may depend on curvature.

The schematic idea is

$$
:\!\Phi^2\!:_M(x)
=
\lim_{y\to x}
\bigl(\Phi_M(x)\Phi_M(y)-H_M(x,y)\mathbf 1\bigr),
$$

where $H_M(x,y)$ is a locally constructed Hadamard parametrix. This formula is only schematic because the rigorous construction uses distributions, test functions, and extension procedures. It captures the essential point: the singular part is universal and geometrically determined, while finite local curvature terms remain as renormalization ambiguities.

For time-ordered products and perturbative interactions, the problem becomes the extension of distributions to coincident-point diagonals. The microlocal and Epstein–Glaser style approach gives a precise meaning to perturbative interacting fields on curved spacetimes, subject to local covariance, scaling, causal factorization, and finite renormalization freedom.

## Locally covariant formulation

The locally covariant framework packages curved-spacetime QFT as a functor

$$
\mathcal A:\mathsf{Loc}\to\mathsf{Alg}.
$$

This has several advantages.

First, it avoids choosing Hilbert spaces too early. A single algebra can have many inequivalent representations, and curved spacetimes often force this flexibility.

Second, it expresses covariance without requiring global symmetries. An admissible embedding $\psi:M\to N$ induces an algebra map

$$
\mathcal A(\psi):\mathcal A(M)\to\mathcal A(N).
$$

Third, it lets fields be defined naturally across all spacetimes. A scalar field is a natural transformation $\Phi:\mathcal D\Rightarrow\mathcal A$, meaning

$$
\mathcal A(\psi)(\Phi_M(f))
=
\Phi_N(\psi_*f).
$$

Fourth, the time-slice axiom replaces ordinary Hamiltonian time evolution. If a subspacetime contains a Cauchy surface, its algebra is isomorphic to the algebra of the full spacetime. Relative Cauchy evolution then measures how the theory responds to compact metric perturbations, and the stress tensor appears as the infinitesimal generator of that response when it exists.

## What can be proved

The strongest rigorous results are for linear fields. For scalar, Dirac, Maxwell-like, and other normally hyperbolic or Green-hyperbolic systems, one can often construct:

- classical solution spaces with symplectic or inner-product structures;
- CCR or CAR algebras of quantum observables;
- functorial algebra assignments on globally hyperbolic spacetimes;
- Hadamard state spaces;
- locally covariant Wick polynomials and stress tensors with controlled ambiguities;
- perturbative interacting fields under suitable renormalization prescriptions.

The word "often" matters. Gauge theories require quotienting constraints or BRST/BV methods. Topological sectors may not be visible in a naive local algebra. Boundaries require boundary conditions and sometimes edge degrees of freedom. Non-globally-hyperbolic backgrounds require extra choices and are not part of the standard category.

## What this framework is not

Curved-spacetime QFT is not quantum gravity. The metric is not quantized. It is also not a complete nonperturbative construction of interacting four-dimensional matter theories on arbitrary curved spacetimes. Perturbative algebraic QFT gives a powerful formal and rigorous perturbative framework, but it does not solve the general constructive existence problem.

It is also not a particle theory in the same sense as flat-spacetime scattering theory. Particle number is generally observer- and state-dependent. Effects such as Hawking radiation and Unruh detector response are real, but their rigorous formulation is usually in terms of states, algebras, KMS properties, correlation functions, and detector models rather than a universal particle basis.

The framework is best understood as a precise middle layer:

$$
\text{fixed classical geometry}
\quad + \quad
\text{quantum local matter}
\quad + \quad
\text{algebraic states and observables}.
$$

## Common pitfalls

**Importing the Minkowski vacuum.** Generic curved spacetimes have no preferred vacuum. A state must be chosen or specified by physical conditions, and Hadamard regularity is a condition on admissibility, not a unique state prescription.

**Equating particles with observables.** Particle concepts may be useful in stationary or asymptotic regimes, but the general theory is formulated in terms of local observables and states.

**Ignoring global hyperbolicity.** The existence of advanced and retarded Green operators, Cauchy evolution, and the time-slice axiom depend on causal well-posedness. Non-globally-hyperbolic spacetimes require extra boundary or chronology choices.

**Treating Hadamard subtraction as ordinary normal ordering.** Normal ordering relative to one state is not generally local and covariant. Hadamard subtraction removes the universal short-distance singularity and leaves finite curvature ambiguities.

**Assuming the stress tensor is unique.** Renormalized stress tensors have finite local curvature ambiguities constrained by conservation, covariance, scaling, and anomaly requirements.

**Confusing fixed-background QFT with semiclassical gravity.** Curved-spacetime QFT takes $g$ as given. Semiclassical gravity adds an equation such as $G_{\mu\nu}=8\pi G\langle T_{\mu\nu}\rangle_{\mathrm{ren}}$, which introduces new consistency and backreaction issues.

## Research status

Linear QFT on globally hyperbolic curved spacetimes is a mature mathematical subject. The algebraic framework, Green-operator construction, quasifree Hadamard states, and microlocal spectrum condition are standard tools. Locally covariant QFT gives a precise way to compare theories on different backgrounds.

Perturbative interacting QFT on curved spacetimes is also highly developed, especially through local covariant Wick products, time-ordered products, and causal perturbation theory. The construction is perturbative: it gives formal power series and renormalized local observables, not generally nonperturbative interacting measures or Hilbert-space theories.

Open and active directions include gauge theories with full global and boundary structure, state-selection principles, semiclassical backreaction, non-globally-hyperbolic situations, algebraic holography, perturbative quantum gravity, and the relation between local covariance and more modern higher-categorical or factorization-algebraic frameworks.

## Exercises

1. **Why no preferred vacuum?** Give one reason why a generic globally hyperbolic spacetime does not have a canonical vacuum state.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A common vacuum-selection criterion in Minkowski spacetime uses time-translation symmetry and positive energy. A generic globally hyperbolic spacetime has no global timelike Killing vector field, hence no distinguished time-translation group and no canonical positive-frequency decomposition. Without such extra structure, there is no natural unique Fock vacuum.

</details>

2. **CCR from the causal propagator.** For the scalar field, explain why $E_M=E_M^{\mathrm{ret}}-E_M^{\mathrm{adv}}$ appears in the commutator.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The causal propagator maps compactly supported sources to homogeneous solutions and encodes the symplectic form on the classical solution space. Quantization turns that symplectic form into the commutator. Therefore the algebraic relation is

$$
[\Phi_M(f),\Phi_M(g)]=iE_M(f,g)\mathbf 1.
$$

The support properties of the retarded and advanced Green operators imply that $E_M(f,g)=0$ when the supports are causally disjoint, giving Einstein causality.

</details>

3. **Hadamard versus normal ordering.** Why is ordinary normal ordering relative to a chosen state not enough for a locally covariant definition of $:\!\Phi^2\!:$?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Normal ordering relative to a chosen state subtracts that state's two-point function. Unless the state is chosen locally and covariantly on every spacetime, the subtraction depends on extra global or state-dependent data. Hadamard subtraction instead removes the universal local singular part determined by the geometry. This is compatible with local covariance, up to allowed finite local curvature ambiguities.

</details>
