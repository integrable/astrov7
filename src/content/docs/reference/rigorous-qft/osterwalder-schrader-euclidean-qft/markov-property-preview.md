## Summary

The Euclidean Markov property says that a local Euclidean field theory has a conditional-independence structure: if a hypersurface separates two regions, then the fields on the two sides are independent once the boundary data on the separating hypersurface are fixed.

In schematic notation,

$$
\mathbb E(F_A G_B\mid\mathcal F_\Sigma)
=
\mathbb E(F_A\mid\mathcal F_\Sigma)
\mathbb E(G_B\mid\mathcal F_\Sigma),
$$

where $F_A$ depends on fields in one region $A$, $G_B$ depends on fields in the other region $B$, and $\mathcal F_\Sigma$ is the information carried by the separating boundary $\Sigma$.

This property is not one of the basic Osterwalder–Schrader axioms in the form used earlier in this chapter. It is a complementary Euclidean expression of locality. Reflection positivity explains how a Hilbert space appears from Euclidean time reflection; the Markov property explains how local Euclidean degrees of freedom glue across boundaries.

## Core idea

The Markov property is the probabilistic face of locality. For a finite-dimensional Markov chain,

$$
X_1-X_2-X_3,
$$

the variables $X_1$ and $X_3$ are conditionally independent given $X_2$. For a Euclidean field, the index set is not a line of times but a continuum of points or regions. The analogue of the middle variable is a separating hypersurface.

If a Euclidean space is split into two regions by a hypersurface $\Sigma$, then a local field theory should behave as though information flows across the split only through $\Sigma$. After conditioning on the field data at $\Sigma$, the two sides no longer communicate.

This is why the Markov property is so useful in constructive QFT and probability. It is a precise version of the path-integral intuition that a local action can be cut and glued:

$$
S_E[\phi]
\approx
S_A[\phi|_A]+S_B[\phi|_B]
\quad
\text{once boundary data on }\Sigma\text{ are fixed.}
$$

The symbol $\approx$ is deliberate. In continuum QFT, fields may not have pointwise boundary values, composite terms may require renormalization, and gauge theories require physical boundary data. The Markov property is a theorem only after the objects involved have been defined precisely.

## Setup and conventions

Let $M=\mathbb R^d$ be Euclidean space. For an open region $U\subset M$, let

$$
\mathcal F_U
$$

denote the sigma-algebra generated by smeared fields $\Phi(f)$ with $\operatorname{supp}f\subset U$. This notation is probabilistic. In a purely correlation-function formulation, the same idea is expressed by saying which test functions are supported in which regions.

Let $A$ and $B$ be closed regions whose intersection is a common boundary-like set

$$
\Sigma=A\cap B,
$$

and suppose $A$ and $B$ cover the region of interest. The Markov property says that the algebras $\mathcal F_A$ and $\mathcal F_B$ are conditionally independent over $\mathcal F_\Sigma$:

$$
\mathbb E(F_A G_B\mid\mathcal F_\Sigma)
=
\mathbb E(F_A\mid\mathcal F_\Sigma)
\mathbb E(G_B\mid\mathcal F_\Sigma)
$$

for suitable bounded or square-integrable observables $F_A$ and $G_B$.

Equivalently, in Hilbert-space language inside $L^2$ of the Euclidean probability space, let $P_U$ be the orthogonal projection onto the closed subspace of functions measurable with respect to $\mathcal F_U$. Then one often writes a projection form of the Markov property as

$$
P_A P_B=P_\Sigma,
$$

under appropriate hypotheses and interpretations. This formula is compact, but it hides domain and boundary subtleties. The conditional-expectation formula is usually more readable on a first encounter.

## The free scalar example

The massive Gaussian free field has covariance

$$
C_m=(-\Delta_E+m^2)^{-1}.
$$

The operator

$$
K=-\Delta_E+m^2
$$

is local: it is a differential operator. Locality of $K$ is the analytic reason the Gaussian field has a Markov property.

For a bounded region $D\subset\mathbb R^d$, the field inside $D$ can be decomposed, conditionally on boundary data, as

$$
\Phi|_D=h_{\partial D}+\Phi_D^0.
$$

Here $h_{\partial D}$ is the solution of the homogeneous elliptic equation

$$
(-\Delta_E+m^2)h_{\partial D}=0
\quad\text{inside }D
$$

with the prescribed boundary data, and $\Phi_D^0$ is an independent Gaussian free field in $D$ with zero boundary condition. This is the continuum analogue of the elementary statement that a Gaussian Markov random field is determined locally by its precision matrix.

The word "boundary data" should be read carefully. In $d\ge2$, the free field is generally a distribution, and its trace on a hypersurface is not an ordinary pointwise function. A rigorous treatment uses Sobolev spaces, Dirichlet forms, or sigma-algebras generated by limiting observables near the boundary. The intuitive formula is correct, but the boundary object is not always an honest classical function.

## Lattice model first

The cleanest way to see the Markov property is to start on a finite lattice. Let $\Lambda$ be a finite graph, and consider real variables $\phi_i$ with Gaussian density

$$
\frac{1}{Z}
\exp\left[
-\frac12\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}(\phi_i-\phi_j)^2
-\frac{m^2}{2}\sum_i\phi_i^2
\right]
\prod_i d\phi_i.
$$

If a set of vertices $\Sigma$ separates $A$ from $B$, then there are no edges directly connecting $A\setminus\Sigma$ to $B\setminus\Sigma$. After the values $\phi_\Sigma$ are fixed, the exponential splits into a factor involving the $A$ variables and a factor involving the $B$ variables:

$$
e^{-S_\Lambda(\phi)}
=
\bigl(e^{-S_A(\phi_A;\phi_\Sigma)}\bigr)
\bigl(e^{-S_B(\phi_B;\phi_\Sigma)}\bigr)
\times
\text{boundary factor}.
$$

This factorization immediately gives conditional independence.

The continuum free field inherits this property in the scaling limit. Constructive QFT often follows the same broad idea, but with renormalization: define cutoff measures, prove estimates uniform in the cutoff, take limits, and then identify the locality or Markov property of the limiting field.

## Markov property versus reflection positivity

Reflection positivity and the Markov property are both Euclidean positivity/locality principles, but they do different jobs.

| Property | What it says | What it gives |
|---|---|---|
| Reflection positivity | Positive-time observables have a positive semidefinite reflected pairing. | Hilbert-space inner product and positive Hamiltonian after OS reconstruction. |
| Markov property | Regions separated by a hypersurface are conditionally independent given boundary data. | Local gluing, transfer-matrix intuition, and probabilistic locality. |

Reflection positivity uses a distinguished time reflection

$$
\theta(\tau,\mathbf x)=(-\tau,\mathbf x).
$$

The Markov property can be formulated for more general separating hypersurfaces. In flat space, reflection positivity is about reconstructing time evolution. The Markov property is about cutting and reassembling local Euclidean fields.

In good examples, the two ideas cooperate. Reflection positivity builds the quantum Hilbert space. Markov locality helps identify how local algebras, domains, and boundary conditions behave.

## A useful finite-dimensional analogy

Consider a centered Gaussian vector

$$
X=(X_A,X_\Sigma,X_B)
$$

with density proportional to

$$
\exp\left(-\frac12 X^T K X\right).
$$

For Gaussian variables, conditional independence between $X_A$ and $X_B$ given $X_\Sigma$ is equivalent to a zero block in the precision matrix:

$$
K_{AB}=0.
$$

This is the finite-dimensional prototype. For a free Euclidean scalar field, the precision operator is

$$
K=-\Delta_E+m^2.
$$

Because $K$ is local, it does not directly couple disjoint regions separated by boundary data. Thus the Gaussian free field is Markov.

By contrast, a Gaussian field with covariance

$$
(-\Delta_E+m^2)^{-s}
$$

has precision operator

$$
(-\Delta_E+m^2)^s.
$$

For non-integer or nonlocal $s$, this operator is generally nonlocal, and the sharp Markov property can fail or require an enlarged boundary extension. This is a good test of the slogan: Markov locality belongs to local differential operators, not to arbitrary covariance kernels.

## Relation to the transfer matrix

A Euclidean lattice theory with reflection positivity often yields a transfer matrix. If time is discretized with lattice spacing $a$, one expects a positive operator $T$ such that

$$
T\approx e^{-aH}.
$$

The Markov property explains why such a transfer matrix is local in time: once the field on a time slice is fixed, the past and future lattice half-spaces factorize. In this sense, the Euclidean field is like a generalized stochastic process in the time direction.

However, the full Euclidean Markov property is more geometric than a one-dimensional Markov process in time. One can ask for conditional independence across spatial cuts, curved hypersurfaces, or boundaries of regions. This is why Markov fields are a natural language for Euclidean locality.

## Interacting fields

For a formal local interaction such as

$$
S_E[\phi]
=
\int_{\mathbb R^d}
\left(
\frac12 |\nabla\phi|^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2+V(\phi)
\right)d^dx,
$$

one expects the Markov property because the action is an integral of a local density. If the region is cut into two parts, the integral splits into two bulk integrals, with communication only through boundary behavior.

In a rigorous interacting continuum theory, this expectation must be handled carefully. The field is distributional, $V(\phi)$ may require Wick ordering, the measure may exist only as a limit, and boundary conditions may renormalize. Low-dimensional constructive models provide important examples where Euclidean locality can be made precise.

A useful conservative phrasing is:

$$
\text{local cutoff Gibbs measures have Markov properties,}
$$

and

$$
\text{continuum Markov properties must be proved after taking limits.}
$$

This keeps the intuition without pretending that the continuum formal expression is already a measure.

## Boundary algebras and defects

In ordinary probability, conditioning on a boundary variable is straightforward. In QFT, boundaries can carry their own degrees of freedom. A boundary condition, interface, or defect may enlarge the information living on $\Sigma$.

For example, gluing two regions in a topological or conformal theory may require summing over boundary sectors rather than merely fixing a scalar boundary value. In gauge theory, the boundary data may include electric flux, holonomy, edge modes, or a choice of extended Hilbert space. In algebraic QFT, the analogous question is expressed through inclusions and intersections of local algebras.

Thus the Markov-property slogan should not be reduced to a single formula in all frameworks. The durable idea is conditional independence over the correct boundary information.

## Common pitfalls

**Pitfall: “Markov means time evolution is memoryless.”** In Euclidean field theory, the Markov property is usually a spatial or spacetime conditional-independence statement across hypersurfaces. It is related to time-slice transfer matrices, but it is broader than a Markov chain in time.

**Pitfall: “Boundary values of fields are ordinary functions.”** Continuum quantum fields are often distributions. Boundary data may be Sobolev-theoretic, limiting, algebraic, or encoded by conditional sigma-algebras.

**Pitfall: “Reflection positivity and Markov locality are the same.”** Reflection positivity reconstructs the Hilbert-space inner product. Markov locality controls gluing across cuts.

**Pitfall: “Every nonlocal effective action is Markov.”** Integrating out degrees of freedom often produces nonlocal interactions. The resulting effective field may fail a sharp Markov property even when the original microscopic theory was local.

**Pitfall: “Gauge fields condition only on the gauge potential at the boundary.”** Gauge-invariant boundary data can include fluxes, holonomies, charges, and edge-mode choices. Naive conditioning on $A_\mu$ may not be gauge invariant.

## Research status

For the free scalar Euclidean field, the Markov property is a standard rigorous fact. It can be seen through Gaussian conditional expectation, through the locality of the operator $-\Delta_E+m^2$, or through the lattice limit.

For many constructive scalar models in low dimensions, Markov or closely related locality properties are part of the rigorous Euclidean picture. But the details are model-dependent and can require renormalized boundary conditions or careful limiting arguments.

For gauge theories, the Markov question is entangled with gauge invariance, constraints, boundary charges, and edge modes. A correct statement must specify the observable algebra and the boundary information being conditioned on. This remains an active meeting point of constructive QFT, lattice gauge theory, algebraic QFT, and modern discussions of extended Hilbert spaces.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Conditional independence in a three-site Gaussian chain

Let $(X_1,X_2,X_3)$ have density proportional to

$$
\exp\left[-\frac12(X_1-X_2)^2-\frac12(X_2-X_3)^2-\frac{m^2}{2}(X_1^2+X_2^2+X_3^2)\right].
$$

Show that $X_1$ and $X_3$ are conditionally independent given $X_2$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Fix $X_2=x_2$. The density as a function of $x_1$ and $x_3$ becomes proportional to

$$
\exp\left[-\frac12(x_1-x_2)^2-\frac{m^2}{2}x_1^2\right]
\exp\left[-\frac12(x_2-x_3)^2-\frac{m^2}{2}x_3^2\right]
$$

times a factor depending only on $x_2$. The conditional density factorizes into a function of $x_1$ times a function of $x_3$. Therefore $X_1$ and $X_3$ are conditionally independent given $X_2$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Precision matrix criterion

For a centered Gaussian vector $(X_A,X_\Sigma,X_B)$ with positive precision matrix $K$, explain why a zero block $K_{AB}=0$ implies conditional independence of $X_A$ and $X_B$ given $X_\Sigma$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The Gaussian density is proportional to

$$
\exp\left(-\frac12 X^T KX\right).
$$

After fixing $X_\Sigma$, the terms involving $X_A$ and $X_B$ contain a direct coupling

$$
X_A^T K_{AB}X_B.
$$

If $K_{AB}=0$, no such direct term remains. The conditional exponent splits into one part depending on $X_A$ and $X_\Sigma$ and another part depending on $X_B$ and $X_\Sigma$. Hence the conditional density factorizes, which is conditional independence.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Harmonic conditional mean

For the massive Gaussian free field in a region $D$, why should the conditional mean $h$ given boundary data satisfy

$$
(-\Delta_E+m^2)h=0
\quad\text{in }D?
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The Euclidean Gaussian action is formally

$$
S_E(\phi)=\frac12\int_D \phi(-\Delta_E+m^2)\phi\,d^dx
$$

up to boundary terms. The conditional mean is the minimizer of this quadratic action among fields with the prescribed boundary data. Varying the field in the interior gives the Euler–Lagrange equation

$$
(-\Delta_E+m^2)h=0.
$$

The random fluctuation around this minimizer has zero boundary condition and covariance given by the Dirichlet Green function.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Nonlocal covariance warning

Why does a Gaussian field with covariance $(-\Delta_E+m^2)^{-1}$ have a sharper Markov interpretation than a Gaussian field with covariance $(-\Delta_E+m^2)^{-1/2}$?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For a Gaussian field, Markov locality is controlled by the precision operator, the inverse covariance. For covariance $(-\Delta_E+m^2)^{-1}$, the precision is the local differential operator $-\Delta_E+m^2$. It couples only infinitesimal neighbors, so boundary conditioning can separate regions.

For covariance $(-\Delta_E+m^2)^{-1/2}$, the precision operator is $(-\Delta_E+m^2)^{1/2}$, which is nonlocal. It can directly couple test functions with separated supports. A sharp Markov property across an ordinary boundary should therefore not be expected without adding extra structure.

</details>
