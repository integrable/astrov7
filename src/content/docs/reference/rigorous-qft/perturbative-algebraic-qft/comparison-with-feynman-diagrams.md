## Summary

Feynman diagrams are not discarded in perturbative algebraic QFT. They are reinterpreted. A diagram is a compact notation for terms in the expansion of time-ordered products, star products, and relative $S$-matrices.

The dictionary is:

| Diagrammatic object | pAQFT meaning |
|---|---|
| line | contraction kernel from a two-point distribution or propagator |
| vertex | functional derivative of a local interaction |
| loop | trace/contraction structure and powers of $\hbar$ |
| divergence | failure of a distribution to extend canonically to a diagonal |
| counterterm | finite local ambiguity supported on a diagonal |
| Feynman rule | special flat-spacetime representation after choices are made |

The important change is logical. In textbook perturbation theory, diagrams often appear to be the definition of the calculation. In pAQFT, the definition is algebraic and local: renormalized time-ordered products and relative $S$-matrices. Diagrams are the resulting bookkeeping device.

This page explains the translation and the limits of the translation. Momentum-space Feynman rules are recovered in familiar flat-spacetime situations, but the pAQFT construction also works locally on curved spacetime, where global momentum conservation and global in/out states may not exist.

## Core idea

The fast slogan is:

$$
\text{Feynman diagrams}
=
\text{pictures of contractions and local insertions}.
$$

That slogan is useful but incomplete. It misses the central rigorous point: contractions of distributions become singular when vertices collide. The real renormalization problem is not that a drawn loop is “infinite.” It is that the distribution assigned to a graph is first defined away from certain diagonals, and one must extend it to those diagonals while preserving locality, covariance, scaling bounds, and identities.

## Setup and conventions

Let $\mathcal A(M)$ be a free pAQFT algebra with product $\star_H$ determined by a chosen two-point distribution $H$ of Hadamard type. For sufficiently regular functionals,

$$
F\star_H G
=
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{\hbar^n}{n!}
\left\langle F^{(n)},H^{\otimes n}G^{(n)}\right\rangle,
$$

with conventional factors of $i$ depending on whether $H$ is chosen as a Wightman, Feynman, or related bidistribution. The displayed formula is schematic: the important point is that the product is built by contracting functional derivatives with a two-point kernel.

A time-ordered product $\cdot_T$ is similarly generated by a Feynman-type bidistribution $H_F$ before renormalization:

$$
F\cdot_T G
\sim
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{\hbar^n}{n!}
\left\langle F^{(n)},H_F^{\otimes n}G^{(n)}\right\rangle.
$$

For local functionals this formula is singular at coincident points. Renormalized time-ordered products $T_n$ are the corrected multilinear maps that replace the naive expression.

We use the convention of this chapter: formal power series are algebraic objects. No convergence of the diagram expansion is assumed.

## The diagram dictionary

For a polynomial interaction such as

$$
V(\phi)=\int d^dx\,g(x)\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi(x)^4,
$$

expanding $\mathcal S(V)$ produces functional derivatives of $V$. Each derivative removes one field factor at a vertex. Pairing derivatives with a propagator produces an edge. A graph is a compact way to record all such pairings.

| Diagram item | Algebraic source | Typical formula shadow |
|---|---|---|
| external leg | derivative of an inserted observable | $F^{(1)}$ or a source derivative |
| internal line | contraction by a two-point kernel | $H_F(x,y)$ or $\Delta_F(x-y)$ |
| vertex | derivative of the interaction | $\delta^k V/\delta\phi(x)^k$ |
| symmetry factor | number of identical contractions | $1/|\operatorname{Aut}\Gamma|$ |
| loop | closed contraction pattern | powers of $\hbar$ and coincident integrations |
| counterterm vertex | local extension ambiguity | derivatives of $\delta_\Delta$ |

The graph is therefore not an independent object. It is a decorated record of functional derivatives, contractions, integrations, and local extension choices.

## Where divergences become diagonals

Consider the second-order contribution in a scalar interaction. Away from the diagonal $x=y$, a typical kernel may look like

$$
t^\circ(x,y)=\Delta_F(x-y)^2.
$$

The difficulty is not drawing the loop. The difficulty is extending $t^\circ$ to $x=y$. Epstein–Glaser renormalization asks for an extension $t$ with controlled scaling degree. If two admissible extensions exist, their difference is local:

$$
t'-t
=
\sum_{|\alpha|\le \omega}
 c_\alpha\,\partial^\alpha\delta(x-y).
$$

In diagram language, this is a counterterm. In pAQFT language, it is finite local freedom in the time-ordered product. The pAQFT statement is sharper because it identifies exactly where the ambiguity lives and which axioms constrain it.

This also explains why coordinate-space causal renormalization is well suited to curved spacetime. Coincidence loci and wavefront sets are local geometric notions; global Fourier transforms are not.

## Momentum-space Feynman rules as a special case

The familiar momentum-space rules require extra structure:

| Extra structure | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Minkowski translation invariance | gives Fourier modes and momentum conservation |
| a preferred vacuum state | fixes the two-point functions used in contractions |
| an adiabatic or scattering limit | connects local observables to global amplitudes |
| a renormalization scheme | fixes the finite local ambiguity |
| asymptotic particles | makes LSZ and S-matrix language applicable |

When these conditions are available, the pAQFT expansion reproduces the usual perturbative expressions. Vertices become momentum-space factors. Lines become propagators. Integrals over internal positions become momentum-conserving delta functions and loop integrals.

When these conditions are absent, the diagrammatic combinatorics can still exist, but the usual momentum-space rules do not. On a general globally hyperbolic curved spacetime, a line is a bidistribution, a vertex is a local functional derivative integrated against the metric volume, and renormalization is the local extension of distributions on configuration spaces.

:::note[Good use of diagrams]
A Feynman diagram is excellent notation for a term in a perturbative expansion. It is a poor substitute for stating the algebra, the product, the time-ordering prescription, the support condition, and the renormalization convention.
:::

## Comparison table

| Question | Textbook diagrammatics | pAQFT answer |
|---|---|---|
| What is the basic object? | Amplitudes or Green functions. | Local algebras, functionals, products, relative $S$-matrices. |
| What is an interaction? | A vertex in a diagram expansion. | A compactly supported local functional $V$. |
| What is a propagator? | A line with momentum-space denominator. | A bidistribution with specified wavefront behavior. |
| What is renormalization? | Regulate divergent integrals and add counterterms. | Extend distributions to diagonals with finite local ambiguity. |
| What is a counterterm? | Extra vertex cancelling scheme-dependent divergences. | Local term supported on coincident configurations. |
| Where is locality used? | Often implicit in the Lagrangian and momentum rules. | Explicitly through causal factorization and support properties. |
| What does the construction prove? | Usually a calculational rule. | Coefficientwise formal local observables satisfying structural axioms. |

Neither column makes the other obsolete. The textbook column is optimized for computations in particle physics. The pAQFT column is optimized for stating what the perturbative construction means.

## Example: φ⁴ at one loop

In flat spacetime, the one-loop correction to a two-point function in $\phi^4$ theory is drawn as a tadpole. In algebraic terms, it comes from contracting two field factors inside a time-ordered product involving $V$ and an inserted quadratic or linear observable. The contraction kernel is evaluated at coincident points, so the naive expression contains a singular object such as $\Delta_F(x,x)$.

In a momentum cutoff calculation, this appears as a divergent loop integral. In Epstein–Glaser form, the same issue is the extension of a distribution to the diagonal. The local ambiguity has the form of a mass counterterm and, depending on dimension and power counting, possibly other local terms.

Thus the diagram says “there is a tadpole contribution.” The rigorous statement says “there is a local extension problem whose ambiguity is a local field polynomial compatible with the axioms.”

## This is the most important part of this page

A Feynman diagram is a representation, not the foundation. The pAQFT foundation is the sequence

$$
\text{functionals}
\longrightarrow
\star\text{-product}
\longrightarrow
T_n
\longrightarrow
\mathcal S(V)
\longrightarrow
\mathcal S_V(F)
\longrightarrow
R_V(F).
$$

Diagrams enter when these operations are expanded in contractions. Their combinatorics is the same combinatorics that physicists use every day. The difference is that pAQFT keeps track of the domain of definition, the singular diagonals, the locality axiom, the finite renormalization freedom, and the status of the construction as a formal series.

That difference is not cosmetic. It is what lets one say precisely which part of a Feynman calculation is mathematics, which part is a convention, which part is an infrared assumption, and which part belongs to a later nonperturbative problem.

## Common pitfalls

**Thinking pAQFT has no diagrams.** It has diagrams whenever one expands the products and time-ordered products. It simply does not take diagrams as primitive.

**Equating divergence with a bad integral only.** Divergent momentum integrals are one representation of the problem. The local problem is extension of distributions to diagonals.

**Using momentum-space rules on curved spacetime.** Curved spacetime generally lacks global translations and global momentum conservation. The local coordinate-space construction survives.

**Forgetting finite renormalization.** A diagram plus a regulator is not a full answer until the finite local prescription is specified.

**Assuming every diagram computes an S-matrix element.** Diagrams can compute local products, time-ordered products, response functions, or interacting fields. S-matrix interpretation needs extra scattering assumptions.

**Confusing regularization with renormalization.** A regulator is a tool. Renormalization is the choice of admissible local extension or, equivalently, finite local prescription.

## Research status

The relation between Epstein–Glaser/pAQFT constructions and Feynman diagrams is established at the perturbative formal level. The diagrammatic expansion is a derived combinatorial representation of time-ordered products and relative $S$-matrices.

The qualifications are also standard. Diagram expansions need not converge. S-matrix interpretations require scattering assumptions. Gauge theories require BV-BRST constraints. Nonperturbative existence is not supplied by drawing or renormalizing all diagrams order by order.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Identify the algebraic source of a line

In the schematic product

$$
F\star_H G
=
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{\hbar^n}{n!}
\left\langle F^{(n)},H^{\otimes n}G^{(n)}\right\rangle,
$$

which part of the formula corresponds to an internal line in a diagram?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Each copy of $H$ contracts one functional derivative of $F$ with one functional derivative of $G$. Diagrammatically, that contraction is drawn as a line. The endpoints of the line correspond to the derivative slots being paired.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Locality of a counterterm

Suppose two extensions of a distribution on $\mathbb R^d\setminus\{0\}$ differ by

$$
t'-t=c\delta.
$$

What does this difference look like in a Feynman diagram expansion?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

It is a local counterterm vertex. The delta distribution forces the relevant points to coincide, so the difference has no nonlocal propagation. In Lagrangian language it changes the coefficient of a local operator compatible with the dimension and symmetry constraints.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why momentum conservation can disappear

Why should one not expect a factor $(2\pi)^d\delta^{(d)}(\sum_i p_i)$ in a generic curved-spacetime pAQFT construction?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

That delta function comes from translation invariance: integrating a product of plane waves over flat spacetime gives conservation of total momentum. A generic curved spacetime has no global translation symmetry and no preferred global plane-wave basis. The local construction uses bidistributions and test functions instead of global momentum eigenstates.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Diagrams versus definitions

A student says: “A $\phi^4$ theory is defined by summing all $\phi^4$ Feynman diagrams.” What is missing from this statement?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The statement omits the algebra of observables, the state or two-point distribution used for contractions, the time-ordered products, the treatment of coincident singularities, the finite renormalization prescription, the formal status of the expansion, and any infrared or scattering assumptions. The diagrams are useful notation after those structures have been specified.

</details>
