## Summary

A **higher-form global symmetry** is a global symmetry whose charged objects are extended operators rather than only local operators. In $d$ spacetime dimensions, a $q$-form symmetry acts on $q$-dimensional charged operators and is generated by topological operators supported on closed codimension-$q+1$ manifolds.

The compact definition is

$$
\text{$q$-form symmetry}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
U_g(\Sigma^{d-q-1})\text{ acts on }W_q(M^q).
$$

Here $M^q$ is the support of a charged $q$-dimensional operator, and $\Sigma^{d-q-1}$ is the support of the corresponding symmetry operator. The action is detected by linking or crossing. If $\Sigma$ is moved without crossing $M$, correlation functions do not change. If it crosses or links $M$, the charged operator transforms.

Ordinary global symmetry is the special case $q=0$. A zero-form symmetry acts on local operators and is generated by codimension-one symmetry operators. A one-form symmetry acts on line operators such as Wilson lines. A two-form symmetry acts on surface operators. The point is not the spin of a particle, but the dimension of the charged object.

The reason this language is powerful is that it makes symmetry independent of a Lagrangian presentation. Wilson lines, ’t Hooft lines, surface defects, strings, domain walls, and branes can carry charges even when no local field visibly carries them.

## Prerequisites

You should know [Defects and Extended Operators](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/defects-extended-operators/), especially Wilson lines, ’t Hooft lines, surface operators, defect fusion, and line-operator algebras. You should also know the basic idea of a topological operator from [Symmetry Defects and Topological Operators](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/ordinary-global-symmetries/symmetry-defects-and-topological-operators/) and the background-field logic from [Background Fields and Gauging](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/background-fields-and-gauging/).

The mathematical minimum is differential-form notation. A $p$-form is naturally integrated over a $p$-dimensional manifold. A closed form obeys $d\omega=0$. The Hodge star $*$ converts $p$-forms into $(d-p)$-forms after a metric is chosen. Later pages introduce higher-form background fields and gauging more carefully.

## Core idea

For an ordinary global symmetry, a charge can be measured on a spatial surface surrounding a local operator. The classic picture is this: put a symmetry surface around an insertion, shrink or move the surface, and record what happens when it crosses the insertion.

Higher-form symmetry keeps the same idea but changes the dimensions.

A $q$-dimensional charged operator is not surrounded by a codimension-one surface. Instead, it is linked by a codimension-$q+1$ topological operator. The linking is what detects charge.

For an Abelian group-like symmetry, the simplest action looks like

$$
U_\alpha(\Sigma)\,W_n(M)
=
e^{i\alpha n\,\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma,M)}\,W_n(M)
$$

inside correlation functions, where $n$ is the charge of $W_n$ and $\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma,M)$ is the linking number when it is defined. This formula should be read as a model example, not as the most general definition. Non-Abelian, non-invertible, fermionic, and categorical refinements need more structure.

The key features are:

1. $U_g(\Sigma)$ is topological.
2. $W_q(M)$ is charged.
3. The action depends only on topology, such as linking or crossing.
4. The result gives Ward identities and selection rules for extended operators.

This is why higher-form symmetry belongs immediately after defects and extended operators: the charged objects are themselves defects.

## The definition

Let $G^{(q)}$ be a $q$-form global symmetry in $d$ spacetime dimensions. For each symmetry element $g$, there is a topological operator

$$
U_g(\Sigma^{d-q-1}),
$$

where $\Sigma^{d-q-1}$ is a closed submanifold. The operator is **topological** in the sense that correlation functions containing $U_g(\Sigma)$ are invariant under smooth deformations of $\Sigma$ that do not cross charged insertions, boundaries, or singularities.

A charged operator is supported on a $q$-dimensional submanifold $M^q$:

$$
W_q(M^q).
$$

The symmetry acts when $\Sigma^{d-q-1}$ links or crosses $M^q$. For a group-like symmetry,

$$
U_g(\Sigma)U_h(\Sigma)=U_{gh}(\Sigma),
\qquad
U_e(\Sigma)=1.
$$

If the symmetry is invertible, $U_g$ has inverse $U_{g^{-1}}$. Non-invertible symmetries relax this group law and are treated in the next chapter.

:::note[Source note]
The convention used here is the modern convention: a $q$-form symmetry acts on $q$-dimensional charged operators. This is the convention of the generalized-global-symmetry literature. Some older papers discuss the same physics using conserved brane charges, flux operators, center symmetry, or topological currents without the phrase “higher-form symmetry.”
:::

## Ordinary symmetry as the zero-form case

For $q=0$, the charged operators are local operators,

$$
\mathcal O_i(x).
$$

The topological symmetry operator has support dimension

$$
d-q-1=d-1.
$$

Thus an ordinary global symmetry is generated by codimension-one topological operators $U_g(\Sigma^{d-1})$. In canonical language, take $\Sigma$ to be a spatial slice and write

$$
U(\alpha)=e^{-i\alpha Q}.
$$

Moving the codimension-one operator past a local insertion implements the ordinary symmetry action,

$$
\mathcal O_i(x)\mapsto g\cdot\mathcal O_i(x).
$$

This is the first reason the higher-form definition is natural: it recovers ordinary symmetry exactly at $q=0$.

The second reason is that ordinary Ward identities already have a topological meaning. Current conservation says that a charge surface can be deformed, as long as it does not cross charged insertions. Higher-form symmetries generalize this deformation principle.

## Continuous Abelian higher-form currents

For a continuous Abelian $q$-form symmetry, there is a conserved current written as a $(q+1)$-form

$$
J_{q+1}.
$$

The conservation equation is

$$
d\,{*J}_{q+1}=0.
$$

The charge is measured on a closed codimension-$q+1$ spatial surface $\Sigma^{d-q-1}$:

$$
Q(\Sigma)=\int_\Sigma {*J}_{q+1}.
$$

The corresponding symmetry operator is

$$
U_\alpha(\Sigma)=\exp\!\left(-i\alpha\int_\Sigma {*J}_{q+1}\right),
$$

up to the sign convention for unitary transformations. With the conventions used in this volume, this sign is chosen to match $U(\alpha)=e^{-i\alpha Q}$ for ordinary symmetries.

When $q=0$, $J_1=j_\mu dx^\mu$ is the ordinary one-form current, and $Q$ is the integral of $j^0$ over a spatial slice. When $q=1$ in four dimensions, $J_2$ is a two-form current, and the charge is measured on a closed two-dimensional spatial surface.

:::note[Convention-sensitive formula]
Some references define the higher-form current as the Hodge dual current, so their conservation law is written $dJ=0$ instead of $d{*J}=0$. This page uses $J_{q+1}$ for the current form that couples to a background $(q+1)$-form gauge field and writes conservation as $d{*J}_{q+1}=0$.
:::

## Ward identities and selection rules

A higher-form Ward identity says that topological charge operators can be moved freely unless they cross charged extended operators.

For an Abelian symmetry, suppose $W_{n_i}(M_i)$ carry charges $n_i$. Insert $U_\alpha(\Sigma)$ in a correlation function. If $\Sigma$ can be deformed away without crossing anything, the correlator is unchanged. If the deformation crosses the charged operators, the correlator picks up phases:

$$
\left\langle U_\alpha(\Sigma)\prod_i W_{n_i}(M_i)\right\rangle
=
\exp\!\left(i\alpha\sum_i n_i\,\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma,M_i)\right)
\left\langle \prod_i W_{n_i}(M_i)\right\rangle.
$$

If $U_\alpha(\Sigma)$ is also topologically removable, a nonzero correlator requires the total linked charge to vanish:

$$
\sum_i n_i\,\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma,M_i)=0
$$

for every allowed symmetry surface $\Sigma$, with the appropriate mod-$N$ version for a finite symmetry.

This is the higher-form analogue of ordinary charge conservation in correlation functions. The only difference is that “which insertions are inside the sphere?” becomes “which extended insertions are linked by the symmetry operator?”

## Background fields

An ordinary zero-form symmetry couples to a background one-form gauge field $A_1$. A $q$-form symmetry couples to a background $(q+1)$-form gauge field

$$
B_{q+1}.
$$

For a continuous Abelian symmetry, the schematic coupling is

$$
S\mapsto S+\int B_{q+1}\wedge {*J}_{q+1}.
$$

The background gauge transformation is

$$
B_{q+1}\mapsto B_{q+1}+d\Lambda_q.
$$

If the current is conserved, the coupling is invariant under this transformation on closed spacetime, up to boundary terms:

$$
\delta S=\int d\Lambda_q\wedge {*J}_{q+1}
=
(-1)^{q+1}\int \Lambda_q\wedge d{*J}_{q+1}.
$$

Thus conservation of the higher-form current is the infinitesimal statement of background higher-form gauge invariance.

This is the third reason higher-form symmetries are not a metaphor. They can be coupled to background fields and gauged, just like ordinary global symmetries. The fields and transformations have higher degree, but the logic is the same.

## Examples

### Ordinary flavor symmetry

A usual $U(1)$ flavor symmetry is a zero-form symmetry. Its charged operators are local fields or local composite operators. Its current is a one-form $J_1=j_\mu dx^\mu$. Its background field is a one-form $A_1=A_\mu dx^\mu$. The coupling is the familiar source coupling

$$
\int A_1\wedge {*J}_1
=
\int d^dx\,A_\mu j^\mu
$$

up to metric and sign conventions.

This example is included to make the reduction clear: higher-form symmetry is not a new replacement for ordinary symmetry; it is a generalization that contains ordinary symmetry.

### Maxwell theory in four dimensions

Free Maxwell theory in four dimensions has electric and magnetic one-form symmetries in the absence of electrically or magnetically charged dynamical matter. The charged operators are line operators.

With schematic normalizations, the electric and magnetic two-form currents can be taken as

$$
J_e\sim \frac{1}{e^2}F,
\qquad
J_m\sim \frac{1}{2\pi}{*F}.
$$

The conservation equation for $J_e$ follows from the Maxwell equation $d{*F}=0$. The conservation equation for $J_m$ follows from the Bianchi identity $dF=0$.

A Wilson line is charged under the electric one-form symmetry. An ’t Hooft line is charged under the magnetic one-form symmetry. If dynamical electric charges are added, Wilson lines can end on charged particles and the electric one-form symmetry is explicitly broken or screened. If dynamical monopoles are added, the magnetic one-form symmetry is similarly affected.

:::note[Source note: Maxwell normalizations]
Different Maxwell normalizations move factors of $e^2$, $2\pi$, and Hodge stars between the current, charge operator, and line operator. The invariant statement is that the equations of motion and Bianchi identity give two closed fluxes when the corresponding dynamical charges are absent.
:::

### Center symmetry in pure Yang–Mills theory

Pure $SU(N)$ Yang–Mills theory has a $\mathbb Z_N$ one-form center symmetry. Its charged objects are Wilson lines. A Wilson line in representation $R$ transforms by its $N$-ality:

$$
W_R(C)\mapsto z^{k_R}W_R(C),
\qquad
z\in \mathbb Z_N.
$$

Adjoint Wilson lines have zero $N$-ality and are neutral. Fundamental Wilson lines have nonzero $N$-ality and are charged.

This symmetry depends on the matter content. If fundamental dynamical matter is present, a fundamental Wilson line can end on a dynamical particle. The corresponding one-form symmetry is then explicitly broken. This is one reason one-form symmetry is so useful in gauge theory: it knows which line operators are genuine, which can end, and which are screened.

### Discrete one-form symmetry from line operators

Finite higher-form symmetries are common. A $\mathbb Z_N$ one-form symmetry has topological surface operators $U_k(\Sigma)$, with $k\in\mathbb Z_N$, acting on line operators by phases

$$
U_k(\Sigma)W_q(C)
=
\exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i kq}{N}\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma,C)\right)
W_q(C).
$$

A correlation function of charged lines is nonzero only if the total charge linked by every closed symmetry surface vanishes modulo $N$.

This is the direct higher-form analogue of ordinary $\mathbb Z_N$ charge conservation, but with linked lines replacing local insertions.

## Charged operators versus charged excitations

A charged operator and a charged excitation are related but not identical.

A Wilson line is an operator supported on a curve. It can create, probe, or transport an external charged particle. A stringlike excitation in the Hilbert space may be a dynamical object carrying the same higher-form charge. The symmetry definition is usually stated in terms of operators, because operators are what correlation functions directly contain.

For ordinary symmetry, local charged fields and particle states are closely related in weakly coupled examples. For higher-form symmetry, the charged objects are often extended operators or branes, and the corresponding dynamical excitations may or may not exist as finite-energy objects. This distinction matters in gauge theory, condensed matter, and gravity.

## Genuineness, endpoints, and screening

A **genuine** extended operator can be defined on a closed support without needing an attached higher-dimensional surface or endpoint data. Higher-form charges are carried by genuine operators.

If a line can end on a local operator, then its would-be one-form charge is not conserved. In gauge theory this is called screening. For example, a Wilson line in a representation carried by dynamical matter can end on a matter field insertion. Such a line is not a stable charged object for an exact one-form symmetry.

The rule of thumb is:

$$
\text{exact one-form charge}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\text{charged line cannot end locally}.
$$

More generally, a charged $q$-dimensional operator for an exact $q$-form symmetry cannot simply terminate on an allowed $(q-1)$-dimensional operator unless the termination carries compensating data.

This is why higher-form symmetry is sensitive to the spectrum of genuine extended operators, not only to the Lagrangian.

## Breaking patterns

Higher-form symmetries can be unbroken, explicitly broken, spontaneously broken, or anomalous, just like ordinary symmetries. The diagnostics look different because the charged operators are extended.

For a one-form symmetry, expectation values of large line operators often show either perimeter-law or area-law behavior:

$$
\langle W(C)\rangle \sim e^{-T\,\operatorname{Perimeter}(C)}
$$

or

$$
\langle W(C)\rangle \sim e^{-\sigma\,\operatorname{Area}(C)}.
$$

In the standard center-symmetry diagnostic for confining gauge theory, an area law for a fundamental Wilson loop corresponds to unbroken electric center one-form symmetry, while a perimeter law is associated with broken or screened behavior depending on the theory and operator.

For a continuous higher-form symmetry, spontaneous breaking can produce generalized Goldstone modes. For example, a free photon in four dimensions can be understood as a Goldstone mode of a broken one-form symmetry, with important caveats about compactness, charged matter, and infrared behavior.

The later page on generalized symmetry breaking treats these statements systematically. Here the important point is that higher-form symmetry realization is diagnosed by extended operators rather than local order parameters.

## Gauging preview

Gauging a higher-form symmetry means summing over its background $(q+1)$-form gauge fields, with appropriate quantization and gauge equivalence. This operation can change the global form of a gauge theory, alter which line or surface operators are genuine, and create dual higher-form symmetries.

For example, quotienting an $SU(N)$ gauge theory by a subgroup of its center can be understood through gauging a corresponding one-form symmetry. The resulting theory has different allowed line operators and different background-field data.

This is not merely adding a new propagating field. Gauging a higher-form symmetry is an operation on global structure. It can change the theory even when the local Lie algebra and local Lagrangian density look the same.

## Anomaly preview

A higher-form global symmetry can have a ’t Hooft anomaly. The operational meaning is the same as for ordinary symmetry: there is an obstruction to coupling the symmetry to background fields and gauging it in a fully invariant way, possibly after allowed counterterms.

Because higher-form symmetries naturally involve extended operators, their anomalies often reveal themselves through phases of linked defects, incompatible background gauge transformations, or anomaly inflow onto domain walls and boundaries.

A common pattern is a mixed anomaly between an ordinary zero-form symmetry and a one-form symmetry. In such cases, background gauge transformations for the ordinary symmetry and the higher-form symmetry cannot be made simultaneously trivial. This leads to strong constraints on RG flow, phases, and boundary theories.

The detailed anomaly technology lives in the Anomalies, ’t Hooft Anomalies, and Symmetry TFT chapters. This page only records that higher-form symmetries participate in the same anomaly logic as ordinary symmetries.

## Common pitfalls

**Thinking “form” means spin.** A one-form symmetry does not mean a spin-one symmetry. It means the charged operators are one-dimensional.

**Identifying a higher-form global symmetry with a higher-form gauge redundancy.** Global symmetries act on physical operators. Gauge redundancies are descriptions. A higher-form gauge field can have a higher-form gauge redundancy, while the QFT may also have higher-form global symmetries.

**Assuming every Wilson line is charged.** A Wilson line can be neutral, screened, or non-genuine. Charge depends on representation, matter content, global form, and allowed endpoints.

**Ignoring boundaries.** A topological operator that is harmless on closed spacetime may end on or transform boundary degrees of freedom. Boundaries can carry anomaly inflow and can break or realize higher-form symmetries differently.

**Using Lie-algebra data only.** Higher-form symmetry often depends on global information: center, quotient, allowed line operators, magnetic charge lattice, and background bundles.

**Forgetting compactness and quantization.** Continuous higher-form symmetry groups, finite subgroups, and background-field periods depend on compactness choices and charge normalization.

**Conflating higher-form with non-invertible.** Higher-form symmetries may be ordinary group-like symmetries acting on extended operators. Non-invertible symmetry is a further generalization.

## Research status

The basic definition of higher-form global symmetry as symmetry acting on extended charged operators is established and widely used. It has become standard in modern discussions of gauge theory, topological phases, duality, anomalies, and line/surface operators.

Several refinements remain active. Higher-group symmetry describes cases where different form degrees mix. Subsystem and fracton symmetries relax topological deformability. Non-invertible symmetries replace group multiplication by defect fusion. Fermionic and orientation-reversing symmetries require spin/Pin and reflection data. Symmetry TFT packages all these structures in one higher-dimensional framework, but the best pedagogical route is still to master ordinary higher-form symmetries first.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Dimensions of symmetry operators

In $d=4$, what is the support dimension of the symmetry operator for a zero-form, one-form, and two-form symmetry? What charged operators do they act on?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The symmetry operator for a $q$-form symmetry has support dimension

$$
d-q-1.
$$

In $d=4$:

- For $q=0$, the symmetry operator has dimension $3$ and acts on local operators.
- For $q=1$, the symmetry operator has dimension $2$ and acts on line operators.
- For $q=2$, the symmetry operator has dimension $1$ and acts on surface operators.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Higher-form selection rule

Let a $\mathbb Z_N$ one-form symmetry act on line operators $W_{q_i}(C_i)$ by charge $q_i\in\mathbb Z_N$. Suppose a closed symmetry surface $\Sigma$ links $C_i$ with linking number $\ell_i$. Derive the condition for a nonzero correlator.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The symmetry surface acts by the phase

$$
\exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i}{N}\sum_i q_i\ell_i\right).
$$

If the surface can be removed topologically, the correlator must equal itself times this phase. Therefore a nonzero correlator requires

$$
\sum_i q_i\ell_i=0\mod N.
$$

This is the one-form analogue of ordinary charge conservation modulo $N$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Maxwell one-form currents

In four-dimensional Maxwell theory without dynamical charges, explain why the equations $dF=0$ and $d{*F}=0$ imply two conserved one-form symmetries.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A one-form symmetry in $d=4$ has a two-form current $J_2$ obeying

$$
d{*J}_2=0.
$$

The Maxwell equation $d{*F}=0$ and the Bianchi identity $dF=0$ give two closed fluxes. Depending on whether one chooses $J_2\sim F$ or $J_2\sim {*F}$, these become electric and magnetic one-form currents, with normalizations fixed by the electric coupling and Dirac quantization.

The corresponding charges are measured on closed two-dimensional surfaces. They act on line operators: Wilson lines for electric charge and ’t Hooft lines for magnetic charge.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Screening and endpoints

Why does adding dynamical matter in the fundamental representation of $SU(N)$ break the $\mathbb Z_N$ electric one-form center symmetry of pure Yang–Mills theory?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In pure Yang–Mills theory, a fundamental Wilson line is a genuine line operator charged under the center one-form symmetry. If dynamical fundamental matter is present, the Wilson line can end on a dynamical charged field. Then the would-be one-form charge carried by the line is not conserved: the line can terminate locally.

An exact one-form symmetry requires charged line operators that cannot end on allowed local operators. Fundamental matter provides such endpoints, so the center one-form symmetry is explicitly broken or screened.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Reduction to ordinary symmetry

Show that the higher-form conservation law

$$
d{*J}_{q+1}=0
$$

reduces to ordinary current conservation for $q=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $q=0$, the current is a one-form

$$
J_1=j_\mu dx^\mu.
$$

The Hodge dual ${*J}_1$ is a $(d-1)$-form. The equation

$$
d{*J}_1=0
$$

is the differential-form version of

$$
\partial_\mu j^\mu=0.
$$

The charge is

$$
Q=\int_{\Sigma^{d-1}} {*J}_1,
$$

which is the usual integral of $j^0$ over a spatial slice.

</details>
