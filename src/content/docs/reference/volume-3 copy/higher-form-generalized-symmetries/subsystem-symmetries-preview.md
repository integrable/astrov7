## Summary

A **subsystem symmetry** is a symmetry whose transformation is supported on a rigid lower-dimensional region of space, such as a row, column, plane, or leaf of a foliation. It is neither an ordinary global symmetry, which acts on all of space, nor a gauge redundancy, which acts independently at every point.

For example, in a two-dimensional lattice model, one might have independent transformations on each row and each column:

$$
U^{x}_i=\text{symmetry acting on row }i,
\qquad
U^{y}_j=\text{symmetry acting on column }j.
$$

This is very different from a single global transformation acting everywhere. It is also different from a higher-form symmetry: higher-form symmetry operators are usually **topological**, while subsystem symmetry operators are often **rigid**. They can move along prescribed submanifolds, but not freely through space.

The reason this page is only a preview is that subsystem symmetries are more sensitive to microscopic structure than ordinary higher-form symmetries. Many important examples live naturally on lattices, foliations, anisotropic continuum theories, or systems with restricted mobility. The full physical story belongs partly to matter physics, fracton phases, lattice models, and nonrelativistic field theory. Here we explain the symmetry language needed before returning to ordinary higher-form symmetry breaking.

## Prerequisites

Read [Higher-Form Symmetries](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/higher-form-generalized-symmetries/higher-form-symmetries/), [Charged Extended Operators](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/higher-form-generalized-symmetries/charged-extended-operators/), and [Higher-Group Symmetries](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/higher-form-generalized-symmetries/higher-group-symmetries/) first.

You should know the distinction between a symmetry generator and a charged operator. You should also know that a $q$-form symmetry in $d$ spacetime dimensions is generated by codimension-$q+1$ topological operators and acts on $q$-dimensional charged operators. Subsystem symmetry modifies that story by replacing topological deformability with rigid geometric support.

## Core idea

The cleanest slogan is:

$$
\text{subsystem symmetry}
=
\text{symmetry on a rigid family of subspaces}.
$$

For an ordinary zero-form symmetry in $D$ spatial dimensions, the charge is integrated over all space:

$$
Q=\int_{\mathbb R^D} d^D x\, j^0(x).
$$

For a higher-form symmetry, the charge is integrated over a closed surface whose position can be smoothly deformed without changing the answer, as long as it does not cross charged operators.

For a subsystem symmetry, the charge may instead be attached to a rigid submanifold $L$, such as a line or plane:

$$
Q_L=\int_L \text{charge density}.
$$

The label $L$ is part of the symmetry data. If $L$ is a row of a lattice, moving it to a nearby diagonal curve is not an allowed deformation. If $L$ is a plane in a foliated continuum theory, moving it off the foliation may not make sense.

This rigidity is the central difference from ordinary higher-form symmetry.

## Three axes of comparison

Subsystem symmetries are easiest to understand by comparing three axes.

| Symmetry type | Generator support | Deformability | Typical charged objects |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ordinary zero-form | All space, or codimension-one spacetime defect | Topological in the Ward-identity sense | Local operators and particles |
| Higher-form | Closed codimension-$q+1$ submanifold | Topological | Extended $q$-dimensional operators |
| Subsystem | Rigid line, plane, row, column, leaf, or fractal subset | Usually rigid or foliation-preserving | Local or extended objects with constrained mobility |

The difference is not merely vocabulary. Topological higher-form symmetry produces linking Ward identities. Subsystem symmetry often produces conservation laws tied to moments, rows, planes, or foliations. These conservation laws can restrict the motion of excitations.

## Simple lattice example

Consider a square lattice with Ising variables $\sigma_{i,j}=\pm 1$. A model may have independent row flips

$$
\sigma_{i,j}\mapsto -\sigma_{i,j}
\quad\text{for all }j\text{ at fixed }i,
$$

and independent column flips

$$
\sigma_{i,j}\mapsto -\sigma_{i,j}
\quad\text{for all }i\text{ at fixed }j.
$$

The symmetry group is roughly a product of many $\mathbb Z_2$ factors, one for each row and column, subject to possible redundancies. The important point is that the symmetry is not a single global $\mathbb Z_2$. It is also not a local gauge symmetry, because the transformation cannot be chosen independently at every site. It is supported on rigid one-dimensional subsystems.

If an operator flips a single spin, it may be charged under both a row symmetry and a column symmetry. A product of spin flips arranged along a line may be neutral under some subsystem charges but charged under others. This is why subsystem selection rules can look more geometric than ordinary charge conservation.

:::note[Preview status]
This toy example is intentionally schematic. Real subsystem-symmetric models include plaquette Ising models, compass models, fracton stabilizer models, tensor gauge theories, and foliated field theories. Their precise symmetry groups can have redundancies, boundary subtleties, and lattice-size dependence.
:::

## Subsystem symmetry versus gauge redundancy

Subsystem symmetry is sometimes mistaken for gauge redundancy because it has many generators. The distinction is physical.

A gauge redundancy identifies different descriptions of the same physical state. Gauge-variant operators are not physical observables. By contrast, a subsystem symmetry can be a genuine physical global symmetry. Operators may transform nontrivially under it, selection rules may follow from it, and it may be spontaneously broken or anomalous in suitable generalized senses.

The safe diagnostic is:

$$
\text{global or subsystem symmetry acts on physical data};
\qquad
\text{gauge redundancy removes descriptive data}.
$$

However, subsystem symmetries sit close to the boundary where generalized versions of Elitzur-type arguments become important. A symmetry acting on too small a subsystem may be impossible to break in the same way as an ordinary global symmetry. This is one reason subsystem symmetry breaking is subtle.

## Subsystem symmetry versus higher-form symmetry

Both subsystem and higher-form symmetries involve extended symmetry operators. The crucial distinction is **topological freedom**.

A one-form symmetry operator in four dimensions is supported on a closed two-surface. It can be deformed freely as long as it does not cross charged line operators. Its action depends only on linking data.

A planar subsystem symmetry operator in three spatial dimensions may be supported on a plane $x=x_0$. It can often be translated to a parallel plane only if the theory has the corresponding foliation symmetry, and it cannot be bent into an arbitrary curved surface. Its support is part of the microscopic or geometric structure.

So the contrast is:

$$
\text{higher-form symmetry: topological support},
\qquad
\text{subsystem symmetry: rigid or foliated support}.
$$

There are hybrid situations. Some foliated topological phases have operators that are topological within leaves but not between leaves. Some subsystem symmetries become higher-form symmetries after coarse-graining, gauging, or duality. This is why the boundary is active research rather than a closed textbook classification.

## Conservation laws and mobility constraints

Subsystem symmetries often imply unusual conservation laws. The most famous consequence is **restricted mobility**.

Suppose a theory conserves not only total charge

$$
Q=\int d^D x\, \rho(x),
$$

but also a dipole moment

$$
P^i=\int d^D x\, x^i\rho(x).
$$

Moving an isolated charge changes the dipole moment. If the dipole moment is exactly conserved, an isolated charge cannot move freely by any local operator. Such an excitation is called a **fracton**. Bound states with zero net charge but nonzero dipole structure may move only in restricted directions.

Not every subsystem symmetry is a fracton symmetry, and not every fracton model is most naturally described by this continuum dipole example. Still, it captures the key idea: symmetry constraints can restrict not only what can be created, but where it can move.

This is one reason subsystem symmetries are important for phases of matter. They can protect degeneracies, constrain dynamics, produce subdimensional excitations, and organize exotic gauge-like theories.

## Foliated viewpoint

A useful continuum language treats subsystem symmetries as tied to a foliation of spacetime or space. A foliation is a decomposition into leaves, such as planes of constant $x$, constant $y$, or constant $z$.

A planar subsystem symmetry might have charges

$$
Q_x(x_0),\qquad Q_y(y_0),\qquad Q_z(z_0),
$$

one for each leaf. A charged excitation is characterized by how it intersects or violates these leafwise charges.

The foliation viewpoint explains why subsystem symmetry is not usually Lorentz-invariant in the ordinary relativistic sense. A preferred family of leaves selects spatial directions. This is natural in many-body systems and lattice models, but it means subsystem symmetries should not be treated as ordinary relativistic internal symmetries without care.

:::note[Source note]
In the modern fracton literature, subsystem and foliated symmetries are often described using exotic tensor gauge fields, foliated gauge fields, or lattice stabilizer constraints. This page only introduces the symmetry logic; it does not choose one formalism as canonical.
:::

## Background fields and gauging preview

Ordinary global symmetries are probed by background gauge fields. Higher-form symmetries are probed by higher-form background gauge fields. Subsystem symmetries also admit background-field ideas, but the background fields are often constrained by the rigid geometry.

For a row symmetry, a background may assign holonomies or twists to each row. For a planar subsystem symmetry, one may need background fields living along leaves of a foliation. For fracton-like tensor gauge theories, the background fields may have restricted gauge transformations, such as transformations involving second derivatives.

Gauging subsystem symmetry can produce fracton phases. The rough analogy is:

$$
\text{gauging ordinary global symmetry}
\longrightarrow
\text{ordinary gauge theory},
$$

while

$$
\text{gauging subsystem symmetry}
\longrightarrow
\text{fracton-like gauge structure}.
$$

This analogy is useful, but incomplete. Subsystem gauging can depend on lattice geometry, foliation data, boundary conditions, and the exact class of allowed operators. It is more geometry-sensitive than gauging an ordinary internal symmetry.

## Anomalies and SSPT phases

Subsystem symmetries can have anomaly-like obstructions. The phrase “subsystem anomaly” is used in the literature for obstructions to realizing a subsystem symmetry in a strictly lower-dimensional standalone system, or for phases whose boundary realizes a subsystem symmetry anomalously.

The corresponding symmetry-protected phases are often called **subsystem symmetry-protected topological phases**, or SSPT phases. They are analogous to ordinary SPT phases but protected by subsystem rather than ordinary global symmetry.

There is an important caveat. Because subsystem symmetries are tied to geometry, the anomaly data may depend on foliation structure, lattice regularization, or restricted classes of defects. The general theory is less standardized than ordinary ’t Hooft anomalies or finite-group SPT phases.

For this volume, the main lesson is:

$$
\text{subsystem symmetry can have obstruction data,}
$$

but its mathematical packaging is not yet as uniform as the packaging of ordinary and higher-form anomalies.

## Relation to fractons

Fracton phases are phases with excitations whose mobility is restricted: immobile fractons, lineons that move along lines, and planons that move in planes. Subsystem symmetries are one route into this physics.

The connection is not one-to-one. Some subsystem-symmetric systems are not fracton phases. Some fracton phases are better described by emergent gauge constraints, foliated topological order, tensor gauge theories, or stabilizer-code language. But subsystem symmetry provides a useful organizing principle because it makes mobility restrictions look like selection rules.

A single isolated fracton may be unable to move because moving it would violate a conserved subsystem charge or moment. A dipole may move in directions that preserve all relevant moments. A lineon may move only along the line compatible with its charge pattern.

The result is a new kind of symmetry thinking: conservation laws constrain the geometry of motion, not only total particle number.

## Common pitfalls

**“Subsystem symmetry is just higher-form symmetry.”** Not usually. Higher-form symmetry operators are topological; subsystem symmetry operators are often rigid and tied to rows, planes, leaves, or fractal subsets.

**“Subsystem symmetry is gauge symmetry.”** Not by default. It can be a physical global symmetry with charged physical operators. Gauging it may produce a gauge-like theory, but the original symmetry need not be redundancy.

**“Many symmetry generators mean local gauge redundancy.”** A row or plane symmetry has many generators, but fewer than an independent transformation at every point. The support dimension matters.

**“Subsystem symmetry is always relativistic.”** Most important examples are lattice or nonrelativistic many-body systems. Relativistic notation can obscure the rigid spatial structure.

**“All fracton physics comes from subsystem symmetry.”** Subsystem symmetry is one organizing route, not the whole subject. Foliated topological order, tensor gauge theory, and stabilizer-code constructions carry additional structure.

**“Subsystem symmetry breaking works just like ordinary symmetry breaking.”** It may not. Generalized Elitzur-type constraints and subdimensional fluctuations can alter what order parameters are allowed.

## Research status

Subsystem symmetry is established as an important organizing idea in lattice models, fracton physics, and some nonrelativistic field theories. Its relationship to ordinary higher-form symmetry is well understood in broad strokes: both involve extended symmetry operators, but subsystem symmetries are typically rigid rather than fully topological.

The active frontier is the general formalism. There is no single universally adopted continuum language covering all subsystem symmetries, fractal symmetries, foliated symmetries, tensor gauge theories, anomalies, and gauging procedures. Different examples are best described by different tools.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Row and column charge

Consider a square lattice with $\mathbb Z_2$ row and column flips. A single spin operator $\sigma_{i,j}$ is odd under row $i$ and column $j$. Under which row and column symmetries is the product

$$
\sigma_{i,j}\sigma_{i,k}
$$

charged?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The operator $\sigma_{i,j}$ is odd under row $i$ and column $j$. The operator $\sigma_{i,k}$ is odd under row $i$ and column $k$.

The product is even under row $i$ because it contains two spins in the same row. It is odd under column $j$ and odd under column $k$, assuming $j\ne k$. It is neutral under all other row and column symmetries.

So the product carries a pair of column charges but no row charge.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Why rigidity matters

Explain why a row symmetry operator in a lattice model is not usually a one-form symmetry operator.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A one-form symmetry operator is topological: it can be smoothly deformed without changing correlation functions, as long as it does not cross charged operators. A row symmetry operator is tied to a specific row of the lattice. It cannot generally be bent into an arbitrary curve or moved to a diagonal path while preserving the symmetry definition. Its support is rigid, so it is a subsystem symmetry operator rather than a topological higher-form symmetry operator.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Dipole conservation and immobility

Suppose total charge $Q$ and dipole moment $P^x$ are conserved. Show that moving a single charge $q$ by a distance $a$ in the $x$ direction changes $P^x$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The dipole moment is

$$
P^x=\int d^D x\, x\rho(x).
$$

Moving a point charge $q$ from $x$ to $x+a$ changes the dipole moment by

$$
\Delta P^x=q(x+a)-qx=qa.
$$

If $P^x$ is exactly conserved and $q a\ne 0$, the move cannot be performed by an allowed local process. This is the simplest selection-rule intuition behind fracton immobility.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Subsystem or ordinary global?

A symmetry flips all spins in a two-dimensional lattice. Another symmetry flips all spins in any chosen row. Which one is ordinary global and which one is subsystem?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The transformation flipping all spins is an ordinary global $\mathbb Z_2$ symmetry. It acts uniformly on the whole spatial system.

The transformation flipping a chosen row is a subsystem symmetry. Its support is a rigid one-dimensional subspace of the two-dimensional lattice.

</details>
