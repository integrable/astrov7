## Summary

A Wilson line asks: what phase does an electrically charged probe acquire when it is parallel transported through a gauge field? An ’t Hooft line asks the magnetic dual question: what happens if the path integral is forced to contain a magnetic flux tube along a curve?

For a curve $C$ in a four-dimensional gauge theory, an ’t Hooft line $T_B(C)$ is a **disorder operator**. It is not usually written as a simple polynomial in the fields. Instead, it is defined by a singular boundary condition on the gauge field near $C$. In an abelian normalization where a small two-sphere $S^2_{\rm link}$ links the line once,

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{S^2_{\rm link}}F=m\in \mathbb Z.
$$

In a nonabelian theory the magnetic charge is a cocharacter $B:U(1)\to G$, modulo gauge equivalence and Weyl transformations. Dyonic Wilson–’t Hooft lines combine magnetic and electric labels. These labels are not decorative; they distinguish gauge theories with the same local Lagrangian density but different global form, allowed defects, dualities, and phase diagnostics.

## Setup and conventions

We use the volume convention

$$
D=d-iA,
$$

with Hermitian generators. Locally $A=A^aT^a$ is a Lie-algebra-valued one-form, and

$$
F=dA-iA\wedge A.
$$

For an abelian gauge field this reduces to $F=dA$. A Wilson line of electric charge $q$ is

$$
W_q(C)=\exp\!\left(iq\oint_C A\right),
$$

and Dirac quantization requires magnetic fluxes to pair integrally with all allowed electric charges.

:::note[Convention-sensitive source note]
Some references use anti-Hermitian generators and write $F=dA+A\wedge A$. Others absorb the gauge coupling into $A$ or into the electric charge. The invariant statement is not the sign in a local formula; it is the holonomy, the flux through a linking sphere, and the integrality of electric-magnetic pairings.
:::

The cleanest geometric setting is a four-dimensional spacetime $M_4$ and an oriented curve $C\subset M_4$. Remove a thin tubular neighborhood of $C$. The boundary of the removed tube is locally

$$
C\times S^2_{\rm link},
$$

where $S^2_{\rm link}$ is a small two-sphere in the three directions transverse to the line.

An ’t Hooft line is an instruction to integrate over gauge fields on $M_4\setminus C$ with prescribed magnetic behavior on this small $S^2$.

## The abelian prototype

Start with compact $U(1)$ gauge theory. An ’t Hooft line of magnetic charge $m\in\mathbb Z$ along $C$ imposes

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{S^2_{\rm link}}F=m.
$$

Equivalently, near a straight line, the gauge field looks like the connection of a Dirac monopole in the transverse three-space. In spherical coordinates $(r,\vartheta,\varphi)$ around the line,

$$
F=\frac{m}{2}\sin\vartheta\,d\vartheta\wedge d\varphi+\cdots,
$$

so that

$$
\int_{S^2}F=2\pi m.
$$

On northern and southern patches one may write

$$
A_N=\frac{m}{2}(1-\cos\vartheta)d\varphi,
\qquad
A_S=-\frac{m}{2}(1+\cos\vartheta)d\varphi,
$$

with

$$
A_N-A_S=m\,d\varphi.
$$

A charge-$q$ Wilson line going around the Dirac string changes by

$$
\exp\!\left(iq\oint m\,d\varphi\right)=\exp(2\pi i qm).
$$

Thus $qm\in\mathbb Z$ for every genuine electric charge $q$. In a normalization where the minimal electric charge is $q=1$, magnetic charges are integers.

The phrase "singular boundary condition" is important. The line operator is not the physical worldline of a smooth finite-energy monopole particle. It is an external probe: the path integral is evaluated in the presence of an imposed magnetic singularity.

## Disorder versus order operators

A Wilson line is an order operator in the sense that it is written directly as a functional of the gauge field:

$$
W_R(C)=\operatorname{Tr}_R P\exp\!\left(i\oint_C A_R\right).
$$

An ’t Hooft line is a disorder operator: it changes the class of field configurations being summed over. This distinction is sharp in a lattice gauge theory. A Wilson loop multiplies a configuration by a holonomy around $C$. An ’t Hooft loop modifies the plaquette variables on a surface ending on $C$, creating a thin magnetic-flux insertion. Moving the auxiliary surface is invisible except when it crosses electrically charged operators; the physical operator is supported on its boundary $C$.

This is the same conceptual move as in two-dimensional spin systems, where a disorder operator is defined by changing couplings along a branch cut ending at the insertion. The branch cut is not the operator; its endpoint or boundary is.

## Nonabelian magnetic charges

For a compact nonabelian gauge group $G$, a magnetic line is specified locally by embedding the abelian monopole singularity into a maximal torus $T\subset G$.

The magnetic charge is a cocharacter

$$
B\in\Lambda_{\rm cochar}(T)=\operatorname{Hom}(U(1),T),
$$

or equivalently an element of the Lie algebra of $T$ satisfying

$$
\exp(2\pi i B)=1.
$$

Near the line one imposes

$$
F=\frac{B}{2}\sin\vartheta\,d\vartheta\wedge d\varphi+\cdots .
$$

Gauge transformations identify magnetic charges related by the Weyl group:

$$
B\sim w(B),\qquad w\in W_G.
$$

This is the first place where the global form of the gauge group becomes unavoidable. The Lie algebra alone does not tell us the cocharacter lattice. For example, theories with gauge algebra $\mathfrak{su}(N)$ but different gauge groups, such as $SU(N)$ and $SU(N)/\mathbb Z_N$, can have the same local gluon fields and the same local stress tensor but different genuine line operators.

A useful slogan is:

$$
\text{electric labels live in weights},\qquad
\text{magnetic labels live in coweights/cocharacters}.
$$

The exact lattice depends on the global form and on which line operators are declared genuine.

## Wilson–’t Hooft lines

A general line in a four-dimensional gauge theory can carry both electric and magnetic data. In a simplified semiclassical notation one writes a Wilson–’t Hooft line as

$$
L_{\lambda,B}(C),
$$

where $B$ is a magnetic cocharacter and $\lambda$ is an electric weight. The pair is identified under the Weyl group,

$$
(\lambda,B)\sim (w\lambda,wB).
$$

This notation is enough for many physics discussions, but there is a precision caveat. Once a magnetic singularity $B$ is imposed, the gauge group along the line is reduced to the stabilizer of $B$. The electric label should really be a representation of this stabilizer, not always a representation of the whole group $G$. For broad orientation, the pair $(\lambda,B)$ is the right mental model; for classification the stabilizer matters.

:::note[Source note]
Kapustin’s analysis of Wilson–’t Hooft operators in four-dimensional gauge theories makes this classification precise in terms of electric and magnetic weights, Weyl equivalence, and the action of shifts of the theta angle. Aharony–Seiberg–Tachikawa emphasize the complementary point that different consistent choices of line operators can define distinct four-dimensional gauge theories even when local operators agree.
:::

## Mutual locality and linking

Line operators can fail to commute when they are linked. In an abelian theory, an electric Wilson line $W_q(C)$ and a magnetic ’t Hooft line $T_m(C')$ obey the schematic linking relation

$$
W_q(C)T_m(C')
=
\exp\!\left(2\pi i qm\,\operatorname{Link}(C,C')\right)
T_m(C')W_q(C).
$$

In a nonabelian theory the phase is replaced by the appropriate electric-magnetic pairing. For center charges in an $SU(N)$-type theory one often sees the schematic relation

$$
W_e(C)T_m(C')
=
\exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i}{N}em\,\operatorname{Link}(C,C')\right)
T_m(C')W_e(C).
$$

This is not merely a curiosity. A set of genuine line operators in a local four-dimensional gauge theory must satisfy mutual locality conditions. Different maximal mutually local choices can correspond to different global forms and discrete theta-like parameters.

The phrase "genuine line" means that the line can be defined by data on the line itself, without choosing an auxiliary higher-dimensional surface. Some lines are only endpoints of surface operators. Those are still important, but they are not genuine line operators of the standalone theory.

## Theta angle and the Witten-effect shift

In a theory with theta term, magnetic probes acquire induced electric charge. In abelian language, a monopole of magnetic charge $m$ has electric charge shifted by

$$
q_{\rm eff}=q+\frac{\theta}{2\pi}m,
$$

up to sign conventions for the theta term. Shifting

$$
\theta\mapsto\theta+2\pi
$$

therefore sends

$$
(q,m)\mapsto(q+m,m)
$$

in the simplest normalization.

This is the Witten effect expressed as a statement about line-operator labels. In nonabelian theories the shift acts on the lattice of Wilson–’t Hooft charges and can permute distinct global-form choices.

:::note[Convention-sensitive source note]
The sign of the Witten-effect shift depends on whether the action contains $+\theta F\wedge F/(8\pi^2)$ or the opposite sign, and on Lorentzian versus Euclidean conventions. The invariant statement is that a $2\pi$ theta shift relabels dyonic line charges by adding magnetic charge to electric charge.
:::

## Phase diagnostics

Wilson and ’t Hooft loops are complementary probes of long-distance gauge dynamics. In a pure gauge theory, a large loop can show an area law or perimeter law:

$$
\langle L(C)\rangle\sim e^{-\sigma\operatorname{Area}(C)}
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
\langle L(C)\rangle\sim e^{-\mu\operatorname{Length}(C)}.
$$

Very roughly:

| Phase intuition | Wilson loop | ’t Hooft loop |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Electric confinement | area law | perimeter law |
| Higgs / magnetic confinement | perimeter law | area law |
| Coulomb phase | power-law refinements | power-law refinements |

This table is a diagnostic, not a theorem in all theories. Dynamical matter can screen Wilson lines. Dynamical monopoles can screen ’t Hooft lines. Massless fields produce power-law factors. Boundaries and finite temperature change the interpretation. Still, the complementary behavior of electric and magnetic line operators is one of the cleanest ways to organize phases of gauge theory.

## Relation to one-form symmetries

A Wilson line may be charged under an electric one-form symmetry. An ’t Hooft line may be charged under a magnetic one-form symmetry. In four-dimensional Maxwell theory without dynamical electric or magnetic charges, there are two continuous one-form symmetries with currents

$$
J_e=\frac{1}{g^2}*F,
\qquad
J_m=\frac{1}{2\pi}F.
$$

Electric Wilson lines are charged under the electric one-form symmetry; magnetic ’t Hooft lines are charged under the magnetic one-form symmetry. Adding dynamical charged matter explicitly breaks or reduces these symmetries because the corresponding lines can end on dynamical particles or monopoles.

This is why line operators are not just observables. They are the charged objects around which generalized symmetries organize the theory.

## Boundaries, endpoints, and dynamical monopoles

A closed ’t Hooft line is a genuine insertion if its magnetic charge is allowed by the theory. An open ’t Hooft line needs an endpoint that absorbs magnetic charge. Possibilities include:

- a dynamical monopole operator or monopole particle,
- a boundary condition that supports the necessary magnetic flux,
- an interface across which the allowed line-operator lattice changes,
- a surface operator whose boundary is the line.

This is the magnetic analog of an open Wilson line ending on charged matter. An open line by itself is usually not a gauge-invariant observable.

The endpoint issue is not cosmetic. It determines whether a one-form symmetry is exact, explicitly broken, or gauged.

## How to use ’t Hooft lines in practice

For many calculations the workflow is:

1. Remove a tubular neighborhood of the proposed line $C$.
2. Impose the magnetic singularity on the boundary $C\times S^2$.
3. Add any required boundary counterterms or supersymmetric scalar singularities.
4. Integrate over gauge fields on the complement.
5. Check gauge invariance under small and large gauge transformations.
6. Check mutual locality with the other genuine line operators.

In semiclassical calculations, the leading contribution often comes from solving the classical equations with the singular boundary condition. In supersymmetric theories, protected Wilson–’t Hooft lines can sometimes be computed by localization. In lattice gauge theory, ’t Hooft loops are often implemented by twisting plaquettes on a surface whose boundary is the loop.

## Common pitfalls

### Mistaking a singularity for a pathology

A disorder operator is defined by a controlled singularity. The singularity is part of the operator definition, not a sign that the calculation is broken.

### Forgetting global form

The gauge algebra does not determine the allowed line operators. $SU(N)$ and $SU(N)/\mathbb Z_N$ gauge theories can have identical perturbative gluon Feynman rules but different Wilson and ’t Hooft line spectra.

### Calling every magnetic object an ’t Hooft line

A smooth finite-energy monopole particle, a local monopole operator in three dimensions, a magnetic flux sector, and a four-dimensional ’t Hooft line are related but not identical objects. The line operator is an insertion supported on a curve.

### Ignoring auxiliary surfaces

Some definitions use a surface ending on the line. The physical line is independent of deformations of that surface only if crossing phases and anomaly constraints are treated correctly.

### Treating area laws as absolute phase labels

Area/perimeter behavior is a powerful diagnostic, but screening by dynamical matter, massless fields, finite temperature, and operator renormalization can modify the naive table.

## Research status

The basic definition of ’t Hooft lines as magnetic disorder operators is standard. What remains rich is the structure they generate: categories of line defects, duality actions, global-form choices, boundary conditions, higher-form symmetries, noninvertible defects obtained by gauging, and protected line defects in supersymmetric theories. Modern line-operator technology is one of the places where old gauge-theory intuition and modern symmetry language genuinely meet rather than politely wave at each other from across the hallway.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Dirac quantization from patching

Use the abelian monopole potentials

$$
A_N=\frac{m}{2}(1-\cos\vartheta)d\varphi,
\qquad
A_S=-\frac{m}{2}(1+\cos\vartheta)d\varphi
$$

on the two patches of $S^2$. Show that a Wilson line of charge $q$ is well defined only if $qm\in\mathbb Z$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

On the overlap of the two patches,

$$
A_N-A_S=m\,d\varphi.
$$

A charge-$q$ wavefunction is related by the gauge transformation

$$
\psi_N=e^{iqm\varphi}\psi_S.
$$

Going once around the equator sends $\varphi\mapsto\varphi+2\pi$, so single-valuedness requires

$$
e^{2\pi iqm}=1.
$$

Therefore $qm\in\mathbb Z$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Linking phase in abelian theory

Assume a charge-$q$ Wilson loop $W_q(C)$ links once with a magnetic ’t Hooft loop $T_m(C')$. Explain why their correlation function obtains a phase $e^{2\pi iqm}$ when the Wilson loop is moved around the magnetic insertion.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The ’t Hooft line imposes magnetic flux

$$
\int_{S^2_{\rm link}}F=2\pi m.
$$

A Wilson loop linked with the magnetic insertion measures the gauge holonomy induced by that flux. In a patch description, moving the electric loop around the magnetic singularity crosses the transition function between monopole patches. The Wilson phase changes by

$$
\exp\!\left(iq\oint m\,d\varphi\right)=e^{2\pi iqm}.
$$

For mutually local genuine lines this phase must be compatible with the operator algebra of the theory.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why global form matters

Suppose two gauge theories have the same Lie algebra $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ but different gauge groups $SU(2)$ and $SO(3)=SU(2)/\mathbb Z_2$. Explain why they can agree on local perturbative physics but disagree on allowed line operators.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Local perturbative fields transform in the adjoint representation, which only sees the Lie algebra. The center of $SU(2)$ acts trivially on adjoint fields, so the local gluon Lagrangian does not distinguish $SU(2)$ from $SO(3)$.

Line operators probe global data. Wilson lines are labeled by representations allowed by the gauge group; $SU(2)$ permits half-integer spin Wilson lines, while $SO(3)$ does not. Magnetic charges are also changed because the cocharacter lattice depends on the global form. Therefore the two theories can have the same local equations and different genuine line spectra.

</details>
