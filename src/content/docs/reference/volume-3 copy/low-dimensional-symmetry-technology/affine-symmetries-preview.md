## Summary

An **affine symmetry** is the infinite-dimensional symmetry generated by the Laurent modes of a chiral current in two-dimensional QFT or CFT. If

$$
J^a(z)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}J_n^a z^{-n-1},
$$

and the current OPE is

$$
J^a(z)J^b(w)
\sim
\frac{k\,\kappa^{ab}}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{i f^{ab}{}_{c}J^c(w)}{z-w},
$$

then the modes obey

$$
[J_m^a,J_n^b]
=
if^{ab}{}_{c}J_{m+n}^c
+
k\,m\,\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

This is the **affine Kac–Moody algebra** associated with the finite-dimensional Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$ at level $k$.

The zero modes $J_0^a$ generate the ordinary Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$. The nonzero modes are the extra local symmetry data made possible by holomorphy. The central extension proportional to $k$ is not optional: it records the short-distance singularity of the current algebra and often the anomaly coefficient.

## Prerequisites

You should know [Current Algebras](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/low-dimensional-symmetry-technology/current-algebras/), [Bosonization](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/low-dimensional-symmetry-technology/bosonization/), and the basic idea of an OPE. Some familiarity with Lie algebras and representations is helpful.

We use holomorphic coordinates $z,\bar z$ and write contour modes as

$$
J_n^a=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint dz\,z^nJ^a(z).
$$

The invariant bilinear form is denoted $\kappa^{ab}$. The level $k$ depends on this normalization.

## Core idea

Start with an ordinary Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$:

$$
[T^a,T^b]=if^{ab}{}_{c}T^c.
$$

In two dimensions, a chiral current $J^a(z)$ produces infinitely many modes $J_n^a$. If one ignored the central extension, these would form the loop algebra

$$
\mathfrak g\otimes \mathbb C[t,t^{-1}],
$$

with bracket

$$
[T^a t^m,T^b t^n]=if^{ab}{}_{c}T^c t^{m+n}.
$$

Quantum mechanically, the local current OPE forces a central extension:

$$
[J_m^a,J_n^b]
=
if^{ab}{}_{c}J_{m+n}^c
+
k\,m\,\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

That central extension is the affine algebra. In physics language, it is the algebra of chiral current modes at level $k$.

The finite-dimensional symmetry is recovered by keeping only the zero modes:

$$
[J_0^a,J_0^b]=if^{ab}{}_{c}J_0^c.
$$

The affine algebra therefore contains the ordinary symmetry but is much more restrictive.

## From current OPE to affine algebra

The derivation is one of the cleanest contour computations in CFT.

Given

$$
J^a(z)J^b(w)
\sim
\frac{k\kappa^{ab}}{(z-w)^2}
+
\frac{i f^{ab}{}_{c}J^c(w)}{z-w},
$$

define modes

$$
J_m^a=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_0 dz\,z^mJ^a(z).
$$

The commutator is obtained by taking one contour around the other:

$$
[J_m^a,J_n^b]
=
\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^2}
\oint_0dw\,w^n
\oint_wdz\,z^mJ^a(z)J^b(w).
$$

The simple pole gives the Lie-algebra term. The double pole gives

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_0dw\,w^n\,k\kappa^{ab}\partial_w(w^m)
=
k\,m\,\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

Thus the central term is exactly the residue of the double pole.

:::note[Source note: signs and $i$]
Some references use anti-Hermitian Lie algebra generators and write the simple-pole term without an explicit $i$. This page uses Hermitian generators, matching the convention $[T^a,T^b]=if^{ab}{}_{c}T^c$.
:::

## Level

The number $k$ is called the **level**. It measures the normalization of the central extension.

In many compact WZW models, $k$ is a nonnegative integer. The integrality comes from the quantization of the Wess–Zumino term and is tied to global topology of the group. In free-fermion realizations, $k$ is determined by the representation and number of chiral fermions.

For a simple compact Lie algebra and the usual CFT normalization, unitarity of integrable highest-weight representations requires

$$
k\in\mathbb Z_{\ge0}.
$$

This statement has assumptions: compactness, positive-definite inner product, standard normalization, and the class of integrable modules. Noncompact, supergroup, logarithmic, and nonunitary theories require more careful language.

The level controls:

| Feature | Role of $k$ |
|---|---|
| Current OPE | Coefficient of the double pole. |
| Mode algebra | Coefficient of the central term. |
| WZW action | Quantized Wess–Zumino coupling. |
| Sugawara stress tensor | Appears in $k+h^\vee$. |
| Anomaly coefficient | Controls background-gauge variation in chiral settings. |
| Allowed integrable modules | Bounds highest weights in compact unitary cases. |

## Highest-weight modules

Affine symmetry organizes the Hilbert space into modules. A highest-weight state $|\lambda\rangle$ obeys

$$
J_n^a|\lambda\rangle=0\qquad n>0,
$$

and the zero modes act in a representation $\lambda$ of $\mathfrak g$:

$$
J_0^a|\lambda\rangle=(T^a_\lambda)|\lambda\rangle.
$$

Negative modes create descendants:

$$
J_{-n_1}^{a_1}\cdots J_{-n_r}^{a_r}|\lambda\rangle,
\qquad n_i>0.
$$

This is analogous to Virasoro descendants, but generated by current modes rather than stress-tensor modes.

In compact WZW models at level $k$, not every finite-dimensional representation of $\mathfrak g$ is allowed as an integrable affine primary. For $\widehat{\mathfrak{su}}(2)_k$, the allowed spins are

$$
j=0,\frac12,1,\ldots,\frac{k}{2}.
$$

That finite list is why rational WZW models have finitely many primary sectors.

## Affine primaries

A local field $\Phi_\lambda(w)$ is an affine primary in representation $\lambda$ if

$$
J^a(z)\Phi_\lambda(w)
\sim
\frac{-(T^a_\lambda)\Phi_\lambda(w)}{z-w},
$$

with no higher pole. In mode language this says

$$
J_n^a|\Phi_\lambda\rangle=0\quad n>0,
$$

and $J_0^a$ acts by the finite-dimensional representation $\lambda$.

Affine primaries are the building blocks of WZW correlators. Their descendants are obtained by acting with negative current modes.

The key point is that affine symmetry is stronger than ordinary global symmetry. Ordinary $\mathfrak g$ symmetry labels fields by zero-mode representations. Affine symmetry additionally controls infinitely many current descendants and imposes differential equations on correlators.

## Sugawara construction

For a simple Lie algebra, the current algebra can build a stress tensor. The **Sugawara stress tensor** is

$$
T(z)
=
\frac{1}{2(k+h^\vee)}
\kappa_{ab}:J^aJ^b:(z),
$$

where $h^\vee$ is the dual Coxeter number and $\kappa_{ab}$ is the inverse bilinear form in the chosen normalization.

The central charge is

$$
c=\frac{k\,\dim\mathfrak g}{k+h^\vee}.
$$

This is one of the great miracles of affine symmetry: if the current algebra is known, a conformal stress tensor can often be constructed from it.

The formula is normalization-sensitive. If $\kappa$ or generator conventions change, the displayed constants must be translated. The invariant content is that the stress tensor is quadratic in currents with a coefficient fixed by the level and Lie algebra.

:::caution[Sugawara is not automatic in every theory]
The Sugawara stress tensor is canonical for simple affine current algebras in WZW-type settings. A theory may have additional degrees of freedom, coset stress tensors, non-semisimple currents, or improvement terms. Always check whether the full stress tensor equals the Sugawara tensor or only contains it as a subalgebra.
:::

## WZW models

The Wess–Zumino–Witten model is the canonical Lagrangian realization of affine symmetry.

The field is a map

$$
g:\Sigma\to G
$$

from a two-dimensional worldsheet to a Lie group. The action contains a sigma-model term plus a Wess–Zumino term. At quantized level $k$, the theory has chiral currents

$$
J(z),\qquad \bar J(\bar z),
$$

generating two commuting affine algebras:

$$
\widehat{\mathfrak g}_k\oplus \widehat{\mathfrak g}_k.
$$

Primary fields transform under left and right affine symmetries. Their correlation functions are highly constrained and satisfy the Knizhnik–Zamolodchikov equations.

The WZW model is a central example because it simultaneously connects:

- current algebra,
- topology of the Wess–Zumino term,
- conformal field theory,
- representation theory,
- anomalies and boundary degrees of freedom,
- rational CFT and modular tensor categories.

This is why affine symmetry appears throughout QFT, statistical mechanics, string theory, and condensed matter.

## Knizhnik–Zamolodchikov equation preview

For affine primary fields $\Phi_i(z_i)$ in representations $R_i$, WZW correlators obey a first-order differential equation. Schematically,

$$
(k+h^\vee)\partial_{z_i}
\left\langle \prod_\ell \Phi_\ell(z_\ell)\right\rangle
=
\sum_{j\ne i}
\frac{T_i^aT_j^a}{z_i-z_j}
\left\langle \prod_\ell \Phi_\ell(z_\ell)\right\rangle.
$$

This is the **Knizhnik–Zamolodchikov equation**. It is the analytic expression of the fact that current algebra plus the Sugawara stress tensor constrain the motion of insertions.

The page does not derive it fully, but the derivation is conceptually straightforward: use the Sugawara expression for $T(z)$, insert it into the conformal Ward identity, and replace current insertions by their contour actions on affine primaries.

## Coset construction

If a theory has affine symmetry $\widehat{\mathfrak g}_k$ and a subalgebra $\widehat{\mathfrak h}_{\ell}$, one can form a coset CFT by subtracting stress tensors:

$$
T_{\mathfrak g/\mathfrak h}=T_{\mathfrak g}-T_{\mathfrak h}.
$$

The central charge is

$$
c_{\mathfrak g/\mathfrak h}=c_{\mathfrak g}-c_{\mathfrak h}.
$$

Cosets produce many important rational CFTs, including familiar minimal models. They are also a conceptual bridge to gauged WZW models and symmetry reduction.

The coset construction is much richer than this preview: selection rules, field identifications, branching functions, modular invariance, and global issues all matter. Those details belong to the CFT and Bootstrap volume.

## Affine symmetry and anomalies

A chiral current algebra at level $k$ often corresponds to a background-gauge anomaly coefficient. In two dimensions, the same current-current OPE coefficient that appears as the affine level also appears in the anomalous variation of the effective action under background gauge transformations.

This is why affine symmetry is not just “more symmetry.” It is also anomaly data.

In boundary problems, a chiral affine algebra can live on the edge of a three-dimensional Chern–Simons theory. The boundary current algebra then realizes the anomaly inflow of the bulk theory. This is one of the cleanest bridges between current algebra, Chern–Simons theory, and the SymTFT viewpoint.

## Common pitfalls

**Thinking affine symmetry is just many copies of ordinary symmetry.** The central term changes the algebra. It is not merely $\mathfrak g$ at each Fourier mode.

**Forgetting the zero modes.** The ordinary Lie algebra is still present as the $J_0^a$ subalgebra.

**Treating the level as a free real number in compact unitary WZW models.** In standard compact WZW models, the level is quantized. Noncompact and nonunitary examples require separate care.

**Assuming the Sugawara tensor is the full stress tensor.** A theory may have extra sectors or coset degrees of freedom.

**Ignoring global group data.** Affine Lie algebra data does not always determine the global form of the current group, allowed line defects, modular invariant, or spectrum.

**Using affine terminology outside two dimensions without translation.** Affine current algebras are especially tied to chiral currents in two-dimensional CFT. Higher-dimensional current algebras have different structures.

## Research status

Affine Kac–Moody algebras, WZW models, Sugawara stress tensors, and coset constructions are standard and well established.

Active research continues in supergroup WZW models, logarithmic and nonunitary current algebras, non-semisimple affine symmetry, boundary current algebra, current algebra in AdS string worldsheets, categorical descriptions of rational CFT, and relations between affine symmetry, defects, and SymTFT.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Zero modes

Starting from

$$
[J_m^a,J_n^b]
=
if^{ab}{}_{c}J_{m+n}^c
+
k\,m\,\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0},
$$

show that the zero modes obey the ordinary Lie algebra.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Set $m=n=0$. The central term is proportional to $m$, so it vanishes:

$$
k\,m\,\kappa^{ab}\delta_{m+n,0}=0.
$$

Thus

$$
[J_0^a,J_0^b]=if^{ab}{}_{c}J_0^c.
$$

The zero modes generate the original finite-dimensional Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Abelian affine algebra

For a $U(1)$ current with

$$
J(z)J(w)\sim\frac{k}{(z-w)^2},
$$

derive the mode algebra.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

There is no Lie-bracket term because $U(1)$ is abelian. The double pole gives

$$
[J_m,J_n]=k\,m\,\delta_{m+n,0}.
$$

This is the Heisenberg current algebra of a chiral boson in this normalization.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Sugawara central charge for SU(2)

For $\mathfrak{su}(2)$, use

$$
\dim\mathfrak{su}(2)=3,\qquad h^\vee=2
$$

to compute the Sugawara central charge at level $k$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The formula is

$$
c=\frac{k\,\dim\mathfrak g}{k+h^\vee}.
$$

For $\mathfrak{su}(2)$,

$$
c=\frac{3k}{k+2}.
$$

For example, $k=1$ gives $c=1$.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Allowed SU(2) integrable weights

For $\widehat{\mathfrak{su}}(2)_k$, the allowed spins are

$$
j=0,\frac12,1,\ldots,\frac{k}{2}.
$$

How many integrable primary sectors are there?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The allowed spins are separated by steps of $1/2$ from $0$ to $k/2$. Therefore there are

$$
k+1
$$

allowed sectors.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Why affine symmetry is stronger

Explain why knowing the finite Lie algebra representation of a field is not enough to know its affine representation.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The finite Lie algebra only tells us the action of the zero modes $J_0^a$. An affine representation also specifies how positive and negative modes act, the level $k$, highest-weight conditions, descendants, and null states. Two fields can transform in the same finite-dimensional representation under $J_0^a$ but belong to different affine modules if the level or descendant structure differs.

</details>
