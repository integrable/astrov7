## Summary

Theta vacua are what happens when the vacuum sector of a gauge theory has a periodic topological direction. In the simplest Yang–Mills story, there are perturbative vacua $|n\rangle$ labeled by an integer winding number. A large gauge transformation shifts the label,

$$
U_k|n\rangle=|n+k\rangle,
\qquad k\in\mathbb Z.
$$

The physical vacuum is not usually one fixed $|n\rangle$. It is a Bloch-wave superposition

$$
|\theta\rangle=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z} e^{-in\theta}|n\rangle,
$$

which transforms by a phase under large gauge transformations:

$$
U_k|\theta\rangle=e^{ik\theta}|\theta\rangle.
$$

The angle $\theta$ labels a one-dimensional representation of the disconnected part of the gauge-transformation group. Equivalently, it is the coefficient of the theta term that weights instanton sectors by $e^{i\theta Q}$.

The analogy with a particle in a periodic potential is excellent but not perfect. The labels $n$ do not describe different local minima of an ordinary scalar potential. They describe gauge-field configurations related by transformations that are not continuously connected to the identity. The correct statement is not “there are infinitely many unrelated vacua.” The correct statement is: the gauge orbit space has a topological circle-like direction, and quantum mechanics allows a phase around it.

:::note[Convention-sensitive sign]
This page uses the same sign convention as the theta-term page:

$$
Z(\theta)=\sum_Q e^{i\theta Q}Z_Q,
\qquad
|\theta\rangle=\sum_n e^{-in\theta}|n\rangle.
$$

If a source defines the large gauge transformation to shift $n$ in the opposite direction, the sign in the Bloch wave changes. Compare phases $e^{i\theta Q}$ and transformation laws, not isolated signs.
:::

## Core idea

Theta vacua are the Hilbert-space version of theta terms. The path-integral statement is

$$
Z(\theta)=\sum_{Q\in\mathbb Z}e^{i\theta Q}Z_Q.
$$

The Hamiltonian statement is that the vacuum sector is acted on by large gauge transformations, and the physical vacuum can carry a phase under that action.

A useful slogan is

$$
\text{theta angle}
=\text{Bloch quasi-momentum on the topological direction of gauge orbit space}.
$$

This is not just poetic. In temporal gauge, classical finite-energy Yang–Mills configurations at early and late times approach pure gauges. Those pure gauges can have different winding numbers. A finite-action Euclidean solution interpolating between them has instanton number

$$
Q=n_+-n_-.
$$

Thus tunneling amplitudes between the integer-labeled perturbative vacua know about $Q$. Passing to the $|\theta\rangle$ basis diagonalizes the action of large gauge transformations and converts tunneling between $n$-sectors into the phase $e^{i\theta Q}$.

## Vacuum labels in Yang–Mills theory

Consider pure Yang–Mills theory with compact simple gauge group, for example $SU(N)$ with $N\ge 2$, on spatial $\mathbb R^3$. Finite energy requires the field strength to vanish at spatial infinity:

$$
F_{ij}(\mathbf x)\to 0
\qquad |\mathbf x|\to\infty.
$$

A vacuum configuration is therefore locally pure gauge,

$$
A_i = i\,g\partial_i g^{-1},
$$

up to the sign convention used for $D_i=\partial_i-iA_i$. Since spatial infinity compactifies $\mathbb R^3$ to $S^3$, vacuum gauge transformations are maps

$$
g:S^3\to SU(N).
$$

Their homotopy classes are labeled by

$$
\pi_3(SU(N))\cong \mathbb Z.
$$

This integer is the source of the vacuum label $n$. A small gauge transformation is continuously connected to the identity and stays within the same label. A large gauge transformation has nonzero winding and shifts the label.

The same data can be described by the Chern–Simons number. With the volume convention

$$
F=dA-iA\wedge A,
$$

a useful Chern–Simons three-form is

$$
\omega_3(A)=\operatorname{tr}\left(A\wedge dA-{2i\over3}A\wedge A\wedge A\right),
$$

so that

$$
d\omega_3(A)=\operatorname{tr}(F\wedge F).
$$

For a spatial gauge field $A$ define, schematically,

$$
N_{\rm CS}[A]={1\over 8\pi^2}\int_{\mathbb R^3}\omega_3(A),
$$

with boundary conditions chosen so the integral is meaningful. Under a large gauge transformation of winding $k$,

$$
N_{\rm CS}[A^g]=N_{\rm CS}[A]+k.
$$

The value of $N_{\rm CS}$ itself is not gauge invariant under large gauge transformations. Its change modulo integers, and the instanton number connecting early and late times, are the invariant ingredients.

:::note[Source note]
Different texts distribute factors of $i$, $g_{
m YM}$, and signs differently between $A$, $F$, and the Chern–Simons form. This page fixes the normalization through

$$
{1\over 8\pi^2}\int_{X_4}\operatorname{tr}(F\wedge F)\in\mathbb Z
$$

for standard $SU(N)$ bundles on closed oriented four-manifolds. The Chern–Simons representative should always be checked against this integer formula.
:::

## Large gauge transformations and the theta basis

Let $|n\rangle$ denote a perturbative vacuum localized near Chern–Simons number $n$. The shift operator $U_k$ acts as

$$
U_k|n\rangle=|n+k\rangle.
$$

The Hamiltonian commutes with gauge transformations. If $U_k$ is treated as a gauge redundancy, physical states should transform consistently under it. Because the disconnected part is $\mathbb Z$, there are one-dimensional characters

$$
\chi_\theta(k)=e^{ik\theta},
\qquad \theta\sim\theta+2\pi.
$$

The corresponding theta state is

$$
|\theta\rangle=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}e^{-in\theta}|n\rangle,
$$

and indeed

$$
U_k|\theta\rangle=e^{ik\theta}|\theta\rangle.
$$

This looks exactly like a Bloch wave in a periodic lattice. The integer $n$ is the lattice-site label, large gauge transformations are translations, and $\theta$ is the quasi-momentum.

There is a small conceptual trap here. A gauge redundancy usually removes labels rather than producing new quantum numbers. The point is that large gauge transformations can act projectively or by a character on the quantum state space once boundary conditions and theta terms are specified. The gauge-invariant physics is not the choice of $n$; it is the choice of character $\theta$.

## Instantons and tunneling

Instantons are the semiclassical events that mix neighboring $n$-sectors. In Euclidean Yang–Mills theory, the topological charge is

$$
Q={1\over 8\pi^2}\int_{X_4}\operatorname{tr}(F\wedge F).
$$

On a Euclidean spacetime stretching from early time to late time, this can be written as a Chern–Simons-number difference:

$$
Q=N_{\rm CS}(+\infty)-N_{\rm CS}(-\infty).
$$

A unit instanton has $Q=+1$ and changes the vacuum label by one unit in the chosen convention. Antiinstantons have $Q=-1$.

Now evaluate the amplitude in the theta basis. If the transition from $|n\rangle$ to $|m\rangle$ has topological charge $Q=m-n$, then the theta-state overlap contributes

$$
e^{im\theta}e^{-in\theta}=e^{i(m-n)\theta}=e^{i\theta Q}.
$$

Therefore the Hamiltonian theta-vacuum construction produces exactly the theta-term weighting in the path integral.

That is the main equivalence:

$$
\boxed{
\text{Bloch phase under large gauge transformations}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\text{theta term } e^{i\theta Q}
}
$$

The box is worth internalizing, but not worshiping. It assumes the usual integer instanton sectors. The statement can be refined by global form, boundary conditions, spin structure, background fields, and fractional topological charge.

## The periodic potential analogy

The theta-vacuum construction is often compared to a quantum particle moving in a periodic potential. Suppose a particle coordinate $q$ has minima near integers and tunneling connects neighboring wells. Bloch states are

$$
|\theta\rangle=\sum_n e^{-in\theta}|n\rangle,
$$

and the energy can depend on $\theta$ through tunneling.

In a dilute-instanton approximation one often finds a leading contribution of the form

$$
E(\theta)-E(0)\propto 1-\cos\theta,
$$

or equivalently an instanton amplitude proportional to

$$
e^{-S_{\rm inst}}e^{i\theta}+e^{-S_{\rm inst}}e^{-i\theta}.
$$

This captures the phase structure of instanton tunneling, but it should not be mistaken for a controlled approximation in all Yang–Mills theories. Pure four-dimensional Yang–Mills is strongly coupled in the infrared. The dilute gas picture is most reliable in weakly coupled settings or deformed theories where instantons or fractional instantons are parametrically controlled.

## Theta vacua versus superselection sectors

Theta labels different quantum theories, or different superselection sectors of a theory with a fixed choice of how large gauge transformations act. Local gauge-invariant operators cannot usually change $\theta$. They act within a fixed theta sector.

One way to say this is that $\theta$ is a choice of boundary condition in the topological direction of gauge orbit space. Another way is that the theta term is a local density only after choosing a representative, but its real content is global.

This also explains why theta is not seen in perturbation theory around a single $|n\rangle$. Ordinary perturbation theory expands near one local region of configuration space. Theta dependence comes from tunneling or from summing over global sectors.

## CP, time reversal, and special theta values

The Yang–Mills instanton number is odd under orientation reversal. In four-dimensional gauge theory, CP or time reversal sends

$$
Q\mapsto -Q.
$$

Therefore the theta term transforms as

$$
\theta Q\mapsto -\theta Q.
$$

If the periodicity is $\theta\sim\theta+2\pi$, CP or time reversal can be a symmetry only when

$$
\theta\equiv -\theta\pmod{2\pi},
$$

namely

$$
\theta=0,\pi\pmod{2\pi}.
$$

At $\theta=0$, the symmetry is the familiar CP-invariant point. At $\theta=\pi$, the symmetry is restored only because of periodicity. This makes $\theta=\pi$ special, not automatically simple. Depending on the theory, this special point may have spontaneous CP breaking, degenerate vacua, a mixed anomaly involving a one-form symmetry, nontrivial domain-wall physics, or a gapless phase.

This is one reason theta vacua remain useful beyond their textbook instanton origin. They are a clean entry point into the modern idea that topology, global symmetry, and anomaly constraints can determine possible infrared behavior.

## QCD and the strong CP angle

In QCD, the observable CP-violating angle is not just the bare Yang–Mills theta angle. Chiral rotations of quark fields shift the theta angle because of the chiral anomaly. The invariant combination is commonly written

$$
\overline\theta=\theta+\arg\det M_q,
$$

where $M_q$ is the quark mass matrix, up to convention-dependent sign choices.

If a quark were exactly massless, a chiral rotation could remove $\overline\theta$. With all quarks massive, $\overline\theta$ is physical. The empirical smallness of strong CP violation is the strong CP problem.

This page will not solve that problem. Its job is to explain why a parameter that multiplies a total derivative can be physically meaningful in the first place. The short answer is: instantons, large gauge transformations, and the topology of gauge orbit space.

## Global-form and boundary caveats

The clean textbook story assumes ordinary $SU(N)$ bundles on closed oriented four-manifolds and no extra background fields. Several refinements are common in modern QFT.

First, the global form of the gauge group matters. The theories with gauge groups $SU(N)$ and $SU(N)/\mathbb Z_N$ have the same Lie algebra but different allowed bundles and different line operators. Their theta periodicities and discrete theta angles can differ.

Second, background fields for one-form symmetries can fractionalize the instanton number. Then a $2\pi$ shift of $\theta$ may not be a symmetry by itself; it may need to be accompanied by a shift of a discrete counterterm.

Third, manifolds with boundary require boundary degrees of freedom, boundary conditions, or Chern–Simons-like terms to make gauge invariance and unitarity precise. On a manifold with boundary, the phrase “theta term is a total derivative” becomes a warning label, not a dismissal.

Fourth, in fermionic theories the answer can depend on spin or Pin structure. The same local density can have different global completions depending on the allowed backgrounds.

## Common pitfalls

**“Theta vacua are ordinary degenerate vacua.”** Not quite. The $|n\rangle$ states are perturbative labels related by large gauge transformations. The physical theta vacua are Bloch-like superpositions or, equivalently, sectors with a chosen large-gauge character.

**“The theta term is irrelevant because it is a total derivative.”** Locally it is a total derivative. Globally it measures instanton number and changes sector weights in the path integral.

**“Large gauge transformations are always pure redundancy with no physics.”** They are redundancies, but quantum states can transform by characters, and background/boundary data can make their action physically diagnostic.

**“Theta periodicity is always $2\pi$.”** Only after specifying the gauge group, allowed bundles, backgrounds, and spin structure. The standard $2\pi$ story is important, but it is not universal.

**“CP at $\theta=\pi$ means the theory is boring.”** The opposite is often true. The point $\theta=\pi$ is where symmetry and periodicity collide, so anomalies, domain walls, or phase transitions frequently appear.

## Research status

The basic theta-vacuum construction in Yang–Mills theory is established textbook material. The integer-labeled Chern–Simons sectors, instanton number, and theta-term weighting are standard.

The detailed infrared physics as a function of theta is highly theory-dependent and often nonperturbative. Questions at $\theta=\pi$, in QCD-like theories, in theories with one-form symmetries, in sigma models, and in theories with nontrivial global form remain active because the anomaly constraints are sharp but do not uniquely determine the phase.

Modern work often reformulates theta vacua using generalized symmetries, anomaly inflow, symmetry TFT, and topological response. That language does not replace the old Bloch-wave picture; it refines it.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Large-gauge eigenvalue

Let $U_k|n\rangle=|n+k\rangle$ and

$$
|\theta\rangle=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}e^{-in\theta}|n\rangle.
$$

Show that $U_k|\theta\rangle=e^{ik\theta}|\theta\rangle$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute directly:

$$
U_k|\theta\rangle
=\sum_n e^{-in\theta}|n+k\rangle.
$$

Let $m=n+k$, so $n=m-k$. Then

$$
U_k|\theta\rangle
=\sum_m e^{-i(m-k)\theta}|m\rangle
=e^{ik\theta}\sum_m e^{-im\theta}|m\rangle
=e^{ik\theta}|\theta\rangle.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Instanton phase from the theta basis

Assume a transition amplitude from $|n\rangle$ to $|m\rangle$ has topological charge $Q=m-n$. Show that the theta-basis overlap gives the phase $e^{i\theta Q}$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The bra contributes the complex conjugate coefficient $e^{im\theta}$, while the ket contributes $e^{-in\theta}$. Their product is

$$
e^{im\theta}e^{-in\theta}=e^{i(m-n)\theta}=e^{i\theta Q}.
$$

This is the Hamiltonian origin of the path-integral sector weight $e^{i\theta Q}$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: CP-invariant theta values

Assume $Q\mapsto -Q$ under CP and $\theta\sim\theta+2\pi$. Which theta values can preserve CP?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The theta term transforms as $\theta Q\mapsto -\theta Q$. CP can be a symmetry only if $\theta$ and $-\theta$ define the same theory:

$$
\theta\equiv -\theta\pmod{2\pi}.
$$

Thus

$$
2\theta\equiv0\pmod{2\pi},
$$

so

$$
\theta=0,\pi\pmod{2\pi}.
$$

</details>
