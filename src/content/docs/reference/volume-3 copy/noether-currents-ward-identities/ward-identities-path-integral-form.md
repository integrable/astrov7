## Summary

The path-integral proof of a Ward identity is one sentence with a lot packed into it:

$$
\text{an integral does not change when we rename the integration variables.}
$$

Let

$$
\mathcal X=\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)
$$

and consider the Lorentzian path integral

$$
\langle\mathcal X\rangle
=\frac{1}{Z}\int\mathcal D\Phi\,e^{iS[\Phi]}\mathcal X[\Phi].
$$

Make a local infinitesimal change of variables

$$
\Phi^I(x)\mapsto \Phi^I(x)+\alpha^a(x)\delta_a\Phi^I(x).
$$

If the action varies as

$$
\delta S
=\int d^dx\,(\partial_\mu\alpha^a)j_a^\mu
+\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\mathcal B_a
$$

and the measure has Jacobian

$$
\mathcal D\Phi\mapsto \mathcal D\Phi\,
\exp\!\left(i\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\mathcal A_a\right),
$$

then the local functional Ward identity is

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=\langle (\mathcal B_a+\mathcal A_a)(x)\mathcal X\rangle
-i\sum_{k=1}^n\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

For an exact nonanomalous symmetry, $\mathcal B_a=\mathcal A_a=0$, and this reduces to the contact-term Ward identity derived in operator language.

## Prerequisites

Read [Ward Identities: Operator Form](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/noether-currents-ward-identities/ward-identities-operator-form/) first if you want the charge-commutator picture. Read [Noether Currents](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/noether-currents-ward-identities/noether-currents/) if the local-parameter derivation of $j_a^\mu$ is rusty.

We use the Lorentzian weight $e^{iS}$ and the same infinitesimal transformation convention as the rest of this chapter:

$$
\delta_a\mathcal O=i[Q_a,\mathcal O].
$$

A Euclidean version is obtained by replacing $e^{iS}$ by $e^{-S_E}$ and continuing the current conventions consistently. The structural identity is the same, but factors of $i$ move.

## Core idea

The operator proof says:

$$
\text{a charge surface can move until it crosses an insertion.}
$$

The path-integral proof says:

$$
\text{a local change of variables can be performed inside the integral.}
$$

These are the same conservation law in different clothing.

The path-integral viewpoint is especially efficient because it separates the three possible contributions to a Ward identity:

$$
\begin{array}{ccl}
\delta S &\Rightarrow& \text{current conservation or explicit breaking},\\
\delta\mathcal X &\Rightarrow& \text{contact terms at insertions},\\
\delta\mathcal D\Phi &\Rightarrow& \text{possible anomaly}.
\end{array}
$$

This separation is why the functional derivation is the natural doorway to anomalies and background fields.

## Setup and conventions

Let $\Phi^I$ denote all dynamical fields. The index $I$ is schematic: it may include species labels, spinor indices, gauge indices, and spacetime dependence when using DeWitt notation.

For an infinitesimal transformation with local parameter $\alpha^a(x)$, write

$$
\delta\Phi^I(x)=\alpha^a(x)R_a^I[\Phi](x).
$$

For a rigid symmetry, $R_a^I$ is the variation generated by the constant parameter transformation. Localizing the parameter is a trick used to extract the current.

Assume the variation of the action has the form

$$
\delta S
=\int d^dx\,(\partial_\mu\alpha^a)j_a^\mu
+\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\mathcal B_a.
$$

The first term defines the current convention. The second term, $\mathcal B_a$, is a classical explicit-breaking term. For an exact classical symmetry,

$$
\mathcal B_a=0.
$$

For compactly supported $\alpha^a$, integrate by parts:

$$
\delta S
=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(-\partial_\mu j_a^\mu+\mathcal B_a).
$$

The insertion varies as

$$
\delta\mathcal X
=\sum_{k=1}^n\alpha^a(x_k)
\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k(x_k)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\delta\mathcal X
=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)
\sum_{k=1}^n\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n.
$$

Finally, allow a possible Jacobian of the functional measure,

$$
\mathcal D\Phi\mapsto\mathcal D\Phi\,\exp\!\left(i\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\mathcal A_a\right).
$$

The density $\mathcal A_a$ is zero for a regulator preserving the symmetry. If no such regulator exists, $\mathcal A_a$ is the anomaly density in this convention.

:::note[Source note]
This page uses the sign convention $\delta S=\int(\partial_\mu\alpha^a)j_a^\mu$ in Lorentzian signature. Many books define the current with the opposite sign, or write the variation after integrating by parts. Always compare the sign of the current coupling to sources before importing a Ward identity from another reference.
:::

## The change-of-variables identity

The basic identity is

$$
\int\mathcal D\Phi'\,e^{iS[\Phi']}\mathcal X[\Phi']
=
\int\mathcal D\Phi\,e^{iS[\Phi]}\mathcal X[\Phi],
$$

because $\Phi'$ is just a new integration coordinate. Expanding to first order gives

$$
0=\langle\delta\mathcal X\rangle
+i\langle\delta S\,\mathcal X\rangle
+i\left\langle\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)\mathcal A_a(x)\,\mathcal X\right\rangle.
$$

Substitute the variations:

$$
\begin{aligned}
0
&=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)
\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle\\
&\quad
+i\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)
\left[-\langle\partial_\mu j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
+\langle\mathcal B_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle
+\langle\mathcal A_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle\right].
\end{aligned}
$$

Because $\alpha^a(x)$ is arbitrary, the integrand must vanish. Therefore

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=\langle(\mathcal B_a+\mathcal A_a)(x)\mathcal X\rangle
-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

This is the local Ward identity.

For an exact nonanomalous symmetry,

$$
\mathcal B_a=\mathcal A_a=0,
$$

so

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

This is the same formula as the operator Ward identity, now derived without mentioning Hilbert space or equal-time commutators.

## Why the parameter must be local

If $\alpha^a$ is constant, invariance only gives the global Ward identity:

$$
\sum_k\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle=0.
$$

That is useful, but it does not identify the current.

To derive a local current conservation equation, one must allow $\alpha^a$ to vary with spacetime. Then the action variation contains

$$
\partial_\mu\alpha^a,
$$

and the coefficient of this derivative is the current. This is exactly the same local-parameter trick used in the Noether-current page.

A constant parameter tests the symmetry. A local parameter extracts the current.

## Example: complex scalar U(1)

Consider a complex scalar field with symmetry

$$
\delta\phi=i\phi,
\qquad
\delta\phi^\dagger=-i\phi^\dagger.
$$

For a local parameter $\alpha(x)$,

$$
\delta\phi=i\alpha(x)\phi,
\qquad
\delta\phi^\dagger=-i\alpha(x)\phi^\dagger.
$$

For the standard kinetic term, the localized variation has the form

$$
\delta S=\int d^dx\,(\partial_\mu\alpha)j^\mu,
$$

with $j^\mu$ determined by the current convention of the page on Noether currents. There is no explicit breaking and no anomaly in this scalar example:

$$
\mathcal B=0,
\qquad
\mathcal A=0.
$$

For

$$
\mathcal X=\phi(y)\phi^\dagger(z),
$$

the insertion variation is

$$
\delta\mathcal X
=i\alpha(y)\phi(y)\phi^\dagger(z)
-i\alpha(z)\phi(y)\phi^\dagger(z).
$$

The Ward identity is therefore

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j^\mu(x)\phi(y)\phi^\dagger(z)\rangle
=\left[\delta^{(d)}(x-y)-\delta^{(d)}(x-z)\right]
\langle\phi(y)\phi^\dagger(z)\rangle.
$$

The path-integral derivation and the operator derivation agree term by term: the two delta functions are the two possible charged insertions hit by the current.

## Explicit breaking terms

Suppose the transformation is not a symmetry of the action. Then $\mathcal B_a$ is nonzero.

A typical example is an axial rotation of a massive Dirac fermion. The mass term is not invariant, so the axial current divergence contains a mass-dependent operator. Schematically,

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_5^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=\langle\mathcal B_5(x)\mathcal X\rangle
-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\cdots\delta_5\mathcal O_k\cdots\rangle,
$$

before adding possible anomaly terms.

The important point is that $\mathcal B_a$ is a bulk term. It contributes even when $x$ is not at an operator insertion. Contact terms, by contrast, are supported at $x=x_k$.

Keeping this distinction clean prevents a common mistake: explicit breaking changes current conservation, but it does not erase the transformation law of the inserted operators.

## Measure Jacobians and anomalies

The path integral exposes a quantum possibility with no classical analog: the action may be invariant, but the functional measure may not be.

Under

$$
\Phi\mapsto\Phi+\alpha^aR_a[\Phi],
$$

the formal measure may transform as

$$
\mathcal D\Phi\mapsto\mathcal D\Phi\,
\exp\!\left(i\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\mathcal A_a\right).
$$

If $\mathcal A_a$ cannot be removed by a local counterterm while preserving the desired symmetries, it is an anomaly. The Ward identity becomes

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=\langle\mathcal A_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle
-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\rangle
$$

for an anomalous but classically exact symmetry.

This is the seed of the Fujikawa method. One regulates the infinite-dimensional Jacobian, computes $\mathcal A_a$, and checks whether the answer can be shifted by local counterterms. The anomaly chapter develops the calculation; this page only identifies where the term enters.

:::note[Convention-sensitive anomaly note]
Some references write the measure Jacobian without the factor $i$, or work in Euclidean signature where the exponent is $-S_E$. Then the anomaly term appears with a different visible factor. The convention-independent statement is that a nontrivial Jacobian contributes a local bulk term to the Ward identity.
:::

## Relation to Schwinger–Dyson equations

Ward identities are special cases of a broader fact: field redefinitions give identities.

Take an arbitrary infinitesimal shift

$$
\Phi^I(x)\mapsto \Phi^I(x)+\varepsilon F^I[\Phi](x)
$$

that is not necessarily a symmetry. The change-of-variables identity gives

$$
0=\left\langle\delta\mathcal X+i\delta S\,\mathcal X+\delta\log\mathcal D\Phi\,\mathcal X\right\rangle.
$$

For a simple bosonic field with a translation-invariant measure, choosing

$$
F(x)=\delta^{(d)}(x-y)
$$

gives the Schwinger–Dyson equation

$$
\left\langle\frac{\delta\mathcal X}{\delta\phi(y)}\right\rangle
+i\left\langle\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(y)}\mathcal X\right\rangle=0.
$$

A Ward identity is the Schwinger–Dyson identity for a transformation that is a symmetry, or almost a symmetry, organized so that $\delta S$ becomes the divergence of a current plus controlled breaking terms.

This perspective is powerful but dangerous if used carelessly: every field redefinition gives an identity, but only symmetry transformations give conserved-current Ward identities.

## Generating functional version

Introduce sources $J_r$ coupled to operators $\mathcal O_r$:

$$
Z[J]=\int\mathcal D\Phi\,\exp\!\left(iS[\Phi]+i\int d^dx\,J_r(x)\mathcal O_r(x)\right).
$$

If the source term is included in the object being transformed, then the Ward identity becomes a functional differential equation for $Z[J]$ or

$$
W[J]=-i\log Z[J].
$$

For a linear symmetry,

$$
\delta_a\mathcal O_r=(T_a)_r{}^s\mathcal O_s,
$$

the source variation needed to keep $\int J_r\mathcal O_r$ invariant is

$$
\delta_a J_s=-J_r(T_a)_r{}^s
$$

up to sign conventions for active versus passive transformations. Invariance of $Z[J]$ then gives a differential equation such as

$$
\int d^dx\,J_r(x)(T_a)_r{}^s
\frac{\delta Z[J]}{i\delta J_s(x)}=0
$$

for a global symmetry, and a local version when a background gauge field is present.

The source viewpoint is the cleanest way to handle contact terms. Contact terms arise when functional derivatives hit local source-dependent terms in $W[J]$.

## Background-field Ward identity

For an internal symmetry, couple the current to a background field $A_\mu^a$:

$$
S\to S+\int d^dx\,A_\mu^a j_a^\mu
$$

up to the sign convention chosen for the current. The generating functional becomes

$$
e^{iW[A]}=\int\mathcal D\Phi\,e^{iS[\Phi;A]}.
$$

Background gauge invariance says that $W[A]$ is invariant under

$$
\delta_\alpha A_\mu^a=D_\mu\alpha^a.
$$

For a nonanomalous theory, this implies schematically

$$
(D_\mu\langle j^\mu\rangle_A)_a=0,
$$

again with source-contact terms when additional operator sources are present.

If $W[A]$ cannot be made invariant, then

$$
\delta_\alpha W[A]=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)\mathcal A_a[A](x),
$$

and the background-field Ward identity is anomalous:

$$
(D_\mu\langle j^\mu\rangle_A)_a=\mathcal A_a[A].
$$

This is the bridge to the [Background Fields and Gauging](/symmetry-anomalies-topology/background-fields-and-gauging/) chapter. There, anomalies become obstructions to gauging the background symmetry.

## Euclidean version

In Euclidean signature,

$$
\langle\mathcal X\rangle_E
=\frac{1}{Z_E}\int\mathcal D\Phi\,e^{-S_E[\Phi]}\mathcal X[\Phi].
$$

A change of variables gives

$$
0=\langle\delta\mathcal X\rangle_E
-\langle\delta S_E\,\mathcal X\rangle_E
+\langle\delta\log\mathcal D\Phi\,\mathcal X\rangle_E.
$$

If

$$
\delta S_E=\int d^dx\,(\partial_\mu\alpha^a)j_{E,a}^\mu+\cdots,
$$

then after integrating by parts, the Euclidean Ward identity has the same logical structure as the Lorentzian one. But the relation between $j_{E,a}^\mu$ and $j_a^\mu$ can include factors of $i$, especially for time components and axial currents.

The safest Euclidean statement is again the integrated one:

$$
\int_{\partial R}d\Sigma_\mu\,
\langle j_{E,a}^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle_E
=\text{symmetry variation of insertions inside }R
+\text{bulk breaking/anomaly terms}.
$$

Then translate factors of $i$ only after fixing the Wick rotation convention.

## Contact terms from sources

Suppose the generating functional contains local source counterterms, for example

$$
W[J]\supset\int d^dx\,c\,J(x)^2.
$$

Then two functional derivatives produce

$$
\frac{\delta^2}{\delta J(x)\delta J(y)}
\int d^dz\,c\,J(z)^2
=2c\,\delta^{(d)}(x-y).
$$

This is a contact term. It is local, scheme-dependent, and invisible at separated points.

The same thing happens for current sources $A_\mu^a$, metric sources $g_{\mu\nu}$, and background fields for higher-form symmetries. Local counterterms in sources shift contact terms in Ward identities. Genuine anomalies are those local variations that cannot be removed while preserving the required symmetries.

This is why careful anomaly calculations are really calculations of local functional dependence on background fields, not merely calculations of separated-point correlators.

## Common pitfalls

**Thinking a change of variables is a physical transformation of the state.** In the path integral, the first move is just re-labeling integration variables. The physical content comes from how the action, insertions, and measure respond.

**Using only constant parameters.** Constant $\alpha$ gives the global Ward identity. Local $\alpha(x)$ is needed to identify $j^\mu$ and derive local current conservation.

**Forgetting insertion variations.** Without $\delta\mathcal X$, one misses the contact terms and obtains the false statement that current divergences vanish inside all correlators.

**Assuming the measure is automatically invariant.** Infinite-dimensional measures require regulators. An anomaly is precisely the failure to find a regulator preserving the symmetry.

**Confusing explicit breaking with anomaly.** Explicit breaking is already present in $\delta S$. An anomaly comes from the regulated measure or, equivalently, from incompatible quantum definitions of composite operators and symmetries.

**Dropping source transformations.** Once sources are turned on, they must transform too. Otherwise the Ward identity will acquire extra terms that are artifacts of holding noninvariant sources fixed.

**Comparing Lorentzian and Euclidean formulas without tracking $i$ factors.** The structural identity is the same, but visible factors of $i$ depend on the convention for $S_E$, currents, and generators.

## Research status

The change-of-variables derivation of Ward identities is established. It underlies Schwinger–Dyson equations, Ward–Takahashi identities, Slavnov–Taylor identities, BRST identities, anomaly calculations, and background-field effective actions.

The research-level subtlety is not the formal identity but its regulated meaning. One must specify the measure, regulator, composite-operator renormalization, current representative, source counterterms, and boundary conditions. These choices can move local terms around. The invariant information is contained in symmetry action, separated-point identities, and anomaly classes that cannot be removed by allowed counterterms.

A good practical rule is:

$$
\text{derive the Ward identity with sources, then differentiate.}
$$

That procedure keeps contact terms visible instead of discovering them later as mysterious delta functions.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Derive the nonanomalous Ward identity

Assume

$$
\delta S=\int d^dx\,(\partial_\mu\alpha^a)j_a^\mu,
\qquad
\delta\log\mathcal D\Phi=0.
$$

Use the change-of-variables identity to derive

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The change of variables gives

$$
0=\langle\delta\mathcal X\rangle+i\langle\delta S\,\mathcal X\rangle.
$$

The insertion variation is

$$
\langle\delta\mathcal X\rangle
=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)
\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

For compactly supported $\alpha^a$,

$$
\delta S=-\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\partial_\mu j_a^\mu.
$$

Therefore

$$
0=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)
\left[
\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle
-i\langle\partial_\mu j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
\right].
$$

Because $\alpha^a(x)$ is arbitrary,

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Add explicit breaking

Now assume

$$
\delta S=\int d^dx\,(\partial_\mu\alpha^a)j_a^\mu
+\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\mathcal B_a.
$$

Derive the Ward identity including $\mathcal B_a$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

After integrating by parts,

$$
\delta S=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(-\partial_\mu j_a^\mu+\mathcal B_a).
$$

The change-of-variables identity gives

$$
0=\langle\delta\mathcal X\rangle+i\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)
\left[-\langle\partial_\mu j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
+\langle\mathcal B_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle\right].
$$

Using the same expression for $\delta\mathcal X$ as in Exercise 1 and equating the coefficient of arbitrary $\alpha^a(x)$ gives

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=\langle\mathcal B_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle
-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Add an anomaly Jacobian

Assume $\mathcal B_a=0$ but

$$
\delta\log\mathcal D\Phi=i\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\mathcal A_a.
$$

Derive the anomalous Ward identity.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The change-of-variables identity is

$$
0=\langle\delta\mathcal X\rangle+i\langle\delta S\,\mathcal X\rangle
+i\left\langle\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\mathcal A_a\,\mathcal X\right\rangle.
$$

With

$$
\delta S=-\int d^dx\,\alpha^a\partial_\mu j_a^\mu,
$$

we get

$$
0=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)
\left[
C_a(x)-i\langle\partial_\mu j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
+i\langle\mathcal A_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle
\right],
$$

where

$$
C_a(x)=\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

Thus

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=\langle\mathcal A_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle-iC_a(x).
$$

That is,

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=\langle\mathcal A_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle
-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Schwinger–Dyson equation as a field redefinition

For a single bosonic field with measure invariant under shifts, take

$$
\phi(x)\mapsto\phi(x)+\varepsilon f(x)
$$

with compactly supported $f$. Derive

$$
\left\langle\frac{\delta\mathcal X}{\delta\phi(x)}\right\rangle
+i\left\langle\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}\mathcal X\right\rangle=0.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The variations are

$$
\delta\mathcal X=\varepsilon\int d^dx\,f(x)\frac{\delta\mathcal X}{\delta\phi(x)},
$$

and

$$
\delta S=\varepsilon\int d^dx\,f(x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}.
$$

The change-of-variables identity is

$$
0=\langle\delta\mathcal X\rangle+i\langle\delta S\,\mathcal X\rangle.
$$

Substitute the variations:

$$
0=\varepsilon\int d^dx\,f(x)
\left[
\left\langle\frac{\delta\mathcal X}{\delta\phi(x)}\right\rangle
+i\left\langle\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}\mathcal X\right\rangle
\right].
$$

Since $f(x)$ is arbitrary, the bracket vanishes pointwise:

$$
\left\langle\frac{\delta\mathcal X}{\delta\phi(x)}\right\rangle
+i\left\langle\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}\mathcal X\right\rangle=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5: Contact term from a source counterterm

Let

$$
W[J]\supset\int d^dx\,cJ(x)^2.
$$

Compute the contribution of this term to

$$
\frac{\delta^2W}{\delta J(x)\delta J(y)}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

First differentiate once:

$$
\frac{\delta}{\delta J(x)}\int d^dz\,cJ(z)^2
=2cJ(x).
$$

Differentiate again:

$$
\frac{\delta}{\delta J(y)}2cJ(x)=2c\delta^{(d)}(x-y).
$$

Thus a local source counterterm contributes a contact term

$$
2c\delta^{(d)}(x-y)
$$

and does not affect separated-point correlators.

</details>
