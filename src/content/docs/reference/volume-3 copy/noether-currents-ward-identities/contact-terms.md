## Summary

A contact term is a contribution to a correlation function that is supported only when two or more insertions collide. The simplest examples are delta functions and derivatives of delta functions. In Ward identities they are not optional fine print. They are the local record of how a conserved charge acts on inserted operators.

For an exact internal continuous symmetry with current $j_a^\mu$ and

$$
\mathcal X=\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n),
$$

current conservation at separated points says

$$
\partial_\mu^x\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle=0,
\qquad x\neq x_k.
$$

The full distributional statement is stronger:

$$
\partial_\mu^x\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_{k=1}^n\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle T\,\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle
+\text{extra local terms}.
$$

The first term is fixed by the charge action. The extra local terms appear when the current collides with composite operators, other currents, boundaries, defects, or background-source insertions. Some are convention-dependent and removable by local counterterms. Others are invariant data, such as anomaly coefficients or current-algebra central terms.

This page isolates the local terms that were deliberately compressed in those derivations.

You should be comfortable with delta functions as distributions. A contact term is defined by how it behaves after smearing against a test function, not by evaluating a product of singular expressions pointwise.

## Core idea

The phrase “the current is conserved” has two meanings.

The separated-point statement is

$$
\partial_\mu j_a^\mu(x)=0
$$

inside correlators when $x$ is not on top of another insertion. This is the statement most Lagrangian derivations produce immediately.

The local Ward identity is a statement about distributions. It says what happens after the current insertion is allowed to collide with the other operators. If $\alpha^a(x)$ is a smooth test function, then

$$
\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)\,
\partial_\mu\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
$$

is determined by the values and derivatives of $\alpha^a$ at the insertion points. A delta function samples $\alpha^a(x_k)$; a derivative of a delta function samples $\partial_\mu\alpha^a(x_k)$; higher derivative contact terms sample higher jets.

So the useful slogan is

$$
\boxed{\text{contact terms are the local bookkeeping of symmetry action at collisions}.}
$$

This is why deleting contact terms gives wrong integrated Ward identities, wrong current algebra, and wrong anomaly matching.

## Setup and conventions

We use the Lorentzian conventions of this volume. The symmetry is generated by Hermitian charges

$$
Q_a(t)=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,j_a^0(t,\mathbf x),
$$

and

$$
U(\alpha)=e^{-i\alpha^aQ_a},
\qquad
\delta_a\mathcal O=i[Q_a,\mathcal O].
$$

Therefore

$$
[Q_a,\mathcal O]=-i\delta_a\mathcal O.
$$

With this pullback convention, the leading contact term in the operator Ward identity is

$$
-i\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)\,\delta_a\mathcal O_k.
$$

A contact term is any term supported on a collision locus. For one current insertion and one local operator at $y$, the most general local contribution has the schematic form

$$
\sum_{r=0}^N C^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_r}(y)\,
\partial_{\mu_1}\cdots\partial_{\mu_r}\delta^{(d)}(x-y),
$$

where $C^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_r}$ is a local operator at $y$. Power counting, symmetries, spin, ghost number, charge, and renormalization scheme restrict which coefficients can occur.

:::note[Convention-sensitive source note]
The sign of the leading Ward-identity contact term depends on three choices: whether operators transform as $U^\dagger\mathcal O U$ or $U\mathcal O U^{-1}$, whether the path integral is Lorentzian or Euclidean, and whether the variation of the action is written as $\delta S=\int (\partial_\mu\alpha)j^\mu$ or with the opposite sign. This page follows the conventions fixed in the previous Ward-identity pages.
:::

## Contact terms from time ordering

Let us first see the mechanism in the operator formalism. For a time-ordered product,

$$
T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal O(y)
=\theta(x^0-y^0)j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal O(y)
+\theta(y^0-x^0)\mathcal O(y)j_a^\mu(x),
$$

up to the usual graded signs for fermionic operators. Differentiating with respect to $x$ gives two kinds of terms:

$$
\partial_\mu^x T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal O(y)
=T\,\partial_\mu j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal O(y)
+\delta(x^0-y^0)[j_a^0(x),\mathcal O(y)].
$$

If the current is conserved at separated points, the first term vanishes away from $x=y$. The second term is supported at equal time. The equal-time commutator has the local form

$$
[j_a^0(t,\mathbf x),\mathcal O(t,\mathbf y)]
=-i\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)\delta_a\mathcal O(t,\mathbf y)
+\text{derivative contact terms}.
$$

Multiplying by $\delta(x^0-y^0)$ gives the spacetime contact term

$$
-i\delta^{(d)}(x-y)\delta_a\mathcal O(y).
$$

This derivation also explains why the local terms are invisible if one studies only separated points. They are produced by differentiating the time-ordering symbol.

## Contact terms from path-integral changes of variables

The same term appears in the path integral for a different reason. Under a local change of variables

$$
\Phi^I(x)\mapsto \Phi^I(x)+\alpha^a(x)\delta_a\Phi^I(x),
$$

the inserted operators vary:

$$
\delta\mathcal X
=\sum_{k=1}^n
\alpha^a(x_k)
\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n.
$$

Writing $\alpha^a(x_k)$ as an integral against a delta function,

$$
\alpha^a(x_k)=\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k),
$$

turns insertion variation into the same local contact term. In other words, the operator proof says “differentiate the time-ordering step functions,” while the path-integral proof says “the inserted operators changed.” These are two languages for the same Ward-identity data.

## Smearing makes the meaning unambiguous

Distributional equations should be tested by smearing. For an exact nonanomalous symmetry,

$$
\partial_\mu\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)\langle T\,\mathcal X_k^{(a)}\rangle,
$$

where

$$
\mathcal X_k^{(a)}
=\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n.
$$

Multiply by $\alpha^a(x)$ and integrate:

$$
\int d^dx\,\alpha^a(x)\partial_\mu\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\alpha^a(x_k)\langle T\,\mathcal X_k^{(a)}\rangle.
$$

After integrating by parts on the left, this becomes

$$
-\int d^dx\,(\partial_\mu\alpha^a)\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\alpha^a(x_k)\langle T\,\mathcal X_k^{(a)}\rangle,
$$

assuming boundary terms vanish. This is the cleanest form of the local Ward identity. It says that a local symmetry rotation supported in a region changes only the operators inside that region.

For constant $\alpha^a$, the left side vanishes and one recovers the global Ward identity

$$
\sum_k\langle T\,\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle=0.
$$

So the contact terms are precisely what makes local conservation imply the global selection rule.

## Derivative contact terms

Not all contact terms are plain delta functions. Derivative contact terms arise naturally when currents are improved, when composite operators are renormalized, or when the symmetry transformation contains derivatives.

For example, suppose

$$
[j^0(t,\mathbf x),\mathcal O(t,\mathbf y)]
=-i\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)\delta\mathcal O(t,\mathbf y)
+\partial_i\!\big[\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)Y^i(t,\mathbf y)\big].
$$

The derivative term contributes locally to

$$
\partial_\mu\langle T\,j^\mu(x)\mathcal O(y)\rangle,
$$

but it integrates to a surface term in the full charge action:

$$
\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,\partial_i\!\big[\delta^{(d-1)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)Y^i(t,\mathbf y)\big]=0
$$

if the spatial boundary contribution vanishes. Thus two currents can generate the same global charge action while differing by local derivative contact terms.

This is not a pathology. It is the local expression of current ambiguity.

## Improvements and scheme dependence

A current is not unique. If $B_a^{\mu\nu}=-B_a^{\nu\mu}$, then

$$
j_a^\mu\mapsto j_a^\mu+\partial_\nu B_a^{\nu\mu}
$$

has the same separated-point conservation law because

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu B_a^{\nu\mu}=0.
$$

The charge changes by a surface term,

$$
Q_a\mapsto Q_a+\frac12\int_{\partial\Sigma}dS_{i\nu}\,B_a^{i\nu},
$$

so on spatial slices without relevant boundary contribution the integrated generator is unchanged.

But correlators of the local current do change:

$$
\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal O(y)\rangle
\mapsto
\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal O(y)\rangle
+\partial_\nu^x\langle B_a^{\nu\mu}(x)\mathcal O(y)\rangle.
$$

If $B_a^{\nu\mu}(x)$ has a singular OPE with $\mathcal O(y)$, the improvement shifts contact terms. This is why local Ward identities for composite operators must be stated together with a choice of renormalized current and operator scheme.

The same idea appears in source language. Add a local counterterm built from background fields and sources. Functional derivatives of that counterterm are pure contact terms in correlation functions. They can shift the definition of the current, stress tensor, and composite operators without changing separated-point correlators.

## Contact terms in current-current correlators

Contact terms become unavoidable once one inserts more than one current. Let $W[A]$ be the generating functional with a background gauge field $A_\mu^a$ for a global symmetry. The renormalized current is schematically

$$
\langle j_a^\mu(x)\rangle_A
=\frac{\delta W[A]}{\delta A_\mu^a(x)}.
$$

Background gauge invariance gives a covariant conservation equation,

$$
D_\mu\langle j^\mu\rangle_A=0,
$$

for a nonanomalous symmetry. Differentiating with respect to $A_\nu^b(y)$ and then setting $A=0$ gives a Ward identity for a two-current correlator. Schematically,

$$
\partial_\mu^x\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)j_b^\nu(y)\rangle
=\text{local term proportional to }f_{ab}{}^c\delta^{(d)}(x-y)\langle j_c^\nu(y)\rangle
+\text{scheme terms}.
$$

The precise sign depends on whether currents are defined by $\delta W/\delta A$ or $-\delta W/\delta A$, and on the convention for the background covariant derivative. The existence of the local term is not convention-dependent. It is required because the current itself transforms in the adjoint representation.

This is the first hint of current algebra: currents are charged operators, so Ward identities with current insertions have contact terms determined by the symmetry algebra.

## Contact terms versus anomalies

A common trap is to call every local term in a Ward identity an anomaly. That is much too coarse.

A removable contact term can be changed by redefining the current or by adding a local counterterm to the generating functional. It is scheme data.

An anomaly is a local term in the symmetry variation that cannot be removed while preserving the desired locality, symmetry, and background-field consistency conditions. In a path-integral Ward identity,

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=\langle \mathcal A_a(x)\mathcal X\rangle
-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)\langle\mathcal X_k^{(a)}\rangle
+\cdots,
$$

$\mathcal A_a$ is not merely a contact term with the inserted operators. It is a local functional of background fields and dynamical fields that persists even in the absence of charged insertions.

For ’t Hooft anomalies, $\mathcal A_a$ is best viewed as an obstruction to gauging the background symmetry. Ordinary insertion contact terms are not obstructions; they are how the symmetry acts.

## Boundaries, interfaces, and defects

On a manifold with boundary, integrating by parts produces boundary terms. A Ward identity can contain terms supported on the boundary:

$$
\partial_\mu j^\mu(x)=\delta_{\partial M}(x)\,\mathcal B(x)+\cdots.
$$

Here $\delta_{\partial M}$ is a delta distribution with support on the boundary. Depending on the boundary condition, $\mathcal B$ may describe explicit symmetry breaking, anomaly inflow, boundary degrees of freedom, or the current that flows into the boundary theory.

Similarly, if a codimension-$p$ defect $D$ is inserted, a bulk Ward identity can acquire a term supported on $D$:

$$
\partial_\mu j^\mu(x)=\delta_D(x)\,\mathcal O_D(x)+\cdots.
$$

This is the local version of a simple physical fact: a symmetry charge surface can end, jump, or transform when it crosses a defect that carries symmetry data.

## A practical taxonomy

When you meet a local term in a Ward identity, ask the following questions.

| Question | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Is it supported only when insertions collide? | It is a contact term. |
| Does it follow from the charge action on an operator? | It is fixed by the representation of the symmetry. |
| Can it be shifted by improving the current or adding a local counterterm? | It is scheme-dependent local data. |
| Does it survive even without charged insertions? | It may be explicit breaking or an anomaly. |
| Is it incompatible with gauging the symmetry background? | It is part of anomaly data. |
| Does it integrate to zero on closed spatial slices? | It may affect local algebra but not global charges. |
| Is it supported on a boundary or defect? | It encodes boundary, interface, or defect symmetry flow. |

This taxonomy is more useful than asking whether a term is “real.” Contact terms are real; the question is what kind of data they encode.

## Example: charged operators under a U(1) current

Let $\mathcal O_k$ have $U(1)$ charge $q_k$ in the convention

$$
[Q,\mathcal O_k]=q_k\mathcal O_k.
$$

Since

$$
\delta\mathcal O_k=i[Q,\mathcal O_k]=iq_k\mathcal O_k,
$$

the Ward identity becomes

$$
\partial_\mu^x\langle T\,j^\mu(x)\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle
=\sum_{k=1}^n q_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\langle T\,\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle,
$$

up to convention-dependent overall signs if the charge assignment is defined with the opposite commutator convention.

Integrating over $x$ gives

$$
\left(\sum_k q_k\right)
\langle T\,\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle=0.
$$

Thus a correlator of charged operators vanishes unless the total charge is zero. The global selection rule is the integrated form of the local contact terms.

## Example: translations and derivative contact terms

The stress tensor current for translations is $T^{\mu\nu}$. For a scalar local operator $\mathcal O(y)$, a translation by $a^\nu$ gives

$$
\delta\mathcal O(y)=a^\nu\partial_\nu\mathcal O(y).
$$

The corresponding Ward identity has the schematic form

$$
\partial_\mu^x\langle T\,T^{\mu\nu}(x)\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)\rangle
=-\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)
\partial_{x_k}^\nu
\langle T\,\mathcal O_1\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle
+\cdots.
$$

For spinning or tensor operators, the dots include additional contact terms implementing rotations of the indices. In curved-space language, these terms are organized by diffeomorphism Ward identities and by the dependence of composite operators on the metric.

This example is a good warning: spacetime symmetries often produce derivative and spin contact terms that are more intricate than the internal-symmetry formula.

## Common pitfalls

**Dropping contact terms because they vanish at separated points.** Ward identities are distributional statements. The separated-point equation is not enough to derive charge actions or selection rules.

**Calling contact terms “anomalies.”** Most contact terms are required even for perfectly nonanomalous symmetries. An anomaly is a nonremovable obstruction term, not just any delta function.

**Assuming the current is unique.** Improvements and local counterterms shift local current correlators and derivative contact terms while leaving many integrated charges unchanged.

**Forgetting composite-operator renormalization.** Products such as $j^\mu(x)\mathcal O(y)$ are singular as $x\to y$. The renormalized product requires local subtractions, and those subtractions are contact terms.

**Mixing Euclidean and Lorentzian signs.** The factor of $i$ in Lorentzian Ward identities moves when one Wick rotates or defines Euclidean currents by functional derivatives of $W_E$.

**Ignoring boundaries.** On manifolds with boundaries, charge conservation can fail by boundary flux even when the bulk current is conserved.

## Research status

The basic contact terms in ordinary Ward identities are settled textbook material. The subtleties arise when one asks which local terms are meaningful in a renormalized QFT with sources.

In modern work, contact terms are treated as part of the local effective action for background fields. Changing local counterterms changes scheme-dependent contact terms. Anomalies, quantized Chern–Simons contact terms, current-algebra levels, and inflow data are more rigid.

In theories with boundaries, defects, generalized symmetries, or non-invertible symmetries, “contact term” language generalizes to local contributions supported on charged defects, junctions, or topological operators. The conceptual lesson remains the same: local Ward identities must be read as equations of distributions.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Recovering the global Ward identity

Assume

$$
\partial_\mu\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)\langle T\,\mathcal X_k^{(a)}\rangle.
$$

Integrate over all spacetime after smearing with a constant test function. Show that the global Ward identity follows.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Take $\alpha^a(x)=\alpha^a$ constant and integrate:

$$
\int d^dx\,\partial_\mu\langle T\,j_a^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\langle T\,\mathcal X_k^{(a)}\rangle.
$$

If the boundary term on the left vanishes, then

$$
0=-i\sum_k\langle T\,\mathcal X_k^{(a)}\rangle.
$$

Therefore

$$
\sum_k\langle T\,\mathcal O_1\cdots\delta_a\mathcal O_k\cdots\mathcal O_n\rangle=0.
$$

This is the global Ward identity. The contact terms are essential: without them the integral would only say $0=0$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Derivative delta terms

Let

$$
C(x,y)=\partial_\mu^x\big[Y^\mu(y)\delta^{(d)}(x-y)\big].
$$

Show that smearing $C(x,y)$ against a test function $\alpha(x)$ gives a derivative of $\alpha$ at $y$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
\int d^dx\,\alpha(x)\partial_\mu^x\big[Y^\mu(y)\delta^{(d)}(x-y)\big].
$$

Integrating by parts in $x$ and dropping the boundary term gives

$$
-\int d^dx\,(\partial_\mu\alpha)(x)Y^\mu(y)\delta^{(d)}(x-y)
=-(\partial_\mu\alpha)(y)Y^\mu(y).
$$

Thus derivative contact terms are detected by local variations whose parameter changes near the insertion.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Current improvement and the charge

Let

$$
j^\mu\mapsto j^\mu+\partial_\nu B^{\nu\mu},
\qquad B^{\nu\mu}=-B^{\mu\nu}.
$$

Show that the divergence is unchanged identically, and show that the charge changes only by a spatial boundary term.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The divergence changes by

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu B^{\nu\mu}.
$$

Since $\partial_\mu\partial_\nu$ is symmetric in $\mu,\nu$ while $B^{\nu\mu}$ is antisymmetric, this vanishes identically:

$$
\partial_\mu\partial_\nu B^{\nu\mu}=0.
$$

The charge shifts by

$$
\Delta Q=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,\partial_\nu B^{\nu 0}.
$$

Because $B^{00}=0$, this is

$$
\Delta Q=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,\partial_i B^{i0}
=\int_{\partial\Sigma}dS_i\,B^{i0}.
$$

If the boundary term vanishes, the integrated charge is unchanged even though local current correlators can shift by contact terms.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Contact term or anomaly?

A theory has a putative current satisfying

$$
\partial_\mu\langle j^\mu(x)\mathcal X\rangle
=-i\sum_k\delta^{(d)}(x-x_k)\langle\mathcal X_k\rangle
+c\,\partial_\mu F^\mu[A](x)\langle\mathcal X\rangle.
$$

Suppose the last term can be removed by adding a local functional of the background field $A$ to $W[A]$. Is it an anomaly?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

If the term can be removed by a local counterterm while preserving the desired symmetries and locality, then it is scheme-dependent contact data, not an anomaly.

An anomaly is a local term in the Ward identity that cannot be removed by an allowed local counterterm. The first term in the equation is also not an anomaly; it is the ordinary contact term representing the action of the charge on the insertions.

</details>
