Canonical Quantization is the chapter in the Foundations of QFT volume where classical field data become operator algebras on a Hilbert space. It is the operator-first route into QFT: start with canonical variables, impose equal-time brackets, build creation and annihilation operators, and let the Hamiltonian generate time evolution.

The chapter exists because free quantum fields are not mysterious if you have seen enough oscillators. A quadratic classical field theory decomposes into normal modes. Canonical quantization turns those modes into oscillator operators, and the occupation numbers become particle numbers.

Read this chapter when you want to understand where mode expansions, Fock states, normal ordering, field commutators, and fermionic signs come from before path integrals and diagrams take over.

$$
\text{classical normal modes}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
(Q_n,P_n)
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
(a_n,a_n^\dagger)
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{Fock states}.
$$

## Prerequisites

You should know the harmonic oscillator, Hamiltonian mechanics, Poisson brackets, and the state-space language of [Fock Space](/foundations/relativistic-particles-and-fields/fock-space/). It also helps to have read [Hamiltonian Field Theory](/foundations/classical-field-theory/hamiltonian-field-theory/), [Constraints](/foundations/classical-field-theory/constraints/), [Bosons and Fermions](/foundations/relativistic-particles-and-fields/bosons-and-fermions/), and [Antiparticles](/foundations/relativistic-particles-and-fields/antiparticles/).

The conventions are those of [Conventions and Normalizations](/foundations/conventions-and-normalizations/): mostly-minus metric, natural units, and plane waves $e^{-ip\cdot x}$.

## Landmarks

| Landmark | Meaning | Where used next |
|---|---|---|
| `$[Q,P]=i$` | Canonical quantization starts by promoting classical canonical pairs to noncommuting operators. | Harmonic oscillator modes, field brackets. |
| `$[a,a^\dagger]=1$` | A bosonic oscillator is generated by creation and annihilation operators. | Fock space, scalar field quantization. |
| `$[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)$` | The canonical bosonic field algebra lives on an equal-time spatial slice. | Free fields, causal commutators. |
| Mode expansion | A free field is written as positive- and negative-frequency oscillator modes. | Klein–Gordon field, Dirac field, propagators. |
| Pauli–Jordan commutator | Equal-time data evolve into a spacetime commutator with causal support. | Microcausality, Wightman functions. |
| Normal ordering | Operator products are reordered relative to a chosen free vacuum. | Wick theorem, perturbation theory. |
| `$\{f,f^\dagger\}=1$` | Fermionic modes obey anticommutation relations and have occupations $0$ or $1$. | Dirac fields, Grassmann path integrals. |

## Common confusions

**The canonical algebra is equal-time, not spacetime.** The relation uses $\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)$ on a time slice. Time evolution is supplied by the Hamiltonian, not by replacing it with a four-dimensional delta function.

**The negative-frequency term is not a negative-energy particle.** In a relativistic field expansion, the $e^{+ip\cdot x}$ term is paired with a creation operator. For charged fields it creates a positive-energy antiparticle.

**Normal ordering is not a cure for all vacuum energy.** It subtracts a chosen free-vacuum c-number and is invaluable in perturbation theory. It does not by itself solve gravity, composite-operator renormalization, or interacting-vacuum questions.

**Fields at points are not ordinary bounded operators.** Pointwise notation is efficient but mathematically idealized. Quantum fields are operator-valued distributions, so smearing and regularization matter.

**Gauge fields are not just four independent oscillators.** Constraints and gauge redundancy must be handled before counting physical degrees of freedom. This is why Maxwell theory has two photon helicities, not four oscillator polarizations.

## Boundaries

This chapter does:

- quantize oscillator modes and equal-time field algebras;
- build free real and complex scalar fields from creation and annihilation operators;
- explain normal ordering, contractions, and vacuum-dependent bookkeeping;
- introduce fermionic anticommutators as the canonical language for spinor fields.

This chapter does not try to do:

- full interacting operator theory;
- covariant gauge quantization, BRST, ghosts, or BV formalism;
- path-integral derivations of Gaussian functionals and determinants;
- rigorous domain and distribution theory for interacting fields.

Those topics belong later in Foundations, Gauge Fields: First Principles, Path Integral Formalism, Mathematical and Rigorous QFT, and Computational QFT and Tools.
