## Summary

An $n$-point correlation function contains all ways in which the operator insertions can talk to one another. Some of those ways are genuinely collective; others are just products of smaller, independent correlations. The genuinely collective part is the **connected correlator**.

With the qft.org Lorentzian source convention,

$$
Z[J]=\left\langle0\left|T\exp\left(i\int d^4x\,J(x)\phi(x)\right)\right|0\right\rangle,
\qquad Z[0]=1,
$$

the full time-ordered correlators are generated by $Z[J]$:

$$
G_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=\left.\frac{1}{i^n}
\frac{\delta^n Z[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}\right|_{J=0}.
$$

The connected correlators are generated by $W[J]$, defined by

$$
\boxed{Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}.}
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{
G_n^{\mathrm c}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=\left.\frac{1}{i^{n-1}}
\frac{\delta^n W[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}\right|_{J=0}.
}
$$

Connected correlators are the QFT analogue of cumulants in probability theory. They are the pieces that vanish when the insertions separate into independent distant experiments. They are also what survive after taking the logarithm of the source functional. Coleman emphasizes this exponentiation theorem diagrammatically: the sum of all vacuum-to-vacuum graphs in a source is the exponential of the sum of connected graphs (Coleman 2019, §§8.4, 13.2, and 32.1). Srednicki uses the same fact in the path-integral expansion, where $Z(J)=\exp[iW(J)]$ and $W$ is the sum of connected diagrams (Srednicki 2007, §§8–9).

## Core idea

A full four-point function can be nonzero for two very different reasons. The four insertions may participate in one common connected process, or the insertions may split into two independent pairs. The full correlator includes both.

<figure class="doc-figure" style="--figure-width: 54rem;">

![Connected and disconnected contributions to a four-point function](/figures/foundations/connected-correlator-decomposition.svg)

<figcaption>

For a theory with vanishing one-point function, the full four-point function equals a connected four-point part plus the three products of connected two-point functions. The connected part is the cumulant: it is the piece not explained by lower-point correlations.

</figcaption>
</figure>

The connected correlator answers a sharper question than the full correlator:

```txt
Full correlator:
  What is the expectation value of this whole product?

Connected correlator:
  What part of the answer cannot be decomposed into independent sub-correlations?
```

That distinction is not cosmetic. It is the difference between measuring genuine interaction data and repeatedly rediscovering that the vacuum can do independent things in independent places.

## Z and W

Let

$$
Z[J]=\left\langle T\exp\left(i\int d^4x\,J(x)\phi(x)\right)\right\rangle,
\qquad Z[0]=1.
$$

Here and below $\langle\cdots\rangle$ means the vacuum expectation value in the theory under discussion. Expanding the exponential gives

$$
Z[J]
=1+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{i^n}{n!}\int d^4x_1\cdots d^4x_n\,
J(x_1)\cdots J(x_n)G_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n),
$$

where

$$
G_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=\langle T\{\phi(x_1)\cdots\phi(x_n)\}\rangle.
$$

Now define

$$
Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
W[J]=-i\log Z[J].
$$

Then $W[J]$ has the expansion

$$
W[J]
=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{i^{n-1}}{n!}\int d^4x_1\cdots d^4x_n\,
J(x_1)\cdots J(x_n)G_n^{\mathrm c}(x_1,\ldots,x_n).
$$

So $Z$ generates all correlators, while $W$ generates connected correlators.

:::note[Conventions]
Some books define $Z[J]=e^{W[J]}$ in Euclidean signature and $Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}$ in Lorentzian signature. qft.org uses the Lorentzian convention $Z=e^{iW}$ in this section. The factors of $i$ in the derivative formula above follow from that choice.
:::

## The first cumulants

The first connected correlator is just the one-point function:

$$
G_1(x)=G_1^{\mathrm c}(x).
$$

The two-point function decomposes as

$$
\boxed{
G_2(x_1,x_2)
=G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_1,x_2)+G_1(x_1)G_1(x_2).
}
$$

Thus

$$
G_2^{\mathrm c}(x_1,x_2)
=G_2(x_1,x_2)-G_1(x_1)G_1(x_2).
$$

The three-point function decomposes as

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_3(1,2,3)=&\,G_3^{\mathrm c}(1,2,3) \\
&+G_2^{\mathrm c}(1,2)G_1(3)
+G_2^{\mathrm c}(1,3)G_1(2)
+G_2^{\mathrm c}(2,3)G_1(1) \\
&+G_1(1)G_1(2)G_1(3),
\end{aligned}
$$

where $G_n(1,\ldots,n)$ abbreviates $G_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$.

For many relativistic vacua of interest,

$$
\langle\phi(x)\rangle=0.
$$

Then the first few identities simplify to

$$
G_2=G_2^{\mathrm c},
\qquad
G_3=G_3^{\mathrm c},
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
G_4(1,2,3,4)=&\,G_4^{\mathrm c}(1,2,3,4) \\
&+G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4)
+G_2(1,3)G_2(2,4)
+G_2(1,4)G_2(2,3).
\end{aligned}
}
$$

This last formula is often the first place where students really see the word “connected” earn its keep.

## Free fields as the benchmark

For a free real scalar field,

$$
Z_0[J]=\exp\left[-\frac12\int d^4x\,d^4y\,J(x)D_F(x-y)J(y)\right].
$$

Since $Z_0=e^{iW_0}$,

$$
W_0[J]
=\frac{i}{2}\int d^4x\,d^4y\,J(x)D_F(x-y)J(y).
$$

Therefore

$$
G_{0,2}^{\mathrm c}(x,y)=D_F(x-y),
$$

and all higher connected correlators vanish:

$$
\boxed{
G_{0,n}^{\mathrm c}=0\qquad(n>2).
}
$$

This is the precise source-functional version of Wick's theorem for a free field. A free Gaussian theory has only a connected two-point cumulant. Every higher full correlator is assembled from pairings.

For example,

$$
G_{0,4}(1,2,3,4)
=D_F(1-2)D_F(3-4)
+D_F(1-3)D_F(2-4)
+D_F(1-4)D_F(2-3).
$$

There is no connected four-point function in the free theory. A nonzero connected four-point function is one diagnostic of interactions.

## Diagrammatic exponentiation

The logarithm in

$$
W[J]=-i\log Z[J]
$$

is the diagrammatic exponentiation theorem in functional form.

Suppose the vacuum-to-vacuum functional in a source is computed as a sum over diagrams. A general disconnected diagram is a product of connected components. If a connected diagram $C_I$ appears $n_I$ times, the combinatorics supplies a factor $1/n_I!$. Summing over all multiplicities gives

$$
\sum_{n_I=0}^{\infty}\frac{C_I^{n_I}}{n_I!}=e^{C_I}.
$$

Multiplying this over all connected diagram types gives

$$
Z[J]=\exp\left(\sum_I C_I[J]\right).
$$

Therefore the logarithm isolates the connected components:

$$
\log Z[J]=\sum_I C_I[J].
$$

This is why connected diagrams appear so naturally in perturbation theory. They are not an extra convention; they are what logarithms do to products.

## Vacuum bubbles

There are two closely related normalizations in common use.

One can define an unnormalized source functional

$$
\mathcal Z[J]
=\langle0|T\exp(i\int J\phi)|0\rangle_{\rm unnormalized}.
$$

Then vacuum diagrams with no source insertions multiply every answer. They are independent of $J$, so they cancel in normalized correlation functions. qft.org usually works with

$$
Z[J]=\frac{\mathcal Z[J]}{\mathcal Z[0]},
\qquad Z[0]=1.
$$

This removes pure vacuum bubbles before differentiating. Connected vacuum bubbles still matter in contexts where the vacuum energy, free energy, or effective action is the object of interest. But for ordinary normalized correlators they are not part of the observable insertion data.

:::caution[Do not throw away every disconnected diagram]
Vacuum bubbles cancel from normalized correlators. Disconnected pieces with external insertions do not generally cancel. They encode products of lower-point correlators and are part of the full correlator. Taking $\log Z$ removes them only when you deliberately ask for connected correlators.
:::

## Momentum-space delta functions

In a translation-invariant vacuum,

$$
G_n^{\mathrm c}(x_1+a,\ldots,x_n+a)=G_n^{\mathrm c}(x_1,\ldots,x_n).
$$

Its Fourier transform therefore contains one overall momentum-conservation delta function:

$$
\boxed{
\widetilde G_n^{\mathrm c}(p_1,\ldots,p_n)
=(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\right)\,\widetilde g_n^{\mathrm c}(p_1,\ldots,p_{n-1}).
}
$$

A disconnected contribution has one such delta function for each connected component. For instance,

$$
G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4)
$$

has separate conservation laws for the pair $(1,2)$ and the pair $(3,4)$. This distinction is important in scattering theory: connected amplitudes describe one common experiment, while disconnected products describe independent experiments occurring side by side.

## Cluster decomposition

Connected correlators are the natural language of the cluster decomposition principle. If two groups of operators are separated by a large spacelike displacement $a$, one expects

$$
\langle T\{A(x_i+a)B(y_j)\}\rangle
\longrightarrow
\langle T\{A(x_i)\}\rangle\langle T\{B(y_j)\}\rangle
$$

under appropriate assumptions on the vacuum and spectrum. Equivalently,

$$
\langle T\{A(x_i+a)B(y_j)\}\rangle_{\mathrm c}
\longrightarrow 0.
$$

For a massive theory with a mass gap, the decay is often exponential at large spacelike separation. For massless theories it can be power-law. At critical points, connected correlators are precisely the objects whose scaling behavior is studied.

:::note[Assumptions]
The strongest cluster statements require a stable translation-invariant vacuum, appropriate locality properties, and spectral control. Long-range order, massless particles, finite volume, finite temperature, gauge constraints, and spontaneous symmetry breaking can all complicate the naive statement.
:::

## Relation to scattering and 1PI functions

Connected correlators are still not the final irreducible objects.

There is a hierarchy:

```txt
Z[J]
  generates all correlators.

W[J] = -i log Z[J]
  generates connected correlators.

Γ[φ]
  generates one-particle-irreducible, or 1PI, vertices.
```

The effective action $\Gamma[\varphi]$ is obtained from $W[J]$ by a Legendre transform. That story belongs later, but it is worth previewing now: connected correlators are the correct objects for describing one common process, while 1PI vertices are the correct objects for describing building blocks that cannot be split by cutting a single internal line.

For scattering, disconnected pieces usually represent spectators or independent subexperiments. The connected amputated correlators are the objects that feed the nontrivial part of the S-matrix after LSZ reduction. This is why a scattering section spends so much time drawing connected diagrams.

## Common pitfalls

### Confusing connected with interacting

A connected correlator can exist in a free theory: the free two-point function is connected. What vanishes in a free Gaussian theory is every connected correlator beyond two points.

### Forgetting one-point functions

The identity

$$
G_2^{\mathrm c}=G_2
$$

holds only when $G_1=0$. In a theory with a condensate, background field, external source, or spontaneous symmetry breaking, subtract the one-point functions.

### Throwing away disconnected external pieces too early

Disconnected external pieces are part of full correlators. They are removed only if you are computing connected correlators, connected amplitudes, or logarithms of generating functionals.

### Ignoring vacuum normalization

An unnormalized $\mathcal Z[J]$ includes pure vacuum bubbles. The normalized functional $Z[J]=\mathcal Z[J]/\mathcal Z[0]$ removes them from ordinary correlators.

### Treating cluster decomposition as automatic

Cluster decomposition is a physical principle with assumptions, not a formal identity. It can fail or require refinement in systems with long-range order, massless modes, gauge constraints, or multiple superselection sectors.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Two-point cumulant

Starting from $Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}$, show that

$$
G_2(x,y)=G_2^{\mathrm c}(x,y)+G_1(x)G_1(y).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate $Z=e^{iW}$ once:

$$
\frac{\delta Z}{\delta J_x}=iW_x Z,
$$

where $W_x=\delta W/\delta J(x)$. Differentiate again:

$$
\frac{\delta^2 Z}{\delta J_x\delta J_y}
=\left(iW_{xy}-W_xW_y\right)Z.
$$

At $J=0$, $Z[0]=1$. Therefore

$$
G_2(x,y)
=\frac{1}{i^2}\left(iW_{xy}-W_xW_y\right)_{J=0}
=\left(\frac{1}{i}W_{xy}\right)_{J=0}+W_xW_y\big|_{J=0}.
$$

Using

$$
G_2^{\mathrm c}(x,y)=\left.\frac{1}{i}W_{xy}\right|_{J=0},
\qquad
G_1(x)=\left.W_x\right|_{J=0},
$$

we get

$$
G_2=G_2^{\mathrm c}+G_1G_1.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Three-point cumulant

Show that

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_3(1,2,3)=&\,G_3^{\mathrm c}(1,2,3)
+G_2^{\mathrm c}(1,2)G_1(3) \\
&+G_2^{\mathrm c}(1,3)G_1(2)
+G_2^{\mathrm c}(2,3)G_1(1)
+G_1(1)G_1(2)G_1(3).
\end{aligned}
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate $Z=e^{iW}$ three times and set $J=0$:

$$
\frac{\delta^3Z}{\delta J_1\delta J_2\delta J_3}
=\left(iW_{123}-W_{12}W_3-W_{13}W_2-W_{23}W_1-iW_1W_2W_3\right)Z.
$$

Multiplying by $1/i^3$ and using

$$
G_1=W_1,
\qquad
G_2^{\mathrm c}=\frac{1}{i}W_{12},
\qquad
G_3^{\mathrm c}=\frac{1}{i^2}W_{123},
$$

gives the stated result.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Free four-point function

Use the free Gaussian source functional

$$
Z_0[J]=\exp\left[-\frac12 JD_FJ\right]
$$

to show that $G_{0,4}^{\mathrm c}=0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Since $Z_0=e^{iW_0}$,

$$
W_0[J]=\frac{i}{2}JD_FJ.
$$

This is quadratic in $J$. Therefore every functional derivative of $W_0$ of order three or higher vanishes:

$$
\frac{\delta^n W_0}{\delta J_1\cdots\delta J_n}=0,
\qquad n>2.
$$

Thus

$$
G_{0,n}^{\mathrm c}=0\qquad(n>2),
$$

and in particular $G_{0,4}^{\mathrm c}=0$.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Four-point decomposition with zero one-point function

Assume $G_1=0$. Derive the decomposition

$$
G_4(1,2,3,4)=G_4^{\mathrm c}(1,2,3,4)+G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4)+G_2(1,3)G_2(2,4)+G_2(1,4)G_2(2,3).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Connected correlators are cumulants. A full four-point function is the sum over all partitions of the four labels into connected blocks. With $G_1=0$, every partition containing a singleton vanishes. The surviving partitions are:

$$
\{1234\},
\qquad
\{12\}\{34\},
\qquad
\{13\}\{24\},
\qquad
\{14\}\{23\}.
$$

The first gives $G_4^{\mathrm c}$. The three pair partitions give the three products of two-point functions. Since $G_1=0$, $G_2=G_2^{\mathrm c}$, yielding the formula above.

</details>

### Exercise 5: One overall delta function

Let $G_n^{\mathrm c}$ be translation invariant. Show that its Fourier transform has one overall momentum-conservation delta function.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Translation invariance means the correlator depends only on coordinate differences. Set

$$
y_i=x_i-x_n,
\qquad i=1,\ldots,n-1,
$$

and keep $x_n$ as the common center variable. In the Fourier transform,

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\cdot x_i
=\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}p_i\cdot y_i+\left(\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\right)\cdot x_n.
$$

The integration over $x_n$ gives

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}\left(\sum_i p_i\right).
$$

The remaining integrations define the reduced momentum-space function.

</details>

### Exercise 6: Cluster decomposition in cumulant language

Suppose $A$ and $B$ are two products of local operators and

$$
\langle A(a)B(0)\rangle\to \langle A\rangle\langle B\rangle
$$

as the spacelike separation $a$ becomes large. Show that the connected correlator of $A(a)$ and $B(0)$ vanishes in that limit.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For two composite insertions $A$ and $B$, the connected two-point function is

$$
\langle A B\rangle_{\mathrm c}=\langle A B\rangle-\langle A\rangle\langle B\rangle.
$$

Using the assumed factorization,

$$
\lim_{a\to\infty}\langle A(a)B(0)\rangle_{\mathrm c}
=\lim_{a\to\infty}\left[\langle A(a)B(0)\rangle-\langle A\rangle\langle B\rangle\right]=0.
$$

This is the cumulant form of cluster decomposition.

</details>
