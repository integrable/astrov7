## Summary

A two-dimensional Euclidean QFT on a torus has two independent cycles. Choosing which cycle is “space” and which is “Euclidean time” is partly a matter of description. **Modular invariance** says that a good theory should not depend on that arbitrary choice.

A flat torus can be written as

$$
E_\tau=\mathbb C/(\mathbb Z+\tau\mathbb Z),
\qquad
\operatorname{Im}\tau>0.
$$

The same torus is described by many different values of $\tau$. The modular group acts by

$$
\tau\mapsto \frac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d},
\qquad
\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\ c&d\end{pmatrix}\in SL(2,\mathbb Z),
$$

with the central sign acting trivially on the ordinary bosonic torus. It is generated by

$$
S:\tau\mapsto-\frac1\tau,
\qquad
T:\tau\mapsto\tau+1.
$$

For a nonchiral bosonic CFT, the torus partition function is expected to obey

$$
Z(\tau,\bar\tau)=Z\!\left(\frac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d},\frac{a\bar\tau+b}{c\bar\tau+d}\right).
$$

This page is a preview, not a full course in two-dimensional CFT. The point is to explain why modular transformations are part of low-dimensional symmetry technology: they constrain spectra, test anomalies, organize orbifolds, explain the Cardy formula, and detect when a would-be set of sectors cannot define a consistent two-dimensional theory.

The page uses standard two-dimensional CFT notation:

$$
q=e^{2\pi i\tau},
\qquad
\bar q=e^{-2\pi i\bar\tau}.
$$

The minus sign in the exponent of $\bar q$ reflects the usual convention in which $\bar\tau$ is the complex conjugate of $\tau$ on an honest Euclidean torus. Some authors write both $q$ and $\bar q$ formally as independent variables; translate carefully.

## Core idea

Put a two-dimensional theory on a Euclidean torus. Quantize along one cycle. The partition function is a trace over the Hilbert space on a circle:

$$
Z(\tau,\bar\tau)
=
\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H}
\left(
q^{L_0-c_L/24}
\bar q^{\bar L_0-c_R/24}
\right).
$$

In a CFT, $L_0$ and $\bar L_0$ measure left- and right-moving scaling dimensions:

$$
\Delta=h+\bar h,
\qquad
s=h-\bar h.
$$

The shifts by $c_L/24$ and $c_R/24$ are Casimir-energy shifts on the circle.

The same torus can be cut open and quantized in a different direction. The modular transformation $S$ exchanges the two primitive cycles. The transformation $T$ performs a Dehn twist. If the theory is truly defined on the torus, these changes of cycle basis should produce the same physical partition function, or a controlled covariance law when spin structures, background fields, or anomalies are present.

That is the physical content of modular invariance.

## The modular group

The modular group is generated by two transformations:

$$
S:\tau\mapsto-\frac1\tau,
\qquad
T:\tau\mapsto\tau+1.
$$

They obey the projective relations

$$
S^2=1,
\qquad
(ST)^3=1
$$

in $PSL(2,\mathbb Z)$, the quotient of $SL(2,\mathbb Z)$ by its central sign.

Geometrically, $SL(2,\mathbb Z)$ changes the basis of cycles:

$$
\begin{pmatrix}a'\\ b'\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}p&q\\ r&s\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}a\\ b\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
ps-qr=1.
$$

The determinant condition preserves the oriented intersection pairing of the one-cycles. Thus modular transformations are not mysterious extra symmetries of a particular model; they are large diffeomorphisms of the torus.

:::note[Terminology]
People often say “modular invariance” even when the correct statement is modular covariance. Fermionic theories, theories with background fields, and anomalous chiral theories may transform among sectors or pick up phases rather than produce a single invariant scalar function.
:::

## Torus partition function in CFT

A compact unitary CFT has a Hilbert space that decomposes into representations of the left and right chiral algebras. In a rational CFT, there are finitely many characters

$$
\chi_i(\tau)
=
\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal H_i}
q^{L_0-c/24}.
$$

A torus partition function can often be written as

$$
Z(\tau,\bar\tau)=\sum_{i,j}M_{ij}\chi_i(\tau)\overline{\chi_j(\tau)},
$$

where $M_{ij}$ is a matrix of nonnegative integers.

The characters transform among themselves:

$$
\chi_i(-1/\tau)=S_i{}^j\chi_j(\tau),
\qquad
\chi_i(\tau+1)=T_i{}^j\chi_j(\tau).
$$

A modular invariant partition function requires

$$
M=S^\dagger M S,
\qquad
M=T^\dagger M T.
$$

This is one reason two-dimensional rational CFT becomes finite-dimensional linear algebra: the continuous geometry of the torus acts through finite matrices on characters.

The simplest choice is the diagonal invariant,

$$
Z_{\mathrm{diag}}=\sum_i |\chi_i(\tau)|^2,
$$

when the same chiral algebra appears on the left and right. More interesting invariants correspond to extensions of the chiral algebra, orbifolds, simple currents, charge conjugation, and exceptional modular invariants.

## Why modular invariance is powerful

Modular invariance relates low temperature and high temperature. Taking

$$
\tau=i\frac{\beta}{2\pi R}
$$

turns the torus into a thermal circle of circumference $\beta$ and a spatial circle of radius $R$. The $S$ transformation exchanges the two cycles, relating $\beta/R$ to $R/\beta$.

In a CFT, this relation controls the asymptotic density of states. Under mild assumptions, the high-energy growth is governed by the vacuum after an $S$ transform. This is the logic behind the Cardy formula:

$$
\rho(\Delta)\sim
\exp\left(
2\pi\sqrt{\frac{c_{\mathrm{eff}}\Delta}{6}}
\right)
$$

in a schematic single-sector notation. More careful versions keep left and right movers, the lowest conformal weight, degeneracies, and possible extended chiral algebras.

Modular invariance also explains why orbifolds need twisted sectors. Projecting to invariant states is not enough. Under the $S$ transformation, a spatial twist becomes a temporal twist. Therefore a modular-invariant orbifold must include all sectors related by modular transformations.

## Spin structures and fermions

Fermionic theories need extra care. On a torus, a fermion has a choice of periodic or antiperiodic boundary condition around each cycle. These are spin structures.

A single spin-structure partition function is generally not modular invariant. Modular transformations permute spin structures. Thus the natural statement is

$$
Z_\alpha(\tau,\bar\tau)\mapsto Z_{\alpha'}(\tau,\bar\tau)
$$

possibly with phases, where $\alpha$ labels the spin structure.

A bosonic theory obtained by summing over spin structures, such as in a GSO-type projection or a fermion-parity gauging construction, can be modular invariant. A spin CFT may instead be modular covariant as a vector of spin-structure partition functions.

:::caution[Do not erase spin data]
For fermionic theories, “the torus partition function” is incomplete unless it says which spin structure is used or whether a sum over spin structures has been performed. Many apparent modular-invariance paradoxes are bookkeeping mistakes about spin structures.
:::

## Background fields and anomalies

One can refine the torus partition function by coupling the theory to background gauge fields for global symmetries. For a finite symmetry, this means twisted boundary conditions around cycles:

$$
Z_{g,h}(\tau,\bar\tau),
\qquad
gh=hg,
$$

where $g$ is a spatial twist and $h$ is a temporal insertion.

Modular transformations act on the pair $(g,h)$:

$$
S:\ (g,h)\mapsto(h,g^{-1}),
\qquad
T:\ (g,h)\mapsto(g,gh),
$$

up to convention-dependent inverses.

If phases obstruct a consistent modular-covariant set of twisted sectors, the symmetry has a ’t Hooft anomaly. This is a low-dimensional way to see the background-field definition of anomalies: coupling to all flat backgrounds fails to be compatible with large diffeomorphisms and gauge transformations.

A gravitational anomaly is also visible on the torus. If $c_L\ne c_R$, the theory is chiral. Under $T:\tau\mapsto\tau+1$, the partition function can pick up a phase controlled by

$$
c_L-c_R.
$$

Such a theory can still be meaningful as a boundary of a higher-dimensional invertible theory, but it is not an ordinary nonanomalous two-dimensional bosonic theory with a scalar modular-invariant partition function.

## Modular bootstrap viewpoint

Modular invariance constrains the spectrum. Since

$$
Z(\tau,\bar\tau)
=
\sum_{\Delta,s}
d_{\Delta,s}
q^{(\Delta+s)/2-c_L/24}
\bar q^{(\Delta-s)/2-c_R/24},
$$

modular invariance imposes infinitely many relations among the degeneracies $d_{\Delta,s}$.

The modular bootstrap uses these constraints, together with unitarity, discreteness assumptions, positivity, and sometimes extended symmetries, to bound spectra and operator dimensions. This is different from the four-point-function bootstrap, but the philosophy is the same: consistency plus positivity can become quantitative physics.

In rational CFT, modular constraints are algebraic. In irrational CFT, they are analytic constraints on an infinite spectrum. In theories with continuous spectra, one must replace sums by integrals and handle convergence carefully.

## Common pitfalls

**“Every torus partition function is invariant by itself.”** Not for fermionic theories, chiral theories, or theories with background fields. Often a vector of partition functions transforms covariantly.

**“Modular invariance is an internal symmetry.”** It is better thought of as invariance under large diffeomorphisms of the torus. It acts on the geometry of the path integral, not on local fields at fixed spacetime point.

**“Projecting to invariant states gives the orbifold.”** Projection is only half the story. Twisted sectors are required by modular covariance.

**“The central charge alone determines the partition function.”** The central charge controls universal transformations and asymptotics, but the full spectrum and chiral algebra matter enormously.

**“Chiral CFTs are inconsistent.”** Chiral theories can be perfectly meaningful, but often as relative theories, boundary theories, or theories with gravitational anomaly rather than ordinary nonanomalous standalone two-dimensional theories.

## Research status

The basic modular transformation theory of two-dimensional CFT is established. For rational CFT, it is a central organizing principle behind characters, fusion, and modular tensor categories. For non-rational CFTs, modular invariance remains a powerful analytic constraint but requires more care.

Active work uses modular covariance in fermionic theories, anomalous symmetry defects, non-invertible symmetry, generalized orbifolds, holomorphic CFT classification, modular bootstrap, and holography. The safe lesson for this volume is durable: torus consistency is one of the sharpest low-dimensional tests of whether a proposed symmetry, spectrum, or gauging is complete.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: The modular S transformation exchanges cycles

A torus is defined by the lattice generated by $1$ and $\tau$. Show that the lattice generated by $1$ and $-1/\tau$ is equivalent to the original lattice after rescaling by $\tau$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The transformed torus has generators

$$
1,\qquad -\frac1\tau.
$$

Multiplying both generators by $\tau$ gives

$$
\tau,\qquad -1.
$$

These generate the same lattice as $1$ and $\tau$ because changing a generator by a sign does not change the lattice. Thus $S:\tau\mapsto-1/\tau$ exchanges the two primitive cycles, up to orientation convention.

</details>

### Exercise 2: T invariance and spin

For a state with conformal weights $(h,\bar h)$, the factor in the partition function contains

$$
q^{h-c_L/24}\bar q^{\bar h-c_R/24}.
$$

What phase is picked up under $\tau\mapsto\tau+1$?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under $T$, $q\mapsto e^{2\pi i}q$ and $\bar q\mapsto e^{-2\pi i}\bar q$. Therefore the state contributes the phase

$$
\exp\left[
2\pi i\left(h-\bar h-\frac{c_L-c_R}{24}\right)
\right].
$$

The quantity $s=h-\bar h$ is the spin. In a nonchiral bosonic modular-invariant theory, the sum of states must combine so that the full partition function is invariant.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why twisted sectors appear

In a finite-symmetry orbifold, explain why projecting onto invariant states is not enough for modular invariance.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Projection inserts group elements along the Euclidean-time cycle. But the modular $S$ transformation exchanges the temporal and spatial cycles. A temporal insertion is therefore mapped to a spatial twist. If the theory includes projected untwisted states but no spatially twisted Hilbert spaces, the set of sectors is not closed under $S$. Twisted sectors are required for modular covariance.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Character modular invariance

Suppose characters transform as $\chi_i\mapsto S_i{}^j\chi_j$ under $S$. Show that

$$
Z=\sum_{i,j}M_{ij}\chi_i\overline{\chi_j}
$$

is invariant under $S$ if $M=S^\dagger M S$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under $S$,

$$
Z\mapsto
\sum_{i,j}M_{ij}S_i{}^k\chi_k\,
\overline{S_j{}^\ell\chi_\ell}.
$$

This is

$$
\sum_{k,\ell}(S^\dagger M S)_{k\ell}\chi_k\overline{\chi_\ell}.
$$

If $S^\dagger M S=M$, the transformed partition function equals the original one.

</details>
