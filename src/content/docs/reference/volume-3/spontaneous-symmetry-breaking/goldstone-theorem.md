## Summary

**Goldstone's theorem** says that an exact continuous internal symmetry cannot be spontaneously broken in a relativistic QFT without producing massless excitations. In its common Lorentz-invariant form:

> If a continuous internal symmetry generated by $Q_A$ is spontaneously broken, and the usual locality, current-conservation, spectral, and vacuum assumptions hold, then the spectrum contains a massless spin-zero particle coupled to the broken current.

The practical diagnostic of breaking is a local operator $\mathcal O_i$ such that

$$
\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle
=
i\langle[Q_A,\mathcal O_i]\rangle
\ne0.
$$

The theorem is then almost forced by the Ward identity. The time-ordered current correlator obeys, with this volume's charge convention,

$$
\partial_\mu\langle T\,j_A^\mu(x)\mathcal O_i(0)\rangle
=
-i\delta^d(x)\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle.
$$

In momentum space, the right-hand side is nonzero as $p\to0$. A regular correlator cannot have a nonzero divergence at zero momentum. Therefore the current correlator must contain a pole,

$$
\langle T\,j_A^\mu(p)\mathcal O_i(-p)\rangle
\sim
\frac{p^\mu}{p^2+i\epsilon}\,\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle,
$$

up to convention-dependent factors of $i$. A pole at $p^2=0$ is the field-theoretic signature of a massless particle: the Goldstone boson.

The slogan is:

$$
\text{broken continuous internal symmetry}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{massless current pole}.
$$

The slogan is not the whole theorem. The assumptions matter, and many interesting systems evade the naive version without violating it.

## Core idea

Let $G$ be a continuous internal symmetry with conserved currents

$$
\partial_\mu j_A^\mu=0,
$$

and charges

$$
Q_A=\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,j_A^0(t,\mathbf x).
$$

A generator $Q_A$ is spontaneously broken if there is a local operator $\mathcal O_i$ for which

$$
\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle
=
i\langle[Q_A,\mathcal O_i]\rangle
\ne0.
$$

The nonzero commutator says that the vacuum is not invariant under $Q_A$ in a way visible to local observables. The current $j_A^\mu$ is conserved away from operator insertions, but the charge acts nontrivially when the current surface crosses $\mathcal O_i$. In correlation functions this appears as a contact term.

The contact term is local, but its consequences are infrared. A nonzero charge action must be carried by states of arbitrarily low energy, because a conserved charge cannot create a finite-energy localized excitation at zero momentum while remaining time independent. In Lorentz-invariant language, the correlator must have spectral support at

$$
p^2=0.
$$

That support is the Goldstone boson.

## Setup and conventions

We assume:

1. a relativistic QFT in flat spacetime;
2. a translation-invariant vacuum $|\Omega\rangle$;
3. an exact continuous internal global symmetry $G$;
4. a locally conserved current $j_A^\mu$;
5. locality or microcausality;
6. a positive-energy spectral representation;
7. a local order parameter detecting the broken generator.

The internal-symmetry restriction excludes spacetime symmetries such as translations, rotations, boosts, dilatations, and supersymmetry. Those have related but distinct Goldstone-counting rules.

We use the convention already adopted in this volume:

$$
\delta_A\mathcal O=i[Q_A,\mathcal O].
$$

For a regulated charge, write

$$
Q_A[f_R]
=
\int d^{d-1}\mathbf x\,f_R(\mathbf x)j_A^0(t,\mathbf x),
$$

where $f_R=1$ near the operator insertion and decays smoothly at large radius. The infinite-volume charge is understood as the limit $R\to\infty$ after evaluating commutators with local operators.

:::note[Convention-sensitive Ward sign]
With $\delta_A\mathcal O=i[Q_A,\mathcal O]$, differentiating a time-ordered product gives

$$
\partial_\mu\langle T\,j_A^\mu(x)\mathcal O_i(0)\rangle
=
-i\delta^d(x)\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle.
$$

If a source uses $U(\epsilon)=e^{+i\epsilon Q}$ instead of $e^{-i\epsilon Q}$, or defines $\delta\mathcal O$ with the opposite sign, this formula changes by signs and factors of $i$. The pole and the massless state do not.
:::

## Statement of the theorem

A useful working statement is:

:::note[Goldstone theorem]
Let $G$ be an exact continuous internal global symmetry of a Lorentz-invariant QFT. Suppose a vacuum $|\Omega\rangle$ spontaneously breaks a generator $Q_A$, meaning that for some local operator $\mathcal O_i$,

$$
\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle\ne0.
$$

If the current is locally conserved, the vacuum is translation invariant, locality and the spectral condition hold, and the relevant charge action is well-defined by regulated charges, then the spectrum contains massless excitations that couple to $j_A^\mu$. In a standard relativistic vacuum with ordinary internal symmetry breaking, the number of Goldstone bosons equals the number of broken generators.
:::

The theorem is often summarized as “one Goldstone boson per broken generator.” That counting statement is correct in the standard Lorentz-invariant internal-symmetry setting. It is not a universal counting law for finite density, nonrelativistic systems, spacetime symmetry breaking, gauge theories, or systems with long-range forces.

## Ward identity proof: the pole must be there

Let

$$
G_{Ai}^\mu(x)=\langle\Omega|T\,j_A^\mu(x)\mathcal O_i(0)|\Omega\rangle.
$$

Away from $x=0$, current conservation gives

$$
\partial_\mu G_{Ai}^\mu(x)=0.
$$

At $x=0$, differentiating the time-ordering produces the equal-time commutator. With our convention,

$$
\partial_\mu G_{Ai}^\mu(x)
=
-i\delta^d(x)\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle.
$$

Fourier transform to momentum space:

$$
G_{Ai}^\mu(p)=\int d^dx\,e^{ip\cdot x}G_{Ai}^\mu(x).
$$

Then

$$
ip_\mu G_{Ai}^\mu(p)
=
-i\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle,
$$

or equivalently, up to the chosen Fourier and Ward-sign convention,

$$
p_\mu G_{Ai}^\mu(p)
=\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle.
$$

If the generator is broken, the right-hand side is nonzero. Near $p=0$, Lorentz covariance implies that the singular part of a vector correlator with a scalar operator has the form

$$
G_{Ai}^\mu(p)=p^\mu F_{Ai}(p^2)+\text{less singular structures}.
$$

Then

$$
p_\mu G_{Ai}^\mu(p)=p^2F_{Ai}(p^2)+\cdots.
$$

For this to approach a nonzero constant as $p\to0$, $F_{Ai}$ must behave as

$$
F_{Ai}(p^2)\sim \frac{\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle}{p^2+i\epsilon}.
$$

Therefore

$$
G_{Ai}^\mu(p)
\sim
\frac{p^\mu}{p^2+i\epsilon}\,\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle.
$$

This is the **Goldstone pole**. The theorem is the interpretation of this pole using the spectral representation: a pole at $p^2=0$ means a massless state couples to the current.

This proof is powerful because it does not require a weakly coupled Lagrangian, a classical Mexican-hat potential, or elementary scalar fields. It uses the current, the broken charge action, locality, and spectral positivity.

## Spectral interpretation

Insert a complete set of energy-momentum eigenstates $|n,p\rangle$ into the Wightman function

$$
\langle\Omega|j_A^\mu(x)\mathcal O_i(0)|\Omega\rangle.
$$

Translation invariance gives factors of $e^{-ip\cdot x}$, and the spectral condition restricts $p^0\ge0$. Singularities of the Fourier transform occur when intermediate states can go on shell.

The Goldstone contribution has the form

$$
\langle\Omega|j_A^\mu(0)|\pi_B(p)\rangle
=
iF_{AB}p^\mu,
$$

where $|\pi_B(p)\rangle$ is a one-Goldstone state and $F_{AB}$ is a matrix of decay constants. The order parameter has a nonzero overlap with the same state:

$$
\langle\pi_B(p)|\mathcal O_i(0)|\Omega\rangle=Z_{Bi}+O(p).
$$

Together these matrix elements generate a pole in the current-order-parameter correlator:

$$
G_{Ai}^\mu(p)
\supset
\frac{iF_{AB}p^\mu Z_{Bi}}{p^2+i\epsilon}.
$$

Taking the divergence gives

$$
p_\mu G_{Ai}^\mu(p)
\supset
\frac{iF_{AB}p^2 Z_{Bi}}{p^2+i\epsilon}
\to iF_{AB}Z_{Bi}.
$$

The Ward identity requires this to reproduce the broken variation $\langle\delta_A\mathcal O_i\rangle$, with the appropriate convention-dependent factor of $i$.

Thus the nonzero order-parameter variation is not merely a static statement about vacua. It is the residue of a massless pole.

## Example: broken U(1) scalar theory

Take a complex scalar with

$$
\mathcal L=\partial_\mu\Phi^\dagger\partial^\mu\Phi
-\frac{\lambda}{4}\left(2\Phi^\dagger\Phi-v^2\right)^2.
$$

The $U(1)$ symmetry acts as

$$
\Phi\mapsto e^{-i\alpha}\Phi.
$$

Choose the vacuum

$$
\langle\Phi\rangle=\frac{v}{\sqrt2}.
$$

A useful parameterization is

$$
\Phi(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\left(v+\rho(x)\right)e^{-i\pi(x)/v}.
$$

The field $\rho$ is radial and massive. The field $\pi$ moves along the vacuum circle and is massless at the level enforced by symmetry.

The Noether current is, with this convention,

$$
j^\mu=i\left(\Phi^\dagger\partial^\mu\Phi-(\partial^\mu\Phi^\dagger)\Phi\right).
$$

Substituting the polar parameterization gives

$$
j^\mu=\frac{(v+\rho)^2}{v}\partial^\mu\pi
=
v\partial^\mu\pi+2\rho\partial^\mu\pi+\cdots.
$$

At leading order,

$$
\langle\Omega|j^\mu(0)|\pi(p)\rangle
=ivp^\mu
$$

up to the one-particle-state normalization convention. Thus the Goldstone decay constant is $F=v$ at tree level.

The symmetry action on the chosen vacuum is visible because

$$
\delta\Phi=-i\Phi,
\qquad
\langle\delta\Phi\rangle=-i\frac{v}{\sqrt2}\ne0.
$$

The current must therefore contain a massless pole. The polar field $\pi$ is a convenient weak-coupling coordinate for that pole.

## Example: O(N) to O(N−1)

Let $\phi^i$ be an $O(N)$ vector with

$$
\langle\phi^i\rangle=v\delta^{iN}.
$$

The unbroken subgroup is $O(N-1)$, acting on $i=1,\dots,N-1$. The broken generators are rotations in the planes $(a,N)$ for

$$
a=1,\dots,N-1.
$$

There are

$$
\dim O(N)-\dim O(N-1)=N-1
$$

broken generators. In a Lorentz-invariant relativistic vacuum, Goldstone's theorem gives $N-1$ massless fields.

A convenient low-energy parameterization is

$$
\phi(x)=\left(\pi^1(x),\dots,\pi^{N-1}(x),\sqrt{v^2-\pi^a(x)\pi^a(x)}\right),
$$

valid locally on the vacuum sphere. The $\pi^a$ are coordinates on

$$
S^{N-1}\simeq O(N)/O(N-1).
$$

Their derivative interactions are the beginning of the nonlinear sigma model.

## Goldstone fields and the vacuum manifold

The previous page explained the vacuum manifold $G/H$. The Goldstone fields are local coordinates on this manifold:

$$
U(x)=e^{i\pi^A(x)X_A/f},
$$

where $X_A$ are broken generators. A slowly varying field configuration moves from one vacuum orientation to a nearby one as a function of spacetime.

The effective action cannot contain a potential for $\pi^A$ if the symmetry is exact, because a constant shift along the vacuum manifold changes no energy. Therefore the leading terms contain derivatives:

$$
\mathcal L_{\text{eff}}
=\frac12 g_{AB}(\pi)\partial_\mu\pi^A\partial^\mu\pi^B+\cdots.
$$

Expanding near a point on $G/H$ gives massless fields. The theorem is the exact version of this geometric intuition.

## Counting: when one per broken generator is true

For a Lorentz-invariant vacuum and ordinary internal symmetry breaking,

$$
N_{\text{Goldstone}}=\dim G-\dim H.
$$

This is the version most often used in relativistic particle physics. Examples:

$$
U(1)\to\{1\}:\qquad N_{\text{Goldstone}}=1,
$$

$$
O(N)\to O(N-1):\qquad N_{\text{Goldstone}}=N-1,
$$

$$
SU(N)_L\times SU(N)_R\to SU(N)_V:\qquad N_{\text{Goldstone}}=N^2-1.
$$

The last example is the symmetry-breaking pattern underlying the pion octet in massless three-flavor QCD, before quark masses and the anomalous axial symmetry are included.

## What the theorem does not say

Goldstone's theorem is precise, but the folk slogan around it is often too broad.

**It does not say every degeneracy gives a massless particle.** Discrete symmetry breaking gives degenerate vacua but no continuous flat direction.

**It does not say the Goldstone is an elementary field in the ultraviolet Lagrangian.** The Goldstone boson can be a composite excitation, as in chiral symmetry breaking.

**It does not say the massless particle is visible in every operator channel.** The theorem guarantees coupling to the broken current and to some order-parameter channel, not to arbitrary local operators.

**It does not apply directly to gauge redundancy.** If the “broken symmetry” is gauged, the would-be Goldstone mode can be absent from the gauge-invariant spectrum. In the Higgs mechanism, it supplies the longitudinal polarization of a massive gauge boson in a gauge-fixed description.

**It does not give the nonrelativistic counting rule.** At finite density or without Lorentz invariance, two broken generators can produce one Goldstone mode with quadratic dispersion. A common modern counting formula is

$$
N_{\text{NG}}=N_{\text{broken}}-\frac12\operatorname{rank}\rho,
\qquad
\rho_{AB}=-\frac{i}{V}\langle[Q_A,Q_B]\rangle,
$$

under appropriate assumptions. This is a different theorem, not a correction to the Lorentz-invariant one.

**It does not override low-dimensional infrared fluctuations.** In two spacetime dimensions, continuous internal symmetry breaking is forbidden under the assumptions of Coleman's theorem. The conditional statement “if it is broken, there is a Goldstone mode” coexists with the separate result that the assumed broken vacuum does not exist in such systems.

## Explicit breaking and pseudo-Goldstone bosons

If the symmetry is not exact, the Ward identity becomes

$$
\partial_\mu j_A^\mu=\mathcal B_A,
$$

where $\mathcal B_A$ is the explicit-breaking operator. The contact-term argument no longer requires a pole at $p^2=0$. Instead, the would-be Goldstone can acquire a small mass.

This is the origin of pseudo-Goldstone bosons. In QCD, chiral symmetry is approximate because the light quark masses are small but nonzero. The pions are light, not exactly massless.

The sharp distinction is:

$$
\text{exact broken symmetry}
\Rightarrow
m_\pi=0,
$$

while

$$
\text{approximate broken symmetry}
\Rightarrow
m_\pi^2\propto \text{explicit breaking}
$$

in many low-energy regimes.

The later pages on explicit versus spontaneous breaking and pseudo-Goldstone bosons develop this carefully.

## Relation to anomalies

An anomaly can modify or obstruct the conservation of a current after quantization. If the anomalous current is not conserved, it cannot be used directly in Goldstone's theorem as an exact symmetry current.

This matters in QCD. The nonabelian chiral symmetry

$$
SU(N_f)_L\times SU(N_f)_R
$$

can be spontaneously broken to $SU(N_f)_V$, producing $N_f^2-1$ pseudo-Goldstone or Goldstone modes depending on quark masses. The axial $U(1)_A$ symmetry is anomalous, so it does not produce an additional light Goldstone boson in the same way.

Anomalies also constrain broken phases through anomaly matching. A Goldstone effective action may need a Wess–Zumino or Wess–Zumino–Witten term so that the infrared theory reproduces the ultraviolet anomaly. Thus anomalies do not merely spoil symmetries; they organize what the Goldstone fields must do.

## Common pitfalls

**Saying “the charge does not annihilate the vacuum” too casually.** In infinite volume, $Q_A|\Omega\rangle$ may not be a normalizable state. The safer statement is that a regulated charge has a nonzero commutator with a local operator in the vacuum.

**Confusing the classical flat direction with the theorem.** Classical potentials are examples. The theorem is a statement about currents, Ward identities, and the spectrum of the quantum theory.

**Forgetting the assumptions.** Internal symmetry, exact conservation, Lorentz-invariant vacuum, locality, and spectral positivity are doing real work.

**Applying one-per-generator counting at finite density.** Nonrelativistic systems can have fewer Goldstone modes than broken generators.

**Calling the Higgs mechanism a violation of Goldstone's theorem.** It is not. A local gauge redundancy is not a physical global symmetry, and the gauge-invariant spectrum is organized differently.

**Forgetting explicit breaking.** A tiny explicit-breaking term turns a Goldstone boson into a pseudo-Goldstone boson. The small mass is not a failure of the theorem because the exact-symmetry assumption has changed.

## Research status

The relativistic internal-symmetry theorem is established textbook QFT.

Active and subtle areas include Goldstone physics for spacetime symmetries, finite density, nonrelativistic systems, dissipative/open systems, fracton and subsystem symmetries, higher-form symmetries, non-invertible symmetries, and phases with topological order. In these settings, the words “broken generator,” “charged operator,” “Goldstone mode,” and “counting” require more structure than in the ordinary relativistic theorem.

Another active interface is anomaly matching: when a symmetry is broken, the Goldstone effective action may carry Wess–Zumino terms or couple to topological sectors so that the infrared reproduces the ultraviolet anomaly.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Ward identity forces a pole

Suppose Lorentz covariance gives a current-scalar correlator of the form

$$
G^\mu(p)=p^\mu F(p^2)
$$

near $p=0$. If the Ward identity requires

$$
p_\mu G^\mu(p)=v
$$

with $v\ne0$, show that $G^\mu$ has a massless pole.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Substitute the Lorentz-covariant form:

$$
p_\mu G^\mu(p)=p^2F(p^2).
$$

The Ward identity says

$$
p^2F(p^2)=v.
$$

Therefore

$$
F(p^2)=\frac{v}{p^2}
$$

near $p=0$, up to less singular terms and the usual $i\epsilon$ prescription in Lorentzian signature. Hence

$$
G^\mu(p)=\frac{vp^\mu}{p^2+i\epsilon}+\cdots.
$$

The pole at $p^2=0$ is the Goldstone pole.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Counting Goldstones in O(N) breaking

Show that the breaking pattern

$$
O(N)\to O(N-1)
$$

has $N-1$ broken generators.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The dimension of $O(N)$ is the number of antisymmetric $N\times N$ matrices:

$$
\dim O(N)=\frac{N(N-1)}{2}.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\dim O(N-1)=\frac{(N-1)(N-2)}{2}.
$$

The difference is

$$
\frac{N(N-1)}{2}-\frac{(N-1)(N-2)}{2}=N-1.
$$

In a Lorentz-invariant vacuum with ordinary internal symmetry breaking, this gives $N-1$ Goldstone bosons.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Current coupling in the broken U(1) model

For

$$
\Phi(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}(v+\rho(x))e^{-i\pi(x)/v},
$$

and

$$
j^\mu=i\left(\Phi^\dagger\partial^\mu\Phi-(\partial^\mu\Phi^\dagger)\Phi\right),
$$

show that the current begins as $j^\mu=v\partial^\mu\pi+\cdots$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Let $r=v+\rho$. Then

$$
\Phi=\frac{r}{\sqrt2}e^{-i\pi/v}.
$$

A direct derivative gives

$$
\Phi^\dagger\partial^\mu\Phi-(\partial^\mu\Phi^\dagger)\Phi
= -i\frac{r^2}{v}\partial^\mu\pi.
$$

Multiplying by $i$ gives

$$
j^\mu=\frac{r^2}{v}\partial^\mu\pi
=\frac{(v+\rho)^2}{v}\partial^\mu\pi.
$$

Expanding around $\rho=0$,

$$
j^\mu=v\partial^\mu\pi+2\rho\partial^\mu\pi+\frac{\rho^2}{v}\partial^\mu\pi.
$$

Thus the current couples linearly to the Goldstone field with strength $v$ at tree level.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Why discrete breaking has no Goldstone pole

A $\mathbb Z_2$ symmetry has two broken vacua. Explain why Goldstone's Ward-identity proof does not apply.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Goldstone's proof starts from a locally conserved current $j^\mu_A$ for a continuous generator $Q_A$. A discrete symmetry has no infinitesimal generator and no Noether current of this kind. Therefore there is no current Ward identity with a nonzero contact term

$$
\partial_\mu\langle Tj^\mu_A(x)\mathcal O(0)\rangle
\propto \delta^d(x)\langle\delta_A\mathcal O\rangle.
$$

Discrete breaking can produce degenerate vacua and domain walls, but it does not force a $p^\mu/p^2$ current pole or a massless Goldstone boson.

</details>
