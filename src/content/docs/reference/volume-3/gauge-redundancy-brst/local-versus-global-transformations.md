## Summary

The phrase “local gauge transformation” is dangerously overloaded. A transformation can have a spacetime-dependent parameter and still be either a pure redundancy, a residual gauge transformation after gauge fixing, a boundary symmetry with a charge, or a large topological transformation. The physics depends on support, boundary conditions, topology, and on which transformations are included in the gauge group being quotiented.

The reliable distinction is:

$$
\text{compactly supported gauge transformation}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\text{redundancy},
$$

while

$$
\text{nontrivial boundary or topological action}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\text{check whether it is a physical symmetry, a sector label, or an identification}.
$$

This page gives a working taxonomy. It explains why Gauss law generates gauge redundancy in the bulk, why boundary terms become charges, why constant transformations can be physical in a global theory but redundant or boundary-sensitive in a gauge theory, and why large gauge transformations are not the same as ordinary small gauge transformations.

The practical payoff is large. This distinction is needed for electric charge in QED, color singlet constraints in non-Abelian gauge theory, theta vacua, Chern–Simons level quantization, Wilson and ’t Hooft lines, anomaly inflow, soft theorems, edge modes, and higher-form symmetries.

## Core idea

Let $\Sigma$ be a spatial slice. A gauge transformation is often a map

$$
g:\Sigma\to G,
$$

or in spacetime,

$$
g:M\to G.
$$

That statement is not yet physics. To define the theory, one must say which maps are quotiented as redundancy, what boundary behavior is allowed, which topological sectors are included, and how matter and line operators transform.

A useful hierarchy is:

| Name | Typical condition | Physical role |
|---|---|---|
| Compactly supported gauge transformation | $g=1$ near $\partial\Sigma$ | Pure redundancy generated by constraints |
| Small gauge transformation | Connected to identity | Usually redundancy if boundary-trivial |
| Residual gauge transformation | Preserves a gauge condition | Can be redundancy or physical, depending on boundary action |
| Boundary/asymptotic transformation | Nontrivial at $\partial\Sigma$ | Can carry a physical charge |
| Constant transformation | $g(x)=g_0$ | Global symmetry if not gauged; subtle in gauge theory |
| Large gauge transformation | Not connected to identity | Can identify sectors, shift theta data, or act projectively |
| Global internal symmetry | Not part of the gauge redundancy | Acts on gauge-invariant physical states/operators |

The central warning is that “local” does not mean “redundant” by itself, and “global” does not mean “physical” by itself.

## Setup and conventions

For Abelian gauge theory in the conventions of this volume,

$$
A_\mu\mapsto A_\mu+\partial_\mu\alpha,
\qquad
\psi\mapsto e^{-iq\alpha}\psi.
$$

For non-Abelian gauge theory, write $A_\mu=A_\mu^aT^a$ and

$$
D_\mu=\partial_\mu+igA_\mu.
$$

With matter transforming as $\psi\mapsto U^{-1}\psi$,

$$
A_\mu\mapsto A_\mu^U
=U^{-1}A_\mu U-\frac{i}{g}U^{-1}\partial_\mu U.
$$

A gauge transformation is called **small** if it lies in the identity component of the relevant transformation group. It is called **large** if it is not continuously deformable to the identity within the allowed class of transformations.

A transformation is called **proper** or **pure gauge** in many Hamiltonian references if it is generated by constraints with no boundary charge. It is called **improper**, **asymptotic**, or **large** in various literatures if it has nontrivial boundary or topological action. Terminology varies, so this page uses descriptive phrases rather than relying on one naming convention.

:::note[Terminology-sensitive source note]
The words “small,” “large,” “proper,” “improper,” “residual,” and “global gauge transformation” are not used identically across high-energy theory, mathematical physics, gravity, and condensed matter. The invariant question is always: is this transformation quotiented as redundancy, or does it act on boundary data, line operators, topological sectors, or physical states?
:::

## Compactly supported transformations: bulk redundancy

A compactly supported gauge transformation is the cleanest example of redundancy. In Abelian gauge theory, take $\alpha(\mathbf x)$ to vanish near the boundary of a spatial slice $\Sigma$. The Gauss-law generator is schematically

$$
G[\alpha]
=\int_\Sigma d^3\mathbf x\,\alpha(\mathbf x)\bigl(\nabla\cdot\mathbf E-\rho\bigr).
$$

On physical states,

$$
G[\alpha]|\text{phys}\rangle=0
$$

for all such $\alpha$. This equation says that physical states do not change under bulk gauge transformations that die at the boundary.

The same logic works non-Abelianly with

$$
G[\alpha]=\int_\Sigma d^3\mathbf x\,\alpha^a\bigl((D_iE^i)^a-\rho^a\bigr),
$$

again with boundary qualifications.

This is the precise Hamiltonian expression of the slogan “gauge symmetry is redundancy.” The generator of a compactly supported gauge transformation vanishes on physical states because it is a constraint.

## Boundary transformations: charges at the edge

Now allow $\alpha$ to approach a nonzero value at the boundary. In Maxwell theory,

$$
\int_\Sigma \alpha\,\nabla\cdot\mathbf E
=\int_{\partial\Sigma}\alpha\,\mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf S
-\int_\Sigma \mathbf E\cdot\nabla\alpha.
$$

The surface term

$$
Q[\alpha_{\partial}]
=\int_{\partial\Sigma}\alpha_{\partial}\,\mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf S
$$

need not vanish. For $\alpha_{\partial}=1$ at spatial infinity, this is the total electric charge:

$$
Q=\int_{S^2_\infty}\mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf S.
$$

This is why the same-looking expression can be either a redundancy generator or a physical charge. If the smearing function dies at the boundary, no charge remains. If it survives at the boundary, the surface term can act on physical states.

Boundary transformations appear in many guises:

- electric charge in QED,
- color constraints and color charges in non-Abelian theories with external sources,
- edge modes in gauge theory on manifolds with boundary,
- asymptotic symmetries and soft charges,
- Chern–Simons boundary current algebras,
- global symmetries living on defects or interfaces.

The moral is not “gauge transformations are sometimes physical” in a vague sense. The precise statement is:

$$
\text{constraint part} = 0\text{ on physical states},
\qquad
\text{boundary part} = \text{possible physical charge}.
$$

## Constant transformations: global or gauge?

A constant transformation can mean different things in different theories.

In a theory with a global $U(1)$ symmetry, the constant phase rotation

$$
\phi\mapsto e^{-i\alpha}\phi
$$

is a physical global symmetry. It has a conserved Noether charge and charged local operators.

In a $U(1)$ gauge theory, the same-looking transformation is part of the gauge transformation law if it is allowed as $\alpha(x)=\alpha_0$. But whether it is pure redundancy depends on boundary conditions and the Hilbert space.

On a compact space without boundary, Gauss law often forces total gauge charge to vanish, so constant gauge transformations act trivially on physical states. On a noncompact space, transformations approaching a constant at infinity can be associated with electric charge measured at infinity. Charged states then transform by a phase under the boundary symmetry.

This explains a common source of confusion. In QED, the electron field $\psi(x)$ is not a gauge-invariant local operator, but charged asymptotic states exist. The charge is tied to electromagnetic flux at infinity and to the dressing of charged states. The ordinary statement “the electron has electric charge” is true, but it is not the same statement as “there is a gauge-invariant local electron operator.”

## Residual transformations after gauge fixing

A gauge condition usually does not remove all gauge freedom.

For example, Lorenz gauge imposes

$$
\partial^\mu A_\mu=0.
$$

Under $A_\mu\mapsto A_\mu+\partial_\mu\alpha$,

$$
\partial^\mu A_\mu\mapsto \partial^\mu A_\mu+\Box\alpha.
$$

So Lorenz gauge is preserved if

$$
\Box\alpha=0.
$$

Such transformations are called residual gauge transformations. They are not automatically physical symmetries. One must still ask whether $\alpha$ is compactly supported, whether it has boundary values, and whether it changes topological data.

Similarly, Coulomb gauge

$$
\nabla\cdot\mathbf A=0
$$

is preserved by transformations satisfying

$$
\nabla^2\alpha=0.
$$

With boundary conditions forcing $\alpha$ to vanish at infinity, this may remove all residual Abelian freedom. With different boundary conditions, constants or angular functions at infinity can remain.

Residual gauge transformations are therefore gauge-condition-preserving transformations, not a separate category of physics by themselves.

## Large gauge transformations: topology, not size

“Large” does not mean large value of the parameter. It means not continuously connected to the identity within the allowed transformation group.

For example, in a non-Abelian gauge theory on a spatial slice compactified to $S^3$, gauge transformations are maps

$$
g:S^3\to G.
$$

For $G=SU(N)$ with $N\ge2$, these maps have disconnected components classified by

$$
\pi_3(SU(N))\cong\mathbb Z.
$$

A transformation with nonzero winding number is large. It cannot be deformed to the identity while staying inside the allowed maps.

Large gauge transformations matter because they can:

- identify gauge-field configurations in different-looking topological sectors,
- shift Chern–Simons functionals by integers,
- require quantization of topological couplings,
- define theta vacua,
- act nontrivially on fermion determinants,
- reveal global anomalies,
- constrain the allowed line operators and global form of the gauge group.

In Yang–Mills theory, instantons and theta vacua are inseparable from this topology. In Chern–Simons theory, invariance under large gauge transformations quantizes the level. In chiral gauge theories, large transformations can detect global anomalies invisible in infinitesimal perturbation theory.

## Global form: same Lie algebra, different theory

Local gauge transformations see the Lie algebra. Global questions see the group.

The Lie algebras of $SU(N)$ and $PSU(N)=SU(N)/\mathbb Z_N$ are the same, but the gauge theories can differ. They allow different bundles, different line operators, and different one-form global symmetries. Thus the phrase “the gauge algebra is $\mathfrak{su}(N)$” does not fully define the theory.

The choice of global form determines which transformations are redundancies and which line operators are genuine. For example, a Wilson line in the fundamental representation is a genuine line operator in an $SU(N)$ gauge theory, but not in a pure $PSU(N)$ gauge theory unless additional surface data are included. Conversely, magnetic line operators are classified differently.

This is the same lesson at a higher level:

$$
\text{local infinitesimal data} \neq \text{full global gauge-theory data}.
$$

## Gauge transformations versus global symmetries after gauging

Suppose a theory has a global group $G$ and we gauge it. What happened to the global symmetry?

The transformations in $G$ that are now part of the gauge redundancy no longer act as ordinary physical global symmetries on local gauge-invariant operators. The charged local operators of the ungauged theory are no longer gauge-invariant by themselves. Some information may reappear in new forms:

- gauge-invariant composites,
- Wilson lines and other extended operators,
- boundary charges,
- magnetic or electric higher-form symmetries,
- quantum symmetries after finite-group gauging,
- topological sectors and discrete theta terms.

For finite group gauging, an especially clean phenomenon occurs. Gauging a finite Abelian group can produce a dual “quantum symmetry” acting on twisted sectors. This is not the original symmetry acting in the old way; it is a new symmetry of the gauged theory.

For continuous gauge theory, the analogous story is richer and often expressed through one-form symmetries, line operators, and global-form choices.

## A decision procedure

When faced with a transformation, do not classify it by vibes. Ask the following questions.

| Diagnostic question | If yes | If no |
|---|---|---|
| Is it part of the gauge group being quotiented? | Candidate redundancy | Candidate physical symmetry or external operation |
| Does it vanish or become identity at the boundary? | Usually pure redundancy | Boundary charge may survive |
| Is it generated by a constraint with no boundary term? | Acts trivially on physical states | May act nontrivially |
| Is it connected to the identity? | Small transformation | Large transformation possible |
| Does it preserve the gauge condition? | Residual in that gauge | Not allowed within that gauge slice |
| Does it act on gauge-invariant local operators? | Physical global symmetry | May still act on lines, boundaries, sectors |
| Does it change line operators or bundle sectors? | Global-form/topological data involved | Local quotient may be enough |

This decision procedure keeps the language honest across canonical quantization, path integrals, lattice gauge theory, Chern–Simons theory, anomaly calculations, and holographic boundary problems.

## Examples

### Maxwell theory on flat space

For $\alpha$ compactly supported in space, $A\mapsto A+d\alpha$ is a redundancy. For $\alpha\to\alpha_\infty$ at spatial infinity, the generator contains

$$
\alpha_\infty\int_{S^2_\infty}\mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf S,
$$

which measures electric charge. Thus the boundary value acts on charged sectors.

### Pure Yang–Mills theory

Small gauge transformations are redundancies. Large gauge transformations classify topological structure and theta vacua. The global form of the gauge group affects which Wilson and ’t Hooft lines are genuine. Pure Yang–Mills theory also has center one-form symmetry when the gauge group and matter content allow it.

### Scalar electrodynamics

The phase rotation of the scalar is not an independent global symmetry after the $U(1)$ is gauged. The local field $\phi(x)$ is gauge-variant. Gauge-invariant diagnostics include $|\phi|^2$, field strengths, Wilson lines, dressed charged operators, and phase behavior of correlation functions.

### Chern–Simons theory

Large gauge transformations shift the Chern–Simons action by a multiple of $2\pi$ times the level. Invariance of $e^{iS}$ requires level quantization. On a manifold with boundary, gauge transformations can induce physical boundary degrees of freedom and current algebras.

## Common pitfalls

**“Local means gauge and global means physical.”** No. A spacetime-dependent transformation can be a physical spacetime symmetry or a spurionic source transformation. A constant transformation can be part of gauge redundancy. The classification depends on the theory and boundary conditions.

**“A residual gauge transformation is automatically a global symmetry.”** Residual means it preserves the chosen gauge condition. It may still be pure redundancy.

**“Large gauge transformations have large parameters.”** Large means topologically disconnected from the identity, not numerically large.

**“A constant gauge transformation is always trivial.”** On a compact space with no boundary and Gauss-law constraints, often yes. On a noncompact space or with boundaries, the constant boundary value can generate a charge.

**“The Lie algebra defines the gauge theory.”** The Lie algebra defines only local infinitesimal structure. The global form of the gauge group, allowed bundles, and line operators can define distinct theories.

**“Boundary charges are optional decoration.”** Boundary charges can determine superselection sectors, edge modes, soft theorems, and anomaly inflow. They are often central, not decorative.

**“Gauge-invariant local operators see everything.”** They may miss line-operator spectra, higher-form symmetries, theta sectors, and boundary data.

## Research status

The distinction between constraint-generated gauge redundancy and physical boundary charge is standard. It is built into canonical quantization, covariant gauge fixing, BRST, and modern treatments of gauge theory on manifolds with boundary.

The frontier is in the refinements. Asymptotic symmetries, memory effects, soft theorems, edge modes, entanglement in gauge theory, celestial holography, higher-form symmetry, and symmetry TFT all sharpen the old question: which transformations are redundancies, and which survive as physical structure? In nonperturbative and topological QFT, the answer can depend delicately on global form, allowed defects, and the sum over bundles.

For everyday perturbative calculations, it is usually safe to quotient compactly supported small gauge transformations and impose BRST. For topology, boundaries, and charged sectors, always reopen the question.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Residual transformations in Lorenz gauge

Show that Lorenz gauge $\partial^\mu A_\mu=0$ is preserved by Abelian gauge transformations with $\Box\alpha=0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under $A_\mu\mapsto A_\mu+\partial_\mu\alpha$,

$$
\partial^\mu A_\mu\mapsto
\partial^\mu A_\mu+\partial^\mu\partial_\mu\alpha
=\partial^\mu A_\mu+\Box\alpha.
$$

If $\partial^\mu A_\mu=0$, the transformed field also satisfies Lorenz gauge exactly when

$$
\Box\alpha=0.
$$

These are residual gauge transformations. Whether they are pure redundancy or boundary symmetries depends on their support and boundary behavior.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Boundary charge from Gauss law

Let $\alpha$ approach a constant $\alpha_\infty$ at spatial infinity in Maxwell theory. Show that the boundary contribution to the Gauss-law generator is $\alpha_\infty Q$, where

$$
Q=\int_{S^2_\infty}\mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf S.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The boundary term is

$$
\int_{S^2_\infty}\alpha\,\mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf S.
$$

If $\alpha\to\alpha_\infty$ on the sphere at infinity, then

$$
\int_{S^2_\infty}\alpha\,\mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf S
=\alpha_\infty\int_{S^2_\infty}\mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf S
=\alpha_\infty Q.
$$

Thus a transformation with nonzero boundary value is associated with the physical electric charge, not merely with a vanishing bulk constraint.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Large Abelian transformation on a circle

Consider a $U(1)$ gauge field on a spatial circle of circumference $L$. A gauge transformation with charge unit $q$ may be written as

$$
\alpha_n(x)=\frac{2\pi n}{qL}x,
\qquad n\in\mathbb Z,
$$

so that $e^{-iq\alpha_n(x)}$ is single-valued around the circle. Show how the spatial holonomy

$$
\exp\left(iq\oint A_x dx\right)
$$

behaves under this transformation.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The gauge field transforms as

$$
A_x\mapsto A_x+\partial_x\alpha_n
=A_x+\frac{2\pi n}{qL}.
$$

Therefore

$$
q\oint A_x dx
\mapsto
q\oint A_x dx+q\int_0^L dx\,\frac{2\pi n}{qL}
=q\oint A_x dx+2\pi n.
$$

The holonomy transforms as

$$
\exp\left(iq\oint A_x dx\right)
\mapsto
\exp\left(iq\oint A_x dx+i2\pi n\right)
=\exp\left(iq\oint A_x dx\right).
$$

So the exponential holonomy is gauge-invariant, while $\oint A_x dx$ itself is defined only modulo $2\pi/q$. The transformation with $n\neq0$ is large: it winds around the gauge group.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Classifying transformations

Classify the following transformations as pure redundancy, boundary symmetry candidate, residual gauge transformation, or large transformation. More than one label may apply.

1. In Maxwell theory on $\mathbb R^3$, $\alpha(\mathbf x)$ has compact support.
2. In Maxwell theory in Lorenz gauge, $\alpha$ satisfies $\Box\alpha=0$ and vanishes at the boundary.
3. In Maxwell theory on $\mathbb R^3$, $\alpha\to1$ at infinity.
4. In $SU(N)$ gauge theory on compactified space $S^3$, $g:S^3\to SU(N)$ has winding number one.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

1. Compact support makes it a pure redundancy generated by Gauss-law constraints.
2. It is residual because it preserves Lorenz gauge. Since it also vanishes at the boundary, it is still pure redundancy.
3. It has nontrivial boundary value, so it is a boundary symmetry candidate whose generator includes electric charge.
4. It is a large gauge transformation because it is not connected to the identity. Depending on the definition of the theory, it may be quotiented, may act on theta-vacuum data, or may reveal a global anomaly.

</details>
