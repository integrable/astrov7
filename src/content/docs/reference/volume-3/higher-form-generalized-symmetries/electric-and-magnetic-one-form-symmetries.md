## Summary

Four-dimensional gauge theories naturally contain **line operators**. Wilson lines carry electric charge. ’t Hooft lines carry magnetic charge. Wilson–’t Hooft lines carry both. These charges are not charges under ordinary zero-form symmetries, because the charged objects are not local operators; they are one-dimensional operators.

The modern statement is:

$$
\text{electric and magnetic charges of lines}
\quad =
\quad
\text{charges under one-form global symmetries}.
$$

A one-form symmetry in four spacetime dimensions is generated by a topological surface operator. The surface links a line. When it links, the line operator is multiplied by a phase.

For compact Maxwell theory without dynamical electrically charged matter or monopoles, there are two continuous one-form symmetries:

$$
U(1)^{(1)}_{\rm e}\times U(1)^{(1)}_{\rm m}.
$$

The electric one-form symmetry acts on Wilson lines. The magnetic one-form symmetry acts on ’t Hooft lines. In more general gauge theories, dynamical particles, monopoles, non-Abelian gauge groups, and global-form choices reduce or modify these symmetries.

This page explains the basic dictionary. It is the bridge between center symmetry and the full line-operator classification used in global forms of gauge theory, duality, confinement, and symmetry TFT.

## Core idea

In ordinary symmetry, a codimension-one topological operator surrounds a local charged operator. For a one-form symmetry in four dimensions, a codimension-two topological operator links a line.

The ordinary zero-form picture is

$$
U_g(S^{d-1})\,\mathcal O_q(0)
=
\chi_q(g)\,\mathcal O_q(0),
$$

where $S^{d-1}$ surrounds the local operator.

The one-form picture in four dimensions is

$$
U_\alpha(\Sigma_2)\,L_q(C)
=
e^{i\alpha q\,\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma_2,C)}
L_q(C).
$$

Here $L_q(C)$ is a line operator supported on a curve $C$, and $\Sigma_2$ is a closed surface. The linking number replaces enclosure.

The physical meaning is simple. A line operator carries a charge that can be measured by a closed surface around it. Electric flux measures Wilson-line charge. Magnetic flux measures ’t Hooft-line charge.

## Compact Maxwell theory

Start with compact $U(1)$ gauge theory in four dimensions and no dynamical electrically charged matter or monopoles. Let $A$ be a $U(1)$ connection and

$$
F=dA
$$

locally. The Wilson line of integer electric charge $n$ is

$$
W_n(C)=\exp\!\left(in\oint_C A\right).
$$

The ’t Hooft line of integer magnetic charge $m$ is defined by imposing magnetic flux through a small linking two-sphere around the line:

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{S^2_{\rm link}}F=m.
$$

A Wilson–’t Hooft line is labeled by a pair

$$
(n,m)\in \mathbb Z\oplus\mathbb Z,
$$

subject to Dirac quantization and possible quotienting by genuine screening or global-form choices.

The Maxwell equations and Bianchi identity give two conserved two-form currents. With a simple normalization,

$$
J_{\rm e}=\frac{1}{g^2}F,
\qquad
J_{\rm m}=\frac{1}{2\pi}{*F}.
$$

The conservation laws are

$$
d{*J_{\rm e}}=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
d{*J_{\rm m}}=0,
$$

using the equation of motion $d{*F}=0$ and the Bianchi identity $dF=0$. The precise signs involving $**$ depend on Lorentzian versus Euclidean signature and orientation; the invariant content is electric-flux conservation and magnetic-flux conservation.

The corresponding charges on a closed spatial two-surface $\Sigma_2$ are

$$
Q_{\rm e}(\Sigma_2)=\int_{\Sigma_2}{*J_{\rm e}},
\qquad
Q_{\rm m}(\Sigma_2)=\int_{\Sigma_2}{*J_{\rm m}}.
$$

They generate electric and magnetic one-form symmetries.

:::note[Normalization note]
Many sources choose different factors of $2\pi$, $g^2$, or $i$ depending on compactness, Euclidean continuation, and whether the current is called $F$, ${*F}$, or $F/(2\pi)$. This page keeps the normalization schematic enough to emphasize the invariant content: the electric symmetry follows from the Maxwell equation, while the magnetic symmetry follows from the Bianchi identity.
:::

## Electric one-form symmetry

The electric one-form symmetry acts on Wilson lines. Its topological operator can be written schematically as

$$
U_{\rm e}(\alpha,\Sigma_2)
=
\exp\!\left(i\alpha\int_{\Sigma_2}{*J_{\rm e}}\right).
$$

When $\Sigma_2$ links a Wilson line $W_n(C)$,

$$
U_{\rm e}(\alpha,\Sigma_2)W_n(C)
=
e^{i\alpha n\,\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma_2,C)}
W_n(C).
$$

The parameter $\alpha$ is periodic when the symmetry is compact. The Wilson line charge is the electric charge $n$.

The existence of this symmetry depends on the absence of dynamical electrically charged matter that can screen the Wilson line. If a dynamical particle of charge $1$ exists, then a unit Wilson line can end on its worldline. A line that can end is not a conserved charged object for an exact one-form symmetry. Equivalently, the electric one-form symmetry is explicitly broken.

If the lightest dynamical charges have charge $N$, then the original electric symmetry may be broken to a finite subgroup. The precise surviving subgroup depends on the normalization of the charge lattice.

## Magnetic one-form symmetry

The magnetic one-form symmetry acts on ’t Hooft lines. It is generated by magnetic-flux surfaces. A schematic topological operator is

$$
U_{\rm m}(\beta,\Sigma_2)
=
\exp\!\left(i\beta\int_{\Sigma_2}{*J_{\rm m}}\right).
$$

When $\Sigma_2$ links an ’t Hooft line $T_m(C)$,

$$
U_{\rm m}(\beta,\Sigma_2)T_m(C)
=
e^{i\beta m\,\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma_2,C)}
T_m(C).
$$

The magnetic symmetry is exact when there are no dynamical monopoles that can screen or end magnetic lines. If dynamical monopoles exist, magnetic one-form symmetry is explicitly broken, possibly to a subgroup.

Magnetic symmetry is often less visible in elementary Lagrangian variables than electric symmetry because the ’t Hooft line is a disorder operator. It is defined by a singular boundary condition rather than by inserting a trace of a path-ordered exponential. But as a symmetry statement it is just as real: a conserved topological surface can measure magnetic charge.

## Wilson–'t Hooft lines and the charge lattice

A dyonic line operator carries both electric and magnetic charge. In compact Abelian gauge theory it is labeled by

$$
(n,m)\in\mathbb Z^2.
$$

The electric and magnetic one-form symmetries act as

$$
U_{\rm e}(\alpha,\Sigma_2)L_{n,m}(C)
=
e^{i\alpha n\,\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma_2,C)}
L_{n,m}(C),
$$

and

$$
U_{\rm m}(\beta,\Sigma_2)L_{n,m}(C)
=
e^{i\beta m\,\operatorname{Link}(\Sigma_2,C)}
L_{n,m}(C).
$$

The two integers are not arbitrary decorations. Their pairing controls mutual locality. If two dyonic lines have charges $(n,m)$ and $(n',m')$, their Dirac pairing is

$$
\langle (n,m),(n',m')\rangle = nm'-mn'.
$$

Mutual locality requires this pairing to be integral in appropriate units; in many topological discussions one also asks for the phase associated with braiding or linking to be trivial for simultaneously genuine lines.

This charge-lattice viewpoint is one of the cleanest ways to see why the global form of a gauge group matters. Different global forms choose different maximal sets of mutually compatible genuine line operators.

## Non-Abelian gauge theory

For a non-Abelian gauge group, electric line charges are representation-theoretic. Wilson lines are labeled by representations of the gauge group or its cover. The electric one-form symmetry is often a subgroup of the center.

For pure $SU(N)$ Yang–Mills,

$$
Z(SU(N))\cong\mathbb Z_N
$$

is an electric one-form symmetry. Wilson lines are charged by their $N$-ality. This was the previous page’s main example.

Magnetic line charges are labeled by magnetic weights or coweights, modulo screening and global-form constraints. For example, the distinction between $SU(N)$ and $PSU(N)=SU(N)/\mathbb Z_N$ affects which electric Wilson lines are genuine and which magnetic ’t Hooft lines are allowed.

A practical summary is:

| Choice | Electric lines | Magnetic lines | One-form symmetry |
|---|---|---|---|
| Simply connected gauge group | More Wilson lines are genuine | Magnetic lines are constrained | Electric center symmetry appears naturally |
| Adjoint quotient gauge group | Some Wilson lines are not genuine | More ’t Hooft lines are genuine | Magnetic/discrete dual symmetry appears naturally |
| Matter in charged representations | Some lines can end | Corresponding one-form symmetry is explicitly broken | Surviving subgroup depends on screening |

The detailed lattice of line operators belongs to Line-Operator Algebras and the global-form pages. This page emphasizes the symmetry meaning.

## Screening and endpoints

A one-form symmetry is broken when charged lines can end.

For electric symmetry, a Wilson line can end on a dynamical charged particle. If the theory contains a dynamical field of electric charge $1$, then a charge-$1$ Wilson line ending at a point is not an illegal object; the endpoint is the worldline of a charged excitation. The electric flux sourced by the line is not conserved as a higher-form charge.

For magnetic symmetry, an ’t Hooft line can end on a dynamical monopole. Magnetic flux is then not conserved as an exact higher-form charge.

This is the higher-form version of explicit breaking. In ordinary zero-form symmetry, a charged local operator can fail to be conserved if the Hamiltonian contains terms that change its charge. In one-form symmetry, a charged line fails to be conserved if it can end on dynamical objects.

Screening is more subtle than complete absence of charge. If only charges multiple of $N$ are dynamical, a continuous one-form symmetry may be reduced to a finite $\mathbb Z_N$ one-form symmetry. This is a common source of discrete higher-form symmetries.

## Confinement and realization

One-form symmetries have phases. Their realization is diagnosed by the behavior of charged line operators.

A Wilson loop expectation value may obey an area law,

$$
\langle W(C)\rangle \sim e^{-T\,\operatorname{Area}(C)},
$$

or a perimeter law,

$$
\langle W(C)\rangle \sim e^{-\mu\,\operatorname{Length}(C)}.
$$

In pure Yang–Mills, an area law for fundamental Wilson loops is a confinement diagnostic. In generalized-symmetry language, it is related to the realization of the electric center one-form symmetry. Roughly, an unbroken one-form symmetry corresponds to area-law behavior for charged loops, while a broken one-form symmetry corresponds to perimeter-law behavior and deconfined behavior. The exact statement depends on dimension, finite temperature, which loop is used, and which symmetry is being discussed.

Magnetic line behavior similarly distinguishes Higgs, Coulomb, and confining regimes in Abelian and non-Abelian theories. In many examples, electric and magnetic descriptions exchange area and perimeter laws under duality.

:::caution[Area law is not a universal definition]
Area/perimeter behavior is a powerful diagnostic, not a standalone definition of confinement in every theory. Dynamical matter, string breaking, finite temperature, nontrivial centers, and global-form choices all affect the interpretation.
:::

## Electric-magnetic duality

Electric and magnetic one-form symmetries make duality more transparent.

In free compact Maxwell theory, electric-magnetic duality exchanges

$$
F \longleftrightarrow {*F}
$$

and maps Wilson lines to ’t Hooft lines. Correspondingly,

$$
U(1)^{(1)}_{\rm e}
\longleftrightarrow
U(1)^{(1)}_{\rm m}.
$$

With theta angle, the charges mix. A dyonic line with charge $(n,m)$ may transform by an $SL(2,\mathbb Z)$ action in theories where the full duality group is present.

In non-Abelian gauge theory, dualities act on line-operator lattices, one-form symmetries, and possible anomalies. A proposed duality must match not only local operators and spectra but also genuine line operators and their higher-form charges.

This is one reason one-form symmetry is so useful: it gives robust, presentation-independent data that any dual description must reproduce.

## Background fields and anomalies

A one-form symmetry can be coupled to a background two-form gauge field $B_2$. This background changes how line operators are defined, transported, and compared across patches.

For a finite $\mathbb Z_N$ one-form symmetry, $B_2$ is a discrete two-form background. Coupling to $B_2$ is essential for detecting mixed anomalies involving center symmetry, time reversal, flavor symmetry, or spacetime structure.

An anomaly is an obstruction to making the partition function gauge invariant under transformations of $B_2$ and any other background fields. If a one-form symmetry has an anomaly, it cannot be gauged by itself as an ordinary standalone operation. It can still exist as a global symmetry and constrain the infrared through anomaly matching.

## Common pitfalls

**“Electric and magnetic one-form symmetries are ordinary electric and magnetic fields.”** No. The symmetries are global symmetries acting on line operators. Electric and magnetic fluxes provide their currents or charges in gauge-theory examples.

**“A Wilson line is always charged under an exact one-form symmetry.”** Only if it is genuine and cannot end on dynamical matter that screens its charge.

**“The magnetic symmetry is optional because ’t Hooft lines are disorder operators.”** Disorder operators are still operators. Magnetic one-form symmetry is often the cleanest language for magnetic flux conservation.

**“The local Lagrangian fixes all line operators.”** It does not. The global form of the gauge group and the allowed line-operator lattice are extra global data.

**“Area law means confinement in every theory.”** Area law is a diagnostic whose interpretation depends on matter content, temperature, screening, and the chosen line.

**“Electric-magnetic duality only exchanges equations of motion.”** It also exchanges line operators, background fields, and one-form symmetry data.

## Research status

The electric and magnetic one-form symmetry viewpoint is now standard in modern QFT. It has clarified the role of line operators, center symmetry, global forms of gauge groups, confinement diagnostics, and duality.

Active research includes mixed anomalies involving one-form symmetries, adjoint QCD, deconfinement transitions, compactification with center-stabilizing deformations, line-operator lattices in strongly coupled theories, higher-group structures involving one-form symmetries, and the symmetry-TFT packaging of line and surface operators.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Maxwell currents

In four-dimensional Maxwell theory without charges, show that $dF=0$ and $d{*F}=0$ can be interpreted as conservation laws for two one-form symmetries.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A one-form symmetry in four dimensions has a two-form current $J$ satisfying

$$
d{*J}=0.
$$

For the electric symmetry, choose $J_{\rm e}\propto F$. Then

$$
d{*J_{\rm e}}\propto d{*F}=0,
$$

which is Maxwell’s equation in the absence of electric charge.

For the magnetic symmetry, choose $J_{\rm m}\propto {*F}$. Then

$$
d{*J_{\rm m}}\propto dF=0,
$$

up to the sign of $**$ in the chosen signature. Thus the equation of motion and the Bianchi identity give two higher-form conservation laws.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Breaking by dynamical charge

Explain why a dynamical particle of electric charge $1$ explicitly breaks the electric one-form symmetry.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A charged Wilson line of unit charge can end on the worldline of a dynamical charge-$1$ particle. A charged object for an exact one-form symmetry should not have an isolated endpoint in the theory; its charge should be measured by any closed surface linking it. If the line can end, electric one-form charge can disappear into the endpoint. Therefore the electric one-form symmetry is explicitly broken.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Center charge of Wilson lines

In pure $SU(N)$ Yang–Mills, a Wilson line in representation $R$ has $N$-ality $n_R$. What phase does it acquire when linked by the generator of the $\mathbb Z_N$ center one-form symmetry?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The generator of the center is

$$
z=e^{2\pi i/N}1_N.
$$

In representation $R$, it acts as

$$
R(z)=e^{2\pi i n_R/N}1_R.
$$

Therefore a Wilson line with $N$-ality $n_R$ linked once by the generator of the center-symmetry surface acquires the phase

$$
e^{2\pi i n_R/N}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Dirac pairing

Two dyonic lines in compact Abelian gauge theory have charges $(n,m)$ and $(n',m')$. The pairing is

$$
\langle (n,m),(n',m')\rangle=nm'-mn'.
$$

What does it mean if this pairing is nonzero?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A nonzero pairing means that the two line operators have nontrivial electric-magnetic mutual statistics: transporting or linking one around the other can produce a phase. In a formulation where both lines are required to be simultaneously genuine local line operators, the allowed charge lattice must obey the appropriate mutual-locality condition. Different choices of maximal mutually local sets correspond to different global forms or discrete theta choices of the gauge theory.

</details>
