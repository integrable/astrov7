## Summary

The **Polchinski equation** is an exact RG equation for a Wilsonian action. It says that lowering a smooth cutoff can be compensated by changing the interaction action so that low-energy physics is unchanged.

For a Euclidean scalar field with cutoff propagator $C_\Lambda$ and interaction part $S^I_\Lambda[\phi]$, a common convention is

$$
\partial_t S^I_\Lambda[\phi]
=\frac12\frac{\delta S^I_\Lambda}{\delta\phi}\cdot \dot C_\Lambda\cdot\frac{\delta S^I_\Lambda}{\delta\phi}
-\frac12\operatorname{Tr}\!\left(\dot C_\Lambda\frac{\delta^2 S^I_\Lambda}{\delta\phi\,\delta\phi}\right),
\qquad
 t\equiv\log\frac{\Lambda_0}{\Lambda},
$$

where

$$
\dot C_\Lambda\equiv \partial_t C_\Lambda=-\Lambda\partial_\Lambda C_\Lambda.
$$

The first term is quadratic in first functional derivatives and joins two Wilsonian vertices. The second term contains a second functional derivative and closes two legs of a Wilsonian vertex. The equation looks one-loop-like, but it is exact because $S^I_\Lambda$ contains all local and nonlocal interactions already generated by integrating out modes above $\Lambda$.

This page uses Euclidean notation. The physical point is not a Lorentzian sign convention; it is the cutoff dependence of a regulated functional integral.

## Core idea

A Wilsonian action $S_\Lambda$ describes physics below a scale $\Lambda$. If we lower $\Lambda$, modes in the removed momentum band no longer appear explicitly. Their effects must be encoded in new couplings in $S_\Lambda$.

The Polchinski equation is a smooth version of this idea. Instead of sharply integrating out a shell, introduce a smooth cutoff propagator $C_\Lambda$ and demand that changing $C_\Lambda$ leaves the full generating functional unchanged after the interaction action is adjusted. The result is a differential equation on theory space:

$$
\partial_t S^I_\Lambda=\mathcal F[S^I_\Lambda].
$$

The equation is exact before we truncate $S^I_\Lambda$. Once we write

$$
S^I_\Lambda=\sum_i g_i(\Lambda)\int d^d x\,\mathcal O_i(x)
$$

and keep only a finite list of operators, we get an approximate finite-dimensional RG flow.

:::note[The equation is exact; the ansatz is not]
The full Wilsonian interaction action contains infinitely many operators, including derivative operators and nonlocal terms at scales near the cutoff. A finite ansatz such as

$$
S^I_\Lambda=\int d^d x\left[V_\Lambda(\phi)+\frac12Z_\Lambda(\phi)(\partial\phi)^2\right]
$$

is a projection of the exact equation, not the exact equation itself.
:::

## Setup and conventions

For concreteness, consider one real scalar field in $d$ Euclidean dimensions. Write the cutoff propagator as

$$
C_\Lambda(p)=\frac{K(p^2/\Lambda^2)}{p^2+m^2},
$$

where $K(y)$ is a smooth profile satisfying

$$
K(y)\approx 1\quad (y\ll 1),
\qquad
K(y)\to 0\quad (y\gg 1).
$$

The Wilsonian action is split into a regulated quadratic part and an interaction part:

$$
S_\Lambda[\phi]
=\frac12\phi\cdot C_\Lambda^{-1}\cdot\phi+S^I_\Lambda[\phi].
$$

The dot notation means integration over repeated continuous variables. In momentum space,

$$
A\cdot B
\equiv
\int\frac{d^d p}{(2\pi)^d}A(p)B(-p),
$$

and

$$
A\cdot C\cdot B
\equiv
\int\frac{d^d p}{(2\pi)^d}A(p)C(p)B(-p)
$$

when $C$ is diagonal in momentum.

We use an infrared RG time

$$
t=\log\frac{\Lambda_0}{\Lambda},
$$

so $t$ increases as the Wilsonian cutoff is lowered. With this choice,

$$
\dot C_\Lambda\equiv\partial_t C_\Lambda=-\Lambda\partial_\Lambda C_\Lambda.
$$

Some authors instead use $\log\Lambda$ or absorb signs into $\dot C_\Lambda$. Before comparing formulas, identify which direction the RG time runs.

## The invariant Gaussian-convolution picture

A useful way to derive the equation is to regard the high-mode integration as a Gaussian convolution. Let $S^I_t$ be chosen so that the following object is independent of $t$:

$$
\mathcal Z[J]
=\exp\!\left(\frac12J\cdot C_t\cdot J\right)
\exp\!\left[-S^I_t\!\left(\frac{\delta}{\delta J}\right)\right]1,
$$

up to vacuum normalization conventions. The Gaussian covariance changes with $t$, and $S^I_t$ must change in compensation.

Equivalently, one can demand that the Boltzmann weight of the interaction obeys the functional heat equation

$$
\partial_t e^{-S^I_t[\phi]}
=-\frac12\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C_t\cdot\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}
 e^{-S^I_t[\phi]}.
$$

Expanding the right-hand side gives

$$
\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C_t\cdot\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}e^{-S^I_t}
=
\left[
\frac{\delta S^I_t}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C_t\cdot\frac{\delta S^I_t}{\delta\phi}
-
\operatorname{Tr}\!\left(\dot C_t\frac{\delta^2S^I_t}{\delta\phi\,\delta\phi}\right)
\right]e^{-S^I_t}.
$$

Since

$$
\partial_t e^{-S^I_t}=-(\partial_t S^I_t)e^{-S^I_t},
$$

we obtain

$$
\partial_t S^I_t
=\frac12\frac{\delta S^I_t}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C_t\cdot\frac{\delta S^I_t}{\delta\phi}
-\frac12\operatorname{Tr}\!\left(\dot C_t\frac{\delta^2S^I_t}{\delta\phi\,\delta\phi}\right).
$$

This is the Polchinski equation in the convention used here.

## Meaning of the two terms

The first term,

$$
\frac12\frac{\delta S^I}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C\cdot\frac{\delta S^I}{\delta\phi},
$$

contains two first functional derivatives. Each derivative removes one field from an interaction. The differentiated propagator $\dot C$ joins the two open legs. Diagrammatically, it connects two Wilsonian vertices by a line restricted to the shell where the cutoff is changing.

This term is often called the **classical term** because it is quadratic in the action, like a Hamilton–Jacobi equation. The name should not be overinterpreted: the vertices are already Wilsonian vertices containing quantum effects from previously integrated modes.

The second term,

$$
-\frac12\operatorname{Tr}\!\left(\dot C\frac{\delta^2S^I}{\delta\phi\,\delta\phi}\right),
$$

contains two functional derivatives acting on the same interaction. It contracts two legs of one Wilsonian vertex with the differentiated propagator. This term is often called the **quantum term** because it has a loop-like trace.

The two terms together express a simple balance:

$$
\text{lower cutoff}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\text{change every Wilsonian vertex so low-energy physics stays fixed}.
$$

## Local expansion and beta functions

To extract ordinary RG equations, expand the interaction action in local operators. For example, near four dimensions,

$$
S^I_\Lambda=
\int d^d x\left[
\frac12 g_2(\Lambda)\phi^2
+\frac{1}{4!}g_4(\Lambda)\phi^4
+\frac{1}{6!}g_6(\Lambda)\phi^6
+c_{2,2}(\Lambda)\phi^2(\partial\phi)^2+\cdots
\right].
$$

Substitute this expansion into the functional equation, expand in powers of fields and momenta, and match coefficients. This gives infinitely many coupled equations,

$$
\frac{d g_i}{dt}=\beta_i(g_2,g_4,g_6,c_{2,2},\ldots).
$$

After passing to dimensionless couplings, the leading terms include the engineering scaling. For a coupling $g_i$ multiplying an operator of dimension $\Delta_i$,

$$
\tilde g_i=\Lambda^{\Delta_i-d}g_i,
$$

and the linearized Wilsonian flow contains

$$
\frac{d\tilde g_i}{dt}=(d-\Delta_i)\tilde g_i+\cdots,
$$

because $t$ increases toward the infrared.

For single-component $\phi^4$ theory in $d=4-\epsilon$, one obtains the familiar schematic structure

$$
\frac{d\tilde\lambda}{dt}
=\epsilon\tilde\lambda
-\frac{3}{16\pi^2}\tilde\lambda^2
+O(\tilde\lambda^3),
$$

in a conventional normalization $\tilde\lambda\phi^4/4!$. The precise derivation depends on the regulator and projection, but the universal one-loop coefficient is the same data that appears in perturbative renormalization.

## Fixed points and eigenoperators

The Polchinski equation naturally lives on theory space. After making all quantities dimensionless and including field rescalings, a fixed point is a solution

$$
\partial_t S^I_* =0.
$$

Perturb around it:

$$
S^I_t=S^I_*+\sum_a u_a(t)\mathcal V_a.
$$

Linearizing gives

$$
\frac{d u_a}{dt}=y_a u_a+\cdots.
$$

The eigenfunctionals $\mathcal V_a$ are the Wilsonian version of scaling operators. Positive $y_a$ means the perturbation grows toward the infrared and is relevant. Negative $y_a$ means it shrinks toward the infrared and is irrelevant. Marginal directions require higher-order analysis.

This is one reason the Polchinski equation is conceptually valuable: it does not start by assuming a finite list of couplings. It makes fixed points, relevant directions, irrelevant directions, redundant directions, and universality part of one functional geometry.

## Relation to perturbative renormalization

Perturbative renormalization usually defines couplings at a scale $\mu$ and computes beta functions by demanding independence of bare quantities. Polchinski's equation instead changes a Wilsonian cutoff $\Lambda$ and flows an action.

The two viewpoints agree on physical predictions but organize the calculation differently.

| Question | Perturbative renormalization | Polchinski flow |
|---|---|---|
| What changes? | renormalized parameters $g(\mu)$ | Wilsonian action $S_\Lambda$ |
| What is fixed? | bare regulated theory | low-energy physics below $\Lambda$ |
| Main equation | beta functions for finitely many couplings | functional equation for an action |
| Natural language | subtraction, schemes, logs | theory space, cutoffs, operator towers |
| Best use | precision perturbative calculations | Wilsonian structure, universality, nonperturbative setup |

The Polchinski equation is not a replacement for Feynman diagrams in every practical calculation. It is a framework that shows why diagrams, counterterms, beta functions, irrelevant operators, and universality fit into the same scale-dependent object.

## Relation to the Wetterich equation

The Polchinski equation flows a Wilsonian interaction action. The Wetterich equation flows an effective average action. Both are exact RG equations, but they are written in different functional coordinates.

A rough mnemonic is:

$$
\text{Polchinski: integrate high modes into }S_\Lambda,
$$

while

$$
\text{Wetterich: release fluctuations into }\Gamma_k.
$$

In careful formulations, Wilsonian and Legendre-type flows can be related by modified Legendre transforms. In practice they lead to different approximation strategies. Polchinski's equation is especially transparent for Wilsonian locality and perturbative renormalizability. Wetterich's equation is especially convenient for effective potentials, phase diagrams, and derivative expansions of the one-particle-irreducible effective action.

## Common pitfalls

**Forgetting the infinite tower.** The exact equation generates every operator allowed by symmetries. Dropping operators is an approximation, not a theorem.

**Confusing cutoff time with subtraction-scale time.** Here $t=\log(\Lambda_0/\Lambda)$ increases toward the infrared. This reverses signs relative to many $\beta_g=\mu\,dg/d\mu$ conventions.

**Ignoring redundant directions.** Some deformations are field redefinitions. Treating them as physical couplings can produce fake scheme dependence or fake critical exponents.

**Trusting a truncation without stability checks.** Regulator dependence and ansatz dependence should decrease as the truncation improves. If they do not, the calculation is sending a flare into the night sky.

**Expecting gauge theories to be painless.** Smooth cutoffs can obscure gauge invariance. Gauge theories require background-field methods, modified Ward identities, BRST-compatible treatments, or other careful constructions.

## Research status

The equation itself is established. It is a functional identity under its assumptions. Its use as a practical nonperturbative tool is a matter of approximation strategy: choice of cutoff profile, operator basis, derivative expansion, field rescaling, regulator optimization, and convergence diagnostics. Much of modern exact-RG work is not about whether the identity is true, but about how much trustworthy physics can be extracted from controlled projections of it.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Derive the sign of the two terms

Starting from

$$
\partial_t e^{-S_t[\phi]}
=-\frac12\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C_t\cdot\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}e^{-S_t[\phi]},
$$

show that

$$
\partial_t S_t
=\frac12\frac{\delta S_t}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C_t\cdot\frac{\delta S_t}{\delta\phi}
-\frac12\operatorname{Tr}\!\left(\dot C_t\frac{\delta^2S_t}{\delta\phi\,\delta\phi}\right).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use

$$
\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}e^{-S_t}
=-\frac{\delta S_t}{\delta\phi}e^{-S_t}
$$

and apply one more functional derivative. In condensed notation,

$$
\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C_t\cdot\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi}e^{-S_t}
=
\left[
\frac{\delta S_t}{\delta\phi}\cdot\dot C_t\cdot\frac{\delta S_t}{\delta\phi}
-
\operatorname{Tr}\!\left(\dot C_t\frac{\delta^2 S_t}{\delta\phi\,\delta\phi}\right)
\right]e^{-S_t}.
$$

Since the left side of the heat equation is

$$
\partial_t e^{-S_t}=-(\partial_tS_t)e^{-S_t},
$$

the stated equation follows after dividing by $e^{-S_t}$ and multiplying by $-1$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Engineering part of Wilsonian flow

Let $S$ contain $g\int d^d x\,\mathcal O(x)$, where $\mathcal O$ has scaling dimension $\Delta$ at a fixed point. Define $\tilde g=\Lambda^{\Delta-d}g$ and $t=\log(\Lambda_0/\Lambda)$. Derive the linear term

$$
\frac{d\tilde g}{dt}=(d-\Delta)\tilde g.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Lowering the cutoff by $\Lambda\to\Lambda e^{-dt}$ and rescaling lengths back to the original cutoff multiplies the perturbation by $e^{(d-\Delta)dt}$. Thus

$$
\tilde g(t+dt)=\tilde g(t)+dt\,(d-\Delta)\tilde g(t)+O(dt^2),
$$

so

$$
\frac{d\tilde g}{dt}=(d-\Delta)\tilde g.
$$

Relevant perturbations have $\Delta<d$ and therefore grow toward the infrared in this convention.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why the equation looks one-loop-like

Explain why the trace term in the Polchinski equation does not mean that the equation is merely a one-loop approximation.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The trace term has the topology of a loop because two legs are contracted with $\dot C_t$. However, the vertex being contracted is a vertex of the full Wilsonian interaction $S^I_t$, not a bare vertex kept only to some finite order. The other term also contains full Wilsonian vertices. Expanding $S^I_t$ perturbatively reproduces loop expansions order by order, but the functional equation itself is exact before that expansion and before any operator truncation.

</details>
