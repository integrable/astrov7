## Summary

An **RG fixed point** is a theory that does not change under the renormalization group. In coordinates $g^i$ on theory space, with RG time $t=\log\mu$, the renormalized-coupling convention of this volume is

$$
\frac{d g^i}{dt}=\beta^i(g),
\qquad t=\log\mu.
$$

A fixed point is a point $g_*^i$ such that

$$
\beta^i(g_*)=0
\qquad\text{for all }i.
$$

At such a point, dimensionless couplings stop running. The theory may still contain fields, operators, correlation functions, states, defects, and nontrivial dynamics; what it lacks is an intrinsic scale generated by the RG flow. This is why fixed points are the home of scale invariance, critical exponents, universality classes, and continuum limits.

The simplest fixed point is the Gaussian fixed point, where the theory is free. The more interesting fixed points are interacting: the Wilson–Fisher fixed point of scalar $\phi^4$ theory below four dimensions, the Ising CFT in two and three dimensions, many supersymmetric conformal theories, and strongly coupled gauge-theory fixed points. In all these cases, the fixed point is not a calculation trick. It is the theory that controls the long-distance or short-distance behavior of nearby theories.

:::note[One sentence to remember]
A fixed point is not merely where a beta function vanishes; it is the scale-invariant theory whose nearby perturbations explain phases, tuning, scaling laws, and universality.
:::

## Core idea

The RG changes the scale at which a theory is described. Usually, the coordinates used to describe the theory change. Couplings run, masses rescale, and operators mix. A fixed point is special because after the rescaling step the theory comes back to itself.

This does not mean that nothing happens. It means that the change of scale is represented as a symmetry action on operators, not as a drift through theory space. Correlation functions become scale-covariant power laws. Operators can be organized by scaling dimensions. Perturbations can be classified by whether they grow or shrink under coarse graining.

The central mental picture is:

$$
\text{fixed point}
\quad+\quad
\text{small perturbations}
\quad\rightsquigarrow\quad
\text{phases and scaling laws nearby}.
$$

A critical point in statistical physics is not just a place where a correlation length happens to be large. It is a trajectory in microscopic parameter space that approaches an RG fixed point closely enough that the fixed-point theory controls observables over a wide range of distances.

## Setup and conventions

There are two common RG time conventions.

For renormalized couplings, this volume uses

$$
\frac{d g^i}{dt}=\beta^i(g),
\qquad t=\log\mu,
$$

so increasing $t$ means moving toward the ultraviolet when $\mu$ is an energy scale.

For Wilsonian and critical-phenomena discussions, it is often clearer to use an infrared RG time

$$
\ell=\log\frac{\Lambda_0}{\Lambda},
$$

which increases as the cutoff $\Lambda$ is lowered and distances are coarse-grained. Then

$$
\frac{d g^i}{d\ell}=\mathcal B^i(g),
$$

where $\mathcal B^i$ is the beta-function vector field in IR time. If $\mu$ is identified with the sliding cutoff, then locally $\mathcal B^i=-\beta^i$. The fixed-point condition is the same:

$$
\beta^i(g_*)=0
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
\mathcal B^i(g_*)=0.
$$

When discussing relevance in critical phenomena, this chapter uses the IR convention: a perturbation is relevant if it grows as $\ell$ increases.

In this chapter, the epsilon expansion usually means $d=4-\epsilon$. This is the statistical-field-theory convention near the upper critical dimension, and it should not be confused with the dimensional-regularization convention $d=4-2\epsilon$ used on some perturbative-renormalization pages.

## Fixed points as zeros of a vector field

A beta function is a vector field on theory space. In coordinates $g^i$, the RG trajectory through a point $g^i(0)$ is the solution of

$$
\frac{d g^i}{dt}=\beta^i(g).
$$

A fixed point is a zero of this vector field. If the initial condition is exactly $g(0)=g_*$, then

$$
g^i(t)=g_*^i
$$

for all $t$.

This coordinate description is useful but slightly dangerous. A change of renormalization scheme is a coordinate transformation on theory space:

$$
g^i\longmapsto g^{\prime i}(g).
$$

The beta functions transform as a vector field,

$$
\beta^{\prime i}(g')=\frac{\partial g^{\prime i}}{\partial g^j}\beta^j(g).
$$

Therefore the statement that a point is fixed is coordinate invariant, provided the change of coordinates is nonsingular there. The numerical coordinates of the point may change, but the vanishing of the vector field does not.

This is why it is better to say “the theory is at a fixed point” than “this particular coupling has this particular fixed value.” Couplings are coordinates. The fixed-point theory is the object.

## Fixed points as scale-invariant theories

At a fixed point, dimensionless couplings do not run. Correlation functions therefore obey scale-covariant behavior. For a scalar scaling operator $\mathcal O$ with dimension $\Delta$, the two-point function has the form

$$
\langle \mathcal O(x)\mathcal O(0)\rangle
=\frac{C_{\mathcal O}}{|x|^{2\Delta}},
$$

up to contact terms, normalization conventions, and possible operator mixing.

This power law is not a coincidence. Away from a fixed point, the theory usually contains a scale such as a mass $m$, a correlation length $\xi$, a confinement scale $\Lambda_{\text{QFT}}$, or an external temperature $T$. At a fixed point, no such intrinsic scale is generated by the RG flow.

The stress tensor expresses the same idea. In a classically scale-invariant theory, the trace $T^\mu{}_\mu$ is expected to vanish up to improvement terms. Quantum mechanically, scale invariance can fail because couplings run. Schematically,

$$
T^\mu{}_\mu
=\beta^i\mathcal O_i+\text{mass terms}+\text{anomalies}+\text{contact terms}.
$$

At a massless fixed point, the $\beta^i\mathcal O_i$ part vanishes. In curved space or in the presence of backgrounds, trace anomalies may still remain. Those are not ordinary running couplings; they encode geometric or background-field responses.

## Critical points as fixed points plus tuning

A continuous phase transition is controlled by an RG fixed point. The microscopic theory need not equal the fixed-point theory. It only needs to approach it under coarse graining.

Suppose a statistical system has a reduced temperature

$$
\tau=\frac{T-T_c}{T_c}.
$$

At $\tau=0$, the system is critical. The correlation length diverges,

$$
\xi\sim |\tau|^{-\nu},
$$

and long-distance observables are controlled by a fixed point. For $\tau\ne0$, the RG trajectory initially flows near the fixed point but eventually turns away, producing a finite correlation length.

The tuning problem is geometric. A fixed point usually has some relevant directions. To reach it in the infrared, the corresponding couplings must be tuned to special values. The surface of points that flow into the fixed point is called the **critical surface**. Its codimension is the number of relevant directions, assuming no extra constraints from symmetries or redundancies.

For the ordinary Ising critical point, one usually tunes the temperature. If a magnetic field is allowed, it is another relevant perturbation and must also be tuned to zero to reach the Ising fixed point. Thus the phrase “the critical temperature” silently assumes a symmetry setting in which the magnetic field has already been set to zero.

## Gaussian and interacting fixed points

The **Gaussian fixed point** is the fixed point of a free theory. For a scalar field,

$$
S_*=\frac{1}{2}\int d^d x\,(\partial_\mu\phi)^2
$$

is Gaussian. Correlation functions follow from Wick’s theorem, and operator dimensions are engineering dimensions plus normal-ordering subtleties.

Gaussian fixed points are crucial because perturbation theory often begins near them. In $d=4$, the scalar quartic coupling is classically marginal. In $d=4-\epsilon$, the dimensionless quartic coupling $u$ has the schematic one-loop beta function

$$
\beta_u=-\epsilon u+\frac{N+8}{6}u^2+O(u^3),
$$

for the $O(N)$ model in a common normalization. Besides the Gaussian fixed point $u=0$, there is an interacting fixed point

$$
u_*=\frac{6\epsilon}{N+8}+O(\epsilon^2).
$$

This is the Wilson–Fisher fixed point. It is weakly coupled when $\epsilon$ is small, but it describes strongly fluctuating critical behavior in three dimensions after setting $\epsilon=1$ and improving the expansion.

Interacting fixed points are not “free theories plus small corrections” in principle. In two-dimensional minimal models, three-dimensional Ising theory, many gauge theories, and non-Lagrangian theories, fixed-point data are often most naturally described by operators, symmetries, and correlation functions rather than by a weakly coupled Lagrangian.

## UV and IR fixed points

A fixed point can control short-distance physics, long-distance physics, or both, depending on the flow.

A **UV fixed point** is approached as energy increases. Asymptotically free gauge theories provide the classic example: the gauge coupling flows toward $g=0$ in the ultraviolet. The UV fixed point is Gaussian, but the theory is not trivial, because running away from the fixed point toward the infrared generates a scale.

An **IR fixed point** is approached as energy decreases. Critical statistical systems, conformal phases, and some gauge theories with enough matter have IR fixed points. In this case, many microscopic details become irrelevant at long distances.

The same fixed point can be UV for one set of trajectories and IR for another. The classification is not a property of the point alone. It depends on which directions in theory space are being followed.

This is the source of a useful slogan:

$$
\text{fixed points are destinations only after a direction of RG time has been chosen}.
$$

## Fixed points and continuum limits

A continuum field theory can arise from a lattice system when the lattice spacing $a$ becomes negligible compared with all physical lengths. Near a critical point, the correlation length in lattice units diverges:

$$
\frac{\xi}{a}\longrightarrow\infty.
$$

This is the operational meaning of the continuum limit. One tunes microscopic couplings toward a critical surface so that long-distance physics is governed by a fixed point while the lattice spacing disappears from dimensionless observables.

This explains why fixed points are not only about phase transitions. They are also about defining quantum field theories. A fixed point gives a scale-invariant continuum theory. Perturbing it by relevant operators gives a nearby massive or flowing theory with a controlled continuum limit.

For example, the continuum Ising field theory can be viewed as the critical Ising fixed point plus relevant perturbations by the energy operator and spin operator. The continuum massive scalar theory can be viewed as the Gaussian fixed point plus a relevant mass perturbation.

## Fixed-point data

A fixed point is not fully characterized by the coordinates $g_*^i$. Coordinates are scheme dependent and may not exist globally. The intrinsic data include:

| Data | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Symmetries | Ordinary, spacetime, generalized, and emergent symmetries of the fixed-point theory. |
| Scaling operators | Local operators that transform with definite scaling dimensions. |
| Scaling dimensions | The exponents $\Delta_a$ controlling operator scaling and correlator power laws. |
| OPE data | Coefficients controlling short-distance products of local operators. |
| Relevant perturbations | Operators with $\Delta<d$ that move the theory away from the fixed point in the IR. |
| Marginal directions | Operators with $\Delta=d$ whose fate depends on nonlinear beta functions. |
| Universal amplitudes and ratios | Scheme-independent combinations of observables near the fixed point. |
| Defect and boundary data | Line, surface, boundary, and interface observables when present. |

In conformal theories, the operator dimensions and OPE coefficients are often called **CFT data**. In less symmetric scale-invariant settings, the same spirit remains: the fixed point is specified by universal operator and correlation-function data, not by a favorite coordinate chart.

## Examples

### Free massless scalar field

The Gaussian scalar fixed point has action

$$
S_*=\frac{1}{2}\int d^d x\,(\partial\phi)^2.
$$

Its field dimension is

$$
\Delta_\phi=\frac{d-2}{2}.
$$

The mass perturbation

$$
\frac{1}{2} m^2\phi^2
$$

is relevant for $d>2$, because $m^2$ has positive mass dimension. The quartic perturbation $\phi^4$ is marginal in $d=4$, relevant below four dimensions, and irrelevant above four dimensions.

### Wilson–Fisher fixed point

The $O(N)$ model in $d=4-\epsilon$ has an interacting fixed point at

$$
u_*=\frac{6\epsilon}{N+8}+O(\epsilon^2)
$$

in the normalization used above. This fixed point controls the critical behavior of many systems with an $N$-component order parameter. For $N=1$, it is the Ising universality class; for $N=2$, it is the XY universality class; for $N=3$, it is the Heisenberg universality class, modulo physical complications such as anisotropies and long-range forces.

### Asymptotically free gauge theory

In a non-Abelian gauge theory with sufficiently little matter, the one-loop beta function is negative:

$$
\beta_g=-b_0 g^3+O(g^5),
\qquad b_0>0.
$$

Then $g=0$ is a UV fixed point. The theory becomes weakly coupled at short distances, while dimensional transmutation generates a scale in the infrared.

### Infrared conformal gauge theories

Some gauge theories with enough matter are believed or known in controlled limits to flow to interacting IR fixed points. In such theories, the long-distance physics is neither confining nor free, but scale invariant. The precise conformal window of non-supersymmetric gauge theories is a major nonperturbative question.

## Why fixed points explain universality

Universality is the statement that many microscopic systems share the same long-distance behavior. Fixed points explain why.

Under RG flow, irrelevant perturbations shrink. If two microscopic Hamiltonians differ only by irrelevant details once projected near the fixed point, then their long-distance correlation functions, critical exponents, and scaling forms agree. They may have different lattice spacings, different short-distance interactions, and different nonuniversal amplitudes, but the same fixed-point data.

The fixed point therefore plays the role of an attractor in theory space. A universality class is roughly a basin of attraction, together with the symmetry and relevant-operator data specifying which fixed point and which critical surface are being approached.

## Common pitfalls

**Thinking a fixed point must be free.** The Gaussian fixed point is free. Many of the most important fixed points are interacting.

**Confusing a zero of one beta function with a fixed point of the whole theory.** A true fixed point requires all beta functions for all allowed couplings to vanish, after accounting for redundancies and symmetries.

**Forgetting dimensionful parameters.** A massless coupling beta function may vanish while a mass term is still present. A scale-invariant fixed point requires all relevant scale-setting deformations to be tuned away or controlled.

**Treating coordinates as invariant.** The numerical value of $g_*^i$ can change under a scheme transformation. Critical exponents and scaling dimensions are the invariant data.

**Calling a long plateau a fixed point.** An RG flow may move slowly near a small beta function or near an approximate fixed point. That produces walking or crossover, not necessarily exact scale invariance.

**Ignoring unstable directions.** A fixed point can control physics even if it is unstable. Critical points are usually unstable in at least one direction; that is why tuning is required.

## Research status

The conceptual role of RG fixed points in critical phenomena, continuum limits, and universality is established. The Gaussian and Wilson–Fisher fixed points are standard examples; many perturbative, numerical, and exact methods compute their data.

Active work includes the classification of strongly coupled fixed points, conformal windows in gauge theories, emergent symmetry at criticality, nonunitary fixed points, multicritical behavior, nonrelativistic scale-invariant systems, boundary and defect fixed points, and the precise relation between scale and conformal invariance under different assumptions.

A recurring theme is that fixed points can be easier to define than to solve. The equation $\beta=0$ is short. The fixed-point data may encode a whole nontrivial quantum field theory.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Fixed point under a scheme change

Let $g'=f(g)$ be a smooth one-coupling scheme transformation with $f'(g_*)\ne0$. Show that if $\beta(g_*)=0$, then $\beta'(g'_*)=0$, where $g'_*=f(g_*)$ and

$$
\beta'(g')=\frac{d f}{d g}\beta(g).
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Evaluate the transformed beta function at $g'_*=f(g_*)$:

$$
\beta'(g'_*)
=\frac{d f}{d g}\bigg|_{g_*}\beta(g_*).
$$

Since $\beta(g_*)=0$, this gives

$$
\beta'(g'_*)=0.
$$

The condition $f'(g_*)\ne0$ ensures that the coordinate transformation is nonsingular near the fixed point. The location changes from $g_*$ to $g'_*$, but the fixed-point property remains.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Quartic scalar coupling near four dimensions

For the $O(N)$ model in $d=4-\epsilon$, suppose the dimensionless quartic coupling $u$ has beta function

$$
\beta_u=-\epsilon u+\frac{N+8}{6}u^2+O(u^3).
$$

Find the two fixed points visible at this order.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Set $\beta_u=0$ and ignore $O(u^3)$ terms:

$$
-\epsilon u+\frac{N+8}{6}u^2=0.
$$

Factor out $u$:

$$
u\left(-\epsilon+\frac{N+8}{6}u\right)=0.
$$

Therefore the fixed points are

$$
u_*=0
$$

and

$$
u_*=\frac{6\epsilon}{N+8}.
$$

The first is the Gaussian fixed point. The second is the Wilson–Fisher fixed point, perturbatively accessible when $\epsilon$ is small and positive.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why a finite correlation length breaks scale invariance

Assume a two-point function behaves at large distance as

$$
G(r)\sim e^{-r/\xi}.
$$

Show that this is incompatible with exact scale invariance for finite nonzero $\xi$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Exact scale invariance for a scalar operator of dimension $\Delta$ would require

$$
G(br)=b^{-2\Delta}G(r)
$$

for all $b>0$. But an exponential correlator gives

$$
G(br)\sim e^{-br/\xi}.
$$

The ratio is

$$
\frac{G(br)}{G(r)}\sim e^{-(b-1)r/\xi},
$$

which is not a pure power of $b$ independent of $r$. The length $\xi$ is a preferred scale. Exact scale invariance is recovered only in the limiting cases $\xi=\infty$ for critical power-law correlations, or trivially when correlations vanish beyond all finite distances in a degenerate limit.

</details>
