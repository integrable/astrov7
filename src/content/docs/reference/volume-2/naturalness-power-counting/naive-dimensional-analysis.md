## Summary

**Naive dimensional analysis**, usually abbreviated **NDA**, is dimensional analysis with loop factors and field normalization put back in. Ordinary dimensional analysis says that a dimension-$\Delta_i$ operator in four dimensions appears as

$$
\mathcal L\supset \frac{C_i}{\Lambda^{\Delta_i-4}}\mathcal O_i.
$$

It does not say whether $C_i$ should be order one, loop-suppressed, proportional to a weak coupling, enhanced by strong dynamics, suppressed by a symmetry-breaking spurion, or zero. NDA gives a disciplined language for estimating that dimensionless coefficient.

For weakly coupled EFTs, one usually keeps explicit couplings and loop factors such as

$$
\ell_4\equiv \frac{1}{16\pi^2}.
$$

For strongly coupled four-dimensional EFTs, a common convention is

$$
\mathcal L_{\text{NDA}}
\sim
\frac{\Lambda^4}{16\pi^2}
\widehat{\mathcal L}\!\left(
\frac{\partial}{\Lambda},
\frac{4\pi\phi}{\Lambda},
\frac{4\pi\psi}{\Lambda^{3/2}},
\frac{gA}{\Lambda},
\cdots
\right).
$$

Equivalently, with $f\equiv\Lambda/(4\pi)$,

$$
\mathcal L_{\text{NDA}}
\sim
f^2\Lambda^2
\widehat{\mathcal L}\!\left(
\frac{\partial}{\Lambda},
\frac{\phi}{f},
\frac{\psi}{f\sqrt\Lambda},
\frac{gA}{\Lambda},
\cdots
\right).
$$

The coefficients inside $\widehat{\mathcal L}$ are expected to be order one only after this normalization, and only for operators allowed by the symmetries and generated by the dynamics.

:::note[NDA is not matching]
NDA estimates the size that would be unsurprising. Matching computes the coefficient in a specified UV theory and scheme. If a coefficient matters numerically, do the matching or use data.
:::

## Core idea

NDA has one job: make the phrase "order one coefficient" meaningful.

A dimension-six operator can be written as

$$
\mathcal L\supset \frac{C}{\Lambda^2}\mathcal O_6.
$$

The symbol $C$ is dimensionless, but its natural size depends on how the operator is generated. It might be

$$
C\sim 1,
\qquad
C\sim g_*^2,
\qquad
C\sim \frac{g_*^2}{16\pi^2},
\qquad
C\sim \epsilon,
\qquad
C=0.
$$

These are not small variations on the same answer. They encode different physical information: tree versus loop generation, weak versus strong coupling, symmetry breaking, and selection rules.

NDA separates these ingredients:

| Ingredient | What it controls |
|---|---|
| Operator dimension | powers of $1/\Lambda$ |
| Loop order | powers of $1/(16\pi^2)$ |
| Weak coupling | explicit powers of $g$, $y$, or $\lambda$ |
| Strong coupling | powers of $4\pi$ or $g_*$ |
| Symmetry breaking | spurions such as $\epsilon$ |
| Multiplicity | color, flavor, large-$N$, and species factors |
| Kinematics | powers of $Q/\Lambda$, velocity, or collinear scaling |

The useful slogan is

$$
\text{dimensions fix scale powers; NDA fixes a sensible coefficient normalization.}
$$

## Setup and conventions

This page uses four-dimensional relativistic normalization unless stated otherwise. The common loop factor is

$$
\ell_4=\frac{1}{16\pi^2}.
$$

The EFT breakdown scale or heavy scale is $\Lambda$. For a strongly coupled sector, we often introduce an amplitude scale $f$ with

$$
\Lambda\sim4\pi f.
$$

The relation is parametric. It is not an exact prediction for the first resonance mass.

The default strong-sector NDA convention is

$$
\mathcal L
\sim
\frac{\Lambda^4}{16\pi^2}
\widehat{\mathcal L}\!\left(
\frac{\partial}{\Lambda},
\frac{4\pi\phi}{\Lambda},
\frac{4\pi\psi}{\Lambda^{3/2}},
\frac{gA}{\Lambda},
\cdots
\right).
$$

Gauge fields are written through $gA_\mu/\Lambda$, $gF_{\mu\nu}/\Lambda^2$, or covariant derivatives because a weak gauge field can couple to a strong sector without itself being strongly coupled. Other conventions move some $g$ and $4\pi$ factors into operator definitions. That is fine if the convention is stated.

## Why the loop factor is 1 over 16π²

In four dimensions, a typical loop integral contains

$$
\int\frac{d^4 k}{(2\pi)^4}.
$$

For logarithmic integrals, angular integration and the denominator $(2\pi)^4$ leave the characteristic factor

$$
\frac{1}{16\pi^2}.
$$

For example, schematically,

$$
\int^\Lambda\frac{d^4 k}{(2\pi)^4}\frac{1}{(k^2+M^2)^2}
\sim
\frac{1}{16\pi^2}\log\frac{\Lambda^2}{M^2}
+\text{finite terms}.
$$

So in a weakly coupled four-dimensional theory, a contribution with $L$ loops is often estimated as

$$
\mathcal A
\sim
\left(\prod_a g_a^{n_a}\right)
\left(\frac{1}{16\pi^2}\right)^L
\left(\frac{Q}{\Lambda}\right)^p.
$$

This formula is not universal. Nonrelativistic systems, collinear modes, thresholds, low dimensions, finite density, large multiplicities, and strong dynamics can all change the expansion parameter. But for ordinary four-dimensional perturbative EFTs, it is the right baseline.

## Weakly coupled NDA

In a weakly coupled EFT, keep the weak couplings explicit.

Suppose a heavy particle of mass $M$ and coupling $g_*$ generates a dimension-six operator at tree level. Then

$$
\mathcal L_{\text{tree}}
\supset
\frac{g_*^2}{M^2}\mathcal O_6.
$$

If the same operator is first generated at one loop, then a typical estimate is

$$
\mathcal L_{\text{1-loop}}
\supset
\frac{g_*^2}{16\pi^2M^2}\mathcal O_6,
$$

or with additional powers of $g_*$ if the loop has more vertices.

The $1/M^2$ comes from dimensional analysis. The $g_*^2$ comes from vertices. The $1/(16\pi^2)$ comes from loop integration. These pieces should remain visible.

A useful weak-coupling estimate therefore has the structure

$$
C_i\sim
(\text{couplings})
\left(\frac{1}{16\pi^2}\right)^L
(\text{group and symmetry factors}).
$$

## Strongly coupled NDA

In a strongly coupled sector, explicit perturbative couplings may not exist. NDA instead asks for the normalization in which loops become order one at the cutoff.

If an effective coupling $g_*$ controls interactions, loop corrections are strong when

$$
\frac{g_*^2}{16\pi^2}\sim1.
$$

Thus a maximally strong four-dimensional coupling is estimated as

$$
g_*\sim4\pi.
$$

This motivates the strong-sector normalization

$$
\mathcal L
\sim
\frac{\Lambda^4}{16\pi^2}
\widehat{\mathcal L}\!\left(
\frac{\partial}{\Lambda},
\frac{4\pi\phi}{\Lambda},
\frac{4\pi\psi}{\Lambda^{3/2}}
\right).
$$

Expanding this expression, an operator with $n_s$ scalar fields, $n_f$ fermion fields, and $k$ derivatives has the estimate

$$
\mathcal L\supset
c_{n_s n_f k}
(4\pi)^{n_s+n_f-2}
\frac{\partial^k\phi^{n_s}\psi^{n_f}}{\Lambda^{n_s+3n_f/2+k-4}}.
$$

The coefficient $c_{n_s n_f k}$ is expected to be order one in this NDA convention if the operator is allowed and generated by strong dynamics.

This estimate is not a replacement for knowing the theory. It is a normalization convention for ignorance.

## The meaning of Λ ∼ 4πf

The relation

$$
\Lambda\sim4\pi f
$$

is especially transparent for Goldstone EFTs. The leading two-derivative Lagrangian has the schematic form

$$
\mathcal L_2\sim f^2(\partial U)^2,
\qquad
U=\exp\!\left(\frac{i\pi}{f}\right).
$$

Expanding $U$ gives interactions suppressed by powers of $1/f$. A loop built from these derivative interactions is controlled by

$$
\frac{Q^2}{16\pi^2f^2}.
$$

The loop expansion breaks down when

$$
\frac{Q^2}{16\pi^2f^2}\sim1,
$$

so

$$
Q\sim4\pi f.
$$

In real systems, resonances or new degrees of freedom can appear below $4\pi f$. The estimate says where the EFT would become strongly coupled if nothing else intervened.

## Example: chiral perturbation theory

Chiral perturbation theory is the cleanest classroom for NDA. The leading pion Lagrangian scales as

$$
\mathcal L_2\sim f_\pi^2 Q^2.
$$

A one-loop correction from the leading Lagrangian is suppressed by

$$
\frac{Q^2}{16\pi^2f_\pi^2}
=\frac{Q^2}{(4\pi f_\pi)^2}.
$$

Local four-derivative operators have the schematic form

$$
\mathcal L_4\sim L_i Q^4.
$$

NDA estimates

$$
L_i\sim\frac{1}{16\pi^2}
$$

in the conventional chiral normalization. This is why the local $Q^4$ counterterms and one-loop corrections from $\mathcal L_2$ enter at the same chiral order.

## Example: four-fermion operators

A four-fermion operator has dimension six:

$$
\mathcal O_{4\psi}=(\bar\psi\Gamma\psi)(\bar\psi\Gamma\psi).
$$

Plain dimensional analysis writes

$$
\mathcal L\supset \frac{C}{\Lambda^2}\mathcal O_{4\psi}.
$$

If it is generated by weak tree-level exchange with coupling $g$, then

$$
C\sim g^2.
$$

If it is generated by a weak loop with four vertices, a common estimate is

$$
C\sim \frac{g^4}{16\pi^2}.
$$

If it is generated by a strongly coupled sector with $g_*\sim4\pi$, then

$$
C\sim g_*^2\sim16\pi^2.
$$

All three estimates have the same mass dimension. They describe different ultraviolet dynamics.

## Example: scalar potential estimates

For a generic strongly coupled scalar sector,

$$
\mathcal L\sim\frac{\Lambda^4}{16\pi^2}
\widehat{\mathcal L}\!\left(\frac{4\pi\phi}{\Lambda}\right).
$$

The quadratic and quartic terms scale as

$$
\mathcal L\supset
c_2\Lambda^2\phi^2
+c_4(4\pi)^2\phi^4+
\cdots,
$$

with $c_2,c_4\sim O(1)$ in this convention. Thus a generic scalar in a strong sector is not expected to be light compared with $\Lambda$ unless symmetry, dynamics, or tuning explains it.

For a weakly coupled scalar with quartic coupling $\lambda\ll1$, the same strong estimate is wrong. One keeps $\lambda$ explicit and estimates threshold corrections according to the actual couplings.

## Spurions and symmetry-breaking factors

NDA never overrides symmetry. If a symmetry forbids an operator, the NDA estimate is zero. If the operator is allowed only after inserting a small spurion $\epsilon$, the coefficient carries powers of $\epsilon$.

For example, if a shift symmetry

$$
\phi\to\phi+c
$$

is broken only by a small parameter $\epsilon$, a potential may scale as

$$
V(\phi)
\sim
\epsilon\frac{\Lambda^4}{16\pi^2}
\widehat V\!\left(\frac{4\pi\phi}{\Lambda}\right),
$$

rather than with the unsuppressed strong-sector estimate. If two spurion insertions are needed, use $\epsilon^2$.

A careful estimate has the structure

$$
C_i\sim
(\text{NDA size})
\times(\text{spurions})
\times(\text{selection rules})
\times(\text{group factors}).
$$

Only after those factors are included does "order one" mean anything.

## A practical checklist

When estimating a Wilson coefficient, ask these questions in order.

| Question | What it determines |
|---|---|
| What is the operator dimension? | The power of $1/\Lambda$. |
| Is the operator tree-generated or loop-generated? | Powers of $1/(16\pi^2)$. |
| Is the UV interaction weak or strong? | Explicit $g_*$ or $4\pi$ normalization. |
| Which symmetries constrain it? | Spurions and selection rules. |
| Is the basis fixed? | Field redefinitions, EOM operators, and Fierz identities. |
| Are there multiplicities? | Color, flavor, species, and large-$N$ factors. |
| Are there special kinematics? | Velocity, collinear, threshold, or chiral counting. |

A compact weak-coupling estimate might look like

$$
\frac{C_i}{\Lambda^{\Delta_i-4}}
\sim
\frac{g_*^{n_*}}{(16\pi^2)^L}
\frac{\epsilon^r}{\Lambda^{\Delta_i-4}}.
$$

A compact strong-sector estimate might look like

$$
\frac{C_i}{\Lambda^{\Delta_i-4}}
\sim
(4\pi)^{N_i-2}
\frac{\epsilon^r}{\Lambda^{\Delta_i-4}},
$$

where $N_i$ counts strongly coupled fields in the operator, in the convention used on this page.

## Common pitfalls

**Using NDA before choosing a normalization.** The statement "the coefficient is order one" is meaningless until fields, operators, couplings, and $\Lambda$ are defined.

**Putting $4\pi$ everywhere.** The factors of $4\pi$ follow from loop normalization and strong-coupling assumptions. They are not decorative seasoning.

**Mixing weak and strong estimates.** A coefficient $g^2/(16\pi^2)$ and a coefficient $16\pi^2$ can multiply operators of the same dimension. They correspond to different UV dynamics.

**Forgetting symmetries.** NDA estimates allowed terms after symmetry constraints are imposed. It does not resurrect forbidden operators.

**Treating $\Lambda=4\pi f$ as exact.** The relation is a parametric estimate. Resonances, large-$N$ effects, thresholds, and weak couplings can shift the actual breakdown scale.

**Comparing coefficients across bases without rescaling.** Field redefinitions and operator normalization can move powers of $4\pi$, $g$, and spurions between coefficients and operators.

## Research status

The basic NDA logic is standard. The active issues are practical and interpretive: which power counting is appropriate for a given EFT, how to combine NDA with flavor spurions or helicity selection rules, how to handle strong sectors with large $N$, and how to choose useful normalizations in SMEFT, HEFT, chiral EFT, and cosmological EFTs.

NDA should be treated as a controlled estimate with stated assumptions. When precision matters, it must be replaced by matching, data, lattice input, bootstrap input, or another nonperturbative determination.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Scalar NDA coefficient

Starting from

$$
\mathcal L\sim \frac{\Lambda^4}{16\pi^2}
\widehat{\mathcal L}\!\left(\frac{\partial}{\Lambda},\frac{4\pi\phi}{\Lambda}\right),
$$

derive the NDA estimate for the coefficient of a term $\partial^D\phi^n$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The relevant contribution is

$$
\frac{\Lambda^4}{16\pi^2}
\left(\frac{\partial}{\Lambda}\right)^D
\left(\frac{4\pi\phi}{\Lambda}\right)^n.
$$

Pulling out fields and derivatives gives

$$
\frac{(4\pi)^n}{16\pi^2}
\Lambda^{4-D-n}\partial^D\phi^n.
$$

Since $16\pi^2=(4\pi)^2$, the coefficient is

$$
c_{D,n}^{\text{NDA}}
\sim
(4\pi)^{n-2}\Lambda^{4-D-n}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Chiral loop size

In a Goldstone EFT, the leading two-derivative Lagrangian scales as $f^2Q^2$. A one-loop correction from the leading Lagrangian is suppressed by $Q^2/(16\pi^2f^2)$. Show that the natural breakdown scale is $\Lambda\sim4\pi f$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The loop expansion parameter is

$$
\frac{Q^2}{16\pi^2f^2}.
$$

Perturbation theory stops being parametrically small when this is order one:

$$
\frac{Q^2}{16\pi^2f^2}\sim1.
$$

Therefore

$$
Q\sim4\pi f.
$$

This motivates $\Lambda\sim4\pi f$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Weak versus strong four-fermion estimates

A dimension-six four-fermion operator is written as

$$
\mathcal L\supset \frac{C}{\Lambda^2}(\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\psi)(\bar\psi\gamma_\mu\psi).
$$

Estimate $C$ for tree-level weak exchange with coupling $g$, one-loop weak generation with coupling $g$, and strong generation with $g_*\sim4\pi$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Tree-level weak exchange gives

$$
C\sim g^2.
$$

One-loop weak generation with four vertices commonly gives

$$
C\sim \frac{g^4}{16\pi^2},
$$

up to group, flavor, and numerical factors.

Strong generation with $g_*\sim4\pi$ gives

$$
C\sim g_*^2\sim16\pi^2.
$$

All three estimates multiply the same $1/\Lambda^2$. The difference lies in the UV dynamics encoded in $C$.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Spurion suppression

A symmetry forbids an operator unless a spurion $\epsilon$ is inserted twice. If the unsuppressed NDA estimate is $C_i^{\text{NDA}}$, what is the symmetry-aware estimate?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Two spurion insertions give a factor $\epsilon^2$. Therefore

$$
C_i\sim \epsilon^2 C_i^{\text{NDA}}.
$$

The coefficient is naturally small if $\epsilon$ is a controlled symmetry-breaking parameter.

</details>
