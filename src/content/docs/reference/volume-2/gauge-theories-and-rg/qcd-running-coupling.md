## Summary

The **QCD running coupling** is the scale-dependent strong coupling used in perturbative quantum chromodynamics. In the conventions of this volume,

$$
\alpha_s(\mu)\equiv \frac{g_s^2(\mu)}{4\pi},
\qquad
\beta_{g_s}\equiv \left.\mu\frac{d g_s}{d\mu}\right|_{\text{bare data fixed}}.
$$

For $SU(3)$ gauge theory with $n_f$ active Dirac quark flavors, the beta function begins

$$
\beta_{g_s}
=-\frac{b_0}{16\pi^2}g_s^3
-\frac{b_1}{(16\pi^2)^2}g_s^5
+O(g_s^7),
$$

where, in the usual normalization,

$$
b_0=11-\frac{2}{3}n_f,
\qquad
b_1=102-\frac{38}{3}n_f.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\mu\frac{d\alpha_s}{d\mu}
=-\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\alpha_s^2
-\frac{b_1}{8\pi^2}\alpha_s^3
+O(\alpha_s^4).
$$

For the physically relevant range of active flavors below the far ultraviolet, $b_0>0$. Therefore

$$
\mu\uparrow
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\alpha_s(\mu)\downarrow.
$$

This is **asymptotic freedom**. QCD becomes weakly coupled at short distances and strongly coupled at long distances.

At one loop, the solution is

$$
\alpha_s(\mu)
=\frac{2\pi}{b_0\log(\mu/\Lambda)}
=\frac{4\pi}{b_0\log(\mu^2/\Lambda^2)},
$$

where $\Lambda$ is the RG-invariant scale generated by dimensional transmutation. In practice one writes $\Lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}^{(n_f)}$ to emphasize both the scheme and the number of active quark flavors.

:::note[What the running coupling is not]
The running coupling is not a force law by itself. It is a scale-dependent parameter in a chosen renormalization scheme. Physical predictions come from combining running couplings, Wilson coefficients, matrix elements, parton distributions, threshold matching, and infrared-safe observable definitions.
:::

## Core idea

QCD is the non-Abelian gauge theory of color. Its gauge group is $SU(3)_c$, and quarks transform in the fundamental representation. The gluons themselves carry color charge because the group is non-Abelian. That single fact changes the sign of the beta function relative to QED.

At short distances, gluon and ghost antiscreening dominates quark screening. The result is

$$
\beta_{g_s}<0
$$

for sufficiently few active quark flavors. The coupling gets smaller as the renormalization scale grows.

At long distances, the same equation says that perturbation theory eventually fails. The one-loop formula has a denominator that vanishes at $\mu=\Lambda$. One should not read this as a literal physical pole in an observable. It is the perturbative warning label that the infrared theory is no longer described by small-$\alpha_s$ expansion. Confinement, chiral symmetry breaking, hadronization, and the mass gap are nonperturbative questions.

The conceptual chain is

$$
\text{non-Abelian self-interaction}
\longrightarrow
\beta_{g_s}<0
\longrightarrow
\text{asymptotic freedom}
\longrightarrow
\text{dimensional transmutation}
\longrightarrow
\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}.
$$

## Setup and conventions

The QCD gauge field is

$$
G_\mu=G_\mu^a T^a,
$$

where $T^a$ are fundamental $SU(3)$ generators satisfying

$$
\operatorname{tr}(T^aT^b)=\frac12\delta^{ab}.
$$

The field strength is

$$
G_{\mu\nu}^a
=\partial_\mu G_\nu^a-\partial_\nu G_\mu^a
+g_s f^{abc}G_\mu^bG_\nu^c.
$$

The quark covariant derivative is

$$
D_\mu q=(\partial_\mu+i g_s G_\mu^aT^a)q,
$$

and the QCD Lagrangian is

$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{QCD}}
=-\frac14 G_{\mu\nu}^aG^{a\mu\nu}
+\sum_{i=1}^{n_f}\bar q_i(i\gamma^\mu D_\mu-m_i)q_i,
$$

before gauge fixing and ghosts are added for perturbative calculations.

For a general simple gauge group $G$ with Dirac fermions in representation $R$, the one-loop coefficient is

$$
b_0=\frac{11}{3}C_A-\frac{4}{3}T(R)n_f.
$$

For QCD,

$$
C_A=3,
\qquad
T(F)=\frac12,
$$

so

$$
b_0=11-\frac{2}{3}n_f.
$$

The two-loop coefficient quoted above is for $SU(3)$ with fundamental Dirac quarks. In a mass-independent scheme such as $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$, the first two coefficients are universal within the usual class of analytic coupling redefinitions. Higher coefficients are scheme dependent.

## One-loop solution

At one loop,

$$
\mu\frac{d\alpha_s}{d\mu}
=-\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\alpha_s^2.
$$

Separating variables gives

$$
\frac{d\alpha_s}{\alpha_s^2}
=-\frac{b_0}{2\pi}d\log\mu.
$$

Integrating between $\mu_0$ and $\mu$ gives

$$
\frac{1}{\alpha_s(\mu)}
=\frac{1}{\alpha_s(\mu_0)}
+\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\log\frac{\mu}{\mu_0}.
$$

Thus $1/\alpha_s$ grows logarithmically toward the UV. This is the simplest quantitative form of asymptotic freedom.

Equivalently, define the scale $\Lambda$ by

$$
\frac{1}{\alpha_s(\mu)}
=\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\log\frac{\mu}{\Lambda}.
$$

Solving for $\Lambda$ gives

$$
\Lambda
=\mu\exp\left[-\frac{2\pi}{b_0\alpha_s(\mu)}\right]
$$

at one loop. A dimensionless boundary condition $\alpha_s(\mu)$ has been traded for a dimensionful scale $\Lambda$. This is dimensional transmutation.

The exponential dependence is important. A moderately small UV coupling can generate a much smaller IR scale. This is why QCD can have a hadronic scale much lower than a high reference scale without requiring a dimensionful parameter in the massless classical gauge action.

## Active flavors and thresholds

The symbol $n_f$ counts **active** quark flavors at the scale where the coupling is being run. A quark with mass far above the scale of a process should not contribute as an ordinary light degree of freedom to low-energy running. A quark much lighter than the scale normally should.

In a mass-dependent scheme, decoupling can appear smoothly in the beta function. In $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$, decoupling is not automatic in the same way because the scheme subtracts ultraviolet poles without directly knowing about physical thresholds. The standard practice is therefore:

$$
\text{run above a threshold with }n_f+1,
\qquad
\text{match near the heavy mass},
\qquad
\text{run below with }n_f.
$$

At leading order the coupling is continuous across the threshold if the matching scale is chosen near the heavy mass. Beyond leading order there are finite matching corrections.

This is why one often writes

$$
\alpha_s^{(n_f)}(\mu),
\qquad
\Lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}^{(n_f)}.
$$

The superscript is not pedantry. It records which EFT is being used.

## What Lambda QCD means

The scale $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$ is often described as "the scale where QCD becomes strong." That is acceptable as a first sentence, but it needs refinement.

First, $\Lambda$ is scheme dependent. The value of $\Lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}$ is not the same as the value of $\Lambda$ in a momentum-subtraction scheme or in a physical effective-charge scheme.

Second, $\Lambda$ depends on the number of active flavors in the EFT. A five-flavor $\Lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}^{(5)}$ is not the same parameter as a three-flavor $\Lambda_{\overline{\mathrm{MS}}}^{(3)}$.

Third, $\Lambda$ is not equal to a universal hadron mass. It sets the order of magnitude for nonperturbative QCD, but hadron masses, string tensions, condensates, and matrix elements are distinct observables.

The safe statement is:

$$
\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}
\quad\text{is the RG-invariant scale associated with the running QCD coupling in a specified scheme and flavor theory.}
$$

## Physical meaning at high and low scales

At high momentum transfer, where $\alpha_s(Q)$ is small, QCD can be used perturbatively. This is the basis for hard-scattering calculations, jet physics, short-distance coefficient functions, operator product expansions, and many collider predictions.

At low momentum transfer, $\alpha_s$ grows. Perturbation theory loses parametric control. This does not mean QCD stops existing. It means that the weak-coupling coordinate chart fails. The same theory must be studied using other tools: lattice gauge theory, effective theories such as chiral perturbation theory, heavy-quark effective theory, phenomenological models, large-$N$ ideas, dispersion relations, sum rules, or other nonperturbative methods.

A good mental picture is:

| Regime | Coupling behavior | Useful language |
|---|---|---|
| $Q\gg \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$ | small $\alpha_s(Q)$ | perturbative quarks and gluons, Wilson coefficients |
| $Q\sim \text{a few}\,\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$ | moderate coupling | resummation, matching, EFTs, data-guided matrix elements |
| $Q\lesssim \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$ | strong coupling | hadrons, confinement, chiral dynamics, lattice methods |

The boundaries are not sharp phase transitions. They are statements about which description is efficient.

## Scheme dependence and physical observables

The coupling $\alpha_s(\mu)$ depends on the scheme. A different scheme defines a different coordinate on the same physics:

$$
\alpha_s'=\alpha_s+c_1\alpha_s^2+c_2\alpha_s^3+\cdots.
$$

Under such a change, beta-function coefficients beyond the universal ones change. Observables do not.

This is why a prediction should never stop at "use $\alpha_s(Q)$". A well-defined perturbative prediction specifies the observable, the renormalization scheme, the factorization scheme if hadrons or partons are involved, the order in perturbation theory, the active-flavor theory, the matching conditions at thresholds, and the scale choices used to estimate perturbative uncertainty.

The running coupling is one ingredient in a controlled calculation, not the calculation itself.

## Relation to confinement

The running coupling strongly hints at confinement but does not prove it. The one-loop formula says that the theory becomes strongly coupled in the infrared. It does not by itself derive an area law for Wilson loops, a mass gap, a linear potential at all relevant distances, or a hadron spectrum.

The conceptual relation is:

$$
\text{asymptotic freedom}
\quad\text{explains weak short-distance QCD},
$$

while

$$
\text{confinement}
\quad\text{is a nonperturbative long-distance property}.
$$

They belong to the same theory, but one is not a perturbative proof of the other. The deep beauty of QCD is exactly this two-faced character: quarks and gluons are the right short-distance variables, while hadrons are the right long-distance variables.

## A useful comparison with QED

At one loop, QED and QCD have opposite running signs in their familiar regimes:

$$
\mu\frac{d\alpha}{d\mu}>0
\qquad\text{for QED with charged matter},
$$

but

$$
\mu\frac{d\alpha_s}{d\mu}<0
\qquad\text{for QCD with sufficiently few quark flavors}.
$$

QED charge screening says that a long-distance probe sees less charge than a short-distance probe. QCD antiscreening says that color charge becomes weaker at short distances.

The phrase "antiscreening" can be useful, but do not take the classical dielectric analogy too literally. The gauge-invariant statement is the sign and structure of the beta function and the behavior of physical short-distance observables.

## Common pitfalls

**Using one value of $n_f$ at all scales.** The active-flavor theory changes when crossing heavy-quark thresholds. In mass-independent schemes this requires matching.

**Treating $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$ as a directly measured hadron mass.** It is an RG scale in a specified scheme and flavor theory. It is related to the hadronic scale, not identical to every hadronic observable.

**Trusting the one-loop formula in the infrared.** The one-loop expression is useful for understanding dimensional transmutation and high-scale running. It is not a controlled description once the coupling becomes large.

**Calling the infrared blow-up a Landau pole.** The one-loop denominator vanishing in QCD is better understood as the breakdown of perturbative running near strong coupling. Unlike the QED Landau-pole problem, it occurs in the infrared where the theory is expected to be replaced by nonperturbative hadronic physics.

**Forgetting that $\alpha_s$ is scheme dependent.** The strong coupling is not a raw observable. It is extracted from observables within a specified perturbative and nonperturbative framework.

**Equating asymptotic freedom with confinement.** Asymptotic freedom is perturbative UV behavior. Confinement is an IR nonperturbative phenomenon.

## Research status

The qualitative picture of perturbative QCD running is established. The one-loop discovery of asymptotic freedom is one of the central results of modern quantum field theory, and higher-loop running is a precision tool.

The active research begins where this page stops: high-order extraction of $\alpha_s$, matching across thresholds, resummation for multiscale observables, nonperturbative definitions of physical couplings, lattice determinations, confinement, hadronization, and QCD in extreme environments.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Solve the one-loop running equation

Starting from

$$
\mu\frac{d\alpha_s}{d\mu}
=-\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\alpha_s^2,
$$

show that

$$
\frac{1}{\alpha_s(\mu)}
=\frac{1}{\alpha_s(\mu_0)}
+\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\log\frac{\mu}{\mu_0}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Write the equation as

$$
\frac{d\alpha_s}{\alpha_s^2}
=-\frac{b_0}{2\pi}d\log\mu.
$$

Integrating from $\mu_0$ to $\mu$ gives

$$
\int_{\alpha_s(\mu_0)}^{\alpha_s(\mu)}\frac{d\alpha}{\alpha^2}
=-\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\int_{\mu_0}^{\mu}d\log\mu'.
$$

Since

$$
\int \frac{d\alpha}{\alpha^2}=-\frac{1}{\alpha},
$$

we find

$$
-\frac{1}{\alpha_s(\mu)}+\frac{1}{\alpha_s(\mu_0)}
=-\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\log\frac{\mu}{\mu_0}.
$$

Rearranging gives the desired expression.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Count active flavors in the one-loop coefficient

For $SU(3)$ QCD, compute $b_0$ for $n_f=3$, $4$, $5$, and $6$. For which of these is the one-loop theory asymptotically free?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For $SU(3)$,

$$
b_0=11-\frac{2}{3}n_f.
$$

Thus

$$
b_0(n_f=3)=9,
\qquad
b_0(n_f=4)=\frac{25}{3},
$$

$$
b_0(n_f=5)=\frac{23}{3},
\qquad
b_0(n_f=6)=7.
$$

All are positive, so

$$
\beta_{g_s}=-\frac{b_0}{16\pi^2}g_s^3+\cdots
$$

is negative at weak coupling in all four cases. The theory is asymptotically free for each of these active-flavor counts.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Dimensional transmutation at one loop

Use the one-loop running solution to show that

$$
\Lambda=\mu\exp\left[-\frac{2\pi}{b_0\alpha_s(\mu)}\right]
$$

is independent of $\mu$ at one-loop order.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Take the logarithm:

$$
\log\Lambda=\log\mu-\frac{2\pi}{b_0\alpha_s(\mu)}.
$$

Differentiate with respect to $\log\mu$:

$$
\frac{d\log\Lambda}{d\log\mu}
=1+\frac{2\pi}{b_0}\frac{1}{\alpha_s^2}
\frac{d\alpha_s}{d\log\mu}.
$$

Using

$$
\frac{d\alpha_s}{d\log\mu}
=-\frac{b_0}{2\pi}\alpha_s^2,
$$

we get

$$
\frac{d\log\Lambda}{d\log\mu}
=1-1=0.
$$

Thus $\Lambda$ is RG invariant at one loop.

</details>
