## Summary

An **equations-of-motion operator** is a local operator proportional to a functional derivative of the action. For fields $\phi^a(x)$ and a local functional $F^a[\phi](x)$, the basic form is

$$
\mathcal E_F(x)
=
F^a[\phi](x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}.
$$

Classically, $\delta S/\delta\phi^a=0$ on solutions. Quantum mechanically, the correct statement is subtler: insertions of $\mathcal E_F$ in separated correlation functions reduce to contact terms. Integrated EOM operators are generated by local field redefinitions,

$$
\phi^a(x)\to \phi^a(x)+\varepsilon F^a[\phi](x),
$$

so their coefficients are usually redundant coordinates in theory space rather than independent physical couplings.

The phrase "use the equations of motion" therefore has several different meanings. In a classical Lagrangian, it may mean simplifying expressions modulo terms that vanish on solutions. In an EFT basis, it often means removing redundant operators by a field redefinition. In local correlation functions, it means replacing an EOM insertion by contact terms. In gauge theory, it must be interpreted inside the gauge-invariant or BRST-cohomological operator space.

:::note[The safe slogan]
EOM operators are not literally zero operators. They are zero only after specifying the observable, the contact-term convention, and the allowed field redefinitions.
:::

## Core idea

The equations of motion are relations among fields that hold on classical solutions. But a quantum path integral sums over all field configurations, not only solutions. Therefore it is not correct to insert $\delta S/\delta\phi$ into a correlator and set it to zero by fiat.

What survives quantum mechanically is invariance of the path integral under changes of integration variables. For a local variation

$$
\phi^a(x)\to \phi^a(x)+\varepsilon F^a[\phi](x),
$$

the action changes by

$$
\delta S
=
\varepsilon\int d^d x\,
F^a[\phi](x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}
+O(\varepsilon^2).
$$

If the change of variables is legitimate, the path integral is unchanged. The insertion of $F^a\delta S/\delta\phi^a$ must then be compensated by the transformation of other operator insertions and, when present, by the transformation of the measure. These compensations are local. That is why EOM operators do not affect separated physical data but do affect contact terms and operator definitions.

The central lesson is

$$
\text{EOM operator}
=\text{redundant direction}
+\text{contact-term bookkeeping}.
$$

Both pieces matter. Dropping the first too late makes EFT bases bloated. Dropping the second too early makes Ward identities, OPEs, off-shell correlators, and composite-operator renormalization wrong.

## Setup and conventions

Let the Euclidean generating functional be

$$
Z[J]=\int \mathcal D\phi\,
\exp\left[-S[\phi]+\int d^d x\,J_a(x)\phi^a(x)\right].
$$

The index $a$ is collective: it can include species, spin, flavor, gauge, and spacetime indices. The functional derivative of the action is denoted

$$
\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}.
$$

Given a local functional $F^a[\phi](x)$, define the EOM operator

$$
\mathcal E_F(x)
\equiv
F^a[\phi](x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}.
$$

The word local means that $F^a[\phi](x)$ is built from fields and finitely many derivatives evaluated at $x$. A nonlocal field redefinition can change long-distance physics and is not covered by the simple redundancy statement.

We ignore boundary terms at first. They return later because boundaries and defects are exactly where many naive EOM simplifications fail.

## The Schwinger–Dyson identity

Let $X[\phi]$ be any product or functional of fields. The path integral of a total functional derivative gives

$$
0=
\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi^a(x)}
\left(F^a[\phi](x)X[\phi]e^{-S[\phi]}\right),
$$

assuming the measure and boundary behavior are harmless. Expanding the derivative gives

$$
\left\langle
F^a(x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}X
\right\rangle
=
\left\langle
\frac{\delta F^a(x)}{\delta\phi^a(x)}X
+F^a(x)\frac{\delta X}{\delta\phi^a(x)}
\right\rangle.
$$

This is the basic quantum meaning of the equation of motion. The right-hand side is local: it is supported where the EOM insertion collides with another insertion, or where the variation of $F^a$ itself produces a local measure/Jacobian term.

For a product of local operators

$$
X=\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n),
$$

the derivative $\delta X/\delta\phi^a(x)$ is a sum of terms supported at $x=x_i$ and at derivatives of delta functions. Thus, for $x$ separated from all insertion points,

$$
\left\langle
\mathcal E_F(x)\,
\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)
\right\rangle=0
$$

up to possible measure terms and operator-renormalization conventions. The equality is a statement about separated points, not about distributions at coincident points.

:::tip[The phrase to remember]
Equations of motion hold inside correlators **modulo contact terms**.
:::

## Free scalar example

Consider the Euclidean free scalar action

$$
S=\int d^d x\left[
\frac12\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\mu\phi+
\frac12m^2\phi^2
\right].
$$

The functional derivative is

$$
\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}=(-\partial^2+m^2)\phi(x).
$$

Apply the Schwinger–Dyson identity with $F=1$ and $X=\phi(y)$:

$$
\left\langle
(-\partial_x^2+m^2)\phi(x)\,\phi(y)
\right\rangle
=\delta^{(d)}(x-y).
$$

Therefore the two-point function $G(x-y)=\langle\phi(x)\phi(y)\rangle$ obeys

$$
(-\partial_x^2+m^2)G(x-y)=\delta^{(d)}(x-y).
$$

Away from $x=y$, the right-hand side vanishes, so the equation of motion holds. At $x=y$, the contact term is essential: it is precisely what makes the propagator a Green function.

This is the smallest example of the whole story. The EOM insertion is not the zero distribution. It vanishes only at separated points. The contact term is not a bug; it is the inverse-operator relation.

## Interacting scalar example

For

$$
S=\int d^d x\left[
\frac12(\partial\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2+\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4
\right],
$$

the equation-of-motion functional is

$$
\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi}
=-\partial^2\phi+m^2\phi+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^3.
$$

The operator

$$
\mathcal E_F=F(\phi)\left(-\partial^2\phi+m^2\phi+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^3\right)
$$

is redundant when generated by the field redefinition $\phi\to\phi+\varepsilon F(\phi)$. For instance, with $F=\phi^3$,

$$
\phi^3\partial^2\phi
\simeq
m^2\phi^4+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^6,
$$

where $\simeq$ means equality modulo an EOM operator and total derivatives in separated on-shell observables. More explicitly,

$$
\phi^3(-\partial^2\phi+m^2\phi+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^3)
$$

is the redundant combination. Eliminating $\phi^3\partial^2\phi$ shifts the coefficients of $\phi^4$ and $\phi^6$. No observable has disappeared; only the coordinate system on the space of actions has changed.

This is why EOM reduction in EFT basis building always shifts Wilson coefficients. It is not an act of deletion. It is a change of basis.

## Field redefinitions and the equivalence theorem

Let the action be deformed by an integrated EOM operator,

$$
S\to S+c\int d^d x\,
F^a[\phi](x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}.
$$

To first order in $c$, this is undone by

$$
\phi^a\to\phi^a-cF^a[\phi].
$$

This is the field-theoretic reason EOM operators are called redundant. A local, invertible field redefinition changes the variables used in the path integral. It cannot change the $S$-matrix or other consistently transformed physical observables.

The corresponding statement for scattering is often called the **equivalence theorem**: local field redefinitions do not change on-shell scattering amplitudes, provided the external states and normalizations are treated consistently. Off-shell Green functions generally do change, because the interpolating fields have changed.

The distinction is crucial:

| Object | Effect of an EOM field redefinition |
|---|---|
| On-shell $S$-matrix | unchanged, after consistent LSZ normalization |
| Off-shell Green functions | generally changed |
| Local composite operators | transformed and mixed with contact terms |
| Wilson coefficients | shifted by a basis change |
| Operator basis size | can often be reduced |

So EOM operators are disposable only after specifying the kind of observable being computed.

## LSZ and on-shell amplitudes

In perturbative scattering, an EOM operator often produces a factor that cancels an external propagator or vanishes on shell. Schematically, for a scalar external leg,

$$
(\partial^2+m^2)\phi
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
-(p^2-m^2)\widetilde\phi(p)
$$

in mostly-minus Lorentzian conventions. LSZ reduction extracts residues at $p^2=m^2$. Thus factors proportional to $p^2-m^2$ vanish on external on-shell legs, except when they cancel a neighboring pole and produce a contact term or a redefinition of another vertex.

This is the amplitude version of the Schwinger–Dyson statement. EOM operators either vanish on shell or reshuffle local interactions. They do not generate independent $S$-matrix data.

This logic is especially useful in EFT. Suppose two higher-dimension operators differ by

$$
\mathcal O_1-\mathcal O_2
=
F^a\frac{\delta S_{\text{leading}}}{\delta\phi^a}
+\partial_\mu K^\mu.
$$

Then they give the same on-shell amplitudes after shifting lower-order coefficients consistently. A minimal on-shell EFT basis should keep only one representative.

## Composite operators and local bases

Composite-operator bases are more delicate than Lagrangian bases. When classifying local insertions, one often has to decide whether to keep EOM operators.

There are two common choices:

| Basis choice | Advantage | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Keep EOM operators | Renormalization and contact terms can be tracked explicitly. | The basis contains redundant directions. |
| Quotient by EOM operators | Physical deformations and on-shell amplitudes are not overcounted. | Contact terms and off-shell identities must be reconstructed when needed. |

In many EFT applications, the quotient basis is preferred because observables are on-shell amplitudes or inclusive rates. In renormalization of composite operators, especially off shell, it can be useful to keep EOM operators temporarily because they appear as counterterms and help close the operator space under renormalization.

The practical rule is:

$$
\text{do not remove EOM operators before deciding what the operator basis is meant to compute.}
$$

A basis for on-shell scattering, a basis for off-shell Green functions, a basis for lattice matching, and a basis for CFT operator data need not be organized identically.

## OPE interpretation

The OPE is an expansion of products of operators as distributions. EOM operators enter this story through contact terms and through relations among local operators.

For a free scalar, the equation

$$
(-\partial^2+m^2)G(x)=\delta^{(d)}(x)
$$

already says that applying the EOM operator to a two-point function produces a contact singularity. In an OPE, such terms appear as delta functions or derivatives of delta functions multiplying lower-dimension operators.

In a fixed-point theory, EOM relations can also become relations among conformal descendants and composite operators. For a free massless scalar,

$$
\partial^2\phi=0
$$

is a shortening condition: the descendant $\partial^2\phi$ is null away from contact terms. In the Wilson-Fisher theory near four dimensions, the interacting equation of motion relates a descendant of $\phi$ to a composite operator:

$$
\partial^2\phi\sim \phi^3
$$

with convention-dependent proportionality. This relation is one reason the operator spectrum reorganizes as one moves from the Gaussian fixed point to the interacting fixed point.

Thus EOM operators are not peripheral bookkeeping. They help determine which local operators are independent in the short-distance operator algebra.

## Gauge theories and BRST caveats

Gauge theories require extra care because not every field redefinition is compatible with the chosen gauge-invariant operator space. Three facts are worth separating.

First, gauge-invariant EOM reductions should use gauge-covariant equations of motion. For Yang-Mills theory with matter, a schematic gauge-field equation is

$$
D_\mu F^{\mu\nu}=J^\nu.
$$

Operators proportional to this equation can often be reshuffled into matter-current operators or removed by gauge-covariant field redefinitions.

Second, gauge-fixed formulations introduce ghosts, gauge-fixing terms, and BRST symmetry. In that language, physical local operators are organized by BRST cohomology. EOM operators can mix with BRST-exact operators and gauge-variant operators during renormalization. The physical quotient is not simply "set classical EOM to zero"; it is the appropriate cohomological quotient.

Third, anomalous Jacobians can spoil a naive field-redefinition argument. If the path-integral measure is not invariant, a change of variables may generate an anomaly. Then the EOM-like manipulation has physical content through the measure term.

For ordinary gauge-invariant EFT basis reduction, these caveats are manageable. For anomaly calculations, current algebra, or BRST proofs, they are load-bearing.

## Boundaries, defects, and sources

EOM simplifications often rely on integration by parts and on discarding total derivatives. With a boundary, a defect, or a nontrivial asymptotic condition, the total derivative becomes a boundary operator:

$$
\int_M d^d x\,\partial_\mu K^\mu
=
\int_{\partial M} d^{d-1}x\,n_\mu K^\mu.
$$

Therefore an operator that is redundant in infinite flat space can induce physical boundary terms in a finite region, on an interface, or near a defect.

External sources also matter. If a source couples to a field or composite operator, then a field redefinition changes the source coupling. Off-shell generating functionals transform, and EOM insertions can encode contact terms involving source variations.

This is why the safe statement is never simply "EOM operators vanish." The safe statement is always conditional:

$$
\text{EOM operators vanish modulo contact, boundary, source, and measure terms.}
$$

## A decision table

When using EOM operators, ask what you are computing.

| Situation | Can EOM operators be removed? | What to watch |
|---|---|---|
| Classical on-shell action comparison | usually yes | boundary terms and global conditions |
| Tree-level on-shell EFT amplitudes | yes | coefficient shifts from field redefinitions |
| Loop-level on-shell EFT amplitudes | yes, if done consistently | counterterm basis and scheme dependence |
| Off-shell Green functions | not as zero operators | contact terms and field definitions |
| Composite-operator renormalization | often keep temporarily | mixing with EOM and total derivatives |
| CFT operator spectrum | quotient by null/EOM relations | contact terms and shortening conditions |
| Gauge-invariant operator bases | yes with covariant EOM | BRST and gauge-fixing subtleties |
| Boundaries and defects | not automatically | induced boundary or defect operators |
| Anomaly calculations | dangerous | measure Jacobians may be physical |

This table is the operational heart of the page. EOM operators are simple only when the observable has already been specified.

## Common pitfalls

**Setting the equation of motion to zero inside a path integral.** The path integral sums over off-shell configurations. The correct quantum statement is a Schwinger–Dyson identity with contact terms.

**Dropping contact terms in a Ward identity.** Ward identities often are contact-term equations. Throwing away EOM contact terms can throw away the symmetry action on operator insertions.

**Confusing on-shell and off-shell bases.** An operator that is redundant for $S$-matrix elements may still be needed to renormalize off-shell Green functions.

**Using the wrong equation of motion.** In EFT basis reduction, use the lower-order EOM appropriate to the order in the power counting. Using higher-order EOM can double count or move terms inconsistently.

**Ignoring coefficient shifts.** Removing an EOM operator changes the coefficients of other operators. Observables are unchanged only after these shifts are included.

**Forgetting gauge covariance.** Gauge theories require gauge-covariant EOM reductions or BRST-cohomological language. Gauge-fixed EOM manipulations can obscure physical operator relations.

**Forgetting boundaries.** Total derivatives and EOM terms can become boundary or defect interactions. This is not a small print issue in finite-volume, brane, interface, or entanglement calculations.

## Research status

The basic statements on this page are settled: Schwinger–Dyson identities give the quantum equations of motion, EOM operators contribute contact terms, and local field redefinitions remove redundant couplings from on-shell observables.

Active and technical work continues in several directions. Automated EFT bases must implement EOM reduction without losing flavor, gauge, derivative, or Hilbert-series structure. Gauge theories require BRST-aware treatments of physical operator cohomology. Boundary, defect, and entanglement problems require careful treatment of terms that would be discarded in infinite-volume scattering. Functional RG truncations must decide how to handle redundant directions and reparametrization dependence. In CFT and bootstrap settings, null descendants, equations of motion, and multiplet recombination remain important conceptual bridges between fixed-point data and Lagrangian deformations.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Free scalar contact term

For the Euclidean free scalar action

$$
S=\int d^d x\left[\frac12(\partial\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2\right],
$$

use the Schwinger–Dyson identity to show that

$$
\left\langle(-\partial_x^2+m^2)\phi(x)\phi(y)\right\rangle
=\delta^{(d)}(x-y).
$$

Explain why this does not contradict the statement that the equation of motion holds at separated points.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use

$$
0=\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi(x)}\left(\phi(y)e^{-S[\phi]}\right).
$$

Expanding the derivative gives

$$
0=\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\left[\delta^{(d)}(x-y)-\phi(y)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}\right]e^{-S[\phi]}.
$$

Since

$$
\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}=(-\partial_x^2+m^2)\phi(x),
$$

we obtain

$$
\left\langle(-\partial_x^2+m^2)\phi(x)\phi(y)\right\rangle
=\delta^{(d)}(x-y).
$$

For $x\ne y$, the delta function vanishes, so the EOM insertion gives zero. At $x=y$, the contact term is essential: it is the defining Green-function singularity.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Removing a scalar EOM operator

Consider a scalar EFT with leading Euclidean action

$$
S_0=\int d^d x\left[\frac12(\partial\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2+\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4\right].
$$

Show that the operator $\phi^3\partial^2\phi$ can be traded for non-derivative operators modulo equations of motion and total derivatives. State what is meant by "traded."

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The leading equation of motion is

$$
-\partial^2\phi+m^2\phi+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^3=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\partial^2\phi=m^2\phi+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^3
$$

inside separated on-shell observables, modulo contact terms. Multiplying by $\phi^3$ gives

$$
\phi^3\partial^2\phi
\simeq
m^2\phi^4+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^6.
$$

The symbol $\simeq$ means that the difference between the two sides is an EOM operator. Equivalently, adding $c\int \phi^3\partial^2\phi$ to the action can be replaced by shifting the coefficients of $\phi^4$ and $\phi^6$, together with a local field redefinition. Observables are unchanged only after the coefficient shifts are included.

</details>

### Exercise 3: EOM insertion and LSZ

Suppose an interaction contains an operator of the schematic Lorentzian form

$$
\mathcal O_E=F(\phi)(\Box+m^2)\phi.
$$

Explain why this operator does not define an independent on-shell amplitude with one external scalar leg created by the final $\phi$ factor.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In momentum space, the factor $(\Box+m^2)\phi$ contributes a factor proportional to $-(p^2-m^2)$ for the corresponding external leg in mostly-minus conventions. LSZ reduction evaluates the residue at the pole $p^2=m^2$. Therefore this factor vanishes on shell unless it cancels an adjacent propagator pole before LSZ is applied.

If it cancels a propagator, the result is local and can be reproduced by shifting contact operators. Thus the EOM operator either vanishes on shell or reshuffles local vertices. It does not provide independent $S$-matrix data.

</details>
