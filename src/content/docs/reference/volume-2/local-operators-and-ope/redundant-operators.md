## Summary

A **redundant operator** is a local operator whose coupling changes the coordinates used to describe a theory, rather than changing the physical theory itself. The most common source is a local field redefinition. If

$$
\phi^a(x)\to \phi^a(x)+\varepsilon F^a[\phi](x),
$$

then the action changes, to first order, by

$$
\delta S
=\varepsilon\int d^d x\,
F^a[\phi](x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}
+\text{Jacobian and boundary terms}.
$$

Operators of the form

$$
F^a[\phi]\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a}
$$

are equations-of-motion operators. In separated correlation functions, their insertions reduce to contact terms. Their couplings can usually be removed by field redefinitions, at the price of shifting other couplings.

This matters because theory space has more coordinates than physical directions. In EFT, redundant operators should not be counted as independent Wilson coefficients. In RG, redundant directions are tangent to equivalence classes of actions related by field redefinitions. In CFT language, the physical spectrum of local deformations is a quotient: local operators modulo null, total-derivative, gauge, and equations-of-motion relations appropriate to the problem.

:::note[The local slogan]
A redundant operator is not a useless operator. It is an operator that changes the description rather than the physics.
:::

## Core idea

The same physical theory can be written using different field variables. For example, a scalar field variable $\phi$ and a new variable

$$
\phi'=\phi+\frac{a}{M^2}\phi^3
$$

can describe the same low-energy physics if the action and operator definitions are transformed consistently. The Lagrangian written in terms of $\phi'$ may contain different-looking operators from the Lagrangian written in terms of $\phi$, but the $S$-matrix and separated physical correlators do not change.

This means that the space of all local actions is not the same as the space of physical theories. The former includes coordinates. The latter is obtained by identifying actions related by allowed changes of variables.

The redundant-operator lesson is

$$
\text{operator basis}
\neq
\text{physical deformation basis}
$$

until equations of motion, integration by parts, symmetries, gauge redundancies, and field redefinitions have been handled.

## Setup and conventions

For clarity, use a Euclidean path integral

$$
Z[J]=\int \mathcal D\phi\,e^{-S[\phi]+\int J\phi}.
$$

The Lorentzian version differs by factors of $i$. The local field variables are denoted $\phi^a(x)$, where $a$ may include species, spin, internal indices, and other labels.

A local field redefinition is written

$$
\phi^a(x)\to \phi^a(x)+\varepsilon F^a[\phi](x),
$$

where $F^a[\phi]$ is local in fields and derivatives. To first order,

$$
S[\phi+\varepsilon F]
=S[\phi]+\varepsilon\int d^d x\,
F^a[\phi](x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}+O(\varepsilon^2).
$$

Thus the operator

$$
\mathcal O_F(x)=F^a[\phi](x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}
$$

is generated by changing variables. Such an operator is called redundant when the change of variables is legitimate and when the associated Jacobian and boundary terms are either trivial or absorbed into other local terms.

## Equations of motion inside correlation functions

The classical equation of motion is

$$
\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}=0.
$$

In quantum theory, it is too crude to say that this operator is simply zero. The correct statement is a Schwinger–Dyson identity. For any functional $X[\phi]$,

$$
0=\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi^a(x)}
\left(F^a[\phi](x)X[\phi]e^{-S[\phi]}\right).
$$

Expanding the derivative gives

$$
\left\langle
F^a(x)\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a(x)}X
\right\rangle
=
\left\langle
\frac{\delta F^a(x)}{\delta\phi^a(x)}X
+F^a(x)\frac{\delta X}{\delta\phi^a(x)}
\right\rangle.
$$

The right-hand side is made of contact terms when $X$ is a product of local operators at separated points. Therefore, away from coincident insertions,

$$
F^a\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a}
$$

acts like zero inside separated correlators.

This is the precise reason equations-of-motion operators can often be removed from an operator basis. They do not vanish as formal expressions, but their effect is local contact-term bookkeeping and field-coordinate change.

## Field redefinitions and action coordinates

Suppose an action contains a small deformation

$$
S\to S+c\int d^d x\,\mathcal O_F(x),
\qquad
\mathcal O_F=F^a\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi^a}.
$$

To first order in $c$, this deformation is undone by the field redefinition

$$
\phi^a\to \phi^a-cF^a.
$$

Therefore $c$ is not an independent physical coupling. It is a coordinate parameter.

The field redefinition may shift other terms in the action. This is not a problem; it is the point. Removing one redundant operator changes the numerical values of other couplings. If those other couplings are fitted or matched consistently, observables are unchanged.

A useful way to say this is:

$$
\text{redundant operator}
=\text{tangent vector to a field-redefinition orbit}.
$$

Physical theory space is the quotient by such orbits.

## Simple scalar example

Consider a scalar theory with leading action

$$
S_0=\int d^d x\left[
\frac12(\partial\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2+\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4
\right].
$$

The equation of motion is

$$
-\partial^2\phi+m^2\phi+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^3=0.
$$

Now consider the higher-dimension operator

$$
\mathcal O=\phi^3\partial^2\phi.
$$

After integrating by parts,

$$
\int d^d x\,\phi^3\partial^2\phi
=-3\int d^d x\,\phi^2(\partial\phi)^2
$$

up to a boundary term. But using the leading equation of motion gives

$$
\phi^3\partial^2\phi
= m^2\phi^4+\frac{\lambda}{3!}\phi^6
$$

inside separated correlators, up to contact terms and convention signs depending on Euclidean versus Lorentzian action conventions.

Equivalently, make a field redefinition

$$
\phi\to \phi+\frac{a}{M^2}\phi^3.
$$

The kinetic term changes as

$$
\frac12(\partial\phi)^2
\to
\frac12(\partial\phi)^2
+\frac{3a}{M^2}\phi^2(\partial\phi)^2+O(M^{-4}),
$$

while the mass and quartic terms also shift. Therefore an operator such as $\phi^2(\partial\phi)^2$ can be traded for non-derivative operators at the same order, depending on the chosen basis.

The physics is not that one expression is true and the other false. The physics is that they are related by a change of field coordinates.

## Redundant versus irrelevant

Redundant and irrelevant are different concepts.

An irrelevant operator is classified by scaling near a fixed point:

$$
\Delta>d.
$$

A redundant operator is classified by its relation to a field redefinition or an operator identity. An operator can be both irrelevant and redundant, but it need not be.

For example, in an EFT, many high-dimension operators are physically meaningful irrelevant deformations. They encode real low-energy effects of heavy physics. Other high-dimension operators are redundant and can be removed by field redefinitions. Power counting alone does not decide this.

The distinction matters in practical EFT matching. If a heavy theory generates a redundant operator, one may remove it, but its effect reappears as shifts in the coefficients of nonredundant operators. Dropping it without shifting the rest of the basis is a mistake.

## Redundant versus total derivative

A total derivative has the form

$$
\mathcal O=\partial_\mu V^\mu.
$$

On spacetime without boundary, its integral vanishes under ordinary assumptions:

$$
\int d^d x\,\partial_\mu V^\mu=0.
$$

This makes it removable from the bulk action. But total derivatives and redundant operators are not identical ideas.

A total derivative may matter when:

- spacetime has a boundary;
- defects or interfaces are present;
- fields have nontrivial topology;
- the total derivative is a topological density;
- the coefficient is quantized or periodic.

The theta term in a gauge theory is locally a total derivative, but it is not generally redundant. It can affect physics through topological sectors. So the rule is not "total derivatives never matter." The rule is "check boundaries, topology, and operator definitions before discarding them."

## Redundant operators in RG flow

In Wilsonian RG, an action is a point in theory space:

$$
S=\sum_i g^i\int d^d x\,\mathcal O_i(x).
$$

A field redefinition moves $S$ along a redundant direction. The beta function can have components along such directions. These components describe how the chosen coordinates change under RG; they do not necessarily describe physical changes.

This is why the words essential and inessential couplings are useful:

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| essential coupling | changes physical observables or universal data |
| inessential coupling | can be removed by a field redefinition or normalization choice |
| redundant operator | local tangent direction associated with such a coordinate change |

A simple example is field normalization. The coefficient of the kinetic term can be changed by rescaling the field:

$$
\phi\to Z^{1/2}\phi.
$$

One may keep the kinetic term canonically normalized and put the effect into anomalous dimensions, or choose a different convention. The anomalous dimension is not fake, but part of it reflects how the field coordinate is normalized. Physical scaling dimensions of gauge-invariant or well-defined operators are the invariant output.

## EFT operator bases

In an effective field theory, one writes all local operators allowed by symmetries up to a chosen order:

$$
\mathcal L_{\text{EFT}}
=\mathcal L_{\text{leading}}
+\sum_i\frac{C_i}{M^{\Delta_i-d}}\mathcal O_i.
$$

Before counting independent Wilson coefficients, one usually removes redundancies using:

- integration by parts;
- algebraic identities;
- Bianchi identities;
- Fierz identities;
- leading equations of motion;
- field redefinitions;
- symmetry and gauge constraints.

The result is an operator basis. A basis is not unique. Different bases are related by coefficient transformations. Predictions agree when matching, running, and matrix elements are transformed consistently.

This is why statements such as "this operator is absent" are basis-dependent unless the operator belongs to an invariant class, is forbidden by symmetry, or corresponds to a physical amplitude structure that cannot be removed.

## Gauge theories and BRST language

Gauge theories require extra care. A change of variables must respect gauge redundancy, gauge fixing, ghosts, and the measure. In gauge-fixed language, many unphysical deformations are BRST-exact:

$$
\mathcal O=\{Q_{\text{BRST}},\mathcal V\}.
$$

BRST-exact insertions decouple from physical BRST-closed observables under suitable assumptions. This is the gauge-theory cousin of the equations-of-motion story.

However, careless field redefinitions in gauge theories can obscure Ward identities, Slavnov–Taylor identities, anomalies, or boundary charges. A gauge-variant operator may be removable from physical on-shell amplitudes but still useful in intermediate renormalization. Conversely, a topological or anomalous term may look like a total derivative locally but remain physically meaningful.

The robust statement is cohomological: physical local deformations are represented by appropriate cohomology classes, not arbitrary local expressions.

## Redundant operators and the OPE

The OPE is usually written in a basis of local operators. If that basis includes redundant operators, OPE coefficients involving them can appear. But separated physical correlators are insensitive to redundant directions except through contact terms and basis changes.

This affects the interpretation of conformal perturbations. Deforming a CFT by

$$
\int d^d x\,\mathcal O(x)
$$

is physically meaningful only if $\mathcal O$ represents a genuine deformation class. If $\mathcal O$ is a total derivative, null operator, equations-of-motion operator, or pure gauge variation, its integrated deformation may be trivial or boundary-supported.

In CFT language, descendants are not automatically redundant. For example, $\partial_\mu\mathcal O$ integrates to a boundary term in flat space without boundary, but a descendant such as $\partial^2\mathcal O$ can affect contact terms and improvements. Conserved-current descendants, null states, and equations-of-motion relations require separate analysis.

So the safe rule is:

$$
\text{first identify the physical operator quotient, then interpret OPE data.}
$$

## A diagnostic checklist

When deciding whether an operator is redundant, ask:

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Is it proportional to the leading equations of motion? | likely removable by field redefinition |
| Does removing it shift other operators? | almost always yes |
| Are there boundaries, defects, or topology? | total derivatives may become physical |
| Is the field redefinition local and invertible perturbatively? | nonlocal or singular changes can change physics |
| Does the measure produce a nontrivial Jacobian? | anomalies can make a transformation physical |
| Is gauge symmetry involved? | use gauge-invariant or BRST-cohomological language |
| Are we computing off-shell Green functions? | redundant operators can affect off-shell quantities |
| Are we truncating an EFT or RG equation? | basis changes can move errors between orders |

This checklist is less glamorous than a one-line rule, but it prevents expensive mistakes.

## Common pitfalls

**Saying equations-of-motion operators are zero.** They vanish only in a qualified sense: inside separated physical correlators, up to contact terms and after consistent field redefinitions.

**Dropping a redundant operator without shifting the rest of the basis.** Removing an operator changes other coefficients. If you do not transform the coefficients, you changed the theory.

**Confusing off-shell and on-shell statements.** Redundant operators may affect off-shell Green functions, gauge-dependent quantities, or intermediate renormalization constants while leaving on-shell observables unchanged.

**Treating total derivatives as always irrelevant.** Boundaries, defects, theta terms, winding sectors, and anomalies can make total derivatives physical.

**Using classical equations of motion beyond their order.** In EFT, equations of motion are used order by order. Using higher-order corrected equations carelessly can double count or remove physical terms incorrectly.

**Ignoring gauge cohomology.** In gauge theories, physical deformations are not just gauge-invariant-looking expressions. BRST cohomology and anomaly structure matter.

**Counting Lagrangian terms instead of physical parameters.** A long Lagrangian may contain fewer physical coefficients than it seems. A short Lagrangian may hide complicated field-coordinate choices.

## Research status

The basic role of redundant operators is established in perturbative QFT, Wilsonian RG, and EFT. Field redefinitions do not change physical observables when they are local, invertible, and handled consistently. Equations-of-motion operators and integration-by-parts relations are standard tools for reducing EFT operator bases.

Subtleties remain active in several settings: gauge theories with BRST and BV structure, anomalous transformations, boundary and defect QFT, curved backgrounds, nonlocal field redefinitions, nonperturbative definitions of theory space, and truncated functional RG calculations where redundant directions can contaminate approximate critical exponents.

In modern EFT practice, redundant-operator management is not a philosophical afterthought. It is part of the computational infrastructure behind matching, running, and basis translation.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Equation-of-motion insertion

In a Euclidean scalar path integral, show that

$$
\left\langle
\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}\phi(y_1)\cdots\phi(y_n)
\right\rangle
=
\sum_{r=1}^n\delta^{(d)}(x-y_r)
\left\langle
\phi(y_1)\cdots\widehat{\phi(y_r)}\cdots\phi(y_n)
\right\rangle,
$$

where the hat means that the factor is omitted.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use the Schwinger–Dyson identity

$$
0=\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\frac{\delta}{\delta\phi(x)}
\left[\phi(y_1)\cdots\phi(y_n)e^{-S[\phi]}\right].
$$

The derivative acting on $e^{-S}$ gives

$$
-\frac{\delta S}{\delta\phi(x)}
\phi(y_1)\cdots\phi(y_n)e^{-S}.
$$

The derivative acting on the product gives

$$
\sum_{r=1}^n\delta^{(d)}(x-y_r)
\phi(y_1)\cdots\widehat{\phi(y_r)}\cdots\phi(y_n)e^{-S}.
$$

Moving the first term to the other side gives the stated identity. The result is purely a contact term in $x$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Removing a derivative operator

For the scalar action

$$
S_0=\int d^d x\left[\frac12(\partial\phi)^2+V(\phi)\right],
$$

show that the field redefinition

$$
\phi\to \phi+\varepsilon F(\phi)
$$

generates the first-order change

$$
\delta S_0
=\varepsilon\int d^d x\,
F(\phi)\left[-\partial^2\phi+V'(\phi)\right]
$$

up to boundary terms.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The variation is

$$
\delta S_0
=\int d^d x\left[
\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\mu\delta\phi+V'(\phi)\delta\phi
\right].
$$

Integrating the first term by parts gives

$$
\int d^d x\,\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\mu\delta\phi
=-\int d^d x\,(\partial^2\phi)\delta\phi
$$

up to a boundary term. With $\delta\phi=\varepsilon F(\phi)$,

$$
\delta S_0
=\varepsilon\int d^d x\,
F(\phi)\left[-\partial^2\phi+V'(\phi)\right].
$$

Thus the generated operator is proportional to the equation of motion.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Basis dependence of Wilson coefficients

Suppose an EFT contains

$$
\mathcal L=\mathcal L_0+\frac{c_1}{M^2}\mathcal O_1+\frac{c_2}{M^2}\mathcal O_2,
$$

and $\mathcal O_2$ is redundant with

$$
\int\mathcal O_2 = \int\left(a\mathcal O_1+\mathcal O_{\text{EOM}}\right).
$$

What happens to $c_1$ when $\mathcal O_2$ is removed?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Since the integrated redundant operator differs from $a\mathcal O_1$ by an equations-of-motion term, removing $\mathcal O_2$ by a field redefinition shifts the coefficient of $\mathcal O_1$. The Lagrangian can be rewritten as

$$
\mathcal L
=\mathcal L_0+\frac{c_1+a c_2}{M^2}\mathcal O_1
+\frac{c_2}{M^2}\mathcal O_{\text{EOM}}.
$$

The EOM term is removed, leaving

$$
c_1' = c_1+a c_2.
$$

Thus one cannot simply set $c_2=0$ while leaving all other coefficients unchanged.

</details>
