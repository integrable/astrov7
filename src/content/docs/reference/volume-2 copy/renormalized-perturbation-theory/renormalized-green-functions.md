## Summary

A **renormalized Green function** is a correlation function expressed in terms of renormalized fields and renormalized parameters, after the local ultraviolet counterterms have been included.

For a scalar field with

$$
\phi_0=Z_\phi^{1/2}\phi,
$$

the simplest separated-point relation is

$$
G_R^{(n)}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
Z_\phi^{-n/2}
G_0^{(n)}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
$$

with the bare parameters inside $G_0^{(n)}$ expressed in terms of renormalized parameters. This formula is not the whole story — counterterms, contact terms, composite-operator insertions, gauge fixing, and infrared issues matter — but it is the right first anchor.

Renormalized Green functions come in several related forms:

| Object | Meaning | Generated by |
|---|---|---|
| $G_R^{(n)}$ | ordinary time-ordered or Euclidean $n$-point function | $Z_R[J]$ |
| $G_{R,c}^{(n)}$ | connected $n$-point function | $W_R[J]$ |
| $\Gamma_R^{(n)}$ | one-particle-irreducible vertex function | $\Gamma_R[\varphi]$ |
| amputated Green function | external propagators removed | LSZ and scattering calculations |

They are not all observables. They are scheme-defined building blocks from which RG equations, matching conditions, amplitudes, response functions, lattice comparisons, and operator-renormalization statements are made precise.

The practical rule is:

$$
\text{renormalize the action, renormalize the fields, then define Green functions by differentiating the renormalized generator.}
$$

You should know [Renormalized Lagrangian](/renormalization-rg-eft/renormalized-perturbation-theory/renormalized-lagrangian/), [Renormalization Conditions](/renormalization-rg-eft/renormalized-perturbation-theory/renormalization-conditions/), [Mass and Coupling Renormalization](/renormalization-rg-eft/renormalized-perturbation-theory/mass-and-coupling-renormalization/), and [Wavefunction Renormalization](/renormalization-rg-eft/renormalized-perturbation-theory/wavefunction-renormalization/). For the origin of the needed local counterterms, review [Renormalization of Correlation Functions](/renormalization-rg-eft/regularization-counterterms/renormalization-of-correlation-functions/).

We use the volume convention $\eta_{\mu\nu}=\operatorname{diag}(+1,-1,-1,-1)$. For generating functionals, this page often uses Euclidean signs because they make the bookkeeping clean:

$$
Z_R[J]=\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\exp\left[-S_R[\phi]+\int d^d x\,J(x)\phi(x)\right].
$$

The ultraviolet renormalization statements do not depend on this Euclidean display. Lorentzian time-ordered Green functions are obtained by the usual analytic-continuation and $i\epsilon$ prescriptions.

## Core idea

Green functions are where renormalization becomes visible as a statement about finite distributions.

Before renormalization, loop corrections to a correlator can diverge when internal points approach each other or when loop momenta become large. Locality says that the ultraviolet part of these singularities can be absorbed into local terms in the action and local rescalings of fields and operators. After this is done, the renormalized Green functions are finite functions or distributions of their external positions and momenta.

This statement has two important qualifications.

First, **separated points are easier than coincident points**. A correlator such as

$$
\langle \phi(x_1)\cdots\phi(x_n)\rangle_R
$$

with all $x_i$ distinct has fewer local ambiguities than a product of fields evaluated at the same point. Composite operators require their own renormalization.

Second, **finite does not mean observable**. Off-shell Green functions depend on the choice of field variables, gauge fixing, renormalization scheme, and subtraction scale. They are useful because they are controlled, not because every one of them is directly measurable.

## Setup and conventions

In Euclidean notation, define the renormalized source functional by

$$
Z_R[J]
=
\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\exp\left[-S_R[\phi]-S_{\text{ct}}[\phi]+\int d^d x\,J\phi\right].
$$

The full Green functions are obtained by differentiating $Z_R[J]$:

$$
G_R^{(n)}(x_1,
\ldots,x_n)
=
\frac{1}{Z_R[0]}
\left.
\frac{\delta^n Z_R[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}
\right|_{J=0}.
$$

The connected generator is

$$
W_R[J]=\log Z_R[J],
$$

and connected Green functions are

$$
G_{R,c}^{(n)}(x_1,
\ldots,x_n)
=
\left.
\frac{\delta^n W_R[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}
\right|_{J=0}.
$$

The classical field associated with the source is

$$
\varphi(x)=\frac{\delta W_R[J]}{\delta J(x)}.
$$

The 1PI effective action is the Legendre transform

$$
\Gamma_R[\varphi]
=
\int d^d x\,J(x)\varphi(x)-W_R[J],
$$

where $J$ is eliminated in favor of $\varphi$. The renormalized 1PI vertices are

$$
\Gamma_R^{(n)}(x_1,
\ldots,x_n)
=
\left.
\frac{\delta^n\Gamma_R[\varphi]}{\delta\varphi(x_1)\cdots\delta\varphi(x_n)}
\right|_{\varphi=0}.
$$

Lorentzian conventions differ by factors of $i$ and signs in the Legendre transform. The roles of $Z_R$, $W_R$, and $\Gamma_R$ are the same.

## Renormalized fields and sources

Suppose

$$
\phi_0=Z_\phi^{1/2}\phi.
$$

If the source term is to describe the same bare insertion,

$$
\int d^d x\,J_0\phi_0
=
\int d^d x\,J\phi,
$$

then the sources are related by

$$
J_0=Z_\phi^{-1/2}J.
$$

Differentiating with respect to $J$ rather than $J_0$ gives the basic relation between bare and renormalized Green functions:

$$
G_R^{(n)}=Z_\phi^{-n/2}G_0^{(n)},
$$

with the bare parameters $m_0$, $g_0$, and the regulator expressed in terms of $m(\mu)$, $g(\mu)$, and the chosen scheme.

This equation is often the cleanest way to remember field renormalization. But it should be read with two caveats:

| Caveat | Meaning |
|---|---|
| contact terms | coincident-point distributions can shift by local terms |
| composite operators | insertions such as $\phi^2$ or $J^\mu$ need their own renormalization |

The elementary field $\phi$ is already a local operator, but it is not the only local operator. Once one studies products at a point, currents, stress tensors, order parameters, or EFT operators, the renormalization problem becomes matrix-valued.

## Connected functions and 1PI vertices

Connected functions remove disconnected products. If

$$
G_R^{(4)}(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)
$$

is a full four-point function, it contains terms like

$$
G_R^{(2)}(x_1,x_2)G_R^{(2)}(x_3,x_4)
$$

as well as genuinely connected information. The connected generator $W_R[J]$ isolates the connected part automatically.

The 1PI effective action goes one step further. It organizes the kernels that cannot be disconnected by cutting a single internal line. The two-point 1PI function is the inverse of the connected propagator, in the sense that

$$
\int d^d z\,
\Gamma_R^{(2)}(x,z)G_{R,c}^{(2)}(z,y)
=
\delta^{(d)}(x-y),
$$

up to sign conventions in Euclidean versus Lorentzian notation.

In momentum space, for a scalar field, this statement becomes the familiar inverse-propagator relation

$$
G_R^{(2)}(p)
=
\frac{1}{\Gamma_R^{(2)}(p)}
$$

in Euclidean conventions, or the corresponding $i$-decorated relation in Lorentzian conventions.

The reason 1PI functions are so useful for renormalization is locality. Counterterms are local vertices, so they appear naturally in $\Gamma_R[\varphi]$ and in the 1PI kernels $\Gamma_R^{(n)}$.

## Momentum-space form

For a translationally invariant vacuum, the renormalized connected $n$-point function has the form

$$
G_{R,c}^{(n)}(p_1,
\ldots,p_n)
=
(2\pi)^d\delta^{(d)}\!\left(\sum_{a=1}^n p_a\right)
\widetilde G_{R,c}^{(n)}(p_1,
\ldots,p_{n-1}).
$$

The delta function expresses momentum conservation. The reduced function $\widetilde G_{R,c}^{(n)}$ is the nontrivial part.

Similarly, one writes

$$
\Gamma_R^{(n)}(p_1,
\ldots,p_n)
=
(2\pi)^d\delta^{(d)}\!\left(\sum_{a=1}^n p_a\right)
\widetilde\Gamma_R^{(n)}(p_1,
\ldots,p_{n-1}).
$$

For a scalar theory, the renormalized two-point 1PI function can be written schematically as

$$
\widetilde\Gamma_R^{(2)}(p)
=p^2+m^2+\Sigma_R(p^2)
$$

in Euclidean signature, or as

$$
D_R^{-1}(p^2)=p^2-m^2-\Pi_R(p^2)
$$

in the Lorentzian inverse-propagator convention used in nearby pages. The two notations encode the same physics after analytic continuation.

## Renormalization conditions as Green-function definitions

A renormalization condition defines parameters by imposing conditions on renormalized Green functions.

In an on-shell scalar scheme, one can impose

$$
D_R^{-1}(m^2)=0,
\qquad
\left.\frac{dD_R^{-1}}{dp^2}\right|_{p^2=m^2}=1.
$$

These conditions say that the renormalized mass is the pole mass and that the pole residue is one.

In a momentum-subtraction scheme for a scalar quartic coupling, one may impose

$$
\widetilde\Gamma_R^{(4)}(p_i\text{ at the subtraction point})=-\lambda.
$$

In an MS or $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ scheme, one instead subtracts pole parts in dimensional regularization and does not impose a direct physical value of the vertex at a finite momentum point.

All of these are definitions of Green functions as finite scheme-dependent objects. They differ by finite local counterterms.

## Amputation and external legs

A connected Green function includes external propagators. An amputated function removes them. Schematically,

$$
G_{R,c}^{(n)}
=
G_R^{(2)}\cdots G_R^{(2)}\,\mathcal A_R^{(n)}
$$

where $\mathcal A_R^{(n)}$ denotes the amputated connected object. For tree-level diagrams this distinction is easy to see: external lines are propagators attached to a central vertex or subdiagram. At loop level the distinction remains essential.

The LSZ formula then relates on-shell scattering amplitudes to residues of amputated time-ordered Green functions. This is why wavefunction renormalization and pole residues matter. A finite off-shell Green function is not yet a physical amplitude; it must be amputated, put on shell, and normalized by the pole residues of the interpolating fields.

This is also why off-shell Green functions can be scheme or gauge dependent while physical $S$-matrix elements are not.

## Contact terms and composite insertions

Renormalized Green functions are distributions. A distribution can be changed by contact terms supported at coincident points, such as

$$
\delta^{(d)}(x-y),
\qquad
\partial^2\delta^{(d)}(x-y).
$$

These contact terms are not nuisances to be swept under the rug. They encode local counterterm freedom, Ward-identity contact terms, operator mixing, and scheme dependence.

For separated elementary-field correlators, the simple factor $Z_\phi^{-n/2}$ is often enough. For composite operators, one needs new renormalization constants. For example,

$$
(\phi^2)_0=Z_{\phi^2}{}^{\phi^2}(\phi^2)_R+Z_{\phi^2}{}^{\mathbf 1}\mathbf 1+
\cdots,
$$

where the ellipsis can include operators with the same symmetries and compatible dimensions. In a general operator basis,

$$
\mathcal O_{a,0}=Z_a{}^b\mathcal O_{b,R}.
$$

This is why composite-operator renormalization is not a decorative advanced topic. It is already implicit whenever one asks for local insertions, currents, stress tensors, EFT operators, or OPE coefficients.

## Callan–Symanzik equation

Because bare Green functions do not depend on the arbitrary renormalization scale, renormalized Green functions obey renormalization group equations.

For a single scalar field, away from contact terms and with masses displayed schematically, the homogeneous form is

$$
\left(
\mu\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu}
+\beta^i\frac{\partial}{\partial g^i}
+\beta_{m^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial m^2}
+n\gamma_\phi
\right)
G_R^{(n)}=0.
$$

Here

$$
\beta^i=\left.\mu\frac{dg^i}{d\mu}\right|_{\text{bare}},
\qquad
\gamma_\phi=\frac12\left.\mu\frac{d\log Z_\phi}{d\mu}\right|_{\text{bare}}.
$$

This sign convention matches the volume conventions. Some books define $\gamma_\phi$ with the opposite sign, so the displayed equation changes accordingly.

At a fixed point with $\beta^i=0$ and no mass deformation, the same equation becomes a scaling statement. Green functions then transform with full scaling dimensions rather than just engineering dimensions. This is the bridge from perturbative renormalization to CFT language.

## Gauge theories and gauge dependence

In gauge theories, Green functions of elementary gauge fields are usually gauge dependent. This is not a flaw. Gauge-fixed Green functions are computational objects. Physical information is extracted from gauge-invariant observables, BRST-invariant quantities, or properly normalized on-shell amplitudes.

The renormalization of gauge-theory Green functions is constrained by Ward identities in QED and by Slavnov–Taylor identities in non-Abelian gauge theories. These identities relate field renormalizations, vertex renormalizations, and coupling renormalization. They are the reason gauge-theory counterterms are not arbitrary independent local terms.

The detailed gauge-theory story belongs later in [Gauge Theories and RG](/renormalization-rg-eft/gauge-theories-and-rg/), but the warning belongs here: never interpret a gauge-fixed off-shell Green function as directly physical without checking what is gauge invariant.

## Common pitfalls

**Calling every Green function an observable.** Correlators are central objects, but off-shell Green functions are usually scheme-dependent and, in gauge theory, gauge-dependent.

**Forgetting disconnected pieces.** Full Green functions, connected Green functions, and 1PI vertices carry different information. Mixing them up creates fake factors and fake poles.

**Amputating too early.** Amputation removes external propagators. It does not replace LSZ, pole residues, or on-shell limits.

**Ignoring contact terms.** Contact terms are part of local QFT. They matter in Ward identities, composite-operator renormalization, stress tensors, and OPEs.

**Assuming $Z_\phi$ renormalizes every local operator.** It renormalizes the elementary field. Composite operators generally mix and need their own renormalization matrix.

**Reading scheme dependence as physical ambiguity.** A renormalized Green function depends on the scheme. Physical predictions do not, once the input definitions and perturbative order are handled consistently.

## Research status

For ordinary perturbative renormalizable theories, the construction of renormalized Green functions is mature. The active issues are mostly about difficult contexts rather than the basic idea.

Important delicate areas include:

| Area | Why it is subtle |
|---|---|
| composite operators | operator mixing, contact terms, and scheme dependence |
| gauge theories | gauge dependence and Slavnov–Taylor constraints |
| infrared physics | massless particles can make off-shell or on-shell limits singular |
| real-time finite-temperature QFT | analytic structure and contour choices are more intricate |
| nonperturbative QFT | defining continuum correlators may require lattice or constructive limits |
| conformal field theory | correlators are finite data, but contact terms and normalization conventions remain important |

A good modern calculation states the fields, sources, scheme, subtraction scale, gauge fixing if any, operator basis, and contact-term conventions whenever they matter.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Field renormalization factor for an n-point function

Assume $\phi_0=Z_\phi^{1/2}\phi$ and define

$$
G_0^{(n)}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\langle\phi_0(x_1)\cdots\phi_0(x_n)\rangle.
$$

Show that the corresponding renormalized separated-point function satisfies

$$
G_R^{(n)}=Z_\phi^{-n/2}G_0^{(n)}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

From

$$
\phi_0=Z_\phi^{1/2}\phi,
$$

we have

$$
\phi=Z_\phi^{-1/2}\phi_0.
$$

Therefore

$$
G_R^{(n)}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\langle\phi(x_1)\cdots\phi(x_n)\rangle
=
Z_\phi^{-n/2}
\langle\phi_0(x_1)\cdots\phi_0(x_n)\rangle.
$$

Thus

$$
G_R^{(n)}=Z_\phi^{-n/2}G_0^{(n)}.
$$

This statement assumes the fields are separated and that no additional composite operator sits at coincident points.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Connected two-point function and 1PI inverse

Let $W[J]$ be the connected generator and $\Gamma[\varphi]=\int J\varphi-W[J]$ its Euclidean Legendre transform. Show schematically that the second derivative of $\Gamma$ is the inverse of the connected two-point function.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

By definition,

$$
\varphi(x)=\frac{\delta W}{\delta J(x)}.
$$

Differentiating with respect to $J(y)$ gives

$$
\frac{\delta\varphi(x)}{\delta J(y)}
=
\frac{\delta^2 W}{\delta J(y)\delta J(x)}
=G_c^{(2)}(x,y).
$$

The Legendre transform implies

$$
\frac{\delta\Gamma}{\delta\varphi(x)}=J(x).
$$

Differentiating with respect to $\varphi(z)$ gives

$$
\frac{\delta J(x)}{\delta\varphi(z)}
=
\Gamma^{(2)}(x,z).
$$

Since $\delta\varphi/\delta J$ and $\delta J/\delta\varphi$ are inverse kernels,

$$
\int d^d z\,\Gamma^{(2)}(x,z)G_c^{(2)}(z,y)
=
\delta^{(d)}(x-y).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Sign of the Callan–Symanzik field term

Using $G_0^{(n)}=Z_\phi^{n/2}G_R^{(n)}$ and

$$
\gamma_\phi=\frac12\left.\mu\frac{d\log Z_\phi}{d\mu}\right|_{\text{bare}},
$$

show why the Callan–Symanzik equation in this volume contains $+n\gamma_\phi G_R^{(n)}$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate the bare Green function at fixed bare parameters:

$$
0=
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}G_0^{(n)}
=
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}
\left(Z_\phi^{n/2}G_R^{(n)}\right).
$$

The derivative of the prefactor gives

$$
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}Z_\phi^{n/2}
=
Z_\phi^{n/2}n\gamma_\phi.
$$

The derivative of $G_R^{(n)}$ includes explicit $\mu$ dependence and implicit dependence through running parameters. Therefore

$$
0=Z_\phi^{n/2}
\left(
\mu\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu}
+\beta^i\frac{\partial}{\partial g^i}
+\beta_{m^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial m^2}
+n\gamma_\phi
\right)G_R^{(n)}.
$$

Dividing by $Z_\phi^{n/2}$ gives the stated sign.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Why φ² is not renormalized by Zφ alone

Explain why the operator $\phi^2(x)$ generally needs its own renormalization constant, even if the field $\phi$ has already been renormalized.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Field renormalization controls insertions of separated elementary fields. The composite operator $\phi^2(x)$ is a product of two fields at the same point, so it probes an additional short-distance singularity as the two fields approach one another.

Therefore the operator inserted at one point can mix with all local operators having the same symmetries and compatible dimension. Schematically,

$$
(\phi^2)_0=Z_{\phi^2}{}^{\phi^2}(\phi^2)_R+Z_{\phi^2}{}^{\mathbf 1}\mathbf 1+\cdots.
$$

The factor $Z_\phi$ accounts for external elementary-field normalization. It does not subtract all coincident-point singularities of composite insertions.

</details>
