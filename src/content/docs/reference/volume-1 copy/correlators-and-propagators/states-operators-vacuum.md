## Summary

Correlation functions are matrix elements of operators. In QFT the most basic ones are vacuum expectation values:

$$
\langle 0|\mathcal O_1(x_1)\mathcal O_2(x_2)\cdots \mathcal O_n(x_n)|0\rangle.
$$

Here $|0\rangle$ is the vacuum state and $\mathcal O_i(x_i)$ are Heisenberg-picture local operators inserted at spacetime points. This formula is the first bridge from the Hilbert-space language of states and operators to the calculational language of Green functions, propagators, spectral densities, Ward identities, and eventually scattering amplitudes.

The slogan is:

```txt
states define what is being averaged,
operators define what is being probed,
correlation functions record the answer.
```

For the Poincaré-invariant vacuum, correlation functions cannot depend on the absolute location of all insertions, only on their relative spacetime separations. This simple observation is already powerful: it implies momentum conservation in Fourier space, selection rules for internal symmetries, and the spectral interpretation of two-point functions.

Coleman's Green-function chapter identifies the physical Green functions with vacuum expectation values of time-ordered products of Heisenberg fields; Weinberg emphasizes that vacuum expectation values of local Heisenberg operators inherit translation invariance and depend only on coordinate differences; Zee motivates time-ordered correlators by translating path-integral insertions back into operator language (Coleman 2019, ch. 13; Weinberg 1995, Vol. I, §§6.4 and 10.1; Zee 2010, chs. I.7–I.8).

## Core idea

The operator formalism of QFT has three ingredients:

1. a Hilbert space of states,
2. operators acting on that Hilbert space,
3. distinguished states, especially the vacuum.

A local operator insertion $\mathcal O(x)$ is not the same thing as a classical measurement performed by a tiny apparatus at $x$. It is a mathematical probe. By inserting operators between states, we form quantities that encode the theory.

For example,

$$
\langle 0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
$$

asks: if the field operator $\phi(y)$ creates whatever state it can from the vacuum, what overlap does that state have with the state created by $\phi(x)$? The answer is a distribution, not an ordinary function.

This is why the correlator language is so central. It is not an optional notation. It is the natural way to package the information carried by states and local operators.

This page assumes flat Minkowski spacetime and a stable Poincaré-invariant vacuum. The generalization to degenerate vacua, thermal states, curved spacetime, gauge theory, and algebraic QFT requires extra care. Those cases keep the same basic idea—matrix elements of operators—but the meaning of “vacuum,” “local operator,” and “state” can become subtler.

## The Heisenberg picture

In the Heisenberg picture, states are time-independent and operators carry the time dependence. For an operator $A$ in ordinary quantum mechanics,

$$
A_H(t)=e^{iHt}A_S e^{-iHt}.
$$

The QFT version uses the four-momentum operator $P^\mu$:

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal O(x)=e^{iP\cdot x}\mathcal O(0)e^{-iP\cdot x}.
}
$$

With the qft.org mostly-minus convention,

$$
P\cdot x=P^0x^0-\mathbf P\cdot\mathbf x.
$$

This formula says that the spacetime label $x$ is generated by translations. The label is not a position eigenvalue of the field. That is one of the conceptual shifts from single-particle quantum mechanics to QFT.

For a scalar field, the transformation law is just the one above. For fields with spin or Lorentz indices, Lorentz transformations also act on the indices. Translations alone are enough for the key facts in this page.

## The vacuum

The vacuum is the state conventionally denoted $|0\rangle$. In a relativistic QFT it is usually assumed to be:

$$
\langle0|0\rangle=1,
$$

and, after choosing the zero of energy,

$$
\boxed{
P^\mu|0\rangle=0.
}
$$

In a free theory, this is the state annihilated by all annihilation operators:

$$
a_s(\mathbf p)|0\rangle=0.
$$

In an interacting theory, the physical vacuum is usually not the same vector as the free Fock vacuum used to define perturbation theory. This distinction is not philosophical hair-splitting. It is why vacuum bubbles appear in perturbation theory and why one often divides by a vacuum-to-vacuum factor in generating functionals.

The vacuum can also fail to be unique. In theories with spontaneous symmetry breaking, there can be many translation-invariant vacua related by a symmetry. Correlation functions must then specify which vacuum is being used:

$$
\langle0_a|\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)|0_a\rangle.
$$

Foundations pages usually assume one chosen vacuum unless stated otherwise.

## Vacuum expectation values

A vacuum expectation value, or VEV, is a matrix element with the vacuum on both sides:

$$
\langle\mathcal O\rangle\equiv \langle0|\mathcal O|0\rangle.
$$

For a product of local operators,

$$
W_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=\langle0|\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)|0\rangle.
$$

Without time ordering, these are often called Wightman functions. We will treat them systematically in [Wightman Functions](/foundations/correlators-and-propagators/wightman-functions/). For now, the important point is that operator order matters:

$$
\langle0|\mathcal O_1(x)\mathcal O_2(y)|0\rangle
\neq
\langle0|\mathcal O_2(y)\mathcal O_1(x)|0\rangle
$$

in general.

For Hermitian operators, the two expressions are related by complex conjugation when the same pair of operators appears in reversed order:

$$
\left(\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle\right)^*
=\langle0|B(y)A(x)|0\rangle.
$$

So even for real scalar fields, the Wightman two-point function is not generally a real symmetric function of $x-y$.

## Translation invariance

Because $P^\mu|0\rangle=0$ and $\langle0|P^\mu=0$, a vacuum expectation value is invariant if all operator insertions are translated together:

$$
\langle0|\mathcal O_1(x_1+a)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n+a)|0\rangle
=
\langle0|\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)|0\rangle.
$$

Therefore an $n$-point function depends on $n-1$ independent coordinate differences, not on all $n$ locations separately.

For a two-point function,

$$
\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle = F_{AB}(x-y).
$$

In Fourier space, the same statement becomes momentum conservation. If

$$
G_n(k_1,\ldots,k_n)
=\int \prod_{j=1}^n d^4x_j\,
 e^{i\sum_j k_j\cdot x_j}
\langle0|\mathcal O_1(x_1)\cdots\mathcal O_n(x_n)|0\rangle,
$$

then translation invariance implies a factor

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}\!\left(\sum_{j=1}^n k_j\right).
$$

This is the correlator version of momentum conservation.

## Spectral decomposition

Insert a complete set of energy-momentum eigenstates between two operators:

$$
\mathbf 1 = \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|,
\qquad
P^\mu|n\rangle=p_n^\mu|n\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle
&=\sum_n \langle0|A(x)|n\rangle\langle n|B(y)|0\rangle \\
&=\sum_n e^{-ip_n\cdot(x-y)}
\langle0|A(0)|n\rangle\langle n|B(0)|0\rangle.
\end{aligned}
$$

This formula is schematic: in infinite volume, the sum includes integrals over continuous momenta and phase space. Its lesson is robust: two-point functions know about the spectrum of states that can be created by the operators.

If $A$ and $B$ carry the wrong quantum numbers, the matrix elements vanish. If $A$ is a scalar field, it couples to scalar one-particle states and scalar multiparticle states. If $A$ is a conserved current, it couples to states with the current's quantum numbers. This is the seed of the [Spectral Representation](/foundations/correlators-and-propagators/spectral-representation/).

## Free scalar example

For the free real scalar field,

$$
\phi(x)=\int d\mu(p)
\left[
 a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}+a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}
\right],
$$

where

$$
d\mu(p)=\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}}.
$$

The vacuum obeys $a(\mathbf p)|0\rangle=0$. The unordered two-point function is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
&=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

This is usually denoted

$$
\boxed{
\Delta^+(x-y)=\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle.
}
$$

The reversed ordering gives

$$
\langle0|\phi(y)\phi(x)|0\rangle=\Delta^+(y-x).
$$

The commutator is their difference:

$$
[\phi(x),\phi(y)]
=\Delta^+(x-y)-\Delta^+(y-x).
$$

With the convention of [Equal-Time Commutators](/foundations/canonical-quantization/equal-time-commutators/), this is

$$
[\phi(x),\phi(y)]=i\Delta(x-y).
$$

Thus the Wightman function, commutator function, and propagator are close relatives, but they are not the same object.

## Complex scalar example

For a complex scalar field,

$$
\phi(x)=\int d\mu(p)
\left[
 a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}+b^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}
\right],
$$

where $a^\dagger$ creates particles and $b^\dagger$ creates antiparticles. The nonzero two-point functions include

$$
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi^\dagger(y)|0\rangle
=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)},
$$

and

$$
\langle0|\phi^\dagger(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}.
$$

But charge conservation gives

$$
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle=0,
\qquad
\langle0|\phi^\dagger(x)\phi^\dagger(y)|0\rangle=0.
$$

The operator product must have total charge zero in order to have a nonzero vacuum expectation value.

## One-point functions

A one-point function is

$$
\langle0|\mathcal O(x)|0\rangle.
$$

Translation invariance makes it independent of $x$:

$$
\langle0|\mathcal O(x)|0\rangle=\langle0|\mathcal O(0)|0\rangle.
$$

If $\mathcal O$ carries nonzero charge under an unbroken internal symmetry, the one-point function must vanish. If $\mathcal O$ is a Lorentz vector, the one-point function must vanish in a Lorentz-invariant vacuum unless there is a fixed vector available. For a scalar operator, a nonzero one-point function is allowed:

$$
\langle0|\phi|0\rangle=v.
$$

In a theory with spontaneous symmetry breaking, such a value can serve as an order parameter. In ordinary perturbation theory around the symmetric free scalar vacuum, one usually has

$$
\langle0|\phi(x)|0\rangle=0.
$$

## Local operators are distributions

Field operators in QFT are not ordinary functions of spacetime. More accurately, they are operator-valued distributions. This means expressions like $\phi(x)$ are meaningful inside correlation functions only with the usual distributional care.

The product

$$
\phi(x)^2
$$

at the same spacetime point is generally singular before regularization or renormalization. This is why normal ordering, composite-operator renormalization, and operator product expansions exist.

A safe mental model is:

$$
\phi(f)=\int d^4x\,f(x)\phi(x)
$$

is an operator smeared against a test function $f$. The pointlike notation $\phi(x)$ is a powerful shorthand, but it is shorthand.

## Time-ordered versus unordered correlators

The unordered product

$$
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
$$

is a Wightman function. The time-ordered product

$$
\langle0|T\{\phi(x)\phi(y)\}|0\rangle
$$

is the Feynman two-point function. Perturbation theory, path integrals with ordinary Lorentzian sources, and Feynman diagrams naturally produce time-ordered Green functions.

This page deliberately starts with the unordered operator product because it shows the underlying Hilbert-space meaning. The next page adds time ordering because that is the object most often called a Green function in perturbative QFT.

## Common pitfalls

**Thinking the vacuum means “nothing happens.”** The vacuum has no particles relative to a chosen particle basis, but it can have nonzero fluctuations, condensates, and nontrivial correlation functions.

**Using the free vacuum when the physical vacuum is required.** In interacting QFT, the physical vacuum is not generally the bare Fock vacuum. Perturbation theory hides this using interaction-picture machinery and vacuum normalization factors.

**Confusing field labels with position operators.** In QFT, $x$ labels where an operator is inserted. It is not the eigenvalue of a particle-position operator.

**Forgetting operator order.** $\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle$ and $\langle0|B(y)A(x)|0\rangle$ are generally different distributions.

**Multiplying fields at the same point too casually.** Coincident local products are usually singular and require a definition.

**Calling every two-point function “the propagator.”** Wightman functions, commutator functions, retarded functions, and Feynman propagators are related but distinct.

## Research status

- **Established:** Vacuum expectation values, Heisenberg-picture operators, translation invariance, and free-field two-point functions are textbook-standard.
- **Subtle:** The physical vacuum in interacting QFT, operator-valued distributions, composite-operator renormalization, gauge-invariant operator algebras, and degenerate vacua require care.
- **Out of scope here:** Algebraic QFT, constructive definitions of interacting fields, thermal states, Schwinger–Keldysh correlators, and curved-spacetime vacua.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Prove translation invariance of a two-point function

Assume $P^\mu|0\rangle=0$ and

$$
\mathcal O(x)=e^{iP\cdot x}\mathcal O(0)e^{-iP\cdot x}.
$$

Show that

$$
\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle
$$

depends only on $x-y$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use the translation formula:

$$
A(x)=e^{iP\cdot x}A(0)e^{-iP\cdot x},
\qquad
B(y)=e^{iP\cdot y}B(0)e^{-iP\cdot y}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle
&=\langle0|e^{iP\cdot x}A(0)e^{-iP\cdot x}e^{iP\cdot y}B(0)e^{-iP\cdot y}|0\rangle \\
&=\langle0|A(0)e^{-iP\cdot(x-y)}B(0)|0\rangle,
\end{aligned}
$$

because $\langle0|e^{iP\cdot x}=\langle0|$ and $e^{-iP\cdot y}|0\rangle=|0\rangle$. The answer depends only on $x-y$.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Insert a complete set of states

Starting from

$$
\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle,
$$

insert $\mathbf 1=\sum_n|n\rangle\langle n|$ with $P^\mu|n\rangle=p_n^\mu|n\rangle$ and derive

$$
\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle
=\sum_n e^{-ip_n\cdot(x-y)}
\langle0|A(0)|n\rangle\langle n|B(0)|0\rangle.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Insert the identity between $A(x)$ and $B(y)$:

$$
\langle0|A(x)B(y)|0\rangle
=\sum_n\langle0|A(x)|n\rangle\langle n|B(y)|0\rangle.
$$

Now

$$
\langle0|A(x)|n\rangle
=e^{-ip_n\cdot x}\langle0|A(0)|n\rangle,
$$

and

$$
\langle n|B(y)|0\rangle
=e^{ip_n\cdot y}\langle n|B(0)|0\rangle.
$$

Multiplying gives the result.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Compute the free scalar Wightman function

Use the real scalar mode expansion and $a(\mathbf p)|0\rangle=0$ to show that

$$
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use

$$
\phi(x)=\int d\mu(p)
\left[a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}+a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}\right].
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
&=\int d\mu(p)d\mu(q)\,
 e^{-ip\cdot x}e^{iq\cdot y}
\langle0|a(\mathbf p)a^\dagger(\mathbf q)|0\rangle.
\end{aligned}
$$

All other terms vanish because $a$ annihilates the vacuum on the right or $a^\dagger$ annihilates the bra vacuum on the left. Using

$$
\langle0|a(\mathbf p)a^\dagger(\mathbf q)|0\rangle
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf q),
$$

and

$$
d\mu(p)=\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}},
$$

the $q$ integral collapses and gives

$$
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Use charge conservation

Let $Q|0\rangle=0$ and suppose $[Q,\mathcal O]=q\mathcal O$. Show that $\langle0|\mathcal O|0\rangle=0$ if $q\neq0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Take the vacuum expectation value of the commutator:

$$
\langle0|[Q,\mathcal O]|0\rangle
=\langle0|Q\mathcal O|0\rangle-\langle0|\mathcal O Q|0\rangle=0,
$$

because $Q|0\rangle=0$ and $\langle0|Q=0$. But the commutator also equals $q\mathcal O$, so

$$
q\langle0|\mathcal O|0\rangle=0.
$$

If $q\neq0$, then

$$
\langle0|\mathcal O|0\rangle=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5: Momentum conservation from translation invariance

Suppose an $n$-point function is invariant under $x_i\mapsto x_i+a$ for all $i$. Show that its Fourier transform contains

$$
(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}\!\left(\sum_i k_i\right)
$$

when all momenta are taken to flow into the correlator.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Let

$$
G(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=G(x_1+a,\ldots,x_n+a)
$$

for every constant four-vector $a$. Its Fourier transform is

$$
\widetilde G(k_1,\ldots,k_n)
=\int\prod_i d^4x_i\,
 e^{i\sum_i k_i\cdot x_i}G(x_1,\ldots,x_n).
$$

Change variables to one common position and relative separations, for example

$$
X=x_n,
\qquad
 r_i=x_i-x_n\quad (i=1,\ldots,n-1).
$$

Translation invariance says $G$ depends only on the $r_i$. The phase becomes

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n k_i\cdot x_i
=\left(\sum_{i=1}^n k_i\right)\cdot X
+\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}k_i\cdot r_i.
$$

The integral over $X$ gives

$$
\int d^4X\,e^{i(\sum_i k_i)\cdot X}
=(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}\!\left(\sum_i k_i\right).
$$

This is momentum conservation in the correlator.

</details>
