## Summary

Lorentz representations answer a deceptively simple question:

```txt
When an observer rotates or boosts their frame, how do the components of a field transform?
```

A scalar field has one component and transforms trivially. A vector field has four components and transforms by the vector representation. A Weyl spinor has two complex components and transforms under one of the two inequivalent spinor representations of the proper Lorentz group. A Dirac spinor combines both chiral spinor representations.

The most useful classification in four-dimensional QFT uses the double cover

$$
SL(2,\mathbb C)\to SO^+(1,3).
$$

The complexified Lorentz algebra splits into two commuting $su(2)$ algebras, so finite-dimensional irreducible representations are labeled by two spins:

$$
\boxed{(j_L,j_R),
\qquad
j_L,j_R=0,\frac12,1,\frac32,\ldots.}
$$

Important examples are

$$
(0,0)\quad\text{scalar},
\qquad
\left(\frac12,0\right)\quad\text{left Weyl spinor},
\qquad
\left(0,\frac12\right)\quad\text{right Weyl spinor},
$$

and

$$
\left(\frac12,\frac12\right)\quad\text{Lorentz vector}.
$$

This page classifies field representations. It does **not** replace the classification of physical one-particle states by Poincaré little groups. That distinction is one of the load-bearing beams of QFT: local fields transform in finite-dimensional, generally nonunitary Lorentz representations; particle states transform in unitary representations of the Poincaré group.

Weinberg derives fields by asking how Lorentz covariance, locality, and particle representations fit together (Weinberg 1995, Vol. I, ch. 5). Srednicki's spinor chapters use the same two-component representation theory to build Weyl, Dirac, and Majorana fields (Srednicki 2007, §§33–40). Coleman's chapters on Lorentz representations and the Dirac equation give a compact route from rotations and boosts to spinor fields and bilinears (Coleman 2019, chs. 18–21).

## Two representation problems

There are two related but different representation problems in relativistic QFT.

The first is the **particle-state problem**. Physical one-particle states form unitary representations of the Poincaré group. Massive particles are classified by the little group $SO(3)$ and have spin $s$. Massless finite-helicity particles are classified by the massless little group, whose finite-helicity representations are labeled by helicity $\lambda$.

The second is the **local-field problem**. A local field $\Phi_A(x)$ has components labeled by $A$. Under a Lorentz transformation,

$$
U(\Lambda)\Phi_A(x)U(\Lambda)^{-1}
=D(\Lambda^{-1})_A{}^B\Phi_B(\Lambda x),
$$

where $D(\Lambda)$ is a finite-dimensional representation acting on field components.

These are not the same representation. A Dirac field has four complex components, but a massive spin-one-half particle has two spin states and an antiparticle has two more. A vector potential $A_\mu$ has four components, but a photon has two helicities. A Proca field has four components with one constraint, corresponding to three massive spin-one polarizations.

:::tip[Field components are not automatically degrees of freedom]
The number of components in a Lorentz field representation is a bookkeeping fact. The number of physical polarizations is a Hilbert-space fact, after equations of motion, constraints, gauge redundancy, and positivity are taken into account.
:::

## Lorentz algebra as two rotation algebras

Let $J_i$ be rotation generators and $K_i$ boost generators. The Lorentz algebra is

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k,
\qquad
[J_i,K_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}K_k,
\qquad
[K_i,K_j]=-i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k.
$$

The minus sign in $[K,K]$ reflects the noncompactness of the Lorentz group. Now define

$$
\boxed{
A_i=\frac12(J_i+iK_i),
\qquad
B_i=\frac12(J_i-iK_i).
}
$$

Then

$$
[A_i,A_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}A_k,
\qquad
[B_i,B_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}B_k,
\qquad
[A_i,B_j]=0.
$$

So the complexified Lorentz algebra is

$$
\boxed{
\mathfrak{so}(1,3)_\mathbb C
\simeq
\mathfrak{su}(2)_L\oplus\mathfrak{su}(2)_R.
}
$$

The finite-dimensional irreducible representations are therefore labeled by two ordinary spin labels:

$$
(j_L,j_R).
$$

The dimension is

$$
\boxed{
\dim(j_L,j_R)=(2j_L+1)(2j_R+1).
}
$$

The ordinary spatial rotation subgroup is the diagonal subgroup generated by

$$
J_i=A_i+B_i.
$$

Therefore a Lorentz representation $(j_L,j_R)$ decomposes under rotations as

$$
j=|j_L-j_R|,
|j_L-j_R|+1,
\ldots,
j_L+j_R.
$$

This last formula explains a common surprise: a four-vector $(\frac12,\frac12)$ decomposes under rotations as spin $0\oplus1$. Its time component is rotationally scalar and its spatial components form a rotational vector.

## Basic finite-dimensional representations

The common Lorentz representations used in four-dimensional QFT are:

| Representation | Dimension | Field type | Rotation content |
|---:|---:|---|---|
| $(0,0)$ | $1$ | scalar | $j=0$ |
| $(\frac12,0)$ | $2$ | left-handed Weyl spinor | $j=\frac12$ |
| $(0,\frac12)$ | $2$ | right-handed Weyl spinor | $j=\frac12$ |
| $(\frac12,\frac12)$ | $4$ | Lorentz vector | $j=0\oplus1$ |
| $(1,0)$ | $3$ | self-dual antisymmetric tensor | $j=1$ |
| $(0,1)$ | $3$ | anti-self-dual antisymmetric tensor | $j=1$ |
| $(1,1)$ | $9$ | symmetric traceless rank-two tensor | $j=0\oplus1\oplus2$ |

A Dirac spinor is reducible as a Lorentz representation:

$$
\boxed{
\Psi_D\in
\left(\frac12,0\right)\oplus
\left(0,\frac12\right).
}
$$

A Majorana spinor uses the same Lorentz representation as a Dirac spinor, but with a reality condition relating the two chiral pieces when the internal representation allows it.

## Scalars

A scalar field transforms as

$$
U(\Lambda)\phi(x)U(\Lambda)^{-1}=\phi(\Lambda x)
$$

in the active convention used here. Infinitesimally, the spin part of the Lorentz generator vanishes:

$$
\Sigma^{\mu\nu}=0.
$$

This does not mean scalar particles are “nonrelativistic” or “featureless.” It means the field component itself carries no Lorentz index or spinor index. A scalar particle can still have mass, internal charge, interactions, and nontrivial scattering.

The basic scalar kinetic term is Lorentz invariant because

$$
\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi
$$

contracts the vector index on the derivative. The field representation is trivial; the derivative supplies the vector transformation.

## Vectors

A contravariant vector field transforms with the Lorentz matrix:

$$
V^\mu(x)\mapsto
\Lambda^\mu{}_{\nu}V^\nu(\Lambda^{-1}x)
$$

in a passive convention, or equivalently with the corresponding inverse matrix in the active operator convention. The representation is

$$
\left(\frac12,\frac12\right).
$$

The vector representation has four components, but under spatial rotations it splits into

$$
\left(\frac12,\frac12\right)
\downarrow_{SO(3)}
0\oplus1.
$$

This is why $A^0$ is rotationally scalar while $\mathbf A$ is rotationally vectorial. It is also why a Lorentz vector is not automatically a spin-one particle. Dynamics and constraints decide which components are physical.

For a vector field $A_\mu$, the antisymmetric field strength

$$
F_{\mu\nu}=\partial_\mu A_\nu-\partial_\nu A_\mu
$$

transforms as an antisymmetric rank-two tensor. In four dimensions this tensor decomposes as

$$
\boxed{
F_{\mu\nu}:
\quad
(1,0)\oplus(0,1).
}
$$

The two pieces may be written as self-dual and anti-self-dual combinations,

$$
F^{\pm}_{\mu\nu}
=\frac12\left(F_{\mu\nu}\mp i\widetilde F_{\mu\nu}\right),
$$

with the precise sign tied to the convention for $\epsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$ and the Hodge dual.

## Weyl spinors

The two inequivalent fundamental spinor representations are

$$
\left(\frac12,0\right),
\qquad
\left(0,\frac12\right).
$$

In two-component notation, a left-handed Weyl spinor is often written

$$
\chi_\alpha,
\qquad
\alpha=1,2,
$$

and a right-handed Weyl spinor is often written with a dotted index,

$$
\xi^{\dagger\dot\alpha},
\qquad
\dot\alpha=1,2.
$$

Hermitian conjugation exchanges the two Lorentz representations:

$$
\left(\frac12,0\right)^*
\simeq
\left(0,\frac12\right).
$$

This is not an internal-symmetry statement. It is a spacetime representation statement. Internal gauge charges add another layer: a Weyl field may transform under both the Lorentz group and an internal group.

The invariant tensor $\epsilon^{\alpha\beta}$ lets two left-handed spinors form a Lorentz scalar:

$$
\xi\chi
=\xi^\alpha\chi_\alpha.
$$

A left-right pair instead forms a vector:

$$
\chi^\dagger\bar\sigma^\mu\chi.
$$

This is the two-component origin of the vector current $\overline\psi\gamma^\mu\psi$ in four-component notation.

## Dirac and Majorana spinors

A Dirac spinor packages a left-handed and a right-handed Weyl representation:

$$
\Psi_D=
\begin{pmatrix}
\chi_\alpha\\
\xi^{\dagger\dot\alpha}
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\Psi_D\in
\left(\frac12,0\right)\oplus
\left(0,\frac12\right).
$$

The gamma matrices are intertwining objects between the chiral pieces. The Lorentz generators in the Dirac representation are

$$
\boxed{
\Sigma^{\mu\nu}
=\frac{i}{4}[\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu].
}
$$

They satisfy the Lorentz algebra on spinor components.

A Majorana spinor is not a new Lorentz representation. It is a reality condition on the same reducible spinor representation:

$$
\Psi_M^c=\Psi_M.
$$

Such a condition is compatible with Lorentz symmetry in four dimensions, but internal symmetries may forbid it. A field with an unbroken U(1) charge cannot be Majorana, because charge conjugation reverses that charge.

## Tensor products

Because the Lorentz algebra splits into two $su(2)$ algebras, tensor products are just two independent angular-momentum tensor products:

$$
(j_L,j_R)\otimes(k_L,k_R)
=
\bigoplus_{\ell_L=|j_L-k_L|}^{j_L+k_L}
\bigoplus_{\ell_R=|j_R-k_R|}^{j_R+k_R}
(\ell_L,\ell_R).
$$

For example,

$$
\left(\frac12,0\right)\otimes
\left(0,\frac12\right)
=
\left(\frac12,\frac12\right),
$$

which is the vector representation. This is why a left-right spinor bilinear can carry a vector index.

Two left-handed Weyl spinors give

$$
\left(\frac12,0\right)\otimes
\left(\frac12,0\right)
=
(0,0)\oplus(1,0).
$$

The antisymmetric spinor contraction is the scalar; the symmetric product is the self-dual tensor representation. Similarly,

$$
\left(0,\frac12\right)\otimes
\left(0,\frac12\right)
=
(0,0)\oplus(0,1).
$$

This is the representation-theoretic reason the electromagnetic field strength can be written in spinor notation as a pair of symmetric two-spinors.

## Building Lorentz-invariant terms

A local Lagrangian density must transform as a Lorentz scalar, up to a total derivative. In representation language, every term must contain an overall $(0,0)$.

Examples:

$$
\partial_\mu\phi\partial^\mu\phi
$$

is a scalar because the two vector indices are contracted.

$$
\overline\psi\psi
$$

is a scalar because the left-right spinor pieces are paired appropriately.

$$
\overline\psi\gamma^\mu\psi\,A_\mu
$$

is a scalar because the spinor bilinear transforms as a vector and is contracted with a vector field.

$$
F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}
$$

is a scalar, while

$$
F_{\mu\nu}\widetilde F^{\mu\nu}
$$

is a pseudoscalar: invariant under proper Lorentz transformations but odd under parity, depending on convention.

A useful workflow is:

```txt
choose field representations
choose derivatives and conjugates
form tensor products
keep only Lorentz scalars
then impose internal symmetries, locality, dimensions, and gauge redundancy
```

## Parity and chirality

The proper orthochronous Lorentz group does not contain parity. Parity reverses spatial orientation and exchanges the two chiral Lorentz representations:

$$
\left(\frac12,0\right)
\longleftrightarrow
\left(0,\frac12\right).
$$

Therefore a theory of a single isolated Weyl field is not parity invariant by itself. A parity-invariant spinor theory must contain fields that can be paired under this exchange.

This is why Dirac spinors are natural in parity-invariant theories and Weyl spinors are natural in chiral gauge theories. The Standard Model is chiral: left- and right-handed fermions do not transform in the same way under the electroweak gauge group.

## Finite-dimensional does not mean unitary

The Lorentz group is noncompact. Apart from the trivial representation, its finite-dimensional representations are not unitary. This is not a problem for local fields: field components are not by themselves a Hilbert-space basis with positive-definite norm.

The Hilbert space of physical states must carry a unitary representation of the Poincaré group. Those representations are typically infinite-dimensional because momenta vary continuously. The finite-dimensional Lorentz representation of a field tells you how its components transform at a point; the unitary Poincaré representation of states tells you how physical particles transform.

This distinction is why the phrase “a spinor transforms nonunitarily under boosts” is not a contradiction. The spinor **field components** transform by a finite-dimensional nonunitary matrix, while the full quantum theory acts unitarily on states.

## Common pitfalls

**Calling a vector field a spin-one particle.** A Lorentz vector field is a field representation. A physical spin-one particle is a state representation. Maxwell and Proca fields show why this difference matters.

**Forgetting the double cover.** Spinors are not representations of $SO^+(1,3)$ itself but of its double cover $SL(2,\mathbb C)$. A $2\pi$ rotation acts as $-1$ on spinors.

**Confusing chirality with helicity.** Chirality labels Lorentz representations. Helicity labels the spin projection of a particle state along its momentum. For massless particles these are tightly related; for massive particles they are not the same.

**Assuming parity is automatic.** The connected Lorentz group allows a single Weyl representation. Parity exchanges left and right representations and may be absent.

**Treating representation labels as dynamics.** The label $(j_L,j_R)$ tells you how components transform. It does not determine the mass, interactions, gauge charges, or whether a field is constrained.

## Research status

- **Established:** The classification of finite-dimensional Lorentz representations by $(j_L,j_R)$ and the construction of scalars, vectors, tensors, Weyl spinors, and Dirac spinors are textbook-standard.
- **Subtle:** The relation between field components and physical degrees of freedom depends on constraints, gauge redundancy, equations of motion, and the Hilbert-space inner product.
- **Beyond this page:** In curved spacetime, the relevant local structure is the tangent-space Lorentz group and spin bundle. In supersymmetry, fields are assembled into supermultiplets that extend Lorentz representation theory with fermionic symmetry generators.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Two commuting rotation algebras

Starting from

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k,
\qquad
[J_i,K_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}K_k,
\qquad
[K_i,K_j]=-i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k,
$$

show that

$$
A_i=\frac12(J_i+iK_i),
\qquad
B_i=\frac12(J_i-iK_i)
$$

satisfy

$$
[A_i,A_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}A_k,
\qquad
[B_i,B_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}B_k,
\qquad
[A_i,B_j]=0.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
[A_i,A_j]
=\frac14[J_i+iK_i,J_j+iK_j].
$$

Expanding,

$$
[A_i,A_j]
=\frac14\left(
[J_i,J_j]+i[J_i,K_j]+i[K_i,J_j]-[K_i,K_j]
\right).
$$

Use the Lorentz algebra:

$$
[J_i,J_j]=i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k,
$$

$$
i[J_i,K_j]+i[K_i,J_j]
=i(i\epsilon_{ijk}K_k)+i(i\epsilon_{ijk}K_k)
=-2\epsilon_{ijk}K_k,
$$

and

$$
-[K_i,K_j]=+i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k.
$$

Thus

$$
[A_i,A_j]
=\frac12 i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k-
\frac12\epsilon_{ijk}K_k
=i\epsilon_{ijk}\frac12(J_k+iK_k)
=i\epsilon_{ijk}A_k.
$$

The $B$ computation is the complex conjugate. For the mixed commutator,

$$
[A_i,B_j]
=\frac14[J_i+iK_i,J_j-iK_j],
$$

and the $J$ and $K$ terms cancel, giving zero.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Dimension of a Lorentz representation

Find the dimension of the Lorentz representation $(1,\frac12)$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The dimension is

$$
(2j_L+1)(2j_R+1).
$$

For $(1,\frac12)$,

$$
(2\cdot1+1)(2\cdot\tfrac12+1)=3\cdot2=6.
$$

So

$$
\boxed{\dim(1,\tfrac12)=6.}
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Rotation content of a vector

Use the diagonal rotation subgroup to show that

$$
\left(\frac12,\frac12\right)
$$

contains rotational spins $0$ and $1$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Under rotations, the total spin comes from adding $j_L=\frac12$ and $j_R=\frac12$:

$$
j=|j_L-j_R|,\ldots,j_L+j_R.
$$

Therefore

$$
j=0,1.
$$

So

$$
\boxed{
\left(\frac12,\frac12\right)\downarrow_{SO(3)}=0\oplus1.
}
$$

This matches the fact that a four-vector has one time component and three spatial components under rotations.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Product of two left Weyl spinors

Decompose

$$
\left(\frac12,0\right)\otimes
\left(\frac12,0\right)
$$

into irreducible Lorentz representations.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use angular-momentum addition independently in the left and right factors. For the left factor,

$$
\frac12\otimes\frac12=0\oplus1.
$$

For the right factor,

$$
0\otimes0=0.
$$

Thus

$$
\boxed{
\left(\frac12,0\right)\otimes
\left(\frac12,0\right)
=(0,0)\oplus(1,0).
}
$$

The antisymmetric spinor contraction gives the scalar; the symmetric part gives the $(1,0)$ representation.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Dirac spinor is reducible

Explain why a Dirac spinor is reducible as a representation of the connected Lorentz group.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A Dirac spinor contains a left-handed and a right-handed Weyl spinor:

$$
\Psi_D\in
\left(\frac12,0\right)\oplus
\left(0,\frac12\right).
$$

The two pieces are separately irreducible under the connected Lorentz group. Since the representation is a direct sum of two invariant subspaces, it is reducible.

Parity exchanges the two pieces, but parity is not part of the connected Lorentz group. Thus the reducibility statement is a statement about $SO^+(1,3)$ or its double cover $SL(2,\mathbb C)$.

</details>

### Exercise 6: Why finite-dimensional Lorentz reps are not unitary

Use the boost-boost commutator

$$
[K_i,K_j]=-i\epsilon_{ijk}J_k
$$

to explain why finite-dimensional nontrivial Lorentz representations cannot all be unitary with Hermitian generators on a positive-definite finite-dimensional space.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For a unitary finite-dimensional representation, the generators $J_i$ and $K_i$ would be Hermitian matrices. Define

$$
A_i=\frac12(J_i+iK_i),
\qquad
B_i=\frac12(J_i-iK_i).
$$

If $J_i$ and $K_i$ are Hermitian, then

$$
A_i^\dagger=B_i.
$$

But $A_i$ and $B_i$ generate two independent commuting $su(2)$ algebras. A nontrivial finite-dimensional Lorentz representation generally has independent labels $(j_L,j_R)$. Unitarity would force the boost generators to behave like compact generators, but the Lorentz group is noncompact. Equivalently, noncompact groups have no nontrivial finite-dimensional unitary representations.

The physical resolution is that finite-dimensional Lorentz representations act on local field components, not directly as the full Hilbert-space representation of particle states. The Hilbert-space representation of the connected Poincaré group is unitary and typically infinite-dimensional.

</details>
