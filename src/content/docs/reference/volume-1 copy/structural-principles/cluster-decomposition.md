## Summary

**Cluster decomposition** is the statement that sufficiently distant experiments become independent. If one set of local operations is performed in a region $A$ and another set is performed in a faraway region $B$, then the joint answer should factorize:

$$
\boxed{
\langle O_A O_B\rangle
\longrightarrow
\langle O_A\rangle\langle O_B\rangle
\qquad \text{as the separation between }A\text{ and }B\text{ goes to infinity.}
}
$$

Equivalently, the connected part vanishes:

$$
\boxed{\langle O_A O_B\rangle_{\mathrm c}\longrightarrow0.}
$$

This is not just a nicety. Without it, predictions for an experiment in one laboratory could depend on irrelevant details of another experiment arbitrarily far away. Weinberg treats this as one of the fundamental physical principles behind QFT: distant experiments should have unrelated results, and the connected S-matrix element should contain only one overall momentum-conservation delta function (Weinberg 1995, Vol. I, ch. 4). In the path-integral and diagrammatic language, the same idea says that disconnected diagrams exponentiate and connected diagrams are generated by $W[J]=-i\log Z[J]$ (Coleman 2019, sections 8.4, 13.2, and 32.1; Srednicki 2007, sections 8–9).

The principle has several equivalent-looking forms, but they are not identical in detail:

| Language | Cluster statement |
|---|---|
| Local observables | connected correlators vanish at large spacelike separation |
| Source functionals | $W[J]=-i\log Z[J]$ generates the connected pieces |
| Diagrams | disconnected diagrams are products of connected components |
| Scattering | widely separated experiments have factorized S-matrix elements |
| Momentum space | connected amplitudes have one overall conservation delta function |

Cluster decomposition sits next to [Locality and Microcausality](/foundations/structural-principles/locality-and-microcausality/), [Unitarity](/foundations/structural-principles/unitarity/), and the [Spectral Condition](/foundations/structural-principles/spectral-condition/) as one of the quiet assumptions that make QFT predictive.

:::note[Conventions]
For local operators, translations act as

$$
O(x)=e^{iP\cdot x}O(0)e^{-iP\cdot x}.
$$

For scattering amplitudes, qft.org uses

$$
\langle \beta|T|\alpha\rangle
=(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_\beta-P_\alpha)\mathcal M_{\beta\alpha},
\qquad
S=1+iT.
$$

The phrase “one delta function” below means one overall four-momentum-conservation delta function for a connected scattering amplitude. Products of disconnected components carry one such delta function per component.
:::

:::note[Assumptions]
The cleanest cluster statements assume a translation-invariant vacuum, a positive-norm physical Hilbert space, and a choice of pure vacuum. A mass gap gives exponential clustering for many Euclidean or equal-time correlators, but cluster decomposition can still hold with massless particles through slower power-law decay. Gauge theories, charged sectors, degenerate vacua, finite volume, thermal states, and topological sectors require extra care.
:::

## The local-operator statement

Let $A$ and $B$ be products of local operators in two bounded spacetime regions. Translate $A$ by a large spacelike vector $a$:

$$
A(a)=e^{iP\cdot a}A e^{-iP\cdot a}.
$$

A standard cluster statement is

$$
\boxed{
\lim_{\lambda\to\infty}
\langle0|A(\lambda a)B|0\rangle
=
\langle0|A|0\rangle\langle0|B|0\rangle
}
$$

for spacelike $a$, under the usual structural assumptions. Equivalently,

$$
\lim_{\lambda\to\infty}
\langle0|A(\lambda a)B|0\rangle_{\mathrm c}=0.
$$

For a single scalar operator $O$, this says

$$
\langle O(x)O(y)\rangle
\longrightarrow
\langle O\rangle^2
\qquad \text{as } |\mathbf x-\mathbf y|\to\infty
$$

at equal time, whenever the large-distance limit is meaningful. The connected two-point function is

$$
\langle O(x)O(y)\rangle_{\mathrm c}
=
\langle O(x)O(y)\rangle-\langle O\rangle^2,
$$

so clustering is the statement that this connected part vanishes.

For many operators, the same idea is expressed by cumulants. If the insertions split into two distant groups,

$$
\{x_1,\ldots,x_m\}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\{y_1,\ldots,y_n\},
$$

then

$$
\boxed{
\langle O_1(x_1)\cdots O_m(x_m)B_1(y_1)\cdots B_n(y_n)\rangle_{\mathrm c}
\longrightarrow0
}
$$

when all $x_i$ are translated far away from all $y_j$ while the separations within each group are held fixed.

## Why connected functions are the natural variables

The full correlator is not expected to vanish at large separation. If an operator has a vacuum expectation value, then even two completely independent measurements have a nonzero product contribution:

$$
\langle O(x)O(y)\rangle
\longrightarrow
\langle O\rangle^2.
$$

The connected correlator removes that trivial product. That is why connected functions, not full functions, are the right objects for diagnosing genuine long-distance communication between parts of a system.

The source-functional statement is especially clean. With

$$
Z[J]=\left\langle T\exp\left(i\int d^4x\,J(x)\phi(x)\right)\right\rangle,
\qquad
Z[0]=1,
$$

we define

$$
Z[J]=e^{iW[J]}.
$$

Then $Z[J]$ generates all time-ordered correlators, while $W[J]$ generates connected correlators:

$$
G_n^{\mathrm c}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=\left.\frac{1}{i^{n-1}}\frac{\delta^n W[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}\right|_{J=0}.
$$

So the logarithm is not a formal trick. It is the operation that throws away the pieces that are just independent products of smaller experiments.

## The S-matrix statement

Cluster decomposition has a sharp scattering version. Suppose one group of incoming wave packets undergoes a process

$$
\alpha_A\to\beta_A
$$

in laboratory $A$, and another group undergoes

$$
\alpha_B\to\beta_B
$$

in a distant laboratory $B$. As the spatial separation between the two experiments becomes large, the full S-matrix element should factorize:

$$
\boxed{
S_{\beta_A+\beta_B,\,\alpha_A+\alpha_B}
\longrightarrow
S_{\beta_A\alpha_A}S_{\beta_B\alpha_B}
}
$$

up to the conventional signs and permutations required for identical fermions or bosons.

The connected S-matrix is defined recursively by subtracting all products of smaller pieces:

$$
S_{\beta\alpha}
=\sum_{\text{partitions}}(\pm)
\prod_r S^{\mathrm c}_{\beta_r\alpha_r}.
$$

Then cluster decomposition says

$$
S^{\mathrm c}_{\beta\alpha}\to0
$$

when the external wave packets split into two or more widely separated groups.

This is the scattering analogue of connected correlators. Full S-matrix elements include disconnected products; connected S-matrix elements are the parts that cannot be explained as several independent collisions.

## The momentum-space test

Plane waves are delocalized, so the physical clustering statement is clearest for wave packets. But momentum space gives a powerful diagnostic.

A connected scattering contribution has the form

$$
\langle\beta|T^{\mathrm c}|\alpha\rangle
=(2\pi)^4\delta^{(4)}(P_\beta-P_\alpha)\mathcal M^{\mathrm c}_{\beta\alpha}.
$$

There is only one overall conservation delta function. A disconnected product of two independent connected processes instead carries two conservation delta functions:

$$
\delta^{(4)}(P_{\beta_A}-P_{\alpha_A})
\delta^{(4)}(P_{\beta_B}-P_{\alpha_B}).
$$

That is exactly what one expects for two independent experiments. Each experiment conserves its own four-momentum.

Weinberg’s momentum-space formulation is essentially this: the connected part of the S matrix should not contain additional delta functions beyond the single overall conservation delta function. Extra delta functions in a supposedly connected amplitude would mean that some subset of particles conserves momentum independently. After Fourier transformation, that independence prevents the amplitude from decaying when the corresponding subset is moved far away.

Poles and branch cuts are different. A massless exchanged particle can give a connected amplitude a pole such as

$$
\frac{1}{q^2+i\epsilon},
$$

which produces long-range power-law behavior in position space. That is allowed. Cluster decomposition forbids delta-function-strength correlations tying distant experiments together, not every slow tail.

## Why local interactions produce clustering

Local QFT interactions have the schematic Hamiltonian form

$$
H_{\rm int}(t)=\int d^3x\,\mathcal H_{\rm int}(t,\mathbf x),
$$

where $\mathcal H_{\rm int}$ is built from fields and their derivatives at the same spacetime point. Perturbatively, each local vertex conserves momentum at that vertex. A connected diagram has internal momentum integrations and one leftover overall conservation delta function. A disconnected diagram has one such delta function for each disconnected component.

This is why locality and cluster decomposition are so tightly related in practical QFT. Local interaction densities generate connected diagrams with the right momentum-space structure. Nonlocal couplings with a nondecaying kernel can correlate faraway regions directly. For example,

$$
\int d^3x\,d^3y\,O_1(t,\mathbf x)K(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)O_2(t,\mathbf y)
$$

is harmless at long distance only if $K$ decays sufficiently fast, is local, or is understood as part of a controlled effective expansion.

This is one reason local field theory is so powerful. It is not merely a convenient notation for particles. It is machinery that makes independent distant experiments natural.

## Relation to mass gaps

A mass gap usually strengthens cluster decomposition into exponential decay. In Euclidean signature, a massive scalar propagator in four dimensions behaves at large separation $R$ as

$$
D_E(R;m)=\frac{m}{4\pi^2R}K_1(mR)
\sim
\frac{\sqrt m}{(2\pi)^{3/2}R^{3/2}}e^{-mR}.
$$

In an interacting theory with a mass gap $m_{\rm gap}$, connected correlators of suitable local operators often decay like

$$
\langle O(x)O(0)\rangle_{\mathrm c}
\sim e^{-m_{\rm gap}|x|}
\times \text{powers of } |x|.
$$

The precise prefactor depends on the lightest state that couples to $O$.

But cluster decomposition does **not** require a mass gap. Massless fields produce power-law decay. In a four-dimensional conformal field theory, for example,

$$
\langle O(x)O(0)\rangle
\propto \frac{1}{|x|^{2\Delta}}
$$

for a scalar primary with scaling dimension $\Delta>0$. This still tends to zero at large Euclidean separation if the one-point function vanishes. The decay is slow, but it is still clustering.

So the hierarchy is:

$$
\text{mass gap}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\text{often exponential clustering},
$$

while

$$
\text{cluster decomposition}\quad\not\Rightarrow\quad\text{mass gap}.
$$

## Relation to locality

Cluster decomposition and microcausality are different principles.

Microcausality says that spacelike separated local observables commute:

$$
[O_A(x),O_B(y)]=0
\qquad (x-y)^2<0.
$$

It is a **no-signaling** condition. It says operations in one spacelike region cannot change measurement outcomes in another spacelike region.

Cluster decomposition says connected correlations vanish when the separation becomes large:

$$
\langle O_A(x)O_B(y)\rangle_{\mathrm c}\to0.
$$

It is an **independence-at-long-distance** condition. It says faraway experiments do not remain tied together by a connected vacuum correlation of finite strength.

A theory can have nonzero spacelike correlations without violating microcausality. The vacuum of a free scalar field has nonzero spacelike two-point functions, but the commutator vanishes outside the light cone. Cluster decomposition asks what happens to those correlations at very large separation.

```txt
Locality controls signaling.
Cluster decomposition controls far-distance independence.
```

## Vacuum choice and spontaneous symmetry breaking

Cluster decomposition is usually a statement about a **pure vacuum**. This matters in theories with degenerate vacua.

Consider an order parameter $O$ with two possible vacua,

$$
\langle O\rangle_+=+v,
\qquad
\langle O\rangle_-=-v.
$$

In either chosen vacuum,

$$
\langle O(x)O(y)\rangle_\pm
\longrightarrow v^2,
$$

and

$$
\langle O(x)O(y)\rangle_{\mathrm c,\pm}
=\langle O(x)O(y)\rangle_\pm-\langle O\rangle_\pm^2
\longrightarrow0.
$$

So clustering holds in each pure broken vacuum.

Now average the two vacua with equal weight. Then

$$
\langle O\rangle_{\rm mix}=0,
$$

but

$$
\langle O(x)O(y)\rangle_{\rm mix}\longrightarrow v^2.
$$

The connected function in the mixed state does not vanish. This is a standard way cluster decomposition fails: the state is not an extremal vacuum but a statistical mixture, or a formal superposition, of distinct phases.

That is the operational reason physicists usually select one vacuum after spontaneous symmetry breaking. The selected vacuum may not display the full symmetry, but it has the correct long-distance independence properties.

## Gauge theories and charged sectors

Gauge theories add two layers of caution.

First, gauge-variant fields are not physical local observables. Cluster decomposition should be imposed on gauge-invariant local operators, or on the physical Hilbert space after gauge fixing and BRST projection. Statements involving bare charged fields such as $\psi(x)$ in QED are gauge-choice dependent unless the operators are appropriately dressed.

Second, massless gauge fields create long-range fields and infrared subtleties. Correlators of neutral gauge-invariant local operators can still cluster, but charged scattering amplitudes require soft-photon care. The naive S matrix between states containing a fixed finite number of charged particles is not the whole physical story in QED; inclusive observables and dressed asymptotic states are needed.

This does not make cluster decomposition dispensable. It means the relevant “distant experiments” must be expressed in terms of physical observables and physically admissible states.

## Cluster decomposition is not exact factorization of the vacuum

The QFT vacuum is entangled. Even in a free theory, degrees of freedom in separated regions are not literally in a product state. Cluster decomposition does not say

$$
|0\rangle \stackrel{?}{=} |0\rangle_A\otimes |0\rangle_B.
$$

That statement is not even cleanly formulated in continuum QFT without regulating and specifying algebras of regions.

Cluster decomposition says something weaker and more physical: connected correlations of local observables vanish at large separation. There can be short-distance entanglement, algebraic subtleties, and even nonzero correlations at finite distance; none of that contradicts clustering.

## Euclidean viewpoint

In Euclidean QFT and statistical mechanics, cluster decomposition becomes the familiar statement that connected correlation functions decay at large spatial separation. In a massive Euclidean theory,

$$
\langle O(x)O(0)\rangle_{\mathrm c}
\sim e^{-|x|/\xi},
$$

where $\xi$ is the correlation length. Near a critical point, $\xi\to\infty$ and the decay becomes a power law. In a conformal field theory, power-law clustering replaces exponential clustering.

This is the bridge to [Reflection Positivity](/foundations/structural-principles/reflection-positivity/). Euclidean correlators must both cluster correctly and satisfy positivity conditions if they are to reconstruct a unitary Lorentzian QFT.

## Practical diagnostic checklist

When checking whether a proposed theory or state has the right cluster behavior, ask:

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Did you subtract disconnected products? | Full correlators need not vanish; connected ones should. |
| Are you in a pure vacuum? | Mixed phases can violate clustering. |
| Are there massless particles? | Decay may be power-law rather than exponential. |
| Are your operators gauge invariant? | Gauge-variant correlators are not physical tests. |
| Are there extra momentum delta functions? | Extra deltas signal disconnected conservation laws. |
| Are you using wave packets for scattering? | Plane waves obscure spatial separation. |
| Is the theory in finite volume? | True infinite-distance limits require care. |

## Common pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: “A nonzero spacelike correlator violates locality.”**  
No. Locality concerns commutators and causal response. Cluster decomposition concerns the large-distance limit of connected correlations.

**Pitfall 2: “Cluster decomposition means all distant correlations are exactly zero.”**  
No. It is an asymptotic statement. Massive theories often decay exponentially; massless theories often decay as powers.

**Pitfall 3: “A theory with spontaneous symmetry breaking violates cluster decomposition because the order parameter two-point function approaches a constant.”**  
Not in a chosen pure vacuum. The connected correlator subtracts $\langle O\rangle^2$ and can still vanish.

**Pitfall 4: “Disconnected diagrams are mistakes.”**  
No. They are real contributions to full correlators or full S-matrix elements. They are removed only when asking for connected data.

**Pitfall 5: “One conservation delta function per connected component is just notation.”**  
It is more than notation. It is the momentum-space fingerprint of independent clusters.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Two-point clustering with a nonzero one-point function

Let $O$ be a scalar operator with

$$
\langle O\rangle=v.
$$

Suppose

$$
\lim_{R\to\infty}\langle O(R)O(0)\rangle=v^2.
$$

Show that the connected two-point function clusters.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The connected two-point function is

$$
\langle O(R)O(0)\rangle_{\mathrm c}
=\langle O(R)O(0)\rangle-\langle O(R)\rangle\langle O(0)\rangle.
$$

Translation invariance gives

$$
\langle O(R)\rangle=\langle O(0)\rangle=v.
$$

Therefore

$$
\lim_{R\to\infty}\langle O(R)O(0)\rangle_{\mathrm c}
=v^2-v^2=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: A mixed state that fails clustering

Consider two pure vacua with

$$
\langle O\rangle_+=v,
\qquad
\langle O\rangle_-=-v,
$$

and suppose each pure vacuum clusters. Define the mixed state average

$$
\langle X\rangle_{\rm mix}=\frac12\langle X\rangle_++\frac12\langle X\rangle_-.
$$

Show that the mixed state violates cluster decomposition for $O$ if $v\neq0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The one-point function in the mixed state is

$$
\langle O\rangle_{\rm mix}
=\frac12 v+\frac12(-v)=0.
$$

In each pure vacuum,

$$
\lim_{R\to\infty}\langle O(R)O(0)\rangle_\pm=v^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\lim_{R\to\infty}\langle O(R)O(0)\rangle_{\rm mix}=v^2.
$$

The connected correlator in the mixed state is therefore

$$
\lim_{R\to\infty}\langle O(R)O(0)\rangle_{\mathrm c,\rm mix}
=v^2-0=v^2,
$$

which does not vanish. The mixed state is not an extremal vacuum.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Connected four-point factorization

Assume $\langle\phi\rangle=0$. The full four-point function decomposes as

$$
\begin{aligned}
G_4(1,2,3,4)=&\,G_4^{\mathrm c}(1,2,3,4)\\
&+G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4)
+G_2(1,3)G_2(2,4)
+G_2(1,4)G_2(2,3).
\end{aligned}
$$

Translate points $1,2$ far away from points $3,4$, while keeping the separation between $1,2$ fixed and the separation between $3,4$ fixed. Assuming two-point clustering, what should happen to $G_4^{\mathrm c}$ if the full four-point function factorizes into $G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4)$?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

At large separation between the two pairs, two-point clustering gives

$$
G_2(1,3),\ G_2(1,4),\ G_2(2,3),\ G_2(2,4)\to0
$$

if the one-point function vanishes. The four-point decomposition becomes

$$
G_4(1,2,3,4)\to
G_4^{\mathrm c}(1,2,3,4)+G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4).
$$

If the full four-point function factorizes into

$$
G_2(1,2)G_2(3,4),
$$

then the remaining connected part must vanish:

$$
G_4^{\mathrm c}(1,2,3,4)\to0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Extra delta functions and non-clustering

Consider a momentum-space contribution to a supposedly connected two-cluster amplitude that contains an extra factor

$$
\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p'_A-\mathbf p_A)
$$

in addition to the overall momentum-conservation delta function. Explain why this is suspicious from the cluster-decomposition viewpoint.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A delta function in momentum space becomes a nondecaying constraint in position space. If the amplitude contains

$$
\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p'_A-\mathbf p_A),
$$

then the $A$ cluster conserves its three-momentum independently of the rest of the process. After Fourier transformation, this independent conservation law prevents the corresponding piece from depending on the relative position of the $A$ cluster in a way that decays at large separation.

That is the hallmark of a disconnected product, not a genuinely connected interaction between all external particles. A connected amplitude should have only the single overall conservation delta function.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Massive Euclidean propagator and exponential decay

The Euclidean massive scalar propagator in four dimensions is

$$
D_E(R;m)=\frac{m}{4\pi^2R}K_1(mR).
$$

Using

$$
K_1(z)\sim\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2z}}e^{-z}
\qquad (z\to\infty),
$$

show the leading large-$R$ behavior.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Substitute the asymptotic form into the propagator:

$$
D_E(R;m)
\sim
\frac{m}{4\pi^2R}
\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2mR}}e^{-mR}.
$$

This can be written as

$$
D_E(R;m)
\sim
\frac{\sqrt m}{4\pi^2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\frac{e^{-mR}}{R^{3/2}}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
D_E(R;m)
\sim
\frac{\sqrt m}{(2\pi)^{3/2}}\frac{e^{-mR}}{R^{3/2}}.
$$

The key point is the exponential factor $e^{-mR}$, with a power-law prefactor.

</details>

### Exercise 6: Locality versus clustering in a free massless scalar

In four spacetime dimensions, the free massless scalar two-point function has spacelike behavior of the schematic form

$$
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(0)|0\rangle\sim\frac{1}{R^2}
$$

at equal time and large spatial separation $R=|\mathbf x|$. The commutator vanishes at spacelike separation. Explain why this is compatible with both microcausality and cluster decomposition.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Microcausality concerns the commutator:

$$
[\phi(x),\phi(0)]=0
\qquad x^2<0.
$$

That condition is satisfied, so there is no spacelike signaling.

Cluster decomposition concerns the connected correlator at large separation. For a free massless scalar with vanishing one-point function,

$$
\langle\phi(x)\phi(0)\rangle_{\mathrm c}
=\langle\phi(x)\phi(0)\rangle
\sim\frac{1}{R^2}\to0.
$$

The decay is power-law rather than exponential because the theory is massless, but it still tends to zero. Thus both conditions are satisfied.

</details>
