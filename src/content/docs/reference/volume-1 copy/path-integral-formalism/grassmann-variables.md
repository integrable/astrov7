## Summary

Grassmann variables are anticommuting algebraic variables. For generators $\theta_i$,

$$
\boxed{\theta_i\theta_j=-\theta_j\theta_i,\qquad \theta_i^2=0.}
$$

They are not quantum operators, but they imitate the algebraic signs of fermionic operators closely enough to serve as classical variables for fermionic path integrals. A function of finitely many Grassmann variables is a finite polynomial, because every generator squares to zero.

Berezin integration is defined algebraically, not as integration over a numerical range. For one variable,

$$
\boxed{\int d\theta=0,
\qquad
\int d\theta\,\theta=1.}
$$

So integration extracts the coefficient of the top Grassmann monomial. For paired variables $\psi_i,\overline\psi_i$, we choose the paired measure convention

$$
[d\overline\psi d\psi]
=d\overline\psi_Nd\psi_N\cdots d\overline\psi_1d\psi_1,
$$

with the normalization

$$
\boxed{
\int[d\overline\psi d\psi]\,
\exp\!\bigl(-\overline\psi A\psi\bigr)
=\det A.
}
$$

With Grassmann sources $\eta,\overline\eta$,

$$
\boxed{
\int[d\overline\psi d\psi]\,
\exp\!\bigl(-\overline\psi A\psi+\overline\eta\psi+\overline\psi\eta\bigr)
=\det A\,
\exp\!\bigl(\overline\eta A^{-1}\eta\bigr).
}
$$

The determinant in the numerator is the signature feature of fermions. Bosonic Gaussian integration gives inverse determinants; Grassmann Gaussian integration gives determinants. Coleman introduces Grassmann variables precisely because functional integrals over bosonic c-number fields cannot represent anticommuting fermionic degrees of freedom; Srednicki develops the same formalism before deriving the fermionic path integral; Zee emphasizes the compact rules $\int d\eta=0$, $\int d\eta\,\eta=1$, and the determinant identity for the Grassmann Gaussian (Coleman 2019, §29.1; Srednicki 2007, §§43–44; Zee 2010, ch. II.5).

## Core idea

Grassmann variables are bookkeeping devices for fermionic signs. They let us write a path integral in which the variables anticommute before they become quantum fields.

<figcaption>

Grassmann variables are nilpotent and anticommute. Their finite polynomial algebra makes Berezin integration a coefficient-extraction rule. The Gaussian integral gives $\det A$ rather than $(\det A)^{-1/2}$ or $(\det A)^{-1}$. Adding Grassmann sources produces the inverse matrix $A^{-1}$, which becomes the fermion propagator in the field theory.

A good mental picture is:

```txt
bosonic variable:      ordinary coordinate, many powers allowed
Grassmann variable:   anticommuting coordinate, each generator appears at most once
Berezin integral:     projection onto the top coefficient
fermionic Gaussian:   determinant and inverse operator
```

This is weird the first time you see it. Then it becomes one of the slickest pieces of QFT technology.

## Anticommuting generators

A Grassmann algebra is generated by symbols

$$
\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_N
$$

obeying

$$
\theta_i\theta_j=-\theta_j\theta_i.
$$

Setting $i=j$ gives

$$
2\theta_i^2=0,
$$

and over a field of characteristic zero this means

$$
\theta_i^2=0.
$$

A general element is therefore a finite sum:

$$
F(\theta)
=f_0+\frac{1}{1!}f_i\theta_i
+\frac{1}{2!}f_{ij}\theta_i\theta_j
+\frac{1}{3!}f_{ijk}\theta_i\theta_j\theta_k
+\cdots
+f_{12\cdots N}\theta_1\theta_2\cdots\theta_N.
$$

Only the completely antisymmetric parts of the coefficients matter. For instance,

$$
f_{ij}\theta_i\theta_j
=\frac12(f_{ij}-f_{ji})\theta_i\theta_j.
$$

This is the algebraic reason Grassmann variables are tied to exterior algebra.

:::note[Ordinary numbers and Grassmann numbers]
Grassmann variables are not real numbers, complex numbers, or operators on a Hilbert space. They are algebraic generators. In a path integral, the fermionic field variables $\psi(x)$ and $\overline\psi(x)$ are Grassmann-valued integration variables, while the quantum operator fields are recovered through correlation functions.
:::

## Grassmann parity

Every monomial has a parity:

```txt
even:  contains an even number of Grassmann generators
odd:   contains an odd number of Grassmann generators
```

An element with definite parity $|A|\in\{0,1\}$ obeys

$$
AB=(-1)^{|A||B|}BA
$$

when $A$ and $B$ are built from independent Grassmann generators. Even elements commute with everything. Odd elements anticommute with odd elements.

This parity is why signs appear whenever we move Grassmann sources, fermionic fields, functional derivatives, or differentials past each other. When a sign looks mysterious in a fermion calculation, the first move is usually to count how many odd objects crossed.

## Nilpotent functions and exponentials

Because $\theta^2=0$, a function of one Grassmann variable has only two terms:

$$
f(\theta)=a+b\theta.
$$

The exponential truncates:

$$
e^\theta=1+\theta.
$$

For several variables, exponentials still truncate in the Grassmann directions. For example,

$$
e^{\theta_1\theta_2}=1+\theta_1\theta_2,
$$

since $(\theta_1\theta_2)^2=0$. More generally, a finite Grassmann algebra has a highest degree, so every formal power series becomes a finite polynomial in the Grassmann variables.

This is why Berezin integration is exact and algebraic. There is no limiting process hidden in a one-variable Grassmann integral.

## Left and right derivatives

Derivatives with respect to odd variables must remember signs. We use **left derivatives** unless explicitly stated. The left derivative is defined by writing the variation as

$$
\delta F=(\delta\theta_i)
\frac{\partial^L F}{\partial\theta_i}.
$$

The right derivative is defined by

$$
\delta F=\frac{\partial^R F}{\partial\theta_i}(\delta\theta_i).
$$

For a product of definite parity objects, the left derivative obeys the graded Leibniz rule

$$
\boxed{
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_i}(AB)
=\frac{\partial^L A}{\partial\theta_i}B
+(-1)^{|A|}A\frac{\partial^L B}{\partial\theta_i}.
}
$$

For example,

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_1}(\theta_1\theta_2)=\theta_2,
$$

but

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_2}(\theta_1\theta_2)=-\theta_1.
$$

The minus sign comes from moving the derivative past the odd factor $\theta_1$.

:::tip[Choose one derivative convention and stick to it]
Most sign mistakes in fermionic path integrals come from silently switching between left and right derivatives. This page uses left derivatives for displayed differentiation rules and states explicitly when a right derivative is convenient.
:::

## Berezin integration in one variable

Berezin integration is defined by two rules:

$$
\int d\theta=0,
\qquad
\int d\theta\,\theta=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\int d\theta\,(a+b\theta)=b.
$$

This is the same operation as differentiation:

$$
\int d\theta\,f(\theta)
=\frac{\partial^L f}{\partial\theta}.
$$

The most important property is translation invariance. For another Grassmann variable $\xi$,

$$
\int d\theta\,f(\theta+\xi)=\int d\theta\,f(\theta).
$$

Indeed, if $f(\theta)=a+b\theta$, then

$$
f(\theta+\xi)=a+b\theta+b\xi,
$$

and the integral still extracts $b$.

Coleman uses linearity, translation invariance, and a normalization convention to determine the Grassmann integral table. Zee gives the same punchline in the especially compact form $\int d\eta=0$ and $\int d\eta\,\eta=1$ (Coleman 2019, §29.1; Zee 2010, ch. II.5).

## Multiple variables and orientation

Order matters. We choose the convention

$$
\boxed{
\int d\theta_N\cdots d\theta_1\,\theta_1\theta_2\cdots\theta_N=1.
}
$$

Equivalently, the integral extracts the coefficient of the ordered top monomial $\theta_1\cdots\theta_N$.

For two variables,

$$
\int d\theta_2d\theta_1\,\theta_1\theta_2=1,
$$

while

$$
\int d\theta_2d\theta_1\,\theta_2\theta_1=-1.
$$

Changing the order of either the measure or the monomial changes the sign. This is not a nuisance; it is the whole point. Fermionic exchange signs have to live somewhere.

## Change of variables

Let

$$
\theta_i=M_{ij}\theta'_j.
$$

The top monomial transforms as

$$
\theta_1\cdots\theta_N=(\det M)\theta'_1\cdots\theta'_N.
$$

To preserve the normalization of the Berezin integral, the measure must transform oppositely:

$$
\boxed{
d\theta_N\cdots d\theta_1
=(\det M)^{-1}d\theta'_N\cdots d\theta'_1.
}
$$

This is the opposite of ordinary integration. It is also the seed of the fermion determinant. In bosonic Gaussian integration, the measure transformation contributes inverse determinants; in Grassmann Gaussian integration, it contributes determinants.

## Paired variables

Dirac fermions are described by independent Grassmann fields $\psi$ and $\overline\psi$. In finite dimensions we model this by paired variables

$$
\psi_i,
\qquad
\overline\psi_i,
\qquad i=1,\ldots,N,
$$

all of which anticommute with one another.

We use the paired measure

$$
[d\overline\psi d\psi]
=d\overline\psi_Nd\psi_N\cdots d\overline\psi_1d\psi_1.
$$

With this choice, the one-pair normalization is

$$
\int d\overline\psi d\psi\,
\exp(-\overline\psi a\psi)=a.
$$

Check it directly:

$$
e^{-\overline\psi a\psi}
=1-\overline\psi a\psi
=1+a\psi\overline\psi,
$$

and the integral picks the coefficient of $\psi\overline\psi$.

:::caution[Measure conventions differ]
Some books use the opposite paired measure order or write $e^{+\overline\psi A\psi}$ instead of $e^{-\overline\psi A\psi}$. This changes intermediate signs. The invariant content is the consistent determinant/Pfaffian formula and the final correlation functions.
:::

## Grassmann Gaussian determinant

Let

$$
\overline\psi A\psi
=\sum_{i,j=1}^N\overline\psi_iA_{ij}\psi_j,
$$

where $A$ is an ordinary invertible matrix. Then

$$
\boxed{
\int[d\overline\psi d\psi]\,
\exp(-\overline\psi A\psi)=\det A.
}
$$

One way to see this is to change variables so that $A$ is diagonal. Then the integral factorizes into $N$ one-pair integrals:

$$
\int[d\overline\psi d\psi]\,
\exp\left(-\sum_i a_i\overline\psi_i\psi_i\right)
=\prod_i a_i
=\det A.
$$

Coleman makes this contrast explicit: ordinary complex bosonic Gaussians give inverse determinants, while anticommuting Gaussians give determinants (Coleman 2019, §29.1).

## Gaussian with sources

Now add Grassmann sources $\eta_i$ and $\overline\eta_i$, independent of $\psi_i$ and $\overline\psi_i$:

$$
I[\overline\eta,\eta]
=\int[d\overline\psi d\psi]\,
\exp\left(-\overline\psi A\psi
+\overline\eta\psi+\overline\psi\eta\right).
$$

Complete the square by shifting

$$
\psi'=\psi-A^{-1}\eta,
\qquad
\overline\psi'=\overline\psi-\overline\eta A^{-1}.
$$

Berezin translation invariance says the measure is unchanged. The exponent becomes

$$
-\overline\psi A\psi+\overline\eta\psi+\overline\psi\eta
=-\overline\psi' A\psi'+\overline\eta A^{-1}\eta.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{
I[\overline\eta,\eta]
=\det A\,
\exp(\overline\eta A^{-1}\eta).
}
$$

The inverse matrix $A^{-1}$ is what will become the fermion propagator. The determinant is what will become the closed-fermion-loop contribution.

## Propagators from source derivatives

Normalize the Gaussian by dividing by $I[0,0]$:

$$
Z[\overline\eta,\eta]
=\exp(\overline\eta A^{-1}\eta).
$$

Then

$$
\left.
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\overline\eta_i}
Z[\overline\eta,\eta]
\frac{\overleftarrow\partial^R}{\partial\eta_j}
\right|_{\eta=\overline\eta=0}
=(A^{-1})_{ij}.
$$

The right derivative on the second source is a compact way to avoid moving an odd derivative through an odd expression. You can do everything with left derivatives too, but then you must track the extra signs explicitly.

This is the finite-dimensional template for the fermion two-point function.

## Majorana variables and Pfaffians

A Majorana fermion has fewer independent components than a Dirac fermion. In finite dimensions the corresponding Gaussian uses one set of Grassmann variables $\chi_i$ and an antisymmetric matrix $B$:

$$
B^T=-B.
$$

The Gaussian is

$$
\boxed{
\int d\chi_N\cdots d\chi_1\,
\exp\left(\frac12\chi_iB_{ij}\chi_j\right)
=\operatorname{Pf}(B),
}
$$

where $\operatorname{Pf}(B)$ is the Pfaffian. It satisfies

$$
\boxed{\operatorname{Pf}(B)^2=\det B.}
$$

For two variables,

$$
B=\begin{pmatrix}0&b\\-b&0\end{pmatrix},
$$

and

$$
\frac12\chi_iB_{ij}\chi_j=b\chi_1\chi_2.
$$

Thus

$$
\int d\chi_2d\chi_1\,e^{b\chi_1\chi_2}=b,
$$

so $\operatorname{Pf}(B)=b$.

Dirac fermions naturally give determinants. Majorana fermions naturally give Pfaffians. That distinction becomes important in theories with real fermion representations, anomalies, lattice fermions, and supersymmetry.

## Why fermions give closed-loop minus signs

The determinant identity can be written as

$$
\det A=e^{\operatorname{Tr}\log A}.
$$

Suppose $A=A_0+V$. Then

$$
\operatorname{Tr}\log(A_0+V)
=\operatorname{Tr}\log A_0
+\operatorname{Tr}\log(1+A_0^{-1}V).
$$

Expanding,

$$
\operatorname{Tr}\log(1+X)
=\operatorname{Tr}X-\frac12\operatorname{Tr}X^2+\frac13\operatorname{Tr}X^3-\cdots.
$$

The alternating signs are not yet the full diagrammatic rule, but they are a preview. When fermions are integrated out, closed fermion loops arise from traces of products of propagators and interaction insertions. In diagrammatic perturbation theory, every closed fermion loop carries an additional minus sign relative to a bosonic loop. In the determinant language, that sign is another face of anticommutation.

## Operator and path-integral dictionary

| Operator language | Grassmann path-integral language |
|---|---|
| fermionic operators anticommute | Grassmann variables anticommute |
| time-ordering produces exchange signs | source derivatives produce graded signs |
| fermion propagator | inverse Grassmann quadratic kernel |
| closed fermion loop | determinant or trace log |
| Majorana field | Pfaffian of an antisymmetric kernel |

This dictionary is not saying Grassmann variables are particles. They are not. It says the algebra of the integration variables reproduces the algebraic signs of fermionic quantum fields.

## Common pitfalls

### Treating Grassmann variables as small real numbers

Grassmann variables are not small real numbers. There is no ordering, no positivity, and no numerical range of integration.

### Forgetting measure order

The sign of a multi-variable Berezin integral depends on the order of the measure and the order of the monomial. Write the measure convention before doing determinant or Pfaffian calculations.

### Treating the bar as complex conjugation

In fermionic path integrals, $\psi$ and $\overline\psi$ are usually independent Grassmann variables. The bar is motivated by the Dirac adjoint, but inside the functional integral it is not ordinary complex conjugation.

### Moving sources without signs

Grassmann sources anticommute with fermion fields and with each other. Moving $\eta$ past $\psi$ costs a minus sign.

### Confusing determinant and inverse determinant

Bosonic Gaussian integration gives inverse determinants. Grassmann Gaussian integration gives determinants. This one sign-flip in determinant power is behind a startling amount of QFT technology.

## Research status

Grassmann algebra and Berezin integration are standard mathematical tools. Their use in perturbative fermionic path integrals is textbook-standard. Nonperturbatively, the definition of the functional measure, the phase of the fermion determinant or Pfaffian, and the behavior under gauge or chiral transformations can become subtle and physically important.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Nilpotent exponential

Let $\theta^2=0$. Show that

$$
e^{a\theta}=1+a\theta
$$

for any ordinary number $a$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Expand the exponential:

$$
e^{a\theta}=1+a\theta+\frac12a^2\theta^2+\frac{1}{3!}a^3\theta^3+\cdots.
$$

Since $\theta^2=0$, all terms of order two and higher vanish. Therefore

$$
e^{a\theta}=1+a\theta.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Left derivative sign

Using left derivatives, show that

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_2}(\theta_1\theta_2)=-\theta_1.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use the graded Leibniz rule:

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_2}(AB)
=\frac{\partial^L A}{\partial\theta_2}B
+(-1)^{|A|}A\frac{\partial^L B}{\partial\theta_2}.
$$

Take $A=\theta_1$ and $B=\theta_2$. Then $|A|=1$, and

$$
\frac{\partial^L\theta_1}{\partial\theta_2}=0,
\qquad
\frac{\partial^L\theta_2}{\partial\theta_2}=1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_2}(\theta_1\theta_2)
=0-\theta_1
=-\theta_1.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: One-pair Gaussian

With the paired convention of this page, show that

$$
\int d\overline\psi d\psi\,
\exp(-a\overline\psi\psi)=a.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Since $(\overline\psi\psi)^2=0$,

$$
\exp(-a\overline\psi\psi)
=1-a\overline\psi\psi.
$$

Use $\overline\psi\psi=-\psi\overline\psi$:

$$
1-a\overline\psi\psi
=1+a\psi\overline\psi.
$$

The integral extracts the coefficient of $\psi\overline\psi$, so

$$
\int d\overline\psi d\psi\,
\exp(-a\overline\psi\psi)=a.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Source Gaussian derivative

For

$$
Z[\overline\eta,\eta]=\exp(\overline\eta_i(A^{-1})_{ij}\eta_j),
$$

show that

$$
\left.
\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\overline\eta_i}
Z
\frac{\overleftarrow\partial^R}{\partial\eta_j}
\right|_{\eta=\overline\eta=0}
=(A^{-1})_{ij}.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate once:

$$
\frac{\partial^L Z}{\partial\overline\eta_i}
=(A^{-1})_{ik}\eta_k Z.
$$

Now take the right derivative with respect to $\eta_j$ and then set the sources to zero. Terms in which the derivative hits $Z$ are proportional to at least one remaining source and vanish at zero source. The derivative of $\eta_k$ gives $\delta_{kj}$. Thus

$$
\left.
\frac{\partial^L Z}{\partial\overline\eta_i}
\frac{\overleftarrow\partial^R}{\partial\eta_j}
\right|_0
=(A^{-1})_{ik}\delta_{kj}
=(A^{-1})_{ij}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5: Pfaffian in two variables

Let $\chi_1,\chi_2$ be Grassmann variables and

$$
B=\begin{pmatrix}0&b\\-b&0\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Show that

$$
\int d\chi_2d\chi_1\,
\exp\left(\frac12\chi_iB_{ij}\chi_j\right)=b.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute the quadratic form:

$$
\frac12\chi_iB_{ij}\chi_j
=\frac12(b\chi_1\chi_2-b\chi_2\chi_1).
$$

Since $\chi_2\chi_1=-\chi_1\chi_2$,

$$
\frac12\chi_iB_{ij}\chi_j
=b\chi_1\chi_2.
$$

The exponential truncates:

$$
e^{b\chi_1\chi_2}=1+b\chi_1\chi_2.
$$

The measure extracts the coefficient of $\chi_1\chi_2$, so the integral is $b$. This equals $\operatorname{Pf}(B)$, and $\operatorname{Pf}(B)^2=b^2=\det B$.

</details>
