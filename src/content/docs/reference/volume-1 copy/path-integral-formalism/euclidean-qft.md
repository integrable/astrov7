## Summary

Euclidean QFT rewrites a Lorentzian path integral with oscillatory weight

$$
\int\mathcal D\phi\,e^{iS_M[\phi]}
$$

as a Euclidean functional integral with damped weight

$$
\int\mathcal D\phi\,e^{-S_E[\phi]}.
$$

For the real scalar field, the Wick rotation

$$
\boxed{t=-i\tau}
$$

takes the mostly-minus Lorentzian action

$$
S_M[\phi]
=\int dt\,d^3x\left[\frac12(\partial_t\phi)^2-\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2-\frac12m^2\phi^2-V_{\mathrm{int}}(\phi)\right]
$$

to the Euclidean action

$$
\boxed{
S_E[\phi]
=\int d\tau\,d^3x\left[\frac12(\partial_\tau\phi)^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2+V_{\mathrm{int}}(\phi)\right].
}
$$

The Euclidean generating functional is then

$$
\boxed{
Z_E[J]
=\frac{1}{\mathcal N_E}
\int\mathcal D\phi\,
\exp\left[-S_E[\phi]+\int d^4x_E\,J(x)\phi(x)\right],
\qquad Z_E[0]=1.
}
$$

Euclidean correlation functions, also called **Schwinger functions**, are generated by

$$
\boxed{
S_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=\left.\frac{\delta^n Z_E[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}\right|_{J=0}.
}
$$

For the free scalar field,

$$
\boxed{
G_E(x-y)
=\int\frac{d^4p_E}{(2\pi)^4}\,
\frac{e^{ip_E\cdot(x-y)}}{p_E^2+m^2},
\qquad
(-\partial_E^2+m^2)G_E(x-y)=\delta^{(4)}(x-y).
}
$$

Euclidean QFT is not just a cosmetic change of variables. It makes convergence, statistical-mechanics analogies, lattice regularization, and instanton methods more natural. But a Euclidean theory corresponds to a unitary Lorentzian QFT only when additional conditions hold. The crucial one is **reflection positivity**, the Euclidean shadow of Hilbert-space positivity. Weinberg lists reflection positivity as one of the Osterwalder–Schrader conditions for reconstructing Lorentzian QFT from Euclidean Green functions, together with smoothness, Euclidean covariance, permutation symmetry, and cluster decomposition (Weinberg 1995, Vol. I, §9 references). Coleman likewise emphasizes that the factor of $i$ in $Z=e^{iW}$ is chosen so that the generating functional has cleaner conventions after rotation to Euclidean space (Coleman 2019, §32.1).

## Core idea

Lorentzian path integrals oscillate. Euclidean path integrals often damp. That one change turns many formal integrals into objects resembling probability measures.

![Wick rotation and Euclidean reconstruction map](/figures/foundations/euclidean-wick-rotation.svg)

<figcaption>

Wick rotation sends the Lorentzian phase $e^{iS_M}$ to the Euclidean weight $e^{-S_E}$. Euclidean correlators are Schwinger functions. Analytic continuation and Osterwalder–Schrader conditions, especially reflection positivity, are what allow Euclidean data to reconstruct a Lorentzian Hilbert space with positive energy and unitarity.

A useful slogan is

```txt
Lorentzian QFT: amplitudes and causal time evolution.
Euclidean QFT: correlations and statistical weights.
```

The slogan is good. The trap is thinking it is the whole theorem.

## Wick rotation for a scalar field

Use qft.org's default mostly-minus convention

$$
\eta_{\mu\nu}=\operatorname{diag}(+,-,-,-).
$$

The Lorentzian scalar Lagrangian is

$$
\mathcal L_M
=\frac12\partial_\mu\phi\partial^\mu\phi-\frac12m^2\phi^2-V_{\mathrm{int}}(\phi)
=\frac12(\partial_t\phi)^2-\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2-\frac12m^2\phi^2-V_{\mathrm{int}}(\phi).
$$

Set

$$
t=-i\tau,
\qquad
\partial_t=i\partial_\tau,
\qquad
dt=-i\,d\tau.
$$

Then

$$
iS_M\longrightarrow -S_E,
$$

with

$$
S_E[\phi]
=\int d\tau\,d^3x\left[
\frac12(\partial_\tau\phi)^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2+V_{\mathrm{int}}(\phi)
\right].
$$

The kinetic and mass terms are now positive for a stable free scalar. This is the basic reason Euclidean scalar path integrals behave better than Lorentzian ones.

For the free theory,

$$
S_{E,0}[\phi]
=\frac12\int d^4x_E\,\phi(-\partial_E^2+m^2)\phi,
$$

where

$$
\partial_E^2=\sum_{a=1}^4\partial_a^2.
$$

The Euclidean operator

$$
-
\partial_E^2+m^2
$$

is positive on nonzero modes when $m^2>0$ and the boundary conditions are reasonable.

## Euclidean source functional

The Euclidean source functional is

$$
Z_E[J]
=\frac{\int\mathcal D\phi\,e^{-S_E[\phi]+\int J\phi}}{\int\mathcal D\phi\,e^{-S_E[\phi]}}.
$$

There are no factors of $i$ in the source derivative rule:

$$
S_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=\left.\frac{\delta^n Z_E[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}\right|_{J=0}.
$$

The connected Euclidean generating functional is usually defined by

$$
Z_E[J]=e^{W_E[J]}.
$$

Then

$$
S_n^{\mathrm c}(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=\left.\frac{\delta^n W_E[J]}{\delta J(x_1)\cdots\delta J(x_n)}\right|_{J=0}.
$$

This is the clean version of the Lorentzian formulas. The price is that real-time causal interpretation is no longer manifest.

## Free Euclidean propagator

The free Euclidean Gaussian gives

$$
Z_{E,0}[J]
=\exp\left[\frac12\int d^4x_E\,d^4y_E\,J(x)G_E(x-y)J(y)\right],
$$

where

$$
(-\partial_E^2+m^2)G_E(x-y)=\delta^{(4)}(x-y).
$$

In momentum space,

$$
\boxed{
G_E(p_E)=\frac{1}{p_E^2+m^2}.
}
$$

Thus

$$
G_E(x)
=\int\frac{d^4p_E}{(2\pi)^4}\,
\frac{e^{ip_E\cdot x}}{p_E^2+m^2}.
$$

Compare this with the Lorentzian Feynman propagator

$$
D_F(p)=\frac{i}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon}.
$$

The Feynman prescription tells us how to rotate the energy contour without crossing poles. In a free theory, the Euclidean propagator is the analytic continuation of the Feynman propagator to imaginary time.

A common compact statement is

$$
G_E(\tau,\mathbf x)=D_F(t=-i\tau,\mathbf x)
$$

for ordered Euclidean times, with the usual convention-dependent care about signs and contours.

## What Schwinger functions know

Euclidean correlators are not time-ordered Lorentzian correlators written with a funny symbol. They are functions on Euclidean space.

For a scalar field, Euclidean invariance says

$$
S_2(x,y)=S_2(|x-y|).
$$

For the free massive scalar in four Euclidean dimensions,

$$
S_2(x)=G_E(x)
$$

falls exponentially at large separation:

$$
G_E(x)\sim e^{-m|x|}
$$

up to powers of $|x|$. This is one reason Euclidean correlators are so useful: masses become correlation lengths.

At short distances, Euclidean correlators reveal operator dimensions and singularities. At long distances, they reveal mass gaps, Goldstone modes, and phases. On a lattice, they are the primary observables.

## Analytic continuation

The route back to Lorentzian physics is analytic continuation. Schematically,

$$
\tau\to it
$$

or, in momentum space,

$$
p_4\to -ip^0.
$$

But analytic continuation is a statement about functions with domains of analyticity, not a license to replace symbols anywhere. Singularities, branch cuts, pole prescriptions, and operator orderings matter.

For the two-point function, the Euclidean momentum-space propagator

$$
G_E(p_4,\mathbf p)=\frac{1}{p_4^2+\mathbf p^2+m^2}
$$

continues to the Feynman propagator by setting

$$
p_4=-i(p^0+i0)
$$

in the appropriate boundary-value sense. The $i0$ remembers which side of the pole the contour passes.

For higher-point functions, analytic continuation also encodes time ordering and branch cuts associated with multiparticle thresholds. This is why the Euclidean formalism is powerful but not brainless. The complex plane always keeps receipts.

## Reflection positivity

A Euclidean correlation function should come from a Lorentzian Hilbert space with positive norm. The Euclidean condition that captures this is reflection positivity.

Let $\Theta$ reflect Euclidean time:

$$
\Theta:(\tau,\mathbf x)\mapsto(-\tau,\mathbf x).
$$

For any collection of test functions supported at positive Euclidean time, reflection positivity says schematically that

$$
\boxed{
\sum_{i,j}c_i^*c_j\,
\left\langle (\Theta\mathcal O_i)\mathcal O_j\right\rangle_E\ge0.
}
$$

The exact statement depends on the class of operators and test functions, but the meaning is simple: reflecting one operator across $\tau=0$ should define a positive inner product.

This is the Euclidean version of

$$
\langle\Psi|\Psi\rangle\ge0.
$$

Without reflection positivity, a Euclidean functional integral can look perfectly well-defined and still fail to describe a unitary Lorentzian QFT.

:::note[Assumptions]
The Osterwalder–Schrader reconstruction theorem needs more than reflection positivity: Euclidean invariance, permutation symmetry, regularity, and clustering also enter. This page uses the theorem only as a guide to the physical meaning of Euclidean correlators, not as a full axiomatic treatment.
:::

## Why Euclidean QFT is useful

Euclidean QFT is not merely easier notation. It changes which structures are visible.

First, convergence improves. For stable scalar potentials, $e^{-S_E}$ suppresses large field configurations rather than oscillating violently.

Second, the connection to statistical mechanics becomes direct. A Euclidean field theory in $d$ dimensions resembles a classical statistical system in $d$ dimensions, with $S_E$ playing the role of energy divided by temperature. Polyakov emphasizes this analogy from the beginning of *Gauge Fields and Strings*, where quantum field theory and statistical mechanics are treated as deeply intertwined.

Third, lattice QFT is naturally Euclidean. Monte Carlo sampling needs a real, preferably positive, weight. The Lorentzian phase $e^{iS}$ is usually unsuitable for importance sampling.

Fourth, semiclassical saddles such as instantons are Euclidean finite-action configurations. Their contribution is weighted by

$$
e^{-S_E[\phi_{\mathrm{cl}}]},
$$

which makes tunneling physics visible.

Fifth, Euclidean short-distance limits are the natural home of the operator product expansion and many conformal-field-theory methods.

## What Wick rotation does not do

Wick rotation does not make every theory convergent. If the Euclidean action is unbounded below, the integral still has a problem.

It does not erase real-time physics. Retarded response, dissipation, transport, and in-in expectation values require more structure than ordinary Euclidean correlators.

It does not automatically preserve gauge fixing. Gauge theories require ghosts, gauge-fixing terms, and careful treatment of zero modes and Gribov issues.

It does not turn every Euclidean model into a unitary relativistic QFT. Reflection positivity can fail.

And it does not justify changing signs by memory. Always derive the Euclidean action from the Lorentzian convention in use.

## Common pitfalls

### Saying Euclidean time is imaginary physical time

Euclidean time is a calculational and structural continuation. It is not an additional physical clock.

### Forgetting reflection positivity

Euclidean invariance and convergence are not enough. A Euclidean theory must satisfy positivity conditions to reconstruct a Hilbert space with positive norm.

### Confusing Euclidean correlators with retarded correlators

Euclidean correlators analytically continue to time-ordered or thermal correlators under appropriate conditions. Retarded correlators require a specific real-time boundary prescription.

### Rotating through poles

The $i\epsilon$ prescription exists to prevent exactly this mistake. Contour rotations are legal only when singularities are accounted for.

### Assuming every lattice action has a good Lorentzian limit

A Euclidean lattice model may be mathematically well-defined but fail reflection positivity, locality, or the desired continuum limit.

### Losing sign conventions

With mostly-minus metric, the stable scalar Lorentzian action rotates to a positive Euclidean kinetic term. With other metric conventions, intermediate signs look different even though the final physics agrees.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Wick-rotate the free scalar action

Starting from

$$
S_M=\int dt\,d^3x\left[\frac12(\partial_t\phi)^2-\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2-\frac12m^2\phi^2\right],
$$

use $t=-i\tau$ to show that $iS_M\to-S_E$, with

$$
S_E=\int d\tau\,d^3x\left[\frac12(\partial_\tau\phi)^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2\right].
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The rotation gives

$$
dt=-i\,d\tau,
\qquad
\partial_t=i\partial_\tau.
$$

Therefore

$$
(\partial_t\phi)^2=-(\partial_\tau\phi)^2.
$$

Substitute into $S_M$:

$$
S_M=(-i)\int d\tau\,d^3x\left[-\frac12(\partial_\tau\phi)^2-\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2-\frac12m^2\phi^2\right].
$$

Thus

$$
iS_M=-\int d\tau\,d^3x\left[\frac12(\partial_\tau\phi)^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2\right]
=-S_E.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Euclidean propagator equation

Show that

$$
G_E(x)=\int\frac{d^4p_E}{(2\pi)^4}\frac{e^{ip_E\cdot x}}{p_E^2+m^2}
$$

satisfies

$$
(-\partial_E^2+m^2)G_E(x)=\delta^{(4)}(x).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Acting on the plane wave gives

$$
-\partial_E^2 e^{ip_E\cdot x}=p_E^2e^{ip_E\cdot x}.
$$

Therefore

$$
(-\partial_E^2+m^2)G_E(x)
=\int\frac{d^4p_E}{(2\pi)^4}\frac{(p_E^2+m^2)e^{ip_E\cdot x}}{p_E^2+m^2}.
$$

So

$$
(-\partial_E^2+m^2)G_E(x)
=\int\frac{d^4p_E}{(2\pi)^4}e^{ip_E\cdot x}
=\delta^{(4)}(x).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Euclidean Gaussian source functional

For

$$
S_{E,0}[\phi]=\frac12\int\phi A\phi,
\qquad
A=-\partial_E^2+m^2,
$$

show that

$$
Z_{E,0}[J]=\exp\left[\frac12\int JG_EJ\right],
$$

where $G_E=A^{-1}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The exponent is

$$
-\frac12\phi A\phi+J\phi.
$$

Complete the square:

$$
-\frac12\phi A\phi+J\phi
=-\frac12(\phi-A^{-1}J)A(\phi-A^{-1}J)+\frac12JA^{-1}J.
$$

The shifted Gaussian is independent of $J$ and cancels against the normalization. Since $A^{-1}=G_E$,

$$
Z_{E,0}[J]=\exp\left[\frac12\int JG_EJ\right].
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: No factors of i in Euclidean derivatives

Starting from

$$
Z_E[J]=\left\langle e^{\int J\phi}\right\rangle_E,
$$

show that

$$
S_2(x,y)=\left.\frac{\delta^2Z_E[J]}{\delta J(x)\delta J(y)}\right|_{J=0}.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate once:

$$
\frac{\delta Z_E}{\delta J(x)}
=\left\langle \phi(x)e^{\int J\phi}\right\rangle_E.
$$

Differentiate again:

$$
\frac{\delta^2 Z_E}{\delta J(x)\delta J(y)}
=\left\langle \phi(x)\phi(y)e^{\int J\phi}\right\rangle_E.
$$

Setting $J=0$ gives

$$
\left.\frac{\delta^2 Z_E}{\delta J(x)\delta J(y)}\right|_{J=0}
=\langle\phi(x)\phi(y)\rangle_E=S_2(x,y).
$$

No factor of $i$ appears because the Euclidean source coupling is $+\int J\phi$, not $i\int J\phi$.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Reflection positivity for a one-particle test state

Let $f(\tau,\mathbf x)$ have support only for $\tau>0$. Write the reflection-positivity condition for the scalar two-point function $S_2(x-y)$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The reflected point is

$$
\Theta x=(-\tau,\mathbf x).
$$

The one-particle test-state positivity condition is

$$
\boxed{
\int_{\tau_x>0}d^4x\int_{\tau_y>0}d^4y\,
 f^*(x)S_2(\Theta x-y)f(y)\ge0.
}
$$

This expression is the Euclidean candidate for $\langle\Psi_f|\Psi_f\rangle$. Reflection positivity says that it must be nonnegative for every such test function $f$.

</details>

### Exercise 6: Mass from Euclidean decay

Suppose a zero-spatial-momentum Euclidean two-point function behaves at large positive $\tau$ as

$$
C(\tau)\sim A e^{-M\tau}.
$$

Explain why $M$ is interpreted as the lightest energy that couples to the operator.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In a Hilbert-space interpretation, a Euclidean time translation by $\tau$ contributes a factor

$$
e^{-H\tau}.
$$

Insert energy eigenstates between the two operators:

$$
C(\tau)=\sum_n |\langle0|\mathcal O|n\rangle|^2e^{-E_n\tau}
$$

for zero spatial momentum, assuming the vacuum energy is subtracted. At large $\tau$, the smallest energy $E_n$ with nonzero matrix element dominates. Therefore

$$
C(\tau)\sim A e^{-M\tau}
$$

with $M$ equal to the lightest energy, or mass at zero momentum, in that channel.

</details>
