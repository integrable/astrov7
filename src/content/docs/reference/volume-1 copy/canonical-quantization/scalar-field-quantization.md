## Summary

The free real scalar field is the first fully relativistic quantum field one should be able to quantize without handwaving. Its Lagrangian density is

$$
\mathcal L=\frac12\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi-\frac12m^2\phi^2,
$$

with conjugate momentum

$$
\pi(t,\mathbf x)=\dot\phi(t,\mathbf x).
$$

Canonical quantization imposes the equal-time algebra

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\phi(t,\mathbf y)]=0,
\qquad
[\pi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]=0,
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
}
$$

The field has the on-shell mode expansion

$$
\boxed{
\phi(x)=\int d\mu(p)\,
\left[a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}+a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}\right],
\qquad
p^0=E_{\mathbf p}=\sqrt{\mathbf p^2+m^2},
}
$$

where

$$
d\mu(p)=\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}}.
$$

The canonical algebra is equivalent to

$$
\boxed{
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p'),
}
$$

with all $[a,a]$ and $[a^\dagger,a^\dagger]$ commutators zero. After choosing the vacuum-energy convention by normal ordering, the Hamiltonian is

$$
\boxed{
:H:=\int d\mu(p)\,E_{\mathbf p}\,a^\dagger(\mathbf p)a(\mathbf p).
}
$$

Thus the quanta created by $a^\dagger(\mathbf p)$ have positive energy $E_{\mathbf p}$ and momentum $\mathbf p$. Srednicki derives exactly this oscillator algebra from the equal-time canonical commutators; Coleman obtains the same scalar field and equal-time commutators from the demand of relativistic causality; Weinberg places scalar fields inside the general canonical formalism for relativistic QFT (Srednicki 2007, §3; Coleman 2019, chs. 3–4; Weinberg 1995, Vol. I, §§5.2 and 7.1–7.2).

## Core idea

The free scalar field is a continuum of harmonic oscillators labelled by momentum. The classical field equation identifies the normal modes. Canonical quantization turns the normal-mode amplitudes into creation and annihilation operators. The resulting Hilbert space is the bosonic Fock space generated by acting on the vacuum with $a^\dagger(\mathbf p)$.

The reason this page matters is that nearly every later construction is a variation on it. Spinors replace commutators by anticommutators. Gauge fields add constraints and polarizations. Interactions complicate time evolution, but the free scalar field is the reference point around which perturbation theory is organized.

:::note[Assumptions]
This page quantizes a free, unconstrained, real bosonic scalar field on flat Minkowski spacetime. The field is treated as an operator-valued distribution; expressions at sharp spacetime points should ultimately be smeared against test functions. We postpone domain questions and interacting-field subtleties.
:::

## Free real scalar theory

The action is

$$
S=\int d^4x\,\mathcal L,
\qquad
\mathcal L=\frac12\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi-\frac12m^2\phi^2.
$$

With the mostly-minus metric,

$$
\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi
=\dot\phi^2-(\nabla\phi)^2,
$$

so

$$
\mathcal L
=\frac12\dot\phi^2-\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2-\frac12m^2\phi^2.
$$

The Euler–Lagrange equation is

$$
(\Box+m^2)\phi=0.
$$

A plane wave $e^{-ip\cdot x}$ solves the field equation exactly when

$$
p^2=m^2,
$$

or

$$
(p^0)^2=\mathbf p^2+m^2.
$$

The positive on-shell energy is

$$
E_{\mathbf p}=\sqrt{\mathbf p^2+m^2}.
$$

The quantum theory will use both phases $e^{-ip\cdot x}$ and $e^{ip\cdot x}$ with $p^0=E_{\mathbf p}>0$. The second phase is not a negative-energy particle; it is the creation part of the Hermitian field.

## Canonical data

The canonical momentum is

$$
\pi(t,\mathbf x)
=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\dot\phi(t,\mathbf x)}
=\dot\phi(t,\mathbf x).
$$

The Hamiltonian density is

$$
\mathcal H
=\pi\dot\phi-\mathcal L
=\frac12\pi^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2.
$$

Thus

$$
H=\int d^3\mathbf x\,
\left[\frac12\pi^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2\right].
$$

This Hamiltonian is positive classically for $m^2\ge 0$. That positivity is one reason the sign conventions in the Lagrangian matter.

Canonical quantization promotes $\phi$ and $\pi$ to Heisenberg-picture operators and imposes

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\phi(t,\mathbf y)]=0,
$$

$$
[\pi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]=0,
$$

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

The time $t$ is the same on both sides. The algebra is an initial-data algebra on a Cauchy slice. Lorentz invariance is not manifest in this line, but it is recovered in the transformation properties and commutator support of the full field.

## Mode expansion

The Hermitian mode expansion is

$$
\phi(x)=\int d\mu(p)\,
\left[a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}+a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}\right],
$$

where

$$
d\mu(p)=\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}},
\qquad
p^0=E_{\mathbf p}>0.
$$

The adjoint of the first term is the second term, so

$$
\phi(x)^\dagger=\phi(x).
$$

The conjugate momentum is

$$
\pi(x)=\dot\phi(x)
=\int d\mu(p)\,
\left[-iE_{\mathbf p}a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}
+iE_{\mathbf p}a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}\right].
$$

This expansion is normalized so that the oscillator commutator is Lorentz-covariant:

$$
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p'),
$$

with

$$
[a(\mathbf p),a(\mathbf p')]=0,
\qquad
[a^\dagger(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf p')]=0.
$$

Other books sometimes put $1/\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p}}$ in the field expansion and use

$$
[\alpha(\mathbf p),\alpha^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p').
$$

The two conventions are related by

$$
a(\mathbf p)=\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p}}\,\alpha(\mathbf p).
$$

The physics is unchanged. The covariant convention used here makes the state normalization simple:

$$
\langle\mathbf p'|\mathbf p\rangle
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p').
$$

## Checking the equal-time algebra

Insert the mode expansion into the equal-time commutator. At $x^0=y^0=t$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
&=\int d\mu(p)\, iE_{\mathbf p}
\left[e^{i\mathbf p\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)}
+e^{-i\mathbf p\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)}\right] \\
&=\frac{i}{2}\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3}
\left[e^{i\mathbf p\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)}
+e^{-i\mathbf p\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)}\right] \\
&=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
\end{aligned}
$$

Similarly,

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\phi(t,\mathbf y)]=0,
\qquad
[\pi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]=0.
$$

The two exponentials in the first computation are both needed. Dropping the creation part of the field would destroy the canonical algebra and, more importantly, locality.

## Hamiltonian and momentum

The stress tensor for the free scalar field gives the conserved four-momentum. In canonical language,

$$
H=P^0=\int d^3\mathbf x\,
\left[\frac12\pi^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2\right],
$$

and

$$
\mathbf P=\int d^3\mathbf x\,\pi\,\nabla\phi.
$$

Substituting the mode expansion gives

$$
H=\frac12\int d\mu(p)\,E_{\mathbf p}
\left[a^\dagger(\mathbf p)a(\mathbf p)+a(\mathbf p)a^\dagger(\mathbf p)\right].
$$

Using the commutator,

$$
H=\int d\mu(p)\,E_{\mathbf p}a^\dagger(\mathbf p)a(\mathbf p)+E_0,
$$

where $E_0$ is the formally divergent zero-point energy. Normal ordering sets this constant to zero in flat-space scattering calculations:

$$
:H:=\int d\mu(p)\,E_{\mathbf p}a^\dagger(\mathbf p)a(\mathbf p).
$$

Similarly,

$$
\mathbf P=\int d\mu(p)\,\mathbf p\,a^\dagger(\mathbf p)a(\mathbf p),
$$

and covariantly

$$
P^\mu=\int d\mu(p)\,p^\mu a^\dagger(\mathbf p)a(\mathbf p)
$$

after the same vacuum-energy convention.

The ladder algebra gives

$$
[H,a^\dagger(\mathbf p)]=E_{\mathbf p}a^\dagger(\mathbf p),
\qquad
[\mathbf P,a^\dagger(\mathbf p)]=\mathbf p\,a^\dagger(\mathbf p).
$$

Thus $a^\dagger(\mathbf p)$ creates a quantum with four-momentum

$$
p^\mu=(E_{\mathbf p},\mathbf p).
$$

## Vacuum and one-particle states

The vacuum is defined by

$$
a(\mathbf p)|0\rangle=0
\qquad
\text{for all }\mathbf p.
$$

The one-particle state is

$$
|\mathbf p\rangle=a^\dagger(\mathbf p)|0\rangle.
$$

Its normalization is

$$
\langle\mathbf p'|\mathbf p\rangle
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p'),
$$

and its energy and momentum are

$$
:H:|\mathbf p\rangle=E_{\mathbf p}|\mathbf p\rangle,
\qquad
\mathbf P|\mathbf p\rangle=\mathbf p|\mathbf p\rangle.
$$

Multiparticle states are generated by repeated creation operators:

$$
|\mathbf p_1,\ldots,\mathbf p_n\rangle
=a^\dagger(\mathbf p_1)\cdots a^\dagger(\mathbf p_n)|0\rangle.
$$

Because the creation operators commute,

$$
a^\dagger(\mathbf p_1)a^\dagger(\mathbf p_2)
=a^\dagger(\mathbf p_2)a^\dagger(\mathbf p_1),
$$

the scalar quanta are bosons. This is the spin-zero example of the spin-statistics connection; the structural theorem is deeper and comes later.

## Local commutator

At unequal times, the field commutator is

$$
[\phi(x),\phi(y)]
=i\Delta(x-y),
$$

where

$$
i\Delta(x-y)
=\int d\mu(p)\,
\left[e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}-e^{ip\cdot(x-y)}\right].
$$

The function $\Delta$ is the Pauli–Jordan function. It obeys the homogeneous Klein–Gordon equation in each argument. Its equal-time limits reproduce the canonical algebra:

$$
\Delta(0,\mathbf x-\mathbf y)=0,
$$

$$
\partial_{x^0}\Delta(x-y)\big|_{x^0=y^0}
=-\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

with the sign convention following from $[\phi,\dot\phi]=i\delta^{(3)}$.

The crucial structural fact is

$$
[\phi(x),\phi(y)]=0
\qquad
\text{for }(x-y)^2<0.
$$

This is microcausality for the free scalar field: local scalar observables commute at spacelike separation. We will derive and interpret this more fully in the later pages on equal-time commutators, Pauli–Jordan functions, and locality.

## Two-point function preview

The vacuum two-point function is

$$
\Delta^+(x-y)
=\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}.
$$

The Feynman propagator is the time-ordered two-point function

$$
D_F(x-y)=\langle0|T\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle.
$$

With qft.org's mostly-minus convention,

$$
D_F(x-y)=\int\frac{d^4p}{(2\pi)^4}\,
\frac{i}{p^2-m^2+i\epsilon}e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}.
$$

The $i\epsilon$ prescription is not part of the canonical algebra itself; it specifies the boundary condition appropriate to time ordering and the vacuum.

## Normal ordering preview

The Hamiltonian before normal ordering contains

$$
E_0=\frac12\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3}E_{\mathbf p},
$$

which diverges quartically with a sharp high-momentum cutoff. In non-gravitational flat-space scattering, only energy differences matter, so it is conventional to define

$$
:H:=H-E_0.
$$

This is not the end of vacuum energy as a physical topic. In curved spacetime or when gravity is dynamical, constant shifts of the energy density matter. The point here is narrower: for the free scalar field in flat-space QFT, the oscillator vacuum-energy constant can be removed from the Hamiltonian used to define particle energies.

The next canonical page, [Normal Ordering](/foundations/canonical-quantization/normal-ordering/), will treat this operation more carefully.

## Common pitfalls

### Confusing the field with a wavefunction

The operator $\phi(x)$ is not the wavefunction of one scalar particle. It creates and annihilates particles. One-particle wavefunctions can be built from matrix elements such as $\langle0|\phi(x)|\Psi\rangle$, but the field itself is an operator-valued distribution.

### Dropping the creation term

The real scalar field needs both

$$
a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}
\quad\text{and}\quad
a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}.
$$

The first term annihilates a particle. The second creates one. Together they make $\phi$ Hermitian and local.

### Calling the second term a negative-energy particle

The physical spectrum has positive energy. The state $a^\dagger(\mathbf p)|0\rangle$ has energy $E_{\mathbf p}>0$. The phase $e^{ip\cdot x}$ is the spacetime dependence of a creation operator, not a negative-energy excitation.

### Mixing normalizations

The covariant commutator

$$
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p')
$$

belongs with the measure $d\mu(p)$. If you switch to the noncovariant commutator without the $2E_{\mathbf p}$, the field expansion must also change.

### Thinking normal ordering solves all infinities

Normal ordering removes the free-theory vacuum-energy constant from flat-space calculations. It does not remove loop divergences in interacting QFT, nor does it settle the cosmological constant problem.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Derive the canonical momentum

Starting from

$$
\mathcal L=\frac12\dot\phi^2-\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2-\frac12m^2\phi^2,
$$

show that $\pi=\dot\phi$ and derive the Hamiltonian density.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

By definition,

$$
\pi=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\dot\phi}=\dot\phi.
$$

The Hamiltonian density is

$$
\mathcal H=\pi\dot\phi-\mathcal L.
$$

Substituting $\pi=\dot\phi$ gives

$$
\mathcal H
=\dot\phi^2-\frac12\dot\phi^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2,
$$

so

$$
\mathcal H
=\frac12\pi^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Check the equal-time field-momentum commutator

Use the mode expansion and oscillator algebra to show that

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

At equal time,

$$
\phi(t,\mathbf x)=\int d\mu(p)
\left[a(\mathbf p)e^{-iE_{\mathbf p}t+i\mathbf p\cdot\mathbf x}
+a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{iE_{\mathbf p}t-i\mathbf p\cdot\mathbf x}\right],
$$

and

$$
\pi(t,\mathbf y)=\int d\mu(p)
\left[-iE_{\mathbf p}a(\mathbf p)e^{-iE_{\mathbf p}t+i\mathbf p\cdot\mathbf y}
+iE_{\mathbf p}a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{iE_{\mathbf p}t-i\mathbf p\cdot\mathbf y}\right].
$$

Only $[a,a^\dagger]$ and $[a^\dagger,a]$ contribute. The result is

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
=\frac{i}{2}\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3}
\left[e^{i\mathbf p\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)}+e^{-i\mathbf p\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)}\right].
$$

Each exponential integrates to the same delta function, so

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Show that the field is Hermitian

Show that the expansion

$$
\phi(x)=\int d\mu(p)\,
\left[a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}+a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}\right]
$$

satisfies $\phi^\dagger(x)=\phi(x)$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Take the Hermitian adjoint:

$$
\phi^\dagger(x)=\int d\mu(p)\,
\left[a^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}+a(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}\right].
$$

The measure and phases are ordinary c-number functions. Reordering the two terms inside the bracket gives the original expression:

$$
\phi^\dagger(x)=\phi(x).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Energy of a one-particle state

Assume

$$
:H:=\int d\mu(p)\,E_{\mathbf p}a^\dagger(\mathbf p)a(\mathbf p)
$$

and

$$
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf q)]
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf q).
$$

Show that

$$
[:H:,a^\dagger(\mathbf q)]=E_{\mathbf q}a^\dagger(\mathbf q).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use $[AB,C]=A[B,C]+[A,C]B$:

$$
[:H:,a^\dagger(\mathbf q)]
=\int d\mu(p)\,E_{\mathbf p}a^\dagger(\mathbf p)
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf q)].
$$

Substitute the oscillator algebra:

$$
[:H:,a^\dagger(\mathbf q)]
=\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}}
E_{\mathbf p}a^\dagger(\mathbf p)(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}
\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf q).
$$

The delta function evaluates the integral:

$$
[:H:,a^\dagger(\mathbf q)]
=E_{\mathbf q}a^\dagger(\mathbf q).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5: Compare two oscillator normalizations

Let

$$
a(\mathbf p)=\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p}}\,\alpha(\mathbf p),
$$

and suppose

$$
[\alpha(\mathbf p),\alpha^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p').
$$

Show that $a(\mathbf p)$ satisfies the covariant oscillator algebra.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p}}\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p'}}
[\alpha(\mathbf p),\alpha^\dagger(\mathbf p')].
$$

Thus

$$
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p}}\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p'}}
\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p').
$$

Inside the delta function, $\mathbf p'=\mathbf p$, so

$$
\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p}}\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p'}}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p')
=2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p').
$$

Therefore

$$
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p').
$$

</details>

### Exercise 6: Vacuum two-point function

Using $a(\mathbf p)|0\rangle=0$, show that

$$
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Insert the two mode expansions. The only term that survives between vacuum states is the one in which the first field annihilates the one-particle state created by the second field:

$$
\langle0|a(\mathbf p)a^\dagger(\mathbf q)|0\rangle
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf q).
$$

Thus

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle0|\phi(x)\phi(y)|0\rangle
&=\int d\mu(p)d\mu(q)\,
 e^{-ip\cdot x}e^{iq\cdot y}
\langle0|a(\mathbf p)a^\dagger(\mathbf q)|0\rangle \\
&=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot x}e^{ip\cdot y} \\
&=\int d\mu(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot(x-y)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

</details>
