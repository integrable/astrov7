## Summary

Canonical quantization promotes classical equal-time Poisson brackets to operator commutators. For a real bosonic field $\phi(t,\mathbf x)$ with conjugate momentum $\pi(t,\mathbf x)$, the canonical algebra is

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\phi(t,\mathbf y)]=0,
$$

$$
[\pi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]=0,
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
}
$$

The phrase “equal time” matters. These commutators are imposed on a fixed Cauchy surface. Time evolution is then generated by the Hamiltonian through the Heisenberg equation

$$
\dot{\mathcal O}=i[H,\mathcal O].
$$

For the free real scalar field, this algebra and the Hamiltonian reproduce the Klein–Gordon equation. For constrained systems, especially gauge fields, the naive canonical brackets must be replaced by brackets among the reduced physical variables or by Dirac brackets.

Srednicki derives the scalar oscillator algebra from the equal-time canonical commutators, while Weinberg presents the canonical variables and equal-time relations in the general canonical formalism. Coleman’s construction of field operators from oscillator modes gives the same algebra from the opposite direction (Srednicki 2007, §3; Weinberg 1995, Vol. I, §7.1; Coleman 2019, chs. 2–4).

## Core idea

At a fixed time, a field theory looks like a mechanical system with infinitely many coordinates: one coordinate $\phi(\mathbf x)$ for each point of space. The canonical momentum $\pi(\mathbf x)$ is the conjugate variable. The Dirac delta function is the identity matrix for this continuous set of coordinates.

For finitely many canonical coordinates,

$$
[q_i,p_j]=i\delta_{ij}.
$$

For fields, the discrete label $i$ is replaced by the continuous label $\mathbf x$:

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

The equal-time algebra is the field version of $[Q_n,P_m]=i\delta_{nm}$. Expanding fields in a complete spatial mode basis turns the Kronecker delta in mode space into the Dirac delta in position space: $\sum_n u_n(\mathbf x)u_n^*(\mathbf y)=\delta^3(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)$.

This algebra is not written in manifestly Lorentz-covariant form because it chooses a time slice. That is not a contradiction. Canonical quantization starts from initial data on a Cauchy surface; Lorentz invariance is a property of the full theory obtained after imposing the correct Hamiltonian, constraints, and transformation laws.

:::note[Assumptions]
This page treats unconstrained bosonic canonical variables. Fermionic fields require anticommutators. Gauge fields and other constrained systems require constraint analysis before the canonical algebra can be imposed on the physical degrees of freedom.
:::

## From Poisson brackets to commutators

For a classical field and its conjugate momentum, the equal-time Poisson bracket is

$$
\{\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)\}=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

with

$$
\{\phi(t,\mathbf x),\phi(t,\mathbf y)\}=0,
\qquad
\{\pi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)\}=0.
$$

Canonical quantization replaces

$$
\{A,B\}\quad\longrightarrow\quad \frac1i[A,B],
$$

or equivalently

$$
[A,B]=i\{A,B\}
$$

for elementary canonical variables. Thus

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

For functionals $F[\phi,\pi]$ and $G[\phi,\pi]$, the classical Poisson bracket is

$$
\{F,G\}
=\int d^3\mathbf z\,
\left(
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\phi(\mathbf z)}
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\pi(\mathbf z)}
-
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\pi(\mathbf z)}
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\phi(\mathbf z)}
\right).
$$

This formula is the field-theory analogue of

$$
\{F,G\}=\sum_i\left(
\frac{\partial F}{\partial q_i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial p_i}
-
\frac{\partial F}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial G}{\partial q_i}
\right).
$$

The delta function appears because

$$
\frac{\delta\phi(\mathbf x)}{\delta\phi(\mathbf y)}
=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

## Equal-time means equal-time

The canonical relation is

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

It is not the statement

$$
[\phi(x),\pi(y)]=i\delta^{(4)}(x-y),
$$

and it is not a statement about arbitrary spacetime-separated points. The canonical algebra gives initial data on one time slice. The commutators at unequal times are determined by time evolution.

For the free scalar field, the unequal-time field commutator is proportional to the Pauli–Jordan function,

$$
[\phi(x),\phi(y)]=i\Delta(x-y),
$$

with initial conditions fixed by the equal-time algebra:

$$
\Delta(0,\mathbf x-\mathbf y)=0,
\qquad
\partial_t\Delta(t,\mathbf x-\mathbf y)\big|_{t=0}
=-\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)
$$

up to sign conventions in the definition of $\Delta$. The causal support of this function is a later topic; the equal-time algebra is the starting point.

## Delta functions as mode completeness

Let $u_n(\mathbf x)$ be a complete orthonormal basis of spatial modes:

$$
\int d^3\mathbf x\,u_n^*(\mathbf x)u_m(\mathbf x)=\delta_{nm},
$$

and

$$
\sum_n u_n(\mathbf x)u_n^*(\mathbf y)=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

Expand

$$
\phi(t,\mathbf x)=\sum_n Q_n(t)u_n(\mathbf x),
\qquad
\pi(t,\mathbf x)=\sum_n P_n(t)u_n^*(\mathbf x).
$$

If

$$
[Q_n,P_m]=i\delta_{nm},
$$

then

$$
\begin{aligned}
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]
&=\sum_{n,m}u_n(\mathbf x)u_m^*(\mathbf y)[Q_n,P_m] \\
&=i\sum_n u_n(\mathbf x)u_n^*(\mathbf y) \\
&=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
\end{aligned}
$$

So the field delta function is not mysterious. It is the completeness relation of the mode basis.

In a finite box with periodic boundary conditions,

$$
u_{\mathbf k}(\mathbf x)=\frac1{\sqrt V}e^{i\mathbf k\cdot\mathbf x},
\qquad
\mathbf k=\frac{2\pi}{L}\mathbf n,
$$

and

$$
\sum_{\mathbf k}\frac1V e^{i\mathbf k\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)}
=\delta_L^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

where $\delta_L^{(3)}$ is the periodic delta function. In infinite volume this becomes

$$
\int\frac{d^3\mathbf k}{(2\pi)^3}e^{i\mathbf k\cdot(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)}
=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

## Generating the equations of motion

For the real Klein–Gordon field in the qft.org mostly-minus convention,

$$
\mathcal L=\frac12\partial_\mu\phi\partial^\mu\phi-\frac12m^2\phi^2
=\frac12\dot\phi^2-\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2-\frac12m^2\phi^2.
$$

The conjugate momentum is

$$
\pi=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\dot\phi}=\dot\phi.
$$

The Hamiltonian is

$$
H=\int d^3\mathbf x\,
\left[
\frac12\pi^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2
\right].
$$

Using

$$
[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)]=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

one obtains

$$
\dot\phi(t,\mathbf x)=i[H,\phi(t,\mathbf x)]=\pi(t,\mathbf x),
$$

and

$$
\dot\pi(t,\mathbf x)=i[H,\pi(t,\mathbf x)]
=\nabla^2\phi(t,\mathbf x)-m^2\phi(t,\mathbf x).
$$

Combining them gives

$$
\ddot\phi-\nabla^2\phi+m^2\phi=0,
$$

or

$$
(\Box+m^2)\phi=0.
$$

Thus the canonical commutation relations are not just bookkeeping. Together with the Hamiltonian, they generate the operator equations of motion.

## Multiple fields

For several real bosonic fields $\phi^A$ with conjugate momenta $\pi_A$, the canonical algebra is

$$
[\phi^A(t,\mathbf x),\pi_B(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^A{}_B\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

with

$$
[\phi^A(t,\mathbf x),\phi^B(t,\mathbf y)]=0,
\qquad
[\pi_A(t,\mathbf x),\pi_B(t,\mathbf y)]=0.
$$

The index $A$ may label field species, internal components, or real components of a complex field.

For fields with a nontrivial field-space metric, derivative couplings, or constraints, the canonical momenta may not be simply $\dot\phi^A$. One must compute

$$
\pi_A=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\dot\phi^A}
$$

before writing the canonical algebra.

## Complex scalar fields

A complex scalar field can be treated as two real fields,

$$
\Phi=\frac1{\sqrt2}(\phi_1+i\phi_2),
$$

or equivalently by treating $\Phi$ and $\Phi^\dagger$ as independent variables in the variational calculus.

For

$$
\mathcal L=\partial_\mu\Phi^\dagger\partial^\mu\Phi-m^2\Phi^\dagger\Phi,
$$

the conjugate momenta are

$$
\pi_\Phi=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\dot\Phi}=\dot\Phi^\dagger,
\qquad
\pi_{\Phi^\dagger}=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\dot\Phi^\dagger}=\dot\Phi.
$$

The nonzero canonical commutators are

$$
[\Phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi_\Phi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

and

$$
[\Phi^\dagger(t,\mathbf x),\pi_{\Phi^\dagger}(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
[\Phi(t,\mathbf x),\dot\Phi^\dagger(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

and

$$
[\Phi^\dagger(t,\mathbf x),\dot\Phi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

This is the canonical foundation for the later mode expansion with separate particle and antiparticle operators.

## Relation to oscillator operators

Using the noncovariant scalar-field expansion

$$
\phi(x)=\int\frac{d^3\mathbf p}{(2\pi)^3}\frac1{\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p}}}
\left[b(\mathbf p)e^{-ip\cdot x}+b^\dagger(\mathbf p)e^{ip\cdot x}\right],
$$

and

$$
\pi(x)=\dot\phi(x),
$$

the equal-time field algebra is equivalent to

$$
[b(\mathbf p),b^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p'),
$$

with

$$
[b(\mathbf p),b(\mathbf p')]=[b^\dagger(\mathbf p),b^\dagger(\mathbf p')]=0.
$$

Using the covariant operators

$$
a(\mathbf p)=\sqrt{2E_{\mathbf p}}\,b(\mathbf p),
$$

this becomes

$$
[a(\mathbf p),a^\dagger(\mathbf p')]
=(2\pi)^3 2E_{\mathbf p}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf p-\mathbf p').
$$

This is the normalization used in the Fock-space pages. The two forms are the same algebra with different placement of the $2E_{\mathbf p}$ factor.

## Smeared fields

Strictly speaking, quantum fields at points are operator-valued distributions. Products and commutators at exactly the same point can be singular. A cleaner version of the canonical algebra uses smeared fields:

$$
\phi(f)=\int d^3\mathbf x\,f(\mathbf x)\phi(t,\mathbf x),
\qquad
\pi(g)=\int d^3\mathbf x\,g(\mathbf x)\pi(t,\mathbf x).
$$

Then

$$
[\phi(f),\pi(g)]
=i\int d^3\mathbf x\,f(\mathbf x)g(\mathbf x).
$$

The delta-function version is a compact distributional notation for this smeared statement.

This caveat becomes essential in rigorous QFT and in composite-operator renormalization. For the first pass through perturbative QFT, the distribution notation is efficient and standard.

## Constraints and gauge fields

The canonical algebra is simple only for unconstrained variables. If the Legendre map is singular, some momenta are constrained and not all fields are independent canonical coordinates.

For example, in Maxwell theory the momentum conjugate to $A_0$ vanishes. The component $A_0$ is not a propagating oscillator. Gauss’s law is a constraint. Imposing

$$
[A_i(t,\mathbf x),\Pi_j(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta_{ij}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)
$$

on all vector components without handling constraints overcounts degrees of freedom.

The correct procedure is one of the following:

1. solve the constraints and quantize only physical variables;
2. fix a gauge and use the appropriate reduced brackets;
3. use Dirac brackets for constrained systems;
4. use a covariant gauge with an enlarged state space and impose physical-state conditions.

The details belong to [Constraints](/foundations/classical-field-theory/constraints/), [Maxwell Field](/foundations/free-fields/maxwell-field/), and [Gauge Fixing Preview](/foundations/gauge-fields-first-principles/gauge-fixing-preview/).

## Fermionic fields

Fermions use anticommutators rather than commutators. Schematically, for a fermionic canonical pair,

$$
\{\psi_\alpha(t,\mathbf x),\pi_\beta(t,\mathbf y)\}
=i\delta_{\alpha\beta}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

up to convention-dependent factors in the definition of the conjugate momentum.

This is not an optional sign choice. Locality, positivity, and Lorentz invariance force integer-spin fields to be quantized with commutators and half-integer-spin fields with anticommutators. The scalar-field page already gives a first warning: quantizing a scalar with anticommutators destroys the expected oscillator Hamiltonian. The full structural statement is the spin-statistics theorem.

Fermionic canonical quantization is treated separately because Grassmann variables, first-order kinetic terms, and constraints all enter.

## Common pitfalls

First, do not write a four-dimensional delta function in the canonical equal-time bracket. The canonical algebra lives on a spatial slice.

Second, do not forget the factor of $i$. With $\hbar=1$, canonical quantization gives $[q,p]=i$, not $1$.

Third, do not impose naive canonical brackets before computing the conjugate momenta. Derivative couplings, field-space metrics, and constraints can change the canonical variables.

Fourth, do not treat pointwise products of fields as ordinary products of functions. Fields are distributions; singularities appear when operators collide.

Fifth, do not confuse equal-time commutativity with full microcausality. Equal-time commutators are initial conditions. Microcausality is the spacelike-separation statement

$$
[\mathcal O(x),\mathcal O'(y)]=0
\qquad
(x-y)^2<0
$$

for appropriate local bosonic observables.

## Research status

This page is textbook-standard material for canonical QFT. The scalar-field algebra is stable and conventional. The main caveats are distributional fields, derivative couplings, fermionic grading, and constrained systems such as gauge theories, where the naive canonical variables must be replaced by physical variables or Dirac brackets.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Delta function from functional derivatives

Using

$$
\frac{\delta\phi(\mathbf x)}{\delta\phi(\mathbf z)}
=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf z),
$$

show that the field-theory Poisson bracket gives

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf x),\pi(\mathbf y)\}
=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Use

$$
\{F,G\}
=\int d^3\mathbf z\,
\left(
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\phi(\mathbf z)}
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\pi(\mathbf z)}
-
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\pi(\mathbf z)}
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\phi(\mathbf z)}
\right).
$$

Set $F=\phi(\mathbf x)$ and $G=\pi(\mathbf y)$. Then

$$
\frac{\delta F}{\delta\phi(\mathbf z)}=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf z),
\qquad
\frac{\delta G}{\delta\pi(\mathbf z)}=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf y-\mathbf z),
$$

while the other two functional derivatives vanish. Thus

$$
\{\phi(\mathbf x),\pi(\mathbf y)\}
=\int d^3\mathbf z\,
\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf z)\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf y-\mathbf z)
=\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Recover the first Hamilton equation

For the real scalar Hamiltonian

$$
H=\int d^3\mathbf y\,
\left[\frac12\pi^2+\frac12(\nabla\phi)^2+\frac12m^2\phi^2\right],
$$

use the canonical algebra to show that

$$
\dot\phi(t,\mathbf x)=i[H,\phi(t,\mathbf x)]=\pi(t,\mathbf x).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Only the $\pi^2$ term contributes:

$$
[H,\phi(\mathbf x)]
=\frac12\int d^3\mathbf y\,[\pi(\mathbf y)^2,\phi(\mathbf x)].
$$

Use

$$
[AB,C]=A[B,C]+[A,C]B
$$

to get

$$
[\pi(\mathbf y)^2,\phi(\mathbf x)]
=\pi(\mathbf y)[\pi(\mathbf y),\phi(\mathbf x)]
+[\pi(\mathbf y),\phi(\mathbf x)]\pi(\mathbf y).
$$

Since

$$
[\pi(\mathbf y),\phi(\mathbf x)]
=-i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

we have

$$
[H,\phi(\mathbf x)]
=-i\int d^3\mathbf y\,\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)\pi(\mathbf y)
=-i\pi(\mathbf x).
$$

Therefore

$$
i[H,\phi(\mathbf x)]=\pi(\mathbf x).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Recover the Klein–Gordon equation

Using the same Hamiltonian, show that

$$
\dot\pi=\nabla^2\phi-m^2\phi.
$$

Then combine this with $\dot\phi=\pi$ to get

$$
(\Box+m^2)\phi=0.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The $\pi^2$ term commutes with $\pi$. The mass term gives

$$
\left[\frac12m^2\int d^3\mathbf y\,\phi(\mathbf y)^2,\pi(\mathbf x)\right]
=i m^2\phi(\mathbf x).
$$

The gradient term gives

$$
\left[\frac12\int d^3\mathbf y\,\partial_i\phi(\mathbf y)\partial_i\phi(\mathbf y),\pi(\mathbf x)\right]
=i\int d^3\mathbf y\,\partial_i\phi(\mathbf y)\partial_i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf y-\mathbf x).
$$

Integrating by parts gives

$$
-i\nabla^2\phi(\mathbf x).
$$

Therefore

$$
[H,\pi(\mathbf x)]
=i\left(m^2\phi(\mathbf x)-\nabla^2\phi(\mathbf x)\right),
$$

and hence

$$
\dot\pi=i[H,\pi]
=\nabla^2\phi-m^2\phi.
$$

Since $\pi=\dot\phi$,

$$
\ddot\phi=\nabla^2\phi-m^2\phi,
$$

or

$$
(\partial_t^2-\nabla^2+m^2)\phi=0.
$$

With qft.org conventions this is $(\Box+m^2)\phi=0$.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Smeared canonical algebra

Define

$$
\phi(f)=\int d^3\mathbf x\,f(\mathbf x)\phi(t,\mathbf x),
\qquad
\pi(g)=\int d^3\mathbf y\,g(\mathbf y)\pi(t,\mathbf y).
$$

Use the canonical commutator to show that

$$
[\phi(f),\pi(g)]=i\int d^3\mathbf x\,f(\mathbf x)g(\mathbf x).
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
\begin{aligned}
[\phi(f),\pi(g)]
&=\int d^3\mathbf x\,d^3\mathbf y\,
 f(\mathbf x)g(\mathbf y)[\phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi(t,\mathbf y)] \\
&=i\int d^3\mathbf x\,d^3\mathbf y\,
 f(\mathbf x)g(\mathbf y)\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y) \\
&=i\int d^3\mathbf x\,f(\mathbf x)g(\mathbf x).
\end{aligned}
$$

</details>

### Exercise 5: Complex scalar canonical pairs

For

$$
\mathcal L=\partial_\mu\Phi^\dagger\partial^\mu\Phi-m^2\Phi^\dagger\Phi,
$$

show that

$$
\pi_\Phi=\dot\Phi^\dagger,
\qquad
\pi_{\Phi^\dagger}=\dot\Phi.
$$

Then write the two nonzero canonical commutators.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In mostly-minus convention,

$$
\partial_\mu\Phi^\dagger\partial^\mu\Phi
=\dot\Phi^\dagger\dot\Phi-\nabla\Phi^\dagger\cdot\nabla\Phi.
$$

Treating $\Phi$ and $\Phi^\dagger$ as independent variables,

$$
\pi_\Phi=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\dot\Phi}=\dot\Phi^\dagger,
\qquad
\pi_{\Phi^\dagger}=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\dot\Phi^\dagger}=\dot\Phi.
$$

The nonzero canonical commutators are

$$
[\Phi(t,\mathbf x),\pi_\Phi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

and

$$
[\Phi^\dagger(t,\mathbf x),\pi_{\Phi^\dagger}(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
[\Phi(t,\mathbf x),\dot\Phi^\dagger(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

and

$$
[\Phi^\dagger(t,\mathbf x),\dot\Phi(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y).
$$

</details>

### Exercise 6: Why constraints obstruct naive brackets

Suppose a theory has a field $\lambda$ whose conjugate momentum is identically zero:

$$
\pi_\lambda=0.
$$

Explain why it is inconsistent to impose an ordinary canonical commutator

$$
[\lambda(t,\mathbf x),\pi_\lambda(t,\mathbf y)]
=i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)
$$

while also imposing $\pi_\lambda=0$ as an operator identity.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

If $\pi_\lambda=0$ as an operator identity, then it commutes with every operator:

$$
[\lambda(t,\mathbf x),\pi_\lambda(t,\mathbf y)]=[\lambda(t,\mathbf x),0]=0.
$$

But the naive canonical commutator would require this same commutator to equal

$$
i\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf x-\mathbf y),
$$

which is not zero. The contradiction means that $\lambda$ and $\pi_\lambda$ are not an unconstrained canonical pair. One must reduce the phase space, fix gauge, or use Dirac brackets.

</details>
