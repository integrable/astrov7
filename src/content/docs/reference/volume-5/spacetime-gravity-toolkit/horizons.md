## Summary

A horizon is a boundary of causal access. Which boundary depends on the question. An event horizon is global and asks what can escape to infinity. A Rindler horizon is observer-dependent and appears even in flat spacetime. A Killing horizon is tied to a spacetime symmetry. Apparent and trapping horizons are more local and are useful in dynamical geometries.

The simplest model is the right Rindler wedge of Minkowski spacetime,

$$
\mathcal R=\{(t,x,\mathbf y):\ x>|t|\}.
$$

Its null boundaries $x=\pm t$ are horizons for uniformly accelerated observers. No curvature is required. This is already enough geometry to make the Unruh effect possible.

For stationary black holes, the central object is a Killing horizon generated by a Killing field $\chi^\mu$ that becomes null on the horizon. Its surface gravity $\kappa$ is defined by

$$
\chi^\nu\nabla_\nu\chi^\mu=\kappa\chi^\mu
\qquad
\text{on the horizon}.
$$

The corresponding Hawking temperature is

$$
T_H=\kappa/(2\pi).
$$

This page is a map of horizon notions. Later pages will compute surface gravity, Hawking radiation, black-hole entropy, quasinormal modes, and holographic black-brane thermodynamics in detail.

The essential physical idea is simple: a horizon is not necessarily a place where something locally dramatic happens. Often it is a boundary of what a class of observers, or an asymptotic region, can receive.

## Core idea

The word horizon is overloaded because physics asks several different causal questions:

| Question | Horizon notion |
|---|---|
| What cannot escape to future null infinity? | Event horizon. |
| What can one accelerated observer never receive? | Rindler horizon. |
| Where does a time-translation symmetry become null? | Killing horizon. |
| Where do outgoing light rays stop expanding on a slice? | Apparent or marginally trapped horizon. |
| What boundary limits observations in an expanding universe? | Cosmological horizon. |
| What bulk region is reconstructible from a boundary region? | Entanglement wedge boundary or related holographic surface. |

These notions coincide in special stationary examples, but they should not be identified in general. Confusing them is one of the fastest ways to misunderstand black-hole physics.

## Setup and conventions

We use mostly-minus signature. A hypersurface $\mathcal H$ is null if its normal is also tangent to the hypersurface. Locally, if $\mathcal H$ is defined by

$$
F(x)=0,
$$

then its normal is

$$
\ell_\mu=\nabla_\mu F.
$$

The hypersurface is null when

$$
g^{\mu\nu}\ell_\mu\ell_\nu=0
\qquad
\text{on }\mathcal H.
$$

A null hypersurface is generated by null curves. For horizons in classical general relativity, these generators are often null geodesics, possibly with a non-affine parameter.

When a null generator $\ell^\mu$ obeys

$$
\ell^\nu\nabla_\nu\ell^\mu=\alpha\ell^\mu,
$$

one can choose an affine parametrization if $\alpha$ is removed by rescaling the parameter. For a Killing horizon, the non-affinity of the Killing generator is the surface gravity $\kappa$.

## Rindler horizons

Minkowski spacetime in two displayed dimensions has

$$
ds^2=dt^2-dx^2.
$$

The right Rindler wedge is $x>|t|$. Introduce coordinates

$$
t=\rho\sinh(a\eta),
\qquad
x=\rho\cosh(a\eta),
\qquad
\rho>0.
$$

Then

$$
ds^2=a^2\rho^2d\eta^2-d\rho^2.
$$

The surfaces $x=\pm t$ correspond to $\rho=0$ and form the Rindler horizon. The vector $\partial_\eta$ is timelike inside the wedge but becomes null on the horizon.

Nothing singular happens to Minkowski spacetime at $\rho=0$. The horizon is observer-dependent: it appears because uniformly accelerated observers remain in one wedge and cannot receive signals from the opposite wedge.

This example is the clean laboratory for three lessons:

1. Horizons need not require curvature.
2. Horizon thermality is tied to restricting observables to a causally limited region.
3. Near any smooth nonextremal Killing horizon, the metric locally resembles Rindler space.

## Event horizons

For an asymptotically flat black-hole spacetime, the future event horizon is the boundary of the region that can send signals to future null infinity $\mathscr I^+$:

$$
\mathcal H^+=\partial J^-(\mathscr I^+).
$$

Equivalently, it is the boundary between events that can escape to infinity and events that cannot.

This definition is global. To know whether a point lies inside the event horizon, one must know the future causal development of the spacetime. That global character is not a weakness; it is exactly what the phrase “cannot escape to infinity” means.

In the Schwarzschild solution,

$$
ds^2=\left(1-2GM/r\right)dt^2
-\left(1-2GM/r\right)^{-1}dr^2-r^2d\Omega_2^2,
$$

the event horizon is at

$$
r_s=2GM.
$$

The Schwarzschild coordinate singularity at $r=r_s$ is not a curvature singularity. It is a bad chart at a regular null surface. Infalling coordinates or Kruskal coordinates continue smoothly across it.

## Killing horizons

A Killing horizon is a null hypersurface whose null generators are orbits of a Killing vector $\chi^\mu$. On the horizon,

$$
\chi^2=g_{\mu\nu}\chi^\mu\chi^\nu=0.
$$

The defining non-affinity equation is

$$
\chi^\nu\nabla_\nu\chi^\mu=\kappa\chi^\mu
\qquad
\text{on }\mathcal H.
$$

The constant $\kappa$ is the surface gravity when the Killing field has been normalized. For an asymptotically flat stationary black hole, the usual normalization is that the time-translation Killing field has unit norm at infinity.

For Schwarzschild,

$$
\kappa=1/(4GM),
\qquad
T_H=1/(8\pi GM).
$$

A stationary rotating black hole has horizon generator

$$
\chi=\partial_t+\Omega_H\partial_\phi,
$$

where $\Omega_H$ is the angular velocity of the horizon. Charged or rotating black holes also have chemical potentials in the first law.

## Surface gravity as near-horizon acceleration

For a static metric of the form

$$
ds^2=f(r)dt^2-f(r)^{-1}dr^2-r^2d\Omega^2,
$$

with a nonextremal horizon at $r=r_h$ where $f(r_h)=0$ and $f'(r_h)\neq0$, the surface gravity is

$$
\kappa=|f'(r_h)|/2.
$$

Near the horizon,

$$
f(r)\simeq f'(r_h)(r-r_h).
$$

After a change of radial coordinate, the two-dimensional $(t,r)$ part becomes Rindler-like:

$$
ds^2\simeq \kappa^2\rho^2dt^2-d\rho^2.
$$

This is why Rindler physics is the universal local model of a smooth nonextremal horizon. Extremal horizons, with $\kappa=0$, are different: the near-horizon geometry and thermodynamics require separate care.

## Apparent and trapping horizons

Event horizons are global. In dynamical spacetimes, one often wants a more local diagnostic. This is where trapped surfaces and apparent horizons enter.

Take a closed spacelike two-surface $S$ in four dimensions. It has two independent future-directed null normal congruences, often called outgoing and ingoing. Their expansions are $\theta_+$ and $\theta_-$. Roughly, $\theta$ measures the fractional rate of change of a small transverse area element along the null congruence:

$$
\theta=(1/A)dA/d\lambda.
$$

A trapped surface has

$$
\theta_+<0,
\qquad
\theta_-<0.
$$

A marginally outer trapped surface has

$$
\theta_+=0,
\qquad
\theta_-<0.
$$

An apparent horizon is often defined, relative to a chosen spacelike slice, as the outer boundary of trapped surfaces on that slice. This slice dependence is not a bug; apparent horizons are designed for local or quasi-local diagnostics, especially in numerical relativity.

## Cosmological horizons

In cosmology, horizons often arise because expansion limits causal access. In de Sitter spacetime, a static patch has metric

$$
ds^2=(1-r^2/L^2)dt^2
-(1-r^2/L^2)^{-1}dr^2-r^2d\Omega_2^2.
$$

The cosmological horizon is at

$$
r=L.
$$

The surface gravity is

$$
\kappa=1/L,
$$

and the associated Gibbons–Hawking temperature is

$$
T_{\mathrm{dS}}=1/(2\pi L).
$$

This is not a black-hole horizon. It is a horizon for a static-patch observer in a spacetime with positive cosmological constant. The observer has access only to a finite causal diamond.

## Horizons and QFT

Horizons matter for QFT because they split the field algebra into causally restricted regions. A global state can be pure, while its restriction to one side of a horizon is mixed. This is the basic structural reason behind thermality in Rindler, black-hole, and de Sitter settings.

Three examples organize much of the subject:

| Setting | Geometric horizon | QFT effect |
|---|---|---|
| Rindler wedge in Minkowski space | Observer horizon | Unruh effect. |
| Stationary black hole | Event/Killing horizon | Hawking radiation. |
| de Sitter static patch | Cosmological horizon | Gibbons–Hawking temperature. |

In each case, the temperature is related to a surface gravity:

$$
T=\kappa/(2\pi).
$$

The equality is simple; the interpretation is not. It involves the choice of state, the observer or asymptotic region, and the algebra of accessible observables.

## Horizons in holography

In gauge/gravity duality, horizons in the bulk often encode thermal or dissipative behavior in the boundary QFT. A planar AdS black brane is dual to a thermal state of the boundary field theory. Its Hawking temperature is the field-theory temperature.

The horizon also controls transport. For many classical gravity duals, low-frequency dissipation is governed by ingoing boundary conditions at the black-brane horizon. This is why horizon regularity becomes a retarded Green-function prescription in holography.

There is also a deeper quantum-information meaning. Entanglement wedges, quantum extremal surfaces, and islands are not the same thing as classical event horizons, but they are part of the modern story of how causal access, entanglement, and bulk reconstruction fit together.

## Common pitfalls

**Calling every null surface an event horizon.** A null surface is local geometry. An event horizon is a global causal boundary defined relative to future infinity or another asymptotic destination.

**Thinking a horizon must be a curvature singularity.** Smooth horizons are common. The Schwarzschild horizon is regular; the singularity is at $r=0$.

**Confusing Rindler and black-hole horizons.** Rindler horizons are observer-dependent in flat spacetime. Black-hole event horizons are global features of the spacetime.

**Forgetting normalization of the Killing field.** Surface gravity depends on the normalization of $\chi^\mu$. In asymptotically flat black holes, normalization at infinity fixes it.

**Equating apparent horizons with event horizons.** They coincide in special stationary cases but differ in dynamical situations.

**Using $T=\kappa/(2\pi)$ without asking which state and observer are being described.** Temperature is an operational statement about correlations or detector response, not only a geometric number.

## Research status

The classical definitions of event, Killing, Rindler, apparent, trapping, and cosmological horizons are standard. The thermality of stationary horizons is also a cornerstone of QFT in curved spacetime and black-hole thermodynamics.

The frontier is dynamical and quantum. Evaporating horizons, generalized entropy, quantum extremal surfaces, islands, black-hole microstates, firewall-like puzzles, and horizon reconstruction in holography all refine the simple classical picture. The right habit is to state which horizon notion is being used before drawing physical conclusions.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Rindler metric

Starting from two-dimensional Minkowski spacetime,

$$
ds^2=dt^2-dx^2,
$$

use

$$
t=\rho\sinh(a\eta),
\qquad
x=\rho\cosh(a\eta)
$$

to show that

$$
ds^2=a^2\rho^2d\eta^2-d\rho^2.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate:

$$
dt=\sinh(a\eta)d\rho+a\rho\cosh(a\eta)d\eta,
$$

$$
dx=\cosh(a\eta)d\rho+a\rho\sinh(a\eta)d\eta.
$$

Then

$$
dt^2-dx^2
=(\sinh^2(a\eta)-\cosh^2(a\eta))d\rho^2
+a^2\rho^2(\cosh^2(a\eta)-\sinh^2(a\eta))d\eta^2.
$$

The cross terms cancel, and $\cosh^2u-\sinh^2u=1$, so

$$
ds^2=a^2\rho^2d\eta^2-d\rho^2.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Schwarzschild surface gravity

For

$$
f(r)=1-2GM/r,
$$

show that the static-metric formula $\kappa=|f'(r_h)|/2$ gives $\kappa=1/(4GM)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The horizon is at $r_h=2GM$. Since

$$
f'(r)=2GM/r^2,
$$

we find

$$
f'(r_h)=\frac{2GM}{(2GM)^2}=\frac{1}{2GM}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\kappa=\frac12 f'(r_h)=\frac{1}{4GM}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 3: Extremal horizon warning

Consider a static metric with $f(r_h)=0$ and $f'(r_h)=0$. What does the formula $\kappa=|f'(r_h)|/2$ say, and why should the near-horizon geometry not be assumed to be ordinary Rindler?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The formula gives $\kappa=0$. A nonextremal horizon has a simple zero of $f(r)$, so near the horizon $f(r)$ is linear in $r-r_h$ and the metric becomes Rindler-like after a change of variables.

If $f'(r_h)=0$, the zero is higher order. The same Rindler approximation does not follow. Extremal charged black holes are the classic example: the near-horizon region has different geometry and zero Hawking temperature in the stationary semiclassical treatment.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Event horizon versus apparent horizon

Explain in one paragraph why an apparent horizon can be useful in numerical relativity even though it is slice-dependent.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

An event horizon is defined globally and requires knowledge of the future spacetime. In a numerical evolution, that information is not locally available while the simulation is running. Apparent or marginally trapped horizons can be located on individual spacelike slices using the expansions of null congruences. They are slice-dependent, but they provide practical quasi-local diagnostics of strong-field regions and black-hole formation.

</details>
