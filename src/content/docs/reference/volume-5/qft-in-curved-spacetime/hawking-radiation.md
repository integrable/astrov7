## Summary

**Hawking radiation** is the prediction that a black hole is not perfectly black once quantum fields are included. For a stationary black hole with horizon-generating Killing field of surface gravity $\kappa$, the Hawking temperature is

$$
T_H=\frac{\kappa}{2\pi}.
$$

For a four-dimensional Schwarzschild black hole,

$$
\kappa=\frac{1}{4GM},
\qquad
T_H=\frac{1}{8\pi GM}.
$$

The central mechanism is the same one already seen in [Bogoliubov Transformations](/spacetime-gravity-holography/qft-in-curved-spacetime/bogoliubov-transformations/): modes that are positive frequency in the far past are not purely positive frequency in the far future. Near the horizon, an exponential relation between null coordinates creates positive/negative-frequency mixing. The resulting outgoing occupation numbers are thermal,

$$
\langle N_\omega\rangle
=\frac{1}{e^{\omega/T_H}-1}
$$

for an ideal bosonic channel before scattering by the exterior potential. Real black holes add greybody factors, angular momentum barriers, spin dependence, and, eventually, backreaction.

The cleanest statement is not “virtual particles pop out of the horizon.” That image is memorable but too sloppy for serious work. The precise statement is: a quantum field placed in a state regular at the future horizon has outgoing flux at future null infinity, and the near-horizon redshift fixes its temperature.

## Core idea

Hawking radiation combines three facts:

| Ingredient | Role |
|---|---|
| Horizon | Separates modes that escape to infinity from modes that fall into the black hole. |
| Exponential redshift | Relates affine null coordinates near the horizon to retarded time at infinity. |
| Quantum positive frequency | Turns this exponential relation into Bogoliubov mixing. |

The near-horizon relation is the essential formula. In a collapse geometry, outgoing rays that reach future null infinity at retarded time $u$ originate near the forming horizon with an affine coordinate $U$ satisfying, at late times,

$$
U\simeq -A e^{-\kappa u},
\qquad
A>0.
$$

This exponential is the black-hole version of the Rindler transformation. Fourier transforming across it produces a thermal ratio of Bogoliubov coefficients,

$$
\frac{|\beta_\omega|^2}{|\alpha_\omega|^2}
=e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}.
$$

Together with the bosonic normalization identity $|\alpha|^2-|\beta|^2=1$, this gives

$$
|\beta_\omega|^2
=\frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa}-1}.
$$

Thus the temperature is $T_H=\kappa/(2\pi)$.

## Setup and conventions

We use mostly-minus Lorentzian signature and the volume convention

$$
\kappa_D^2=8\pi G_D.
$$

A stationary Killing horizon is generated by a Killing field $\chi^\mu$ satisfying

$$
\chi^2=0
\qquad
\text{on the horizon}.
$$

Surface gravity is defined by

$$
\chi^\nu\nabla_\nu\chi^\mu=\kappa\chi^\mu
\qquad
\text{on the horizon}.
$$

The associated temperature is

$$
T_H=\frac{\kappa}{2\pi}.
$$

For a static metric of the form

$$
ds^2=f(r)dt^2-f(r)^{-1}dr^2-r^2d\Omega_2^2,
$$

with a nonextremal horizon at $r=r_h$, where $f(r_h)=0$ and $f'(r_h)\neq0$, the surface gravity is

$$
\kappa=\frac{|f'(r_h)|}{2}.
$$

For Schwarzschild,

$$
f(r)=1-\frac{2GM}{r},
\qquad
r_h=2GM,
$$

so

$$
\kappa=\frac{1}{4GM},
\qquad
T_H=\frac{1}{8\pi GM}.
$$

## Near-horizon Rindler form

Near a smooth nonextremal horizon, write

$$
f(r)\simeq f'(r_h)(r-r_h).
$$

Introduce a proper-distance coordinate $\rho$ so that the two-dimensional time-radial metric becomes

$$
ds^2\simeq \kappa^2\rho^2dt^2-d\rho^2.
$$

This is Rindler space. The same geometry appeared in the [Unruh Effect](/spacetime-gravity-holography/qft-in-curved-spacetime/unruh-effect/) page, but the physical interpretation is different. Rindler thermality is observer-dependent in flat spacetime. Hawking radiation is radiation reaching infinity from a black-hole spacetime.

The local Rindler form explains the universality of the temperature formula. It does not by itself compute greybody factors, fluxes, or backreaction. Those depend on the geometry outside the near-horizon region.

## Euclidean derivation of the temperature

The Euclidean argument is short and powerful. Wick rotate the static time coordinate,

$$
t=-i\tau.
$$

Near the horizon, the Euclidean metric takes the form

$$
ds_E^2\simeq d\rho^2+\kappa^2\rho^2d\tau^2.
$$

This is the flat plane in polar coordinates if and only if the angular coordinate $\kappa\tau$ has period $2\pi$. Therefore

$$
\tau\sim \tau+\frac{2\pi}{\kappa}.
$$

A Euclidean time period $\beta$ corresponds to temperature $T=1/\beta$, so

$$
T_H=\frac{\kappa}{2\pi}.
$$

This derivation is elegant, but it is not a complete radiation calculation. It shows the temperature of the regular Euclidean saddle. The Lorentzian particle flux at infinity also requires the state, causal propagation, and scattering through the exterior region.

## Bogoliubov derivation in one paragraph

In a collapse spacetime, define outgoing modes at future null infinity $\mathscr I^+$ by

$$
f_\omega^{\mathrm{out}}\sim e^{-i\omega u}.
$$

Trace such a mode backwards in time. Near the past side of the forming horizon, the affine null coordinate $U$ is related to retarded time by

$$
U\simeq -A e^{-\kappa u}.
$$

Therefore

$$
f_\omega^{\mathrm{out}}
\sim e^{-i\omega u}
\sim (-U)^{i\omega/\kappa}.
$$

This function is not purely positive frequency with respect to the affine coordinate $U$. Its Fourier transform contains both positive and negative frequencies, and analytic continuation around the branch point at $U=0$ gives

$$
\frac{|\beta_\omega|^2}{|\alpha_\omega|^2}
=e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}.
$$

The Planck factor follows. The derivation is really a statement about analytic structure and exponential redshift.

## Greybody factors

The ideal Planck spectrum is modified by scattering outside the horizon. After decomposing a field into angular momentum channels, the radial equation has an effective potential. Some outgoing modes produced near the horizon are reflected back into the black hole, while some transmit to infinity.

For a Schwarzschild black hole, the observed bosonic occupation number in a channel labeled by frequency $\omega$ and angular momentum $\ell$ has the schematic form

$$
\langle N_{\omega\ell}\rangle
=\frac{\Gamma_{\omega\ell}}
{e^{\omega/T_H}-1},
$$

where $0\leq\Gamma_{\omega\ell}\leq1$ is the greybody factor. For fermions the denominator is $e^{\omega/T_H}+1$.

For rotating or charged black holes, the Boltzmann factor involves the horizon generator. The relevant energy is

$$
\omega-m\Omega_H-q\Phi_H,
$$

so the thermal denominator contains

$$
\exp\left(\frac{\omega-m\Omega_H-q\Phi_H}{T_H}\right).
$$

This same combination appears in black-hole thermodynamics through the first law.

## Vacuum states near a black hole

The phrase “the vacuum” is dangerous near a black hole. Different physical requirements define different states:

| State | Rough meaning | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Boulware | Empty at infinity for static exterior modes. | Singular on the horizon. |
| Hartle–Hawking | Thermal equilibrium state of an eternal black hole. | Regular on both future and past horizons. |
| Unruh | Collapse state with no incoming radiation from past infinity. | Regular on the future horizon and radiating at future infinity. |

For astrophysical black holes formed by collapse, the Unruh state is the relevant idealization. It gives outgoing Hawking radiation without an incoming thermal bath from infinity.

The Hartle–Hawking state is instead useful for eternal black holes in thermal equilibrium. It is especially important in Euclidean black-hole thermodynamics and AdS black-hole physics.

## Flux and evaporation

Hawking radiation carries positive energy to infinity. Energy conservation then implies that the black-hole mass decreases. For a four-dimensional Schwarzschild black hole, dimensional analysis gives

$$
\frac{dM}{dt}\sim -\frac{1}{G^2M^2},
$$

up to a numerical coefficient depending on the particle content and greybody factors. Therefore the evaporation time scales as

$$
t_{\mathrm{evap}}\sim G^2M^3.
$$

This scaling is robust in the semiclassical regime $M\gg M_{\mathrm{Pl}}$. The final endpoint is not determined by fixed-background QFT; as the mass approaches the Planck scale, the semiclassical approximation breaks down.

## Trans-Planckian sensitivity

Tracing late Hawking quanta backwards toward the horizon gives exponentially high frequencies. This is the trans-Planckian problem. It asks why a calculation that appears to use arbitrarily short wavelengths gives a robust low-energy thermal answer.

The modern view is nuanced. The original derivation uses modes whose past frequencies exceed the Planck scale, so fixed-background QFT is being extrapolated beyond its obvious domain. However, many analogue, effective, and robustness arguments show that the thermal result depends mainly on horizon regularity, causal structure, and exponential redshift, not on microscopic Lorentz-invariant physics at arbitrarily high frequency.

This does not mean quantum gravity is irrelevant to black holes. It means the leading Hawking temperature is a low-energy consequence of a smooth horizon. The information problem and the endpoint of evaporation are different questions.

## Common pitfalls

**Taking the virtual-particle cartoon literally.** Hawking radiation is a statement about quantum fields, states, horizons, and asymptotic particle definitions. The cartoon is not a derivation.

**Ignoring greybody factors.** The near-horizon spectrum is thermal, but the spectrum at infinity is filtered by the exterior geometry.

**Confusing Unruh and Hartle–Hawking states.** The collapse state radiating into empty infinity is not the same as a black hole in a thermal bath.

**Thinking the horizon must be locally violent.** In the semiclassical calculation, the future horizon is smooth for an infalling observer in the Unruh state. The drama appears in global particle definitions and late-time entanglement.

**Using $T_H=\kappa/(2\pi)$ without fixing the Killing normalization.** Surface gravity depends on the normalization of the horizon generator. Asymptotically flat black holes use unit time translation at infinity.

**Expecting fixed-background QFT to solve the information problem.** Hawking's calculation gives thermality and flux in a semiclassical approximation. It does not by itself provide the microscopic unitary description of evaporation.

## Research status

The leading Hawking effect for quantum fields on a fixed black-hole background is established. Its derivations by Bogoliubov transformations, Euclidean periodicity, detector response, and algebraic KMS methods are mutually reinforcing.

The active frontier begins when one asks for a complete quantum description of the black hole. Backreaction, Page curves, islands, replica wormholes, microscopic entropy, and the final state of evaporation are not resolved by the fixed-background calculation alone. Hawking radiation is therefore both a settled result and the doorway to the sharpest puzzles of quantum gravity.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Schwarzschild temperature

For

$$
f(r)=1-\frac{2GM}{r},
$$

use $\kappa=|f'(r_h)|/2$ to derive

$$
T_H=\frac{1}{8\pi GM}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The horizon is at $r_h=2GM$. Differentiate:

$$
f'(r)=\frac{2GM}{r^2}.
$$

At the horizon,

$$
f'(r_h)=\frac{2GM}{(2GM)^2}=\frac{1}{2GM}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\kappa=\frac12 f'(r_h)=\frac{1}{4GM},
$$

and

$$
T_H=\frac{\kappa}{2\pi}=\frac{1}{8\pi GM}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Euclidean smoothness

Starting from the near-horizon Euclidean metric

$$
ds_E^2=d\rho^2+\kappa^2\rho^2d\tau^2,
$$

show that smoothness at $\rho=0$ requires $\tau\sim\tau+2\pi/\kappa$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Define an angular variable

$$
\theta=\kappa\tau.
$$

Then

$$
ds_E^2=d\rho^2+\rho^2d\theta^2.
$$

This is the flat Euclidean plane in polar coordinates only if $\theta$ has period $2\pi$. Thus

$$
\kappa\tau\sim\kappa\tau+2\pi,
$$

so

$$
\tau\sim\tau+\frac{2\pi}{\kappa}.
$$

The inverse temperature is $\beta=2\pi/\kappa$, hence $T_H=\kappa/(2\pi)$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Planck factor from Bogoliubov normalization

Suppose a bosonic Bogoliubov transformation obeys

$$
|\alpha_\omega|^2-|\beta_\omega|^2=1,
\qquad
\frac{|\beta_\omega|^2}{|\alpha_\omega|^2}
=e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}.
$$

Show that

$$
|\beta_\omega|^2=\frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa}-1}.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Let

$$
r=e^{-2\pi\omega/\kappa}.
$$

The ratio says $|\beta|^2=r|\alpha|^2$. The normalization condition gives

$$
|\alpha|^2-r|\alpha|^2=1,
$$

so

$$
|\alpha|^2=\frac{1}{1-r}.
$$

Therefore

$$
|\beta|^2=\frac{r}{1-r}
=\frac{1}{r^{-1}-1}
=\frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/\kappa}-1}.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: Evaporation scaling

Use $T_H\sim 1/(GM)$ and horizon area $A\sim G^2M^2$ to estimate the mass-loss scaling $dM/dt\sim -1/(G^2M^2)$ in four dimensions.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A rough blackbody power estimate is

$$
P\sim A T_H^4.
$$

Using

$$
A\sim (GM)^2=G^2M^2,
\qquad
T_H\sim (GM)^{-1},
$$

we get

$$
P\sim G^2M^2\frac{1}{G^4M^4}
=\frac{1}{G^2M^2}.
$$

Since the radiated power decreases the mass-energy,

$$
\frac{dM}{dt}\sim -P
\sim -\frac{1}{G^2M^2}.
$$

Greybody factors and particle species change the numerical coefficient, not this scaling.

</details>
