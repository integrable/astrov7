## Summary

Algebraic QFT defines a quantum field theory by assigning an algebra of observables to each spacetime region. Instead of beginning with a Lagrangian density, a Feynman expansion, or a path-integral measure, it begins with the map

$$
\mathcal O\longmapsto \mathcal A(\mathcal O),
$$

where $\mathcal O$ is an open spacetime region and $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ is the algebra of observables measurable inside that region.

The slogan is:

$$
\text{fields are coordinatizations; local observables are the intrinsic data.}
$$

This page is a preview, not a theorem-first course. Its job is to explain why the algebraic viewpoint belongs in a nonperturbative volume, what data a local net contains, how states and Hilbert spaces enter, and why this perspective is especially good at separating physical observables from choices of field variables.

The punchline is simple and rather bracing: in algebraic QFT, the theory is not primarily a formula for $\mathcal D\phi$. The theory is the pattern of local quantum-mechanical algebras and their states.

You should have read [Canonical Hamiltonian Definition](/nonperturbative-qft/nonperturbative-definitions/canonical-hamiltonian-definition/), [Reflection Positivity](/nonperturbative-qft/nonperturbative-definitions/reflection-positivity/), and [Constructive QFT Preview](/nonperturbative-qft/nonperturbative-definitions/constructive-qft-preview/). It also helps to know the basic idea of local operators and equal-time commutators from introductory QFT.

No operator-algebra background is assumed. We use the words *algebra*, *state*, and *representation* in their physics-facing sense first, and only gesture toward the deeper mathematical machinery.

## Core idea

Ordinary QFT courses often present a field $\phi(x)$ and then build observables from it. Algebraic QFT reverses the emphasis. The physically meaningful object is not the individual field coordinate but the family of algebras generated by all observables localized in spacetime regions.

A local net is a rule

$$
\mathcal O\subset M\quad\mapsto\quad \mathcal A(\mathcal O),
$$

where $M$ is spacetime. If $\mathcal O_1$ lies inside $\mathcal O_2$, then every observable measurable in $\mathcal O_1$ should also be measurable in $\mathcal O_2$:

$$
\mathcal O_1\subset \mathcal O_2
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)\subset\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2).
$$

If two regions are spacelike separated, measurements in one region should commute with measurements in the other:

$$
[\mathcal A,\mathcal B]=0,
\qquad
\mathcal A\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1),\quad
\mathcal B\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2),
$$

whenever $\mathcal O_1$ and $\mathcal O_2$ are spacelike separated. This is locality in its cleanest observable form.

This looks abstract because it has deliberately removed the scaffolding. But the scaffolding is exactly where many convention-dependent choices live: gauge fixing, elementary fields, redundant variables, regularization, and perturbative coordinates on theory space.

## Setup and conventions

We work on flat Lorentzian spacetime unless stated otherwise. A spacetime region is denoted by $\mathcal O$. Its associated algebra is $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$. Think of $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ as generated by bounded functions of smeared local fields,

$$
\phi(f)=\int d^dx\,f(x)\phi(x),
\qquad
\operatorname{supp}f\subset\mathcal O,
$$

but do not take this heuristic too literally. In rigorous algebraic QFT, the local algebras are often $C^*$-algebras or von Neumann algebras, and the unbounded fields are secondary objects affiliated with them.

A state is a positive normalized linear functional

$$
\omega:\mathcal A\to\mathbb C,
\qquad
\omega(1)=1,
\qquad
\omega(A^*A)\ge 0.
$$

This generalizes the Hilbert-space formula

$$
\omega(A)=\langle\Psi|A|\Psi\rangle.
$$

The algebraic framework does not assume at the start that there is a unique Hilbert space. Instead, a Hilbert-space representation is built from a chosen state. That is a feature, not a bug: different phases, thermal states, charged sectors, and infinite-volume limits may require inequivalent representations.

## The local net

The basic structure is a triple of ideas:

| Algebraic object | Physical meaning |
|---|---|
| $\mathcal A(\mathcal O)$ | Observables localized in the spacetime region $\mathcal O$. |
| Inclusions $\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)\subset\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)$ | A smaller laboratory is contained in a larger laboratory. |
| Commuting algebras for spacelike separation | Measurements in causally disconnected regions are compatible. |

The word *net* means that the algebras are not isolated objects. They are organized by spacetime inclusion. A theory is not merely one big algebra; it is a big algebra together with its localization structure.

The localization structure is the part that remembers spacetime physics. Without it, one has quantum mechanics with a large algebra. With it, one has a candidate local quantum field theory.

## Haag–Kastler axioms in physics language

Different presentations use slightly different axiom lists. The common physics-facing core is the following.

| Principle | Algebraic statement | Physics meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Isotony | $\mathcal O_1\subset\mathcal O_2\Rightarrow\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)\subset\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)$ | Larger regions contain more observables. |
| Locality | $[\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1),\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)]=0$ for spacelike separation | Relativistic causality of observables. |
| Covariance | Spacetime symmetries act by automorphisms of the net | Poincaré transformations move laboratories consistently. |
| Spectrum condition | Translations have positive energy in vacuum representations | Stability and relativistic energy positivity. |
| Vacuum | There is a distinguished invariant state or vector in vacuum sectors | The theory has a ground state compatible with spacetime symmetries. |
| Additivity | Algebras of larger regions are generated by algebras of suitable smaller regions | Local data determine larger local data. |

The exact mathematical form depends on whether one uses $C^*$-algebras, von Neumann algebras, curved spacetime, Euclidean input, or a specific representation. The lesson for this volume is more modest: a nonperturbative QFT must specify how observables localize, how they commute, and how states evaluate them.

## States before Hilbert spaces

In finite-dimensional quantum mechanics, it is natural to start with a Hilbert space and then define expectation values. In algebraic QFT, especially in infinite volume, it is often better to start with a state on the algebra.

Given a state $\omega$, the Gelfand–Naimark–Segal construction produces

$$
(\mathcal H_\omega,\pi_\omega,\Omega_\omega),
$$

where $\mathcal H_\omega$ is a Hilbert space, $\pi_\omega$ is a representation of the algebra by operators on that Hilbert space, and $\Omega_\omega$ is a cyclic vector such that

$$
\omega(A)=\langle\Omega_\omega|\pi_\omega(A)|\Omega_\omega\rangle.
$$

This is conceptually important. A Hilbert space is not always universal background furniture. It can depend on the state or phase.

For finite systems, inequivalent representations are usually hidden because all reasonable representations are equivalent. In infinite systems, they become physically meaningful. Broken symmetry phases, thermal equilibrium states, charged sectors, and different boundary conditions can correspond to inequivalent representations of the same abstract algebra.

## Why this is nonperturbative

The algebraic viewpoint is nonperturbative because it does not rely on expanding around a free Gaussian field. Its primitive data are local observables, states, and consistency conditions. Perturbation theory may be used to construct examples or compare with Lagrangian QFT, but the definition itself is not a diagrammatic expansion.

This is especially useful for questions where perturbative variables are misleading:

| Question | Algebraic emphasis |
|---|---|
| What is an observable in a gauge theory? | Gauge-invariant local algebras, not gauge-dependent fields. |
| What does locality mean nonperturbatively? | Commutation of algebras for spacelike separated regions. |
| What distinguishes phases? | Inequivalent states and representations. |
| What are charged sectors? | Representations not generated from the vacuum by neutral local observables. |
| What survives field redefinitions? | The local net and its state space. |
| How does QFT work on curved spacetime? | Local covariance rather than global particle states. |

The algebraic viewpoint is therefore not a competitor to physics. It is a way of saying which parts of the physics are invariant under our favorite calculational costumes.

## Fields versus observables

A field $\phi(x)$ is a useful coordinate on the theory. But fields can be redundant, gauge dependent, or singular. Observables are the quantities that can be measured and compared between equivalent descriptions.

For example, in gauge theory the gauge potential $A_\mu$ is not itself gauge invariant. Local gauge-invariant observables include expressions such as

$$
\operatorname{tr}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu},
$$

while extended observables include Wilson loops. The algebraic approach naturally privileges gauge-invariant observables. It does not need to pretend that every useful field variable is directly measurable.

This distinction is also helpful in dualities. Two descriptions may use completely different elementary fields while describing the same local observable net, or at least equivalent observable sectors. The algebraic viewpoint asks for the invariant content beneath the dual coordinates.

## Locality and causality

In canonical QFT one often states microcausality as

$$
[\phi(x),\phi(y)]=0
\qquad
\text{for }(x-y)^2<0
$$

for bosonic fields. Algebraic QFT states the observable version:

$$
[A,B]=0,
\qquad
A\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1),\quad B\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2),
$$

when $\mathcal O_1$ and $\mathcal O_2$ are spacelike separated.

This is better for three reasons.

First, it applies to all observables in a region, not only elementary fields. Second, it avoids gauge-dependent statements about non-observable variables. Third, it survives in interacting theories where pointlike fields are distributional and products at a point need renormalization.

One should not confuse this with saying that correlations vanish at spacelike separation. Vacuum correlations do not generally vanish. Locality says spacelike separated observables commute, so no operation in one region can be used to signal to the other.

## Superselection sectors

A superselection sector is a representation of the observable algebra that cannot be reached from the vacuum sector by acting with local observables.

The slogan is:

$$
\text{local neutral observables cannot create every possible global charge sector.}
$$

In ordinary examples, electric charge labels sectors. A charged state cannot be obtained by applying a compactly supported gauge-invariant operator to the neutral vacuum. More generally, sectors can encode charges, topological data, boundary conditions, or defects.

Algebraic QFT gives a precise language for this. It treats the observable algebra as fundamental and studies its inequivalent representations. Charged fields, when they exist, may be auxiliary objects that interpolate between sectors rather than observables inside the neutral algebra.

This is one reason the algebraic viewpoint talks naturally to modern ideas about higher-form symmetries, line operators, generalized charges, and global forms of gauge groups. Those ideas will be developed later in the volume, but the structural moral already appears here: the allowed observables and sectors are part of the definition of the theory.

## Local algebras are not ordinary finite tensor factors

A common beginner mistake is to imagine that a spatial region $R$ in continuum QFT comes with an ordinary tensor factor

$$
\mathcal H\stackrel{?}{=}\mathcal H_R\otimes\mathcal H_{R^c}.
$$

This is often a useful lattice intuition, but it is not literally true in continuum relativistic QFT. Local algebras in continuum QFT are typically much more subtle than finite-dimensional matrix algebras. In many physically relevant cases they are type III von Neumann algebras, which do not admit a trace or density matrix in the naive finite-dimensional sense.

This does not mean entanglement is meaningless. It means continuum entanglement must be handled with care: regulators, algebras, relative entropy, modular theory, and renormalized quantities matter.

This preview does not develop those topics. The warning is included because it prevents a surprisingly large number of conceptual faceplants.

## Relation to Euclidean and Hamiltonian definitions

The algebraic viewpoint is not isolated from the other definitions in this chapter.

From a **Hamiltonian definition**, one can try to construct local algebras by taking bounded functions of regulated local operators and passing to limits. The Hamiltonian provides time evolution, spectra, and states. The algebraic structure records the local observable content.

From a **Euclidean constructive definition**, one can reconstruct a Hilbert-space QFT under suitable Osterwalder–Schrader conditions, then build local algebras from the reconstructed fields. Reflection positivity is one of the gates through which Euclidean correlators become Hilbert-space and algebraic data.

From a **lattice definition**, the finite-cutoff local algebras are often literal tensor-product subalgebras. The continuum limit is subtle precisely because the limiting local algebras are not just finite tensor factors with $a$ set to zero.

So the algebraic viewpoint is best understood as the intrinsic endpoint: once a QFT exists, what are its local observables, states, representations, and causal relations?

## What algebraic QFT is good at

Algebraic QFT is particularly strong at structural questions.

| Topic | Why the algebraic viewpoint helps |
|---|---|
| Locality | It formulates causality directly for observables. |
| Gauge theory | It separates gauge-invariant observables from redundant field variables. |
| Superselection | It classifies sectors as representations of the observable algebra. |
| Thermal states | KMS states describe equilibrium without relying on particles. |
| Curved spacetime | Local covariance replaces global Poincaré particle language. |
| Entanglement | Local algebras clarify what is meant by restricting observables to a region. |
| Duality | Equivalent physics can be compared at the level of observable nets. |
| Rigorous QFT | It supplies axioms independent of perturbation theory. |

The price is that explicit computations can be harder. A Lagrangian is often a better calculator. Algebraic QFT is often a better lie detector.

## Common pitfalls

**Do not confuse the algebra of fields with the algebra of observables.** Gauge-dependent fields can be excellent calculational devices without being observables. Algebraic QFT usually centers the gauge-invariant observable algebra.

**Do not think “abstract” means “empty.”** The local net encodes locality, causality, symmetry, states, phases, and sectors. That is a lot of physics. The abstraction is doing housekeeping, not running away.

**Do not assume one Hilbert space fits every phase.** Infinite systems can have inequivalent representations. This is one of the main reasons states and representations are treated carefully.

**Do not identify spacelike commutativity with vanishing correlation.** Vacuum correlations may be nonzero at spacelike separation. Locality says the observables commute, not that the state factorizes.

**Do not import finite-dimensional tensor-factor intuition too aggressively.** Continuum local algebras are subtler than ordinary matrix algebras. This matters for entanglement, edge modes, and gauge constraints.

**Do not treat algebraic QFT as a replacement for all other methods.** It is a foundational and structural language. To compute QCD masses, one usually needs lattice methods, EFTs, symmetries, or other tools.

## Research status

**Established.** The Haag–Kastler/algebraic framework is a mature nonperturbative formulation of QFT. It gives precise language for locality, covariance, states, representations, thermal equilibrium, and superselection sectors. It has deep connections to constructive QFT, Wightman QFT, Euclidean reconstruction, operator algebras, modular theory, and QFT on curved spacetime.

**Established.** Many structural results of QFT are most cleanly expressed algebraically: locality as commutation of algebras, charged sectors as representations, thermal equilibrium as KMS states, and the non-factorization subtleties of continuum local regions.

**Active.** Fully explicit algebraic construction and classification of physically central interacting four-dimensional QFTs remains difficult. The framework is clear; producing concrete models with all desired properties is hard.

**Active.** Modern developments around generalized symmetries, defects, entanglement, subregion algebras, gauge constraints, and quantum information continue to interact strongly with algebraic ideas.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Isotony from localization

Suppose a smeared scalar operator is written as $\phi(f)$, where $\operatorname{supp}f\subset\mathcal O$. Explain why $\mathcal O_1\subset\mathcal O_2$ should imply $\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)\subset\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

If $\operatorname{supp}f\subset\mathcal O_1$ and $\mathcal O_1\subset\mathcal O_2$, then also $\operatorname{supp}f\subset\mathcal O_2$. Every smeared field, bounded function of smeared fields, or observable built from data localized in $\mathcal O_1$ is therefore also localized in $\mathcal O_2$. Hence the smaller region’s algebra is contained in the larger region’s algebra.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Locality is not clustering

Let $A\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_1)$ and $B\in\mathcal A(\mathcal O_2)$ with $\mathcal O_1$ spacelike separated from $\mathcal O_2$. Locality says $[A,B]=0$. Does it follow that $\omega(AB)=\omega(A)\omega(B)$ in the vacuum state?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

No. Commutativity of observables is a statement about causal compatibility of measurements, not statistical independence in a particular state. The vacuum of a relativistic QFT generally has spacelike correlations, so $\omega(AB)$ need not factorize. Clustering is a separate large-distance property, and even clustering says factorization only asymptotically under appropriate assumptions.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Why states come before Hilbert spaces

In finite-dimensional quantum mechanics, every density matrix defines a state on the same matrix algebra. Explain why infinite-volume QFT can force more care.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In infinite systems, different phases or charge sectors can give inequivalent representations of the same abstract observable algebra. There may be no single Hilbert space in which all physically relevant states appear as density matrices. Starting from a state $\omega$ and constructing its GNS representation keeps track of which Hilbert space is appropriate for that phase or sector.

</details>
