## Summary

A superselection sector is a part of the Hilbert space that cannot be coherently connected to another part by the chosen algebra of physical observables. If the Hilbert space decomposes as

$$
\mathcal H=\bigoplus_{\alpha}\mathcal H_\alpha,
$$

and every local physical observable $A_{\mathrm{loc}}$ satisfies

$$
A_{\mathrm{loc}}\mathcal H_\alpha\subseteq \mathcal H_\alpha,
$$

then the labels $\alpha$ are superselection labels for the local observable algebra.

Equivalently, let $P_\alpha$ project onto $\mathcal H_\alpha$. Superselection means

$$
P_\alpha A_{\mathrm{loc}}P_\beta=0,
\qquad \alpha\neq\beta,
$$

for all local physical observables. A superposition

$$
|\Psi\rangle=c_\alpha|\psi_\alpha\rangle+c_\beta|\psi_\beta\rangle
$$

then has no locally observable relative phase between $c_\alpha$ and $c_\beta$. For local measurements it behaves like the corresponding incoherent mixture.

This page uses “superselection” in the practical QFT sense: sectors are defined relative to a specified observable algebra. Change the algebra, boundary conditions, or allowed defects, and the sector decomposition can change. That relativity is not a flaw. It is the point.

## Core idea

Superselection is not the statement “these states have different energy.” It is the statement “the allowed physical observables cannot measure interference between these states.”

The smallest useful model is a block diagonal matrix algebra. If all physical observables have the form

$$
A=\begin{pmatrix}
A_1&0\\
0&A_2
\end{pmatrix},
$$

then no measurement built from those observables can detect the relative phase in

$$
c_1|\psi_1\rangle+c_2|\psi_2\rangle.
$$

The phase disappears from every expectation value of $A$. The two blocks are different sectors.

In QFT, the reason for block diagonal structure is usually physical rather than arbitrary: a charge measured at infinity, a boundary condition, a topological flux, a higher-form symmetry charge, a gauge constraint, or an infinite-volume order parameter cannot be changed by a compactly supported local observable.

## Setup and conventions

Let $\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}$ denote the algebra generated by physical local observables in bounded spacetime regions. In a gauge theory, “physical” usually means gauge invariant. In a theory with a chosen global symmetry, one must say whether $\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}$ includes charged local fields or only invariant observables. The answer affects the sector decomposition.

A superselection decomposition is a direct-sum decomposition

$$
\mathcal H=\bigoplus_\alpha\mathcal H_\alpha
$$

such that every $A\in\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}$ is block diagonal:

$$
A=\bigoplus_\alpha A_\alpha,
\qquad
A_\alpha:\mathcal H_\alpha\to\mathcal H_\alpha.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
P_\alpha A P_\beta=0
\quad\text{for}\quad \alpha\neq\beta.
$$

If there are central operators $Z_i$ that commute with all local observables,

$$
[Z_i,A]=0,
\qquad A\in\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}},
$$

then simultaneous eigenvalues of the $Z_i$ often label sectors. The phrase “often” matters: in QFT, especially in infinite volume, the cleanest statement may be representation-theoretic rather than the existence of a simple operator acting inside one Hilbert space.

## Why relative phases disappear

Take two normalized states $|\psi_\alpha\rangle\in\mathcal H_\alpha$ and $|\psi_\beta\rangle\in\mathcal H_\beta$ with $\alpha\neq\beta$. For

$$
|\Psi\rangle=c_\alpha|\psi_\alpha\rangle+c_\beta|\psi_\beta\rangle,
$$

the expectation value of a local observable is

$$
\langle\Psi|A|\Psi\rangle
=|c_\alpha|^2\langle\psi_\alpha|A|\psi_\alpha\rangle
+|c_\beta|^2\langle\psi_\beta|A|\psi_\beta\rangle,
$$

because the cross terms vanish:

$$
\langle\psi_\alpha|A|\psi_\beta\rangle=0.
$$

Thus the pure-state superposition and the mixed state

$$
\rho=|c_\alpha|^2|\psi_\alpha\rangle\langle\psi_\alpha|
+|c_\beta|^2|\psi_\beta\rangle\langle\psi_\beta|
$$

are indistinguishable by $\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}$. The relative phase of $c_\alpha$ and $c_\beta$ is not an observable fact for that algebra.

This is stronger than an ordinary selection rule. A selection rule says that a particular interaction or operator has vanishing matrix elements. A superselection rule says that **every** physical observable in the chosen algebra is block diagonal.

## Common sources of sectors

Superselection sectors appear throughout nonperturbative QFT. The examples below are conceptually different, but the algebraic pattern is the same.

| Source of sector label | What labels the sector | Why local observables cannot change it |
|---|---|---|
| Conserved gauge charge | Electric charge, color singlet constraints, flux at infinity | Gauss-law constraints tie charge to asymptotic fields. |
| Broken discrete symmetry | Infinite-volume order parameter | A compact local operator cannot flip the order parameter everywhere. |
| Boundary condition | Vacuum, spin structure, thermal boundary condition, flux sector | Local bulk operators cannot rewrite asymptotic data. |
| Topological flux | Holonomy or flux through noncontractible cycles | Changing it requires an extended operator or a large rearrangement. |
| Theta sector | Eigenvalue under large-gauge/topological operations | Local operators preserve the sector label. |
| Higher-form symmetry | Charge measured by a topological surface operator | Local operators are neutral; extended charged objects carry the charge. |
| Topological order | Anyon type or global topological state | Local operators cannot distinguish or connect global topological sectors except through exponentially small finite-size effects. |

The table should be read as a map, not a theorem. Each row requires assumptions. For example, charge sectors in gauge theory are sharper than charge sectors for an ordinary global symmetry if the observable algebra includes charged local fields. Topological degeneracies can be exact in ideal topological field theories and only asymptotically protected in microscopic finite systems.

## Degenerate vacua as sectors

The previous page explained how infinite-volume degenerate vacua arise from broken phases. Such vacua are a basic source of superselection.

In a broken $\mathbb Z_2$ phase, choose two pure vacua $|\Omega_+\rangle$ and $|\Omega_-\rangle$ with

$$
\langle\phi\rangle_+=+v,
\qquad
\langle\phi\rangle_-=-v.
$$

A local operator $A_R$ supported in a bounded region $R$ can change the state inside $R$, create particles, or create a finite piece of domain wall. It cannot change the order parameter at spatial infinity. Therefore, in the infinite-volume limit,

$$
\langle\Omega_+|A_R|\Omega_-\rangle=0.
$$

This is the sector statement behind the intuitive phrase “the vacua are distinct.” The global symmetry operator may map $\Omega_+$ to $\Omega_-$, but it is not a compactly supported local observable. Boundary conditions can also select one vacuum rather than the other. Superselection does not say the sectors are metaphysically unreachable; it says local physical observables do not coherently connect them.

For continuous symmetry breaking, the story is similar but more delicate. Different orientations of the order parameter are related by global symmetry transformations, and low-energy Goldstone modes interpolate slowly between nearby orientations. Still, infinite-volume vacua with different asymptotic order-parameter directions are not related by compact local operations.

## Gauge theory and the observable algebra

Gauge theory is where sloppy language about superselection causes the most trouble.

A gauge transformation that dies off at infinity is a redundancy. It does not label different physical sectors. But transformations or fluxes with nontrivial behavior at infinity can label physical charges or boundary data. Gauss's law makes this vivid. In electrodynamics, total charge is measured by the electric flux through a sphere at infinity:

$$
Q=\int_{S^{d-2}_\infty} *F.
$$

A compactly supported gauge-invariant local operator cannot change this flux. Thus sectors with different total electric charge are superselected with respect to the strictly local gauge-invariant observable algebra.

This does not mean charged particles do not exist. It means that charged states require operators with appropriate dressing or boundary behavior. The physical charged operator is not an ordinary compactly supported gauge-invariant scalar. In gauge theories, “local,” “gauge invariant,” and “charged” do not coexist as casually as perturbation theory sometimes makes them appear.

Nonabelian gauge theory adds more structure: center symmetry, Wilson and ’t Hooft line operators, confinement, screening, and matter representations all affect which sectors are meaningful. Those topics are developed later in the confinement and line-operator chapters.

## Higher-form symmetries and extended operators

Modern QFT language makes many superselection statements cleaner by using generalized global symmetries. A $q$-form global symmetry acts on $q$-dimensional charged operators. Ordinary global symmetries are the case $q=0$, where charged objects are local operators. A one-form symmetry, for example, acts on line operators such as Wilson lines.

The symmetry generator is a topological operator supported on a codimension-$(q+1)$ surface. Its ability to move without changing correlation functions is what makes it measure a global sector rather than a local disturbance.

In a theory with an unbroken one-form symmetry, line operators can carry charges that local operators cannot screen. In a confining gauge theory, the behavior of Wilson loops, center symmetry, and line-operator charges can diagnose phases more effectively than any local VEV.

This viewpoint is powerful because it separates two ideas that old language sometimes blends together:

1. gauge redundancy is not physical symmetry;
2. higher-form global symmetry is physical and acts on extended operators.

Superselection sectors are often sectors of extended-operator charge, not just sectors of particle number or local order parameter.

## Theta sectors and topological sectors

Topological sectors are another common source of superselection language. In Yang–Mills theory, field configurations in Euclidean spacetime can be classified by an instanton number under suitable boundary conditions:

$$
Q_{\mathrm{top}}
=\frac{1}{32\pi^2}\int d^4x\,
\epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
\operatorname{tr} F_{\mu\nu}F_{\rho\sigma}.
$$

The theta angle weights these sectors by

$$
\exp(i\theta Q_{\mathrm{top}})
$$

in Lorentzian-signature conventions, or by the corresponding analytically continued Euclidean factor. The physical $\theta$ parameter labels a family of theories or vacua, depending on the formulation and context. Local operators do not shift $\theta$.

This is a good place to be cautious. A sum over instanton number in the Euclidean path integral is not the same thing as a Hilbert-space decomposition into instanton-number sectors at a fixed time. The precise meaning depends on spacetime topology, boundary conditions, allowed gauge transformations, and the observable algebra. The safe statement is that topological data and large-gauge structure can label sectors that local operators cannot change.

## Superselection versus ordinary degeneracy

Ordinary degeneracy and superselection are different.

Suppose two states $|1\rangle$ and $|2\rangle$ have the same energy in a finite-dimensional quantum system. If an allowed observable has

$$
\langle1|A|2\rangle\neq0,
$$

then coherent superpositions are physically meaningful. The degeneracy is ordinary degeneracy, not superselection.

By contrast, sectors can fail to interfere even when their energies are not the main issue. The defining condition is not

$$
E_1=E_2,
$$

but rather

$$
\langle\psi_\alpha|A|\psi_\beta\rangle=0
$$

for all physical local observables $A$ and all $\alpha\neq\beta$.

This distinction is especially important for topological phases. On a finite spatial torus, a topologically ordered system can have several nearly degenerate ground states. Local operators cannot distinguish or mix them except by effects that are exponentially small in system size. In the infinite-volume or ideal TQFT limit, that becomes a superselection-like statement for local observables, while nonlocal line operators can move between or distinguish sectors.

## How to diagnose sectors in practice

A good sector claim should identify the algebra and the label. Use the following checklist.

| Question | Good answer looks like |
|---|---|
| What is the observable algebra? | Local gauge-invariant operators, local charged fields, line operators, boundary-changing operators, or some specified enlargement. |
| What labels the sectors? | Charge at infinity, flux, order parameter at infinity, theta label, spin structure, anyon type, or higher-form charge. |
| What cannot connect them? | Compactly supported local physical operators. |
| What can connect or distinguish them? | Global symmetry operators, nonlocal line/surface operators, boundary conditions, defects, or changing the theory's parameter. |
| Is the statement finite-volume exact? | Sometimes yes, often no; many sector statements become sharp only as $L\to\infty$. |
| Is the label gauge invariant? | It must be physical, not a gauge-copy label. |

When the answer to “what algebra?” is vague, the superselection claim is vague. This is one of those places where mathematical fussiness is actually conceptual kindness.

## Common pitfalls

**Forgetting that sectors are relative to an algebra.** If you include charged fields or nonlocal defects among allowed operations, some sector decompositions change. Always specify the allowed observables.

**Calling gauge copies sectors.** Gauge-equivalent configurations do not label different physical sectors. Physical sectors in gauge theory come from gauge-invariant charges, fluxes, boundary data, line operators, or global structure.

**Confusing selection rules with superselection.** A vanishing matrix element for one operator is not enough. Superselection requires vanishing off-diagonal matrix elements for the whole physical observable algebra.

**Confusing finite-volume near-degeneracy with exact superselection.** In finite volume, tunneling or exponentially small matrix elements can connect states that become separate sectors only in the thermodynamic limit.

**Ignoring extended operators.** Local operators may preserve a sector while line or surface operators change it. In gauge theory and topological phases, the extended-operator algebra is not optional decoration; it is often the main diagnostic.

**Using non-gauge-invariant VEVs as sector labels.** Gauge-fixed order parameters can be useful intermediate diagnostics, but the sector label itself must be expressible physically.

## Research status

**Established.** Superselection relative to an observable algebra is a standard structural idea in QFT. It explains why some coherent superpositions are operationally indistinguishable from mixtures by local physical measurements.

**Established.** Infinite-volume broken phases provide superselection sectors for local observables under standard assumptions. Gauge theories also naturally produce sectors labeled by gauge-invariant asymptotic charges and fluxes.

**Active.** Modern generalized-symmetry language has sharpened sector diagnostics in gauge theory, topological phases, and strongly coupled systems. The role of defects, higher-form symmetries, anomalies, and boundary conditions is an active organizing theme.

**Caveat.** The phrase “the Hilbert space decomposes” can be too naive in infinite-volume QFT. Different sectors may be inequivalent representations of the local algebra rather than subspaces of one preferred Hilbert space. The block-diagonal picture is the right finite-volume intuition, but the infinite-volume statement is often algebraic.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Relative phases are invisible

Let $\mathcal H=\mathcal H_1\oplus\mathcal H_2$ and suppose every observable has the block diagonal form

$$
A=A_1\oplus A_2.
$$

For normalized states $|\psi_i\rangle\in\mathcal H_i$, compare the expectation value of $A$ in

$$
|\Psi\rangle=c_1|\psi_1\rangle+c_2e^{i\varphi}|\psi_2\rangle
$$

with its expectation value in the mixed state

$$
\rho=|c_1|^2|\psi_1\rangle\langle\psi_1|
+|c_2|^2|\psi_2\rangle\langle\psi_2|.
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The block diagonal condition gives

$$
\langle\psi_1|A|\psi_2\rangle=0,
\qquad
\langle\psi_2|A|\psi_1\rangle=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\langle\Psi|A|\Psi\rangle
=|c_1|^2\langle\psi_1|A_1|\psi_1\rangle
+|c_2|^2\langle\psi_2|A_2|\psi_2\rangle,
$$

which is independent of $\varphi$. The mixed state gives the same answer. The relative phase is not measurable by the observable algebra.

</details>

### Exercise 2: Charge sectors from a commuting charge

Suppose a self-adjoint charge $Q$ commutes with every observable $A\in\mathcal A_{\mathrm{loc}}$. Let $|q\rangle$ and $|q'\rangle$ be eigenstates of $Q$ with $q\neq q'$. Show that $\langle q|A|q'\rangle=0$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Since $[Q,A]=0$,

$$
\langle q|QA|q'\rangle=\langle q|AQ|q'\rangle.
$$

The left side is

$$
q\langle q|A|q'\rangle,
$$

while the right side is

$$
q'\langle q|A|q'\rangle.
$$

Thus

$$
(q-q')\langle q|A|q'\rangle=0.
$$

Since $q\neq q'$, the matrix element vanishes. The charge eigenvalue labels superselection sectors for the algebra commuting with $Q$.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Broken Ising sectors

In an infinite-volume broken $\mathbb Z_2$ phase, suppose $\langle\phi\rangle_+=v$ and $\langle\phi\rangle_-=-v$. Explain why a local operator supported in a bounded region cannot map $|\Omega_+\rangle$ to $|\Omega_-\rangle$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A local operator can affect only a bounded region. Acting on $|\Omega_+\rangle$, it may create particles, local disturbances, or a finite defect near its support. Far away from the support, the state still has order parameter $+v$. The state $|\Omega_-\rangle$ has order parameter $-v$ at spatial infinity. Changing that asymptotic order parameter requires a global operation, a boundary condition change, or a domain wall extending to infinity, not a compactly supported local observable. Thus the two vacua are different sectors for the local algebra.

</details>

### Exercise 4: Algebra dependence

Consider a theory with a global $U(1)$ symmetry and a charged local field $\psi$. If the observable algebra contains only $U(1)$-invariant local operators, charge sectors are superselected. What changes if $\psi$ itself is included among allowed local operators?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Invariant local operators commute with the total $U(1)$ charge, so they preserve charge sectors. The argument of Exercise 2 applies. But a charged field $\psi$ has nonzero charge and maps states of one charge to states of another. If $\psi$ is included in the allowed local algebra, then the old charge sectors are no longer superselected with respect to that enlarged algebra. This illustrates why superselection is always relative to the chosen algebra of physical operations.

</details>
