## Summary

A **zero mode** is an eigenfunction of the quadratic fluctuation operator with zero eigenvalue. If $\theta_s$ is a semiclassical saddle and $\theta=\theta_s+\theta_{\mathrm{fluc}}$, the Euclidean action begins as

$$
S_E[\theta_s+\theta_{\mathrm{fluc}}]
=
S_E[\theta_s]
+
\frac12(\theta_{\mathrm{fluc}},\,\Delta_s\theta_{\mathrm{fluc}})+\cdots .
$$

A bosonic zero mode $Z$ obeys

$$
\Delta_s Z=0.
$$

It has no quadratic restoring force. Therefore the ordinary Gaussian factor $\lambda^{-1/2}$ is not defined at $\lambda=0$, and the determinant around the saddle must be written as $\det{}^{\prime}\Delta_s$, with the zero eigenvalues omitted.

Zero modes are not a nuisance to hide under the rug. They usually carry the physics of the saddle: its position, size, orientation, phase, broken symmetry, fermionic selection rules, or gauge redundancy. The correct treatment depends on what kind of zero mode it is.

The most important slogan is:

$$
\text{zero modes are not small oscillators.}
$$

Small oscillators contribute determinants. Exact zero modes must be separated before the determinant is computed.

## Core idea

Suppose $\Delta_s$ has orthonormal eigenfunctions $u_n$,

$$
\Delta_s u_n=\lambda_nu_n,
\qquad
(u_m,u_n)=\delta_{mn},
$$

and expand a bosonic fluctuation as

$$
\theta_{\mathrm{fluc}}=\sum_n c_nu_n.
$$

The quadratic action becomes

$$
\frac12(\theta_{\mathrm{fluc}},\Delta_s\theta_{\mathrm{fluc}})
=
\frac12\sum_n\lambda_n c_n^2.
$$

For $\lambda_n>0$, the corresponding Gaussian integral is

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dc_n}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}
\exp\left(-\frac{\lambda_nc_n^2}{2\hbar}\right)
=
\lambda_n^{-1/2}.
$$

For $\lambda_n=0$, the same expression degenerates into

$$
\int \frac{dc_n}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}},
$$

with no Gaussian suppression. That integral is either divergent, finite only because of an artificial regulator, or not the right variable at all. The right move is to ask: **what does this zero direction represent?**

There are three recurring answers.

| Kind of zero mode | What it means | What replaces the determinant factor |
|---|---|---|
| Physical bosonic zero mode | A continuous family of distinct saddles. | Integral over collective coordinates with a Jacobian. |
| Gauge zero mode | A redundancy in the description. | Gauge fixing, ghosts, and division by the gauge group. |
| Fermion zero mode | A Grassmann direction with no quadratic action. | Grassmann integration and selection rules for operator insertions. |

A fourth object, the **negative mode**, is different. A negative eigenvalue is not a zero mode. It signals an unstable saddle direction and requires a contour prescription. Bounce saddles for false-vacuum decay have one negative mode; ordinary stable instanton or soliton saddles do not.

## Zero modes from saddle families

The most common zero modes are generated by differentiating a family of saddles. Let

$$
\theta_s(x;\gamma^1,\ldots,\gamma^k)
$$

solve the saddle equation for every value of $\gamma^a$:

$$
\left.\frac{\delta S_E}{\delta\theta(x)}\right|_{\theta_s(\gamma)}=0.
$$

Differentiate this equation with respect to $\gamma^a$:

$$
0=
\frac{\partial}{\partial\gamma^a}
\left.\frac{\delta S_E}{\delta\theta(x)}\right|_{\theta_s(\gamma)}
=
\int d^dy\,\Delta_s(x,y)
\frac{\partial\theta_s(y;\gamma)}{\partial\gamma^a}.
$$

Therefore

$$
Z_a(x)=\frac{\partial\theta_s(x;\gamma)}{\partial\gamma^a}
$$

is a zero mode:

$$
\Delta_s Z_a=0.
$$

This is the basic theorem behind translational zero modes, rotational zero modes, scale zero modes, internal-orientation zero modes, and moduli of BPS solitons.

The induced metric on the family is

$$
G_{ab}=(Z_a,Z_b),
$$

so the zero-mode part of the bosonic measure is locally

$$
d\mu_0
=
\frac{d^k\gamma}{(2\pi\hbar)^{k/2}}\sqrt{\det G(\gamma)}.
$$

The remaining nonzero Gaussian modes give $\det{}^{\prime}\Delta_s$.

Thus the schematic one-saddle contribution has the form

$$
Z_s
\sim
 e^{-S_E[\theta_s]/\hbar}
\int d\mu_0\,
(\det{}^{\prime}\Delta_s)^{-1/2}
\left(1+O(\hbar)\right),
$$

up to normalization, determinant ratios, gauge-fixing factors, ghost determinants, and higher-loop corrections.

## Translation zero mode

Let $\phi_s(x-X)$ be a localized saddle whose center is $X^\mu$. Translation invariance gives a family of saddles with the same action. The zero modes are

$$
Z_\mu(x)=\frac{\partial\phi_s(x-X)}{\partial X^\mu}
=-\partial_\mu\phi_s(x-X).
$$

For a one-dimensional Euclidean instanton $q_{\mathrm{inst}}(\tau-\tau_0)$,

$$
Z_{\tau_0}(\tau)
=
-\dot q_{\mathrm{inst}}(\tau-\tau_0).
$$

If the instanton has zero Euclidean energy, then

$$
S_0
=
\int d\tau\,\dot q_{\mathrm{inst}}^2,
$$

so the norm of the translation zero mode is

$$
G_{\tau_0\tau_0}
=
\int d\tau\,\dot q_{\mathrm{inst}}^2
=S_0.
$$

The translation zero-mode measure is therefore

$$
\sqrt{\frac{S_0}{2\pi\hbar}}\,d\tau_0.
$$

This factor is one of the first places where students see that the prefactor of an instanton effect is not arbitrary decoration. It is fixed by the geometry of the saddle family.

## Rotations, phases, sizes, and orientations

Translations are only the simplest example. Other continuous parameters generate other zero modes.

A soliton may have a rotational orientation. A vortex may have a position in the plane and an internal phase. A Yang–Mills instanton on $\mathbb R^4$ has a center $x_0$, size $\rho$, and gauge-orientation moduli. A BPS monopole has a position and a phase even in the one-monopole sector, and multi-monopole sectors have richer moduli spaces.

The rule is always the same:

$$
\text{differentiate the saddle with respect to a physical parameter.}
$$

But there is one catch. In gauge theories, the derivative must be projected into the gauge-fixing slice. A naive derivative of a gauge field with respect to a modulus usually fails the background gauge condition. The corrected zero mode has the form

$$
Z_a=\frac{\partial A_s}{\partial\gamma^a}-D_s\omega_a,
$$

where $\omega_a$ is chosen so that

$$
D_s^\mu Z_{a\mu}=0.
$$

This is not cosmetic. The gauge-adjusted zero mode is what belongs in the physical moduli-space metric.

## Gauge zero modes

Gauge transformations produce directions in field space of the form

$$
\delta_\omega A_\mu=D_\mu\omega.
$$

These are not automatically physical collective coordinates. Many of them are pure gauge copies of the same configuration. Integrating over them would double-count the same field configuration infinitely many times.

The standard treatment is:

1. impose a gauge condition, such as background gauge;
2. include the Faddeev–Popov ghost determinant;
3. remove gauge zero modes from the fluctuation determinant;
4. integrate only over genuine moduli, meaning gauge-inequivalent deformations that preserve the boundary conditions.

A useful distinction is:

$$
\text{small gauge transformation} \neq \text{global orientation modulus}.
$$

A gauge transformation that approaches the identity at infinity is a redundancy. A transformation that changes the embedding or orientation of the saddle relative to fixed boundary data can label a physically distinct point in moduli space. This distinction is model- and boundary-condition-dependent, so gauge-theory semiclassics are less plug-and-play than scalar instantons.

## Fermion zero modes

Fermions behave differently because their path integrals are Grassmann integrals. Let the Euclidean fermion quadratic action be

$$
S_F=\int d^dx\,\bar\psi\, \mathcal D_A\psi,
$$

where $\mathcal D_A$ is a Dirac-type operator in the saddle background $A$. If

$$
\mathcal D_A\psi_i^{(0)}=0,
$$

then expand

$$
\psi(x)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\xi_i\psi_i^{(0)}(x)+\psi_{\perp}(x),
$$

where $\xi_i$ are Grassmann coefficients. The zero-mode coefficients do not appear in the quadratic action. Therefore

$$
\int d\xi_i\,1=0,
\qquad
\int d\xi_i\,\xi_i=1.
$$

A path integral with unsaturated fermion zero modes vanishes. A correlation function is nonzero only if the operator insertions contain enough fermion fields to supply every zero-mode Grassmann variable. This is the origin of instanton selection rules and effective multi-fermion vertices.

For example, in a background with topological charge $Q$, the index theorem relates the chirality imbalance of fermion zero modes to $Q$. In a four-dimensional gauge theory, with common normalization conventions,

$$
n_+ - n_- = 2T(R)Q,
$$

where $R$ is the representation of the fermion and $T(R)$ is defined by

$$
\operatorname{tr}_R(T^aT^b)=T(R)\delta^{ab}.
$$

The sign convention for chirality depends on how $Q$ and $\gamma^5$ are defined, but the invariant lesson is robust: topologically nontrivial gauge backgrounds force chiral fermion zero modes, and those zero modes impose selection rules.

## Normalizable versus non-normalizable modes

Only normalizable zero modes are integrated over as collective coordinates. Normalizability is determined by the same inner product used to define the fluctuation operator.

A non-normalizable deformation usually changes the boundary condition, coupling, source, or asymptotic vacuum. It is not an ordinary fluctuation around the same saddle. In infinite volume this distinction can be subtle. A mode whose norm diverges logarithmically is especially treacherous: it may require an infrared regulator and an interpretation in terms of bulk degrees of freedom rather than a localized soliton modulus.

This is why finite volume is a useful training ground. It discretizes the spectrum and makes the distinction between exact zero modes, small eigenvalues, and continuum threshold modes visible.

## Approximate and lifted zero modes

A mode can be “almost zero” without being an exact zero mode. This happens when a symmetry is weakly broken, when two saddles interact weakly, or when a modulus is lifted by quantum corrections.

Suppose $\gamma$ would be a collective coordinate in an idealized limit, but the action along the valley is not exactly constant:

$$
S_E[\theta_s(\gamma)]
=
S_0+V_{\mathrm{eff}}(\gamma).
$$

Then $\gamma$ is not a zero mode of the exact problem. The correct approximation is not to put a tiny eigenvalue into a determinant blindly. It is often better to keep $\gamma$ as a quasi-collective coordinate and integrate

$$
\int d\gamma\,J(\gamma)
\exp\left[-\frac{S_0+V_{\mathrm{eff}}(\gamma)}{\hbar}\right].
$$

This is common in instanton–anti-instanton pairs, constrained instantons, finite-temperature saddles, and multi-soliton dynamics.

## Relation to negative modes

Zero modes and negative modes are sometimes confused because both obstruct the naive positive Gaussian determinant. They mean different things.

| Mode | Eigenvalue | Meaning | Treatment |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Positive mode | $\lambda>0$ | Stable fluctuation. | Determinant factor. |
| Zero mode | $\lambda=0$ | Symmetry, modulus, gauge redundancy, or fermion selection rule. | Collective coordinate, gauge fixing, or Grassmann saturation. |
| Negative mode | $\lambda<0$ | Instability of the saddle. | Contour deformation; often an imaginary part. |

A false-vacuum bounce has one negative mode because it is a saddle for decay, not a minimum of the Euclidean action. An instanton mediating tunneling between degenerate vacua usually has zero modes but no negative mode. A sphaleron has unstable directions in real-time configuration space. Lumping these together is a reliable way to get the wrong physics.

## Practical checklist

When a fluctuation operator has zero eigenvalues, ask these questions in order.

1. **Is the zero mode exact?** If it depends on an approximation or an infinite-volume limit, treat it as a quasi-zero mode.
2. **Is it normalizable?** If not, it probably changes boundary data rather than describing a collective coordinate.
3. **Is it a gauge redundancy?** If yes, fix the gauge and do not integrate over gauge copies.
4. **Does it come from a physical family of saddles?** If yes, use collective coordinates and the induced metric.
5. **Is it fermionic?** If yes, remember that unsaturated Grassmann zero modes make the path integral vanish.
6. **Is there a negative mode nearby?** If yes, the saddle may compute a decay rate or require a nontrivial contour.
7. **Does the moduli integral converge?** If not, the semiclassical approximation may need an infrared regulator or may not be justified.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: putting zero eigenvalues into the determinant.** The determinant around a saddle with zero modes is a primed determinant. The omitted modes must reappear somewhere else.

**Mistake 2: treating gauge zero modes as physical moduli.** Gauge copies are redundant descriptions. Physical orientation moduli survive only after quotienting by the allowed gauge transformations.

**Mistake 3: forgetting fermion zero-mode saturation.** A fermion determinant can vanish in a topologically nontrivial background. Nonzero correlators require insertions that soak up the Grassmann zero modes.

**Mistake 4: confusing a modulus with a parameter in the Lagrangian.** A modulus is integrated over because it labels a saddle in the same theory. A coupling labels a different theory.

**Mistake 5: ignoring normalizability.** A non-normalizable “mode” is often a variation of boundary conditions, not a degree of freedom of the saddle.

**Mistake 6: using infinite-volume intuition too early.** Finite volume cleanly separates exact zero modes from continuum modes. Taking the infinite-volume limit before classifying the spectrum can hide the real issue.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Differentiating a family of saddles

Let $\theta_s(x;\gamma)$ solve the saddle equation for every $\gamma$. Show that $Z=\partial_\gamma\theta_s$ is a zero mode of the Hessian.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The saddle equation is

$$
\left.\frac{\delta S_E}{\delta\theta(x)}\right|_{\theta_s(\gamma)}=0.
$$

Differentiating with respect to $\gamma$ gives

$$
0
=
\int d^dy\,
\left.\frac{\delta^2S_E}{\delta\theta(x)\delta\theta(y)}\right|_{\theta_s}
\frac{\partial\theta_s(y;\gamma)}{\partial\gamma}.
$$

The kernel multiplying $\partial_\gamma\theta_s$ is the Hessian $\Delta_s$. Hence

$$
\Delta_s\,\partial_\gamma\theta_s=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Translation zero mode of a kink

Let $\phi_k(z-z_0)$ be a static kink solution in one spatial dimension. Show that $\partial_{z_0}\phi_k(z-z_0)$ is a zero mode of the small-fluctuation operator.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The static field equation can be written schematically as

$$
\mathcal E[\phi_k(z-z_0)]=0
$$

for every $z_0$, because translating the kink gives another solution with the same energy. Differentiating with respect to $z_0$ gives

$$
\left.\frac{\delta\mathcal E}{\delta\phi}\right|_{\phi_k}
\frac{\partial\phi_k(z-z_0)}{\partial z_0}=0.
$$

The linearized equation operator is precisely the quadratic fluctuation operator. Therefore $\partial_{z_0}\phi_k$ is a zero mode. Since

$$
\frac{\partial\phi_k(z-z_0)}{\partial z_0}=-\frac{d\phi_k(z-z_0)}{dz},
$$

the translation zero mode is proportional to the spatial derivative of the kink profile.

</details>

### Exercise 3: Grassmann saturation

Suppose a fermion path integral has one zero-mode coefficient $\xi$ and no operator insertion depends on $\xi$. Show that the contribution vanishes. What happens if an insertion contributes a factor proportional to $\xi$?

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For a Grassmann variable,

$$
\int d\xi\,1=0,
\qquad
\int d\xi\,\xi=1.
$$

If the action does not contain $\xi$ and the insertion also does not contain $\xi$, the zero-mode part of the integral is $\int d\xi\,1=0$. If an insertion contains a factor $C\xi$, where $C$ is independent of $\xi$, then the zero-mode integral is

$$
\int d\xi\,C\xi=C.
$$

With $n$ fermion zero modes, a nonzero contribution requires enough insertions to provide the product of all $n$ independent Grassmann coefficients.

</details>
