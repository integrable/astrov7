## Summary

A **collective coordinate** is a coordinate on a continuous family of semiclassical saddles. If

$$
\phi_s(x;\gamma^1,\ldots,\gamma^k)
$$

solves the Euclidean equations of motion for every value of the parameters $\gamma^a$, then changing $\gamma^a$ moves along a flat direction of the action. The tangent vectors

$$
Z_a(x)=\frac{\partial\phi_s(x;\gamma)}{\partial\gamma^a}
$$

are zero modes of the quadratic fluctuation operator, up to gauge-fixing projections when gauge symmetry is present. The Gaussian integral over these directions is divergent because the quadratic action gives no restoring force. The cure is not to invent a small eigenvalue. The cure is to change variables and integrate over the saddle family itself.

For $k$ bosonic collective coordinates, the zero-mode measure has the schematic form

$$
d\mu_0
=
\frac{d^k\gamma}{(2\pi\hbar)^{k/2}}\sqrt{\det G(\gamma)},
\qquad
G_{ab}=(Z_a,Z_b),
$$

where $G_{ab}$ is the metric induced on the moduli space by the field-space inner product. This factor replaces the divergent zero-mode Gaussian integral and accompanies the primed determinant $\det{}^{\prime}\Delta_s$.

Collective coordinates are the practical language of instanton positions, soliton centers, kink momenta, gauge orientations, sizes, phases, and moduli-space dynamics. They are also where many semiclassical errors enter, usually by double-counting gauge copies or forgetting the Jacobian.

## Core idea

The saddle-point expansion assumes that the action is approximately quadratic in every fluctuation direction. Collective coordinates appear when that assumption fails for a good reason: the saddle is part of a continuous family.

The simplest example is translation. If $\phi_s(x)$ is a localized Euclidean saddle in infinite space, then

$$
\phi_s(x-X)
$$

has the same action for every center $X$. The path integral should include all possible centers. Expanding around only $X=0$ and then integrating over the translation zero mode as if it were a harmonic oscillator is the wrong coordinate system.

The correct picture is

$$
\text{field configuration}
=
\text{saddle with collective coordinates}
+
\text{fluctuations normal to the saddle family}.
$$

Symbolically,

$$
\phi(x)=\phi_s(x;\gamma)+\eta_\perp(x),
$$

where $\eta_\perp$ is constrained to be orthogonal to the zero-mode directions. The zero modes are no longer part of the determinant. They are part of the measure over $\gamma$.

## Setup and conventions

Let $S_E[\phi]$ be a Euclidean action and let

$$
\frac{\delta S_E}{\delta\phi(x)}\bigg|_{\phi_s(\gamma)}=0
$$

for a family of configurations $\phi_s(\gamma)$. The fluctuation operator is

$$
\Delta_s(x,y)
=
\left.\frac{\delta^2S_E}{\delta\phi(x)\delta\phi(y)}\right|_{\phi_s(\gamma)}.
$$

Differentiate the saddle equation with respect to $\gamma^a$:

$$
0
=
\frac{\partial}{\partial\gamma^a}
\left.\frac{\delta S_E}{\delta\phi(x)}\right|_{\phi_s(\gamma)}
=
\int d^dy\,\Delta_s(x,y)\frac{\partial\phi_s(y;\gamma)}{\partial\gamma^a}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\Delta_s Z_a=0,
\qquad
Z_a=\partial_a\phi_s.
$$

This is the basic theorem of collective coordinates: differentiating a family of exact saddles gives zero modes.

The inner product defines the metric on the saddle family:

$$
G_{ab}(\gamma)=(Z_a,Z_b).
$$

For a single real scalar in flat Euclidean space,

$$
G_{ab}=\int d^dx\,
\frac{\partial\phi_s(x;\gamma)}{\partial\gamma^a}
\frac{\partial\phi_s(x;\gamma)}{\partial\gamma^b}.
$$

For multiplets, gauge fields, fermions, and curved backgrounds, the inner product includes the appropriate traces, tensor contractions, and volume element.

## Deriving the Jacobian

Start with one zero mode. Let $u_0(x)$ be a normalized zero mode,

$$
(u_0,u_0)=1,
$$

and suppose the saddle family is parametrized by $\gamma$. A small change in the collective coordinate gives

$$
\delta\phi_s(x)=Z_\gamma(x)d\gamma,
\qquad
Z_\gamma=\frac{\partial\phi_s}{\partial\gamma}.
$$

The same displacement may be written in the normalized zero-mode coordinate $c_0$ as

$$
\delta\phi_s(x)=u_0(x)dc_0.
$$

Taking norms gives

$$
dc_0^2=(Z_\gamma,Z_\gamma)d\gamma^2.
$$

Thus

$$
dc_0=\sqrt{G_{\gamma\gamma}}\,d\gamma.
$$

With the normalized path-integral convention in which a Gaussian mode carries $dc/\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}$, the zero-mode factor becomes

$$
\frac{dc_0}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}
=
\sqrt{\frac{G_{\gamma\gamma}}{2\pi\hbar}}\,d\gamma.
$$

For $k$ collective coordinates the same argument gives

$$
\prod_{i=1}^k\frac{dc_i}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}
=
\frac{d^k\gamma}{(2\pi\hbar)^{k/2}}\sqrt{\det G(\gamma)}.
$$

This is the origin of the collective-coordinate measure. The determinant factor in the one-loop formula must be primed because the corresponding $k$ eigenvalues have been replaced by this measure.

## Translation example: an instanton center

For a one-dimensional Euclidean instanton $q_{\mathrm{inst}}(\tau-\tau_0)$, the translation collective coordinate is $\tau_0$. The zero mode is

$$
Z_{\tau_0}(\tau)=\frac{\partial q_{\mathrm{inst}}(\tau-\tau_0)}{\partial\tau_0}
=-\dot q_{\mathrm{inst}}(\tau-\tau_0).
$$

Its norm is

$$
G_{\tau_0\tau_0}
=
\int d\tau\,\dot q_{\mathrm{inst}}^2.
$$

For the usual zero-energy instanton trajectory in quantum mechanics, this equals the instanton action,

$$
G_{\tau_0\tau_0}=S_{\mathrm{inst}}.
$$

Therefore the zero-mode measure contributes

$$
d\mu_0
= d\tau_0\sqrt{\frac{S_{\mathrm{inst}}}{2\pi\hbar}}.
$$

This factor is famous because it explains why a single-instanton contribution in a long Euclidean time interval is proportional to the interval length. The instanton can occur anywhere in time. Dividing by the total time then gives an energy shift, splitting, or rate, depending on the problem.

## Translation example: a kink center

In $1+1$ Lorentzian dimensions, a static kink centered at $X$ has profile

$$
\phi_K(x-X).
$$

The zero mode is

$$
Z_X(x)=\frac{\partial\phi_K(x-X)}{\partial X}=-\partial_x\phi_K(x-X).
$$

The moduli-space metric is

$$
G_{XX}=\int dx\,\left(\partial_x\phi_K\right)^2.
$$

If the kink is slowly moving, take

$$
\phi(t,x)=\phi_K(x-X(t)).
$$

Substitution into the Lorentzian action gives, to leading order in velocity,

$$
L_{\mathrm{eff}}=-M_K+\frac12G_{XX}\dot X^2.
$$

For standard relativistic scalar kinks, $G_{XX}=M_K$ after using the classical equations and the energy relation for the soliton. Then

$$
L_{\mathrm{eff}}=-M_K+\frac12M_K\dot X^2,
$$

which is exactly the nonrelativistic expansion of a particle of mass $M_K$. This is the simplest glimpse of soliton quantization: a classical lump becomes a quantum particle because its center is a collective coordinate.

## Several collective coordinates

A typical instanton or soliton has more than one collective coordinate. Common examples include:

| Collective coordinate | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Position $x_0^\mu$ | Where the localized saddle sits. | Instantons, bounces, monopoles, vortices, kinks. |
| Phase | Global internal $U(1)$ orientation. | Q-balls, charged solitons, some sigma-model lumps. |
| Gauge orientation | Embedding of a solution inside a gauge group. | Yang–Mills instantons, monopoles. |
| Size $\rho$ | Scale of a classically scale-invariant saddle. | BPST instanton in classical Yang–Mills theory. |
| Shape moduli | Continuous deformation preserving the action or energy. | BPS solitons, vortices, monopoles. |
| Fermionic moduli | Grassmann coordinates from fermion zero modes. | Supersymmetric instantons and solitons. |

For multiple coordinates, the metric need not be diagonal. The measure is then controlled by the full Gram determinant:

$$
\sqrt{\det G_{ab}}.
$$

If the zero modes are not orthogonal, replacing the measure by a product of individual norms is wrong. The determinant of the metric is the invariant object.

## Gauge theories and quotienting

Gauge theories require extra care because some directions in field space are not physical motion. A gauge transformation can change the representative of a field configuration without changing the physical configuration. Such gauge-copy directions must be fixed or quotiented, not counted as collective coordinates.

A standard strategy is to impose a background gauge condition on fluctuations. For a gauge field saddle $A_s$, one writes

$$
A=A_s(\gamma)+a
$$

and restricts physical fluctuations by a condition such as

$$
D_s^\mu a_\mu=0.
$$

Then variations of the saddle with respect to moduli must be projected so that the zero modes also satisfy the gauge condition. Schematically,

$$
Z_{a,\mu}
=\frac{\partial A_{s,\mu}}{\partial\gamma^a}-D_{s,\mu}\Omega_a,
\qquad
D_s^\mu Z_{a,\mu}=0.
$$

The functions $\Omega_a$ remove the pure-gauge part of the variation. Physical collective coordinates are not gauge transformations that vanish nowhere; they are parameters that produce genuinely distinct gauge-equivalence classes of saddle configurations, possibly subject to boundary conditions.

This distinction matters acutely for instantons. The instanton center and size are physical moduli. Some global gauge orientations are also moduli, depending on the gauge group and embedding. Ordinary local gauge wiggles are not.

## Fermionic collective coordinates

For fermions, zero modes are Grassmann directions. They are not integrated with an ordinary volume measure. If a fermion operator $D$ has zero modes, expand

$$
\psi(x)=\sum_{\alpha}\eta^\alpha \psi_\alpha^{(0)}(x)+\psi_\perp(x),
$$

where $\eta^\alpha$ are Grassmann collective coordinates. The fermionic path integral contains

$$
\prod_\alpha d\eta^\alpha
$$

and the determinant or Pfaffian is taken over nonzero modes.

A correlation function vanishes unless its operator insertions contain enough fermion fields to soak up the Grassmann integrations. This is why instantons with fermion zero modes generate selection rules and effective multi-fermion operators rather than ordinary vacuum-energy contributions.

In supersymmetric theories, bosonic and fermionic collective coordinates often pair into supermoduli. Supersymmetry can also enforce cancellations between bosonic and fermionic nonzero-mode determinants, making the collective-coordinate measure the dominant nontrivial part of the calculation.

## Volume factors and rates

Collective coordinates often produce extensive factors. If a localized Euclidean saddle in $d$ dimensions has a position $x_0$, then

$$
\int d^dx_0
$$

appears in the measure. In a large Euclidean volume $\mathcal V_d$, this gives a factor proportional to $\mathcal V_d$.

That is not a mistake. It expresses the fact that the event can occur anywhere. To extract an intensive quantity one divides by the corresponding volume.

For example:

- an instanton in quantum mechanics can occur anywhere in Euclidean time, producing a factor $T$;
- a bounce in $d$ Euclidean spacetime dimensions can occur anywhere in spacetime, producing a factor $\mathcal V_d$;
- a domain wall extended in space has collective coordinates associated with its position and shape, leading to worldvolume dynamics rather than a point-event rate.

This is the same logic behind the extraction of decay rates from bounce contributions and energy densities from partition functions.

## Lifted and approximate collective coordinates

Not every small eigenvalue is an exact zero mode. Sometimes a classical theory has a modulus, but quantum corrections, boundary conditions, mass terms, finite temperature, or interactions lift it. Then the collective coordinate acquires an effective potential.

Instead of

$$
S_E[\phi_s(\gamma)]={\mathrm{constant}},
$$

one has

$$
S_{\mathrm{eff}}(\gamma)=S_0+V_{\mathrm{eff}}(\gamma)+\cdots.
$$

The integration over $\gamma$ is still useful, but it is no longer a pure volume factor. This is common for instanton sizes, soliton separations, quasi-zero modes in instanton–anti-instanton configurations, and moduli in theories where exact BPS protection is broken.

Approximate collective coordinates are useful but dangerous. They require a hierarchy: the quasi-zero mode must be much lighter than the modes integrated out. Without such a hierarchy, separating “collective” and “noncollective” motion is merely a guess.

## Moduli-space dynamics

In Lorentzian soliton problems, collective coordinates can become dynamical low-energy variables. Let $\gamma^a(t)$ vary slowly and approximate

$$
\phi(t,\mathbf x)=\phi_s(\mathbf x;\gamma(t)).
$$

Substituting into the action often gives

$$
L_{\mathrm{eff}}
= -M+\frac12G_{ab}(\gamma)\dot\gamma^a\dot\gamma^b+\text{gauge potentials}+\text{higher derivatives}+\text{potentials}.
$$

Thus the moduli-space metric $G_{ab}$ controls slow soliton motion. In favorable BPS systems, this geodesic approximation is quantitatively powerful. In generic non-BPS systems, radiation, lifted moduli, and interactions can spoil the approximation.

The Euclidean collective-coordinate measure and Lorentzian moduli-space dynamics are two faces of the same geometry: both come from the metric induced on the family of classical solutions.

## Common mistakes

**Leaving zero modes in the determinant.** The determinant must be primed over nonzero normal modes. The zero modes are integrated as collective coordinates.

**Forgetting the Jacobian.** Replacing $dc_0$ by $d\gamma$ without the factor $\sqrt{G}$ gives the wrong dimensions and normalization.

**Counting gauge copies as moduli.** Gauge transformations are redundancies unless boundary conditions or global embeddings make them physical. Gauge fixing and quotienting are not optional.

**Assuming all small eigenvalues are exact moduli.** A small eigenvalue may be a lifted modulus, a finite-volume artifact, or a physical low-energy vibration. The distinction matters.

**Double-counting.** Do not integrate over both a zero-mode coefficient and the corresponding collective coordinate.

**Ignoring the induced metric.** For several moduli, the measure involves $\sqrt{\det G}$, not a product of guessed factors unless the coordinates are orthogonal and normalized.

## Limitations and caveats

Collective-coordinate formulas are local on moduli space. If the moduli space has boundaries, singularities, orbifold points, gauge identifications, non-normalizable directions, or infrared-divergent modes, the measure needs additional analysis.

Some moduli are exact only classically. For example, scale invariance can be broken by running couplings, and a classical size modulus can become a direction weighted by a nontrivial effective action. Similarly, multi-instanton and soliton-separated moduli may interact at large but finite separation.

In finite volume, exact symmetries may be modified by boundary conditions. In infinite volume, normalizability determines whether a zero mode is a genuine collective coordinate of the localized object or a bulk fluctuation. Modes with logarithmically divergent norms live in the annoying but important middle zone; they cannot be classified by slogan.

## Research status

- **Established:** Collective coordinates are the standard treatment of exact bosonic zero modes in semiclassical instanton, soliton, and bounce calculations.
- **Established:** The induced metric on the saddle family determines the Jacobian from normalized zero-mode coefficients to moduli-space coordinates.
- **Active:** Measures on singular moduli spaces, quasi-zero modes, fermionic moduli in supersymmetric gauge theories, and infrared-sensitive zero modes remain important in current research.
- **Model-dependent:** Whether a coordinate is exact, lifted, gauge, non-normalizable, or dynamical depends on the theory, boundary conditions, and observable.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: differentiating a family of saddles

Suppose $\phi_s(\gamma)$ satisfies

$$
\left.\frac{\delta S_E}{\delta\phi(x)}\right|_{\phi_s(\gamma)}=0
$$

for every $\gamma$. Show that $Z_\gamma=\partial\phi_s/\partial\gamma$ is a zero mode of the Hessian.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Differentiate the saddle equation with respect to $\gamma$:

$$
0=
\frac{\partial}{\partial\gamma}
\left.\frac{\delta S_E}{\delta\phi(x)}\right|_{\phi_s(\gamma)}.
$$

Using the chain rule,

$$
0=
\int d^dy\,
\left.\frac{\delta^2 S_E}{\delta\phi(x)\delta\phi(y)}\right|_{\phi_s}
\frac{\partial\phi_s(y;\gamma)}{\partial\gamma}.
$$

The operator in brackets is $\Delta_s(x,y)$, so

$$
\Delta_s Z_\gamma=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: one-coordinate Jacobian

Let $Z_\gamma=\partial\phi_s/\partial\gamma$ and $G=(Z_\gamma,Z_\gamma)$. Derive the replacement

$$
\frac{dc_0}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}
=\sqrt{\frac{G}{2\pi\hbar}}\,d\gamma
$$

for a normalized zero-mode coefficient $c_0$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A displacement along the saddle family can be written in two ways:

$$
\delta\phi=Z_\gamma d\gamma=u_0 dc_0,
$$

where $u_0$ is normalized, $(u_0,u_0)=1$. Taking the field-space norm gives

$$
(\delta\phi,\delta\phi)=Gd\gamma^2=dc_0^2.
$$

Thus $dc_0=\sqrt G\,d\gamma$. Dividing by $\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}$ gives the stated formula.

</details>

### Exercise 3: effective kinetic term for a kink center

Let $\phi(t,x)=\phi_K(x-X(t))$ in a relativistic scalar theory with kinetic term $\frac12\dot\phi^2$. Show that the collective coordinate has kinetic term

$$
\frac12G_{XX}\dot X^2,
\qquad
G_{XX}=\int dx\,(\partial_x\phi_K)^2.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Since

$$
\dot\phi(t,x)=\frac{\partial\phi_K(x-X(t))}{\partial X}\dot X
=-\partial_x\phi_K(x-X(t))\dot X,
$$

the kinetic part of the Lagrangian is

$$
\int dx\,\frac12\dot\phi^2
=\frac12\dot X^2\int dx\,(\partial_x\phi_K)^2.
$$

Thus

$$
L_{\mathrm{kin}}=\frac12G_{XX}\dot X^2,
\qquad
G_{XX}=\int dx\,(\partial_x\phi_K)^2.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 4: volume factor from translational moduli

A localized Euclidean saddle in $d$ dimensions has $d$ translational collective coordinates $x_0^\mu$. Explain why its contribution to $Z$ is proportional to the Euclidean spacetime volume, and why this is not an overcounting error.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The translated configurations $\phi_s(x-x_0)$ are distinct saddle configurations centered at different points, assuming translations are a genuine symmetry and the saddle is localized. The collective-coordinate measure contains

$$
\int d^dx_0.
$$

In a large box this integral gives the Euclidean spacetime volume $\mathcal V_d$. This is not overcounting; it counts the number of possible locations where the event can occur. To extract an intensive quantity such as an energy density or decay rate per volume, one divides by the corresponding volume factor.

</details>
