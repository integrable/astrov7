## Summary

The electroweak gauge group is locally

$$
G_{\rm EW}=SU(2)_L\times U(1)_Y.
$$

Here “locally” means that this page fixes the Lie algebra, gauge fields, couplings, and representations. Possible global quotients by common center elements are physical in questions about line operators and global charge quantization, but they are best treated together with color in the full Standard Model gauge-group page.

The local electroweak theory has two independent gauge couplings, $g$ and $g'$, and four gauge fields before symmetry breaking:

$$
W_\mu^1,W_\mu^2,W_\mu^3,
\qquad
B_\mu.
$$

The $SU(2)_L$ fields couple to weak-isospin generators $T^a$, while $B_\mu$ couples to hypercharge $Y$. With the conventions of this volume, the electroweak covariant derivative is

$$
D_\mu
=
\partial_\mu
+igW_\mu^aT^a
+ig'YB_\mu.
$$

The key point is that the electromagnetic charge is not one of the original two labels by itself. In this volume we use

$$
Q=T^3+Y.
$$

That combination becomes the unbroken gauge generator only after the Higgs doublet chooses a neutral vacuum direction. So the photon is not simply $B_\mu$, and the $Z$ boson is not simply $W_\mu^3$. The physical neutral gauge bosons are mixtures of both.

## Core idea

A gauge theory is specified locally by three pieces of data:

1. a gauge group;
2. gauge fields valued in the Lie algebra;
3. representations of the matter fields.

For electroweak theory the gauge group is a product. A field therefore carries two kinds of electroweak representation data:

$$
\text{field}\quad \longmapsto\quad
(\text{representation of }SU(2)_L,\;Y).
$$

The product structure means that the two factors commute on every field:

$$
[T^a,Y]=0.
$$

This does not mean that the physics is just a direct sum of two unrelated forces. The Higgs field carries both weak isospin and hypercharge. Its vacuum direction ties the two neutral gauge fields together and leaves only the diagonal combination generated by $Q=T^3+Y$ unbroken.

The result is the electroweak pattern:

$$
\underbrace{SU(2)_L\times U(1)_Y}_{\text{gauge basis}}
\xrightarrow{\;\langle H\rangle\;}
\underbrace{U(1)_{\rm em}}_{\text{unbroken physical electromagnetism}}.
$$

The Standard Model hides this beautifully compact story behind a zoo of letters: $W^\pm$, $Z$, $\gamma$, $g$, $g'$, $e$, $\theta_W$, $T^3$, $Y$, $Q$. The page you are reading is the translation table.

## Setup and conventions

Throughout this volume,

$$
D_\mu=\partial_\mu+igA_\mu^aT^a,
$$

so the non-Abelian field strength is

$$
F_{\mu\nu}^a
=
\partial_\mu A_\nu^a-
\partial_\nu A_\mu^a
-gf^{abc}A_\mu^bA_\nu^c.
$$

For $SU(2)_L$ we use generators satisfying

$$
[T^a,T^b]=i\epsilon^{abc}T^c.
$$

On a weak doublet,

$$
T^a=\frac{\sigma^a}{2},
$$

where $\sigma^a$ are the Pauli matrices. On a weak singlet,

$$
T^a=0.
$$

The electroweak connection acting on a field of hypercharge $Y$ is

$$
\mathcal A_\mu^{\rm EW}
=
gW_\mu^aT^a+g'YB_\mu.
$$

The covariant derivative is therefore

$$
D_\mu=\partial_\mu+i\mathcal A_\mu^{\rm EW}
=
\partial_\mu+igW_\mu^aT^a+ig'YB_\mu.
$$

The field strengths are

$$
W_{\mu\nu}^a
=
\partial_\mu W_\nu^a-
\partial_\nu W_\mu^a
-g\epsilon^{abc}W_\mu^bW_\nu^c,
$$

and

$$
B_{\mu\nu}=\partial_\mu B_\nu-\partial_\nu B_\mu.
$$

The gauge kinetic Lagrangian is

$$
\mathcal L_{\rm gauge}
=
-\frac14 W_{\mu\nu}^aW^{a\mu\nu}
-\frac14 B_{\mu\nu}B^{\mu\nu}.
$$

Notice a small but important fact: before symmetry breaking, a term like $W_{\mu\nu}^3B^{\mu\nu}$ is not an $SU(2)_L\times U(1)_Y$ gauge-invariant kinetic mixing term. The index $3$ is not an invariant label under local $SU(2)_L$ rotations.

## Gauge transformations

Let

$$
U_L(x)=\exp\!\big(i\alpha^a(x)T^a\big),
\qquad
U_Y(x)=\exp\!\big(i\beta(x)Y\big).
$$

A matter field transforms as

$$
\psi(x)\longmapsto
\psi'(x)=U_L(x)U_Y(x)\psi(x).
$$

The covariant derivative is defined so that

$$
D_\mu'\psi'=U_LU_YD_\mu\psi.
$$

For the Abelian factor, this gives

$$
B_\mu\longmapsto B_\mu'=B_\mu-\frac{1}{g'}\partial_\mu\beta.
$$

For the non-Abelian factor, it is cleaner to transform the matrix-valued field

$$
\mathcal W_\mu\equiv gW_\mu^aT^a.
$$

It transforms as

$$
\mathcal W_\mu
\longmapsto
\mathcal W_\mu'
=
U_L\mathcal W_\mu U_L^{-1}
+i(\partial_\mu U_L)U_L^{-1}.
$$

These signs are not universal across textbooks. They are tied to our choice $D_\mu=\partial_\mu+i\mathcal A_\mu$. If you use $D_\mu=\partial_\mu-i\mathcal A_\mu$, all the corresponding inhomogeneous signs flip.

## Chiral matter is the essential twist

The subscript in $SU(2)_L$ is not merely a historical flourish. It means that the weak-isospin factor acts on left-handed fermions. In the minimal Standard Model, one generation has the electroweak representation data

| Field | $SU(2)_L$ representation | Hypercharge $Y$ | Electric charges from $Q=T^3+Y$ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| $Q_L=(u_L,d_L)^T$ | $\mathbf{2}$ | $1/6$ | $2/3,-1/3$ |
| $u_R$ | $\mathbf{1}$ | $2/3$ | $2/3$ |
| $d_R$ | $\mathbf{1}$ | $-1/3$ | $-1/3$ |
| $L_L=(\nu_L,e_L)^T$ | $\mathbf{2}$ | $-1/2$ | $0,-1$ |
| $e_R$ | $\mathbf{1}$ | $-1$ | $-1$ |
| $H=(H^+,H^0)^T$ | $\mathbf{2}$ | $1/2$ | $1,0$ |

The quark fields in the table also carry color representation data under $SU(3)_c$. For example, $Q_L$, $u_R$, and $d_R$ are color triplets. That color information is invisible to the electroweak charge formula, but it matters for the full Standard Model and for anomaly cancellation.

The table uses right-handed fermion fields such as $u_R$ and $e_R$. Many anomaly calculations instead rewrite every fermion as a left-handed Weyl field, replacing $u_R$ by its left-handed conjugate $u^c_L$. In that notation the hypercharges are conjugated. Same physics, different bookkeeping. Tiny notation goblin, large confusion potential.

## Why hypercharge assignments look strange

The hypercharges in the Standard Model are not guessed separately for each particle. They are constrained by several requirements:

- the observed electric charges must be reproduced by $Q=T^3+Y$;
- the Higgs doublet must have a neutral component that can take a vacuum expectation value;
- Yukawa interactions such as $\bar Q_LHd_R$ and $\bar Q_L\widetilde H u_R$ must be gauge invariant;
- gauge and mixed gravitational anomalies must cancel.

This page focuses on the first two requirements. The later Flavor and Neutrinos chapter explains Yukawa couplings, and the Standard Model anomaly chapter explains anomaly cancellation.

The Higgs doublet is especially important. With

$$
H=\begin{pmatrix}H^+\\ H^0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
Y_H=\frac12,
$$

its components have

$$
Q(H^+)=\frac12+\frac12=1,
\qquad
Q(H^0)=-\frac12+\frac12=0.
$$

Thus a vacuum of the form

$$
\langle H\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}0\\ v\end{pmatrix}
$$

preserves $Q$ but not $T^1$, $T^2$, $T^3$, or $Y$ separately. That single fact is the seed of the whole electroweak mass and mixing structure.

## Gauge basis versus physical basis

Before electroweak symmetry breaking, the natural fields are

$$
W_\mu^1,
\quad
W_\mu^2,
\quad
W_\mu^3,
\quad
B_\mu.
$$

After the Higgs vacuum is chosen, it is natural to define charged combinations

$$
W_\mu^\pm=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}(W_\mu^1\mp iW_\mu^2),
$$

and neutral combinations

$$
A_\mu=\sin\theta_W\,W_\mu^3+\cos\theta_W\,B_\mu,
$$

$$
Z_\mu=\cos\theta_W\,W_\mu^3-\sin\theta_W\,B_\mu.
$$

The weak mixing angle is defined by

$$
\tan\theta_W=\frac{g'}{g},
$$

so that

$$
e=g\sin\theta_W=g'\cos\theta_W.
$$

Then the photon couples to

$$
eQ=e(T^3+Y).
$$

This is the cleanest way to remember why the photon is the $A_\mu$ mixture and why it couples universally to electric charge. The details of the mass matrix belong to the electroweak symmetry breaking page, but the group-theory reason is already visible here.

## The electroweak Lagrangian before symmetry breaking

Ignoring color and Yukawa terms for the moment, the electroweak gauge and matter Lagrangian has the schematic form

$$
\mathcal L_{\rm EW,unbroken}
=
-\frac14W_{\mu\nu}^aW^{a\mu\nu}
-\frac14B_{\mu\nu}B^{\mu\nu}
+\sum_\psi \bar\psi i\gamma^\mu D_\mu\psi
+(D_\mu H)^\dagger D^\mu H
-V(H).
$$

The fermion sum runs over the chiral fields with their appropriate representations. In four-component notation one must remember that the covariant derivative acts differently on $\psi_L$ and $\psi_R$. For example, for a charged lepton,

$$
D_\mu L_L
=
\left(\partial_\mu+igW_\mu^a\frac{\sigma^a}{2}-\frac{i}{2}g'B_\mu\right)L_L,
$$

while

$$
D_\mu e_R
=
\left(\partial_\mu-ig'B_\mu\right)e_R.
$$

The first expression knows about weak isospin. The second does not. This asymmetry is the local-gauge-theory expression of parity violation in weak interactions.

## Why the product group is not optional

One might ask why nature did not simply gauge $U(1)_{\rm em}$ and add massive charged vector bosons by hand. The answer is that massive spin-one particles interacting with chiral currents are extremely constrained by unitarity and renormalizability. The electroweak gauge structure supplies the organizing principle that makes the theory predictive at high energy.

The product group solves several problems at once:

- it allows charged-current interactions through $T^1$ and $T^2$;
- it allows a neutral weak interaction through the combination orthogonal to the photon;
- it makes the photon emerge as the unbroken gauge boson;
- it fixes relations among gauge-boson self-interactions;
- it provides the symmetry structure needed for a renormalizable Higgs mechanism;
- it ties fermion charges, Yukawa couplings, and anomaly cancellation together.

This is why the electroweak gauge group is architecture, not decoration.

## Common pitfalls

**Calling $B_\mu$ the photon.** The hypercharge gauge boson is not the photon. The photon is $A_\mu=\sin\theta_W W_\mu^3+\cos\theta_W B_\mu$ after symmetry breaking.

**Forgetting that $SU(2)_L$ acts only on doublets.** Right-handed charged fermions are weak singlets in the minimal Standard Model. Writing $T^a$ on every fermion without checking the representation is a reliable way to manufacture nonsense.

**Using the wrong hypercharge convention.** This volume uses $Q=T^3+Y$. Some books use $Q=T^3+Y/2$. Their hypercharges are twice ours. Do not mix the tables. This is how factors of two grow teeth.

**Treating gauge symmetry as literally broken redundancy.** The Higgs vacuum does not destroy gauge redundancy. It changes the convenient variables and the spectrum. The surviving physical long-range gauge interaction is generated by $Q$.

**Confusing weak isospin with ordinary spin.** Weak isospin is an internal gauge representation label. It has nothing to do with spatial rotations or Lorentz spin.

**Dropping color too aggressively.** Color is a spectator in many electroweak formulas, but quarks carry both color and electroweak charges. Counting color is essential in loops, anomalies, and decay rates.

**Mixing right-handed fields with left-handed conjugate notation.** In four-component notation $u_R$ has $Y=2/3$. In all-left-handed Weyl notation the conjugate field $u_L^c$ has $Y=-2/3$. Both are correct in their own languages.

**Forgetting the global-form caveat.** The local formula $SU(2)_L\times U(1)_Y$ is enough for the covariant derivative and perturbative electroweak interactions. The exact global gauge group of the full Standard Model involves color and possible discrete quotients, so it belongs in the later Standard Model global-form page.

## Research status

The local $SU(2)_L\times U(1)_Y$ gauge structure of the Standard Model is established. Its central predictions include charged weak currents, neutral weak currents, the photon–$Z$ mixture, massive $W$ and $Z$ bosons, and gauge-boson self-interactions.

Active work lies mostly at the interfaces: precision electroweak fits, possible deviations in Higgs and gauge couplings, neutrino-mass completions, SMEFT operator constraints, flavor puzzles, and models in which the electroweak sector is embedded in a larger symmetry or emerges from more microscopic dynamics.

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Charges of a weak doublet

Let $\Phi=(\phi_1,\phi_2)^T$ be an $SU(2)_L$ doublet with hypercharge $Y$. Using $T^3=\operatorname{diag}(1/2,-1/2)$ and $Q=T^3+Y$, find the electric charges of $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$. Apply the result to $L_L$, $Q_L$, and $H$.

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

For a doublet,

$$
Q\Phi
=
\begin{pmatrix}
Y+\frac12 & 0\\
0 & Y-\frac12
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}\phi_1\\ \phi_2\end{pmatrix}.
$$

So

$$
Q(\phi_1)=Y+\frac12,
\qquad
Q(\phi_2)=Y-\frac12.
$$

For $L_L$ with $Y=-1/2$,

$$
Q(\nu_L)=0,
\qquad
Q(e_L)=-1.
$$

For $Q_L$ with $Y=1/6$,

$$
Q(u_L)=\frac23,
\qquad
Q(d_L)=-\frac13.
$$

For $H$ with $Y=1/2$,

$$
Q(H^+)=1,
\qquad
Q(H^0)=0.
$$

</details>

### Exercise 2: Hypercharge gauge covariance

A field of hypercharge $Y$ transforms as $\psi\to e^{i\beta(x)Y}\psi$. With

$$
D_\mu=\partial_\mu+ig'YB_\mu,
$$

show that $D_\mu\psi$ transforms in the same way if

$$
B_\mu\to B_\mu-\frac{1}{g'}\partial_\mu\beta.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Compute

$$
D_\mu'\psi'
=
\left(\partial_\mu+ig'YB_\mu'\right)e^{i\beta Y}\psi.
$$

This is

$$
e^{i\beta Y}\partial_\mu\psi
+i(\partial_\mu\beta)Y e^{i\beta Y}\psi
+ig'Y\left(B_\mu-\frac{1}{g'}\partial_\mu\beta\right)e^{i\beta Y}\psi.
$$

The two terms proportional to $\partial_\mu\beta$ cancel. Therefore

$$
D_\mu'\psi'
=
e^{i\beta Y}\left(\partial_\mu+ig'YB_\mu\right)\psi
=
e^{i\beta Y}D_\mu\psi.
$$

This confirms gauge covariance with the sign convention used on this page.

</details>

### Exercise 3: The photon coupling

Assume that after symmetry breaking

$$
A_\mu=s_WW_\mu^3+c_WB_\mu,
\qquad
Z_\mu=c_WW_\mu^3-s_WB_\mu,
$$

where $s_W=\sin\theta_W$ and $c_W=\cos\theta_W$. Show that the coefficient of $A_\mu$ in the neutral covariant derivative is $ieQ A_\mu$ if

$$
e=gs_W=g'c_W.
$$

<details><summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Invert the rotation:

$$
W_\mu^3=c_WZ_\mu+s_WA_\mu,
\qquad
B_\mu=-s_WZ_\mu+c_WA_\mu.
$$

The neutral part of the covariant derivative is

$$
i g T^3W_\mu^3+i g'YB_\mu.
$$

The terms proportional to $A_\mu$ are

$$
i\left(gs_WT^3+g'c_WY\right)A_\mu.
$$

If $e=gs_W=g'c_W$, this becomes

$$
ie(T^3+Y)A_\mu=ieQA_\mu.
$$

So the massless field $A_\mu$ couples universally to electric charge.

</details>
